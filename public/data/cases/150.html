Motifs et décision Person who is the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX Personne en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, ON Appel instruit / entendu à Date of decision April 26, 2021 Date de la décision Panel L. Krajcovic Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Alastair Clarke Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX (Appellant) is a citizen of France and the Philippines. He appeals a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) rejecting his claim for refugee protection. DECISION [2] I dismiss the appeal. The RPD was correct in finding that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. BACKGROUND [3] The Appellant's allegations are as follows. The Appellant fears return to France because he fears racial profiling and Islamophobia and that he would not receive help from police. The Appellant fears return to the Philippines because he was stabbed by a relative there in 2013. The Appellant fears return to Kuwait because his father lives there and was abusive towards him. In 2017, the Appellant left Kuwait to study in Canada. He no longer has status in Kuwait. [4] The Appellant signed his Basis of Claim (BOC) form on April 1, 2019. On January 2, 2020, the RPD heard his claim. The RPD rejected the claim on the basis of state protection in France. The Appellant submits that the RPD member erred by denying his request for an adjournment, in its state protection assessment, in not assessing his claim against the Philippines, and in relying on irrelevant facts which led to prejudice and rejection of his claim. NEW EVIDENCE The evidence presented by the Appellant is rejected [5] The Appellant requests to submit new evidence on appeal. According to the law,1 I can only accept evidence that: i. arose after the RPD decision; or ii. was not reasonably available at the time of the decision; or iii. that you could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to bring to the RPD before the decision. [6] If the evidence meets one or more of these requirements, I must decide if the evidence is new, credible and relevant before I can accept it.2 [7] The Appellant requests to submit three items as new evidence: a. Article, "France ends state of emergency after nearly 2 years," dated December 28, 2017;3 b. Article, "Why the French left has a problem with Islamophobia," dated 2019;4 and c. Article, "Constructing Muslims in France: Judicial incertitude, social indignities, and political suspicion,"5 date not specified. Items (a) and (b) are rejected [8] Items (a) and (b) predate the RPD decision. The Appellant does not explain why items (a) and (b) were not reasonably available before the decision or why he could not have been expected to have brought them to the RPD before the decision. As a result, items (a) and (b) do not meet the legislative requirements for new evidence and they are not admitted into evidence. Item (c) is rejected [9] The date of item (c) is not specified. However, even if item (c) were to meet the legislative requirements on the basis that it postdates the RPD decision, it does not meet the jurisprudential requirements as it is not new as there is evidence regarding the treatment of Muslims in France in the National Documentation Package (NDP) which forms part of the RPD record. As a result, item (c) is not admitted into evidence. ORAL HEARING [10] The Appellant requests an oral hearing. However, as there is no new evidence admitted on appeal, there can be no oral hearing pursuant to subsection 110(6) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). ROLE OF THE REFUGEE APPEAL DIVISION (RAD) [11] The RAD applies a standard of correctness for questions of fact, mixed fact and law, and law. I may defer to the RPD in assessing findings involving the credibility of oral testimony if I find that it had a meaningful advantage in the circumstances.6 In this case, I find that the RPD did not have a meaningful advantage. ANALYSIS There was no breach of procedural fairness [12] The RPD member denied the Appellant's request for a change of date and time for the hearing.7 [13] The Appellant submits that the RPD member refused him the right to counsel and to gather evidence. The Appellant submits that the RPD member provided misconceptions of the postponement letter which indicates that he was awaiting a response from Legal Aid after his application was mistakenly closed. The Appellant submits that the RPD member presumed that his crippling financial status led to the unavailability of counsel on time. [14] I find that there was no breach of procedural fairness. I have reviewed Chairperson Guideline 6: Scheduling or Changing the Date or Time of a Proceeding, which states: 3.6.1 The IRB recognizes that parties have the right to be represented by counsel, but this right is not absolute. The opportunity to retain counsel is not unlimited. The parties and any counsel they choose to retain must be ready and able to appear and proceed according to the scheduling requirements of the division and requirements of the legislation. ... 3.8.2 The IRB endeavours to inform self-represented parties of its process sufficiently in advance of the proceeding to help ensure that they will be ready to proceed on the scheduled date. This includes informing them of the right to be represented by counsel. The IRB expects self-represented parties to be prepared to present their case on the scheduled date. ... 3.12.1 The fact that a party is waiting for an application for legal aid to be approved is not generally a sufficient reason to allow an application to change the date or time of a proceeding. However, members may consider delays in the processing of legal aid applications that were beyond the control of the party.8 [15] At the hearing, the Appellant told the RPD member that he was waiting on a response from legal aid and was gathering more documents related to his risk in Kuwait and the Philippines. The RPD member asked him if there were any other reasons related to the request to adjourn, such as medical reasons, to which the Appellant responded that there were not. The RPD member noted that the Appellant was unable to retain a lawyer, partly due to financial considerations and that he had made his claim in April 2019 and refused the application to change the date of the hearing.9 [16] I see no error in the RPD member's refusal of the Appellant's application to change the date of his hearing. The Appellant arrived in Canada in 2017. He filed his refugee claim on April 1, 2019. The first page of the BOC form10 informs claimants that they have the right to be represented by counsel and that they must be available on the date fixed for the hearing. The RPD heard the Appellant's claim on January 2, 2020, nine months later. I find that the Appellant was informed of his right to be represented by counsel sufficiently in advance of his hearing date and that he had ample time to obtain counsel. I also note that the Appellant speaks English fluently, as evidenced by his BOC form11 and him testifying without an interpreter at his RPD hearing. [17] The Guideline states that an adjournment does not have to be granted on the basis that a claimant is waiting for approval from legal aid. Even if the Appellant was awaiting a response from Legal Aid after his application was mistakenly closed, he had nine months from the time he filed his refugee claim to the hearing date. It is the Appellant's responsibility to diligently exercise his right to counsel.12 Considering the foregoing, I find that the Appellant failed to establish that he was denied his right to counsel. [18] Based on the above timelines, I also find that the Appellant had sufficient time to obtain documents. Further, I note that, at the hearing, the RPD member explained to the Appellant that, if there was still a need for the documents after hearing his testimony, he could apply to submit post-hearing disclosure.13 The Appellant stated that he was trying to obtain more documents related to his risk in Kuwait and the Philippines. However, as the RPD member found that the determinative issue was state protection in France, there was no need for documents related to risk in Kuwait and the Philippines. [19] Although the right to counsel is not absolute, the right to a hearing is.14 I have considered whether the Appellant was denied the right to a fair hearing. The Appellant provided disclosure on the date of the hearing, which the RPD member accepted into evidence despite it being late.15 I have reviewed the transcript of the hearing and find that the RPD member fully explained the hearing process to the Appellant. The RPD member explained the procedure to apply for a change in date and time and for the request to submit late disclosure,16 identified the issues,17 explained the concept of state protection,18 provided time for the Appellant to consider any other reasons he could not get help from police in France,19 explained the purpose of final submissions and offered the Appellant a break to prepare his submissions.20 I find that the RPD member took the steps necessary to ensure that the Appellant was able to meaningfully participate in his hearing. I find that there was no breach of procedural fairness or natural justice. The RPD did not err its state protection assessment [20] The RPD found that adequate state protection is available to the Appellant in France. [21] The Appellant submits that the RPD member did not know the situation in France, assumed that state protection was adequate and provided the wrong reasons to reject the claim. The Appellant submits that the RPD member contradicted herself by stating that state protection may or may not be operationally effective. The Appellant submits that the RPD member erred in stating that official impunity is not widespread in France but that all the evidence he provided indicates otherwise. The Appellant submits that Muslims and coloured people are constantly attacked in France. The Appellant submits that his civil status does not protect him from discrimination, racial attacks and human right violations by French people in France. [22] I reject the Appellant's submission that the RPD member did not know the situation in France and assumed that state protection was adequate. The RPD member's reasons make it clear that the finding that there is adequate state protection for the Appellant in France is based upon the objective documentary evidence. The RPD member's reasons refer to objective evidence stating that authorities in France, including police, provide protection to ethnic and religious minorities and that the government investigated and prosecuted numerous crimes and other actions against religious groups, including Muslims. The RPD member also refers to the documentary evidence indicating that observers consider police in France generally effective and that official impunity is not widespread. The RPD member reviews the objective documentary evidence and correctly applies the principles of state protection. I find that the RPD member did not err in her assessment of state protection. [23] I reject the Appellant's submission that the RPD member contradicted herself by stating that state protection may or may not be operationally effective. In my review of the RPD member's reasons, I note that, when defining the test for state protection, the RPD member states "the test is not perfect state protection, it's described as adequate. Whether or not it's operationally effective."21 The RPD member proceeds to consider the objective documentary evidence and conclude that state protection in France is adequate and operationally effective.22 This is confirmed again later in the reasons when the RPD member states that there is operationally effective state protection for you in your particular circumstances and that your fears of inadequate state protection... [or in] not being able to go to the police are speculative and are not supported by sufficient objective evidence.23 [24] I reject the Appellant's submission that all the evidence that he provided indicates that official impunity is widespread in France. I have reviewed the evidence submitted by the Appellant, namely the 2018 Human Rights Watch report on France24 and the United States Department of State (US DOS) "2016 Report on International Religious Freedom - France"25 and do not find evidence of widespread official impunity. This is consistent with the NDP that was before the RPD and the most current NDP. As noted by the RPD member, the US DOS Human Rights report indicates that official impunity is not widespread in France.26 [25] Contrary to the Appellant's submissions that "Muslims and coloured people are constantly attacked" in France,27 the US DOS Human Rights report states: Government observers and NGOs, including the French Council for the Muslim Religion and the Collective against Islamophobia, reported a number of anti-Muslim incidents during the year, including slurs against Muslims, attacks on mosques, and physical assaults. The number of registered violent acts of racism against Muslims rose from 67 in 2016 to 73 in 2017. Over the same period, threats against the Muslim community declined by 58.5 percent, while total anti-Muslim acts declined 34.5 percent, from 185 to 121.28 [26] The US DOS International Religious Freedom report states: Religiously motivated crimes and other incidents against Jews and Muslims occurred, including killings or attempted killings, beatings, threats, hate speech, discrimination, and vandalism. ... According to government statistics, there were 100 crimes targeting Muslims, including an attack against Muslim worshippers outside a mosque, a 17 percent decrease compared with the 121 in 2017. The government also reported an additional 51 acts against Muslim places of worship or cemeteries.29 ... According to the latest government estimates available, the MOI reported registered crimes targeting Muslims (threats or violence) totaled 100, down from 121 in 2017; there were an additional 45 acts of vandalism against Muslim places of worship and six acts of desecration against Muslim cemeteries.30 [27] While I acknowledge that the documentary evidence indicates that discrimination and attacks against Muslims occur in France and that the Appellant being a citizen of France does not protect him from such incidents, considering the small number of incidents and France's Muslim population of 5.72 million people,31 these incidents are rare. I find that there is not a serious possibility that the Appellant would be the victim of such an attack. Further, if the Appellant were to be the victim of such an attack and approach authorities for assistance, I find that adequate protection would be available to him. The RPD was not prejudicial nor did it rely on irrelevant facts to reject the claim [28] The RPD member found that there was not sufficient evidence that, as a result of his particular circumstances, he would be suspected of being a terrorist or of any kind of extremism. [29] The Appellant submits that the RPD member stated facts that were irrelevant to the claim and provided those as reasons to reject his claim. The Appellant submits that, during the hearing, he was asked questions regarding his wife's religion and ethnicity, the religion of his future children, the percentage of non-religious population in France and his level of practise of Islam. The Appellant submits that the statements led to prejudice and rejection of his claim. The Appellant submits that the RPD member speculated that as a French citizen he would not be racially profiled. [30] I reject the Appellant's submissions and find that the RPD did not err. I have reviewed the transcript of the hearing as part of my independent assessment. While I agree that the RPD member asked the Appellant about his ethnicity, his wife's religion and ethnicity, the religion of his future children and his practise of Islam, these questions are relevant to the Appellant's claim that he fears what has happened to others who have been accused of Islamic extremism in France. Contrary to the Appellant's submission that the RPD member engaged in speculation regarding his profile, the RPD member assessed whether there was sufficient evidence that the Appellant could be suspected of being a terrorist or of any kind of extremism. As stated by the RPD member, the evidence indicates that the Appellant has a moderate practise of his faith and therefore, that there was not sufficient evidence that his circumstances and how he practices his faith would, on a balance of probabilities, result in him being suspected of being a terrorist or of any kind of extremism.32 I find the Appellant's submission that the RPD member was prejudicial to be without merit. The RPD did not err in not assessing the Appellant's claim against the Philippines [31] The RPD member found that, as there was adequate state protection for the Appellant in France, he was not a Convention refugee or person in need of protection. [32] The Appellant submits that the RPD member disregarded the claim against the Philippines even though she promised to assess both countries. [33] I reject the Appellant's submission and find that the RPD member did not err. At the beginning of the hearing, the RPD member informed the Appellant that she would be assessing the claims against the Philippines and France,33 which is correct, as the Appellant is a national of these countries. In Ward,34 the Supreme Court of Canada stated that, if a claimant is a national of more than one country, the claimant must show that he or she is a Convention refugee with respect to all such countries. Therefore, once the RPD member found that the Appellant was not a Convention refugee with respect to France, it was unnecessary to consider the claim against the Philippines. CONCLUSION [34] I dismiss the appeal and confirm the decision of the RPD that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA.
