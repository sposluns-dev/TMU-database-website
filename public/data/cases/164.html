Section 96 - Convention Refugee Grounds [57] Given that the evidence with respect to the allegations of harm has been found to be unreliable, what remains is that the claimants are Roma. The claimants state a history of mistreatment. The claimants experienced ongoing harassment, abuse and discrimination because they are Roma. The claimants fear growing anti-Roma sentiment in society and particularly among various groups including Jobbik, a political party, and the Hungarian Guard or similar such organizations which have increased violence against Roma, will lead to their being attacked. [58] The question that remains is whether there is more than a mere possibility the claimants would suffer persecution just because they are Roma. If so, whether there is adequate state protection for them. [59] The answer to these questions needs to be viewed in the context of the country conditions for Roma in Hungary. [60] Whether the harm feared amounts to persecution is questionable. There is documentary evidence that indicates Roma suffer various forms of mistreatment including discrimination in Hungary.22 In some ways the claimants did not experience the forms of mistreatment experienced by other Roma. XXXX was employed as a XXXX, and XXXX stated that XXXX was employed with the XXXX XXXX while she stayed at home23 and she also XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX. This was enough for their needs. In contrast Roma generally experience a much higher rate or unemployment in Hungary. They went to school and received basic education, although they suffered discrimination and sometimes got into physical confrontations. Out in public they suffered name calling, and were always watched where they went into shops as if they were criminals. They were denied service in shops or restaurants on occasion, which they resolved by going to other shops. They were not accepted. They were stopped by police for ID checks. [61] As distasteful and despicable unequal treatment for simply being is, and while there is no doubt that mistreatment they suffered was difficult to endure, it was not serious enough to amount to persecution. Even when considered cumulatively, there is no substantial impact on any of their fundamental human rights, and their existence is not threatened in any significant way. [62] In considering the isolated acts of violence combined with the other sorts of mistreatment, does this now cumulatively rise to the level of persecution? In this case, the harm is not serious or systematic. Fundamental human rights were not significantly impacted and their existence was not threatened. There does not appear to be a methodical effort to mistreat the claimants because they are Roma, but only random acts of non-serious violence. In my view, in the circumstances of this case, this does not amount to persecution. [63] Even if the harm feared is not serious, cumulatively, the effect of all this mistreatment in society results in discouragement and disenfranchisement. Undoubtedly the claimants and Roma in general, do not feel that they are part of the Hungarian society and they are in fact treated differently in many aspects of their lives. When the mistreatment is considered together, it may in some cases rise to the level of serious and systematic harm that would threaten the specific person's future existence or lead to the violation or enjoyment of their core fundamental rights. Here, the claimants suffered from the general climate of intolerance and even disdain for the Roma. [64] The claimants rely on general country conditions for the Roma in Hungary and similarly situated people to support their contention that there is more than a mere possibility of persecution should they return. Sadly, the attitude of some of Hungarian people toward the Roma is certainly discriminatory and prejudicial. While difficult to understand, accept and endure, generally, this does not amount to persecution. The general attitude of some segments of society may have an impact on the claimant's future existence if they become so prevalent, persistent and systematic that their actions confound fundamental human rights. [65] The case may be made that for many Roma they are relegated to subsistence living. Their standard of living is typically much lower than many non-Roma. They are under-educated; they are more frequently under-employed than non-Roma and experience higher unemployment; their housing arrangements are poorer and of a lower standard than non-Roma. This, over time, has caused the Roma to be disadvantaged and this disadvantage perpetuates the poorer conditions for the Roma. Reliance on the state to survive and turning to what we would call the underground economy in some cases then causes resentment in the general population and which further perpetuates poorer conditions and mistreatment of Roma particularly in difficult economic times. There are certain groups, some political groups, which would capitalize on these particular circumstances for their own advantage and this further contributes to the hardship and general intolerance of the Roma. [66] To consider the cumulative effect of all of this is to consider the interplay of complex social issues, including the role of the state in relieving the hardship. Further, the role of the state cannot simply be an examination of how police respond, although this is certainly an indication of state protection. In cases such as these, the protection of the state needs to be assessed more globally to determine whether all relevant state agencies would be forthcoming with serious efforts at adequate protection as an indication of the willingness of the state to offer protection, combined with the operational effect as an indication of the ability of the state to offer protection. Combined the efforts and effects of protection of the state for Roma in Hungary are not indicative of persecution and offset the cumulative effects of mistreatment so that it does not rise to the level of persecution. The documentary evidence indicates that not all Roma are persecuted simply for being Roma. Below is a summary of the state's efforts and the effects. [67] Discrimination is a part of societal behaviour, but when that discrimination threatens a person's fundamental rights or future existence, it then becomes persecution. Generally, the efforts of the state indicate a strong willingness to protect the Roma. The operational effect of those efforts is somewhat more questionable and the evidence mixed. The documentary evidence is mixed with respect to the effect the numerous programs of assistance available to the Roma is having, but serious efforts are being made and there are signs of some positive effects. It should be considered that the battle against discrimination cannot be assessed in a similar way that a battle against crime could. Where the number of arrests and decreases in crime rates may be indicators of progress, with discrimination it is societal attitudes that must change. It is therefore fair to look at what serious efforts the state is making to change those attitudes in addition to what action is being taken against those that perpetrate discrimination that rises to the level of persecution. [68] However, we need also to consider the magnitude of the problem at hand and keep in perspective that any progress made is an achievement. In this case it is not that there is a threat to the claimants' future existence, but it is a matter of the quality of that existence is different than that of other nationals of Hungary. The substantial efforts of the state to provide protection and the small gains made indicate that the Roma, in general, are not being persecuted. [69] The claimants fear mistreatment and unequal treatment because they are Roma. If the claimants face such mistreatment they have recourse to all these programs for assistance. Programs for Inclusion [70] There is evidence that the state does not condone and for the most part does not acquiesce to the racial and discriminatory behaviour by the authorities or the public. The state has undertaken serious and a substantial effort to ensure the future existence of the Roma, and other minorities, is protected. Documentary evidence indicates there are a number of programs developed to assist Roma to find work, to obtain education and with housing. [71] Hungary has laws to combat discrimination wherever it is faced by Roma. The 1993 law for the protection of minorities was reinforced in 2003 with the law on equal treatment and promoting equal opportunity. Then in January 2005 the Equal Treatment Authority was established to supervise proper implementation of law and is empowered to put a stop to contraventions and to impose fines and penalties under other legislation as applicable.24 [72] In April 2007 the state adopted the Strategic Plan of the Decade of Roma Inclusion Programme. The overall objectives of the program are in four priority areas including education, employment, housing and health care with regard to the enforcement of equal treatment, and also in the fields of culture, media and sports.25 Most ministries and county labour affairs centers had special officers for Romani affairs focused on the needs of the Romani community. The Ministry of Education and Culture continued to offer financial incentives to encourage schools to integrate Romani and non-Romani children in the same classrooms and to reintegrate Roma inappropriately placed in remedial programs. The Ministry of Social Affairs and Labor operated a program to finance infrastructure development in Romani communities. The Ministry of Public Administration and Justice (formerly the Ministry of Justice and Law Enforcement) operated an antidiscrimination legal service network that provided free legal aid to Roma in cases where they encountered ethnic discrimination.26 Roma, like the other 12 official minorities, are entitled to elect their own minority self-governments (MSGs), which organize minority activities and handle cultural and educational affairs. The president of each MSG also has the right to attend and speak at local government assemblies.27 [73] In 2011 the state secretary for social integration at the Ministry of Public Administration and Justice continued to play a critical role in advancing Roma affairs within the government. The office harmonized the government's inclusion policy as well as that governing Roma-related government programs (e.g., scholarships, Decade of Roma Inclusion Program). The Ministry of National Resources continued to offer financial incentives to encourage schools to integrate Romani and non-Romani children in the same classrooms and to reintegrate Roma inappropriately placed in remedial programs. [74] On November 30, 2011 the cabinet approved the National Social Inclusion Strategy. The national strategy identifies specific actions the government aims to take to reduce the percentage of the population living under the poverty line, integrate Roma into the labor market, and increase the level of education of Roma. On December 13, 2011 the cabinet adopted the Governmental Action Plan for the implementation of the National Social Inclusion Strategy for 2012-2014.28 [75] In September 2012 the government established the 27-member Roma Coordination Council, chaired by the minister for public administration and justice and co-chaired by the head of national Roma self-government. The new council includes representatives of local Roma self-governments, NGOs, and churches. Employment [76] Under-employment and unemployment of the Roma is recognized as a serious problem that has serious social implications. To address this, the state has implemented a number of programs. Since 2003, billions of forints have been dedicated to employment and opportunity programs to promote employment and training to unemployed Roma. In 2006 the Regional Training Centre in Pécs carried out a complex program in employment with assistance from the National Council for Adult Education to improve the chances of the Roma. Since 2003 there have been 55 complex programs under the auspices of the labour centres to help those in long-term unemployment situations, including the Roma; programs continued in 2008. The government runs a number of programs to improve the employment prospects of Roma that assist Roma in finding employment, offer training sessions to help them develop marketable skills, provide financial incentives to employers who hire minorities, and provide Roma desk officers in every labour exchange centre and employment office. Employers are fined for acting in contravention to the principle of equal treatment which disqualifies them from state aid for two years. For example, in 2008 a company was fined for refusing to hire Roma as cleaners on account of their ethnicity.29 Under these kinds of programs, tens of thousands of Roma have found employment.30 [77] The government opened 200 positions in public administration for Romani college graduates. By the end of 2010, 165 applicants had passed the mandatory civil servant entry exam and 66 were placed in various national and county government offices. While perhaps small in comparison to the number of Roma that need assistance, this initiative shows that the claimant's belief that Roma are not able to obtain other types of employment is clearly not the case. There has been criticism that many of these initiatives have been taken, often with a twin aim of assisting in finding employment and in developing new skills, but these measures are often short-term and can only help a small number of Roma at a time.31 [78] Even so, it cannot be denied that the state has been making serious efforts and there has been some progress. The government has taken a number of initiatives in recent years to reduce exclusion from the labour market, including through specific programmes targeting disadvantaged people or long-term unemployed, many of whom are Roma. The ECRI states many of the measures taken to date have a positive impact and they need to be continued and in some cases intensified in order to achieve lasting results.32 Education [79] Segregation of Roma students was and continues to be a serious problem as it continues to lower education standards and rates for the Roma and, thus contributes to under-employment and poverty. However, wide-ranging programs address segregation through facilitating access to kindergarten, introducing stricter requirements how local authorities draw the boundaries between catchment areas or may organize the composition of classes within schools, and the drawing up of new cognitive tests designed to take better account of cultural differences and socioeconomic disadvantage in testing children's development.33 However, the efforts of the central authorities are frequently hampered by the manner in which local authorities interpret the measures and translate them into practice. Flaws in the assessment system allow for further segregation and parents of Roma and non-Roma contribute by wilfully moving their children to preferred schools. [80] Although progress is much slower than anyone would hope for, there are positive signs. According to the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC), desegregation efforts by the national government in Hungary have been amongst the strongest in the Central European region including efforts to subsidizes schools that integrate Roma and non-Roma in the same classroom or reintegrate Roma inappropriately placed in remedial programs, getting schools to accept disadvantaged children in order to prevent non-Romani parents from withdrawing their children from certain schools and a scholarship program for young Roma which in 2007-2008 there were 11,352 recipients.34 The courts in Hungary have consistently ruled in favour of Roma children against segregation. On 19 November 2008 the Supreme Court upheld a provision of the ruling of first instance in a school segregation lawsuit against primary schools in Hajdúhadháza, according to which two primary schools of the municipality violated the law in an act of illegal segregation and the Ministry of Education and Culture issued a statement agreeing with the judgment.35 In 2010 the Supreme Court issued compensation for segregated children recognizing the value of education and cost of segregation.36 This indicates the justice system, at the highest levels, are sending a message that this type of discrimination cannot continue without costs. [81] These positive signs have not reversed segregation. Hungary acknowledges that there is a lack of equality in access to quality education for Roma pupils, and adds that problems characteristic of a significant proportion of the Roma minority are not due mostly to their ethnic origin, but to their social and health-related status. Regardless of the reasons, efforts to integrate Roma students have not been successful at reducing segregation, but instead it has increased.37 This indicates a failure of the state to provide equal access to education, but not that it denies Roma of education or that it persecutes Roma or that it has given up on the Roma. Social inclusion is a complicated issue and even when best efforts are made, there may be times when programs do not produced desired results. Housing [82] There are several ghettos in Hungary and many of them are predominately populated with Roma. Roma are concentrated in economically disadvantaged areas in rural communities or urban slums. Access to public housing is often contingent upon financial resources which many Roma do not have and the Roma are generally not aware of their rights or judicial recourse available to them.38 The authorities have stated their aim is to reduce segregation in housing by eliminating Roma ghettoes. A program was introduced to refurbish social housing and encourage Roma living in segregated settlements outside towns and villages to move into the refurbished social housing inside the town or village. Further, to ensure that the principle of desegregation is taken into account in the award of state or European Union funding for various projects, the authorities are introducing an equal opportunities subsidy policy. To receive funding for urban development projects, whether or not these are directly related to desegregation efforts, a town will be required to submit a desegregation plan aimed at the elimination of segregated living in the town.39 There are, however, reports of occasional resistance from local authorities and individuals when a Roma family has sought to move into a new neighbourhood sometimes causing damage. Societal attitudes are not easy or quick to change, but the work of the authorities is ongoing and persistent. Although progress is slow, the serious efforts of the state and progress achieved so far must be acknowledged. [83] In 2007, the United Nations reported that the Hungarian government was financing housing renovations in nine Romani localities, "dramatically improving living conditions for some families".40 NGO Roma Civil Rights Foundation (RCRF) accused some municipalities of using a variety of methods to prevent Roma from moving to more desirable neighbourhoods such as auctioning off social housing units to the highest bidder, or evicting Roma from housing under renovation and providing inadequate compensation. On the other hand, authorities will take action against such a manoeuvre. For example, in 2009, the Budapest Court fined the 2nd district of Budapest about 400 Euros per capita for having evicted 40 Roma, half of whom were children, from housing that they were occupying without entitlement.41 [84] Despite these efforts the situation for Roma living in ghettos has worsened over the last decade. Racism [85] There is of course criticism that the efforts are meaningless and that racism prevails. The Supreme Court decision in Hungary which said radio stations erred by not allowing an advertisement referring to "Gypsy crime" to be made in an election might show that racist attitudes pervade even the highest levels in the judiciary. However, the Jobbik Party, often cited as having racist attitudes and policies, wanted to use the phrase "Gypsy criminals" referring to how the Roma are free to do what they want while the rest of Hungarians are fearful of them. The National Election Committee ruled that the radio stations breached election fairness by not allowing the advertisements. The court's decision has to be understood for what it was. The court simply upheld the right of a political party to have equal time for campaigning and that the broadcasters are not responsible for the content. This court case highlights the difficult decisions that are made between competing values. It is understandable that in a former communist country where expression was seriously repressed, there would be a heightened awareness towards the protection of the political exchange. [86] The Minorities Ombudsman receives 800 to 1,000 complaints annually (2011). In 2010, 75 percent of those complaints were related to the Roma and included charges of racial discrimination and unequal treatment (10.4). In 2009, out of 1,012 complaints, 69 complained about the police, 34 the penitentiaries, and 54 the courts; in 2010, out of 1,064 complaints, 73 were police-related, 34 penitentiary-related, and 45 court-related. The annual report on the Minorities Ombudsman's activities indicates that in 2009, out of 1,012 complaints, 603 (61%) involved the Roma, and in 2010, 739 (70%) of 1,064 complaints were Roma-related. On January 1, 2012, the four ombudsmen will be replaced by one ombudsman, the Commissioner of Fundamental Rights, in accordance with Hungary's new constitution. [87] There is also recourse to the Equal Treatment Authority. Since 2005 it has provided individuals with a direct avenue of redress for violations of the prohibition of discrimination in a variety of public- and private-law relationships. In the first year, there were nearly 500 complaints lodged and the number has risen steadily every since.42 In 2010, the ETA received 1,300 complaints and started investigations into 377 of them. Discrimination was determined to have taken place in 40 cases. Most of the cases were complaints of employment-related discrimination, but there were also instances of racism in the public service.43 There are also other remedies such as seeking compensation through the courts, or turning to one of the Parliamentary Commissioners where public authorities are concerned.44 [88] Several sources already cited indicate the efforts are only theoretical but with little real effect. The same lament is repeated in documentary evidence provided by the claimants such as in the declaration provided by Mr. Horvath.45 The documentation indicates that progress is slow. The fact that progress is slow is indication that while not as dramatic or rapid as anyone would wish, there is progress. The effects of the programs for inclusion are not as spectacular as anyone would hope. None the less the willingness of the state is unquestionable and unwavering. The effects, hampered by socio-economic conditions, are still positive in many respects. As far as a measure of whether the Roma suffer persecution in Hungary, the above efforts and effects tend to indicate that the mistreatment is not systemic or serious, and that not all Roma suffer persecution in Hungary. State Protection [89] If I am mistaken, and I do not believe that I am, and the harm feared or anticipated in this case does rise to the level of persecution, and that all Roma face more than a mere possibility of persecution, issue is whether the claimants have rebutted the presumption of state protection. [90] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens except in situations where the state is in a state of complete breakdown.46 To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect absent an admission by the national's state of its inability to protect that national.47 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration, the test is whether the protection offered is adequate.48 The evidence that state protection is not adequate must be reliable and probative and it must also satisfy me, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.49 The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.50 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not be enough to justify a claim that the victims are unable to avail themselves of protection.51 The fact that state protection is not perfect52 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability to provide protection.53 The burden of proof that rests on the claimant increases with the level of democracy of the state in question. The more democratic a state is, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all course of action open to them to demonstrate state protection was or would not be forthcoming.54 [91] In this case the claimants' personal evidence with respect to state response was found to be unreliable. [92] At this point the claimants were in no imminent danger. They could have gone to the police station to make a report, or even instigated a complaint against the officers. The claimants' bad experience with local police on this incident is insufficient to establish that, on a balance of probabilities, the state would not be forthcoming with protection. [93] In the context of the objective documentary evidence I need to examine whether the claimants can reasonably expect the state to be forthcoming with adequate protection or whether the state protection apparatus, and more specifically law enforcement agencies, in Hungary are so deficient as to be inadequate or that there would have been no point in making efforts to seek protection. It is the claimant's responsibility to show with clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, the state would not be forthcoming with serious efforts at adequate protection; that the state is unwilling or unable to provide adequate protection. [94] While there has been progress in many respects, some problems such as extremist violence and harsh rhetoric against ethnic and religious minority groups remain. Extremists increasingly targeted Roma, resulting in injuries and in some cases death and the police sometimes use excessive force, particularly against Roma. Discrimination against Roma in education, housing, employment, and access to social services continued.55 [95] The claimants fear extremist groups, the Jobbik Party, and society in general. The economic downturn and political opportunism of the far-right extremists who seek to blame minorities, including the Roma, for recent events, is primarily responsible for the increase in extremism enabling mistreatment and violence. Violent rhetoric from extremist groups, Jobbik, and the misguided, ill-informed, and opportunistic comments of some politicians, added to the atmosphere allowing for increased violence and societal discrimination against the Roma. Roma families are deprived access to social housing by discriminatory rules and practices of local authorities; and Roma children are still confronted with segregation in schools, which has an impact on education outcomes for these children and leaves them with correspondingly limited future life choices and employment prospects. There has been a sharp rise in racism in public discourse and anti-Roma discourse appears to be becoming increasingly virulent and wide-spread.56 [96] Documentary evidence provided by the claimants is consistent with the Board's own documentary evidence with respect to increasing crimes against the Roma, widespread discrimination and harassment. The affidavit of XXXX XXXX57 outlines the history of discrimination and the situation of the Roma in Hungary indicating that efforts to address the plight of the Roma have not resulted in significant achievements. It should be noted that documentary evidence disclosed by the claimants also concurs in or repeats the evidence disclosed by the Board with respect to the results of state protection efforts; specifically, that while there are examples of failures, there are also examples of success (with specific examples below).58 [97] The claimants fear extremist and racist groups such as the Magyar Garda. The battle against the Magyar Garda (Hungarian Guard) continued in Hungary. The Hungarian Guard was formed in 2007 following violent anti-government demonstrations in 2006. It was formed to preserve the country's traditions and culture and is considered to be the paramilitary wing of the Jobbik party. The Hungarian Guard staged marches in various towns wearing uniforms depicting "Gypsy crime" as a threat to security. After the courts ordered the dissolution of the far-right paramilitary group, they appear to have evolved into several groups with a similar disposition. [98] While neither the Hungarian Guard nor Jobbik have been implicated in any way in the murders of Roma in 2008 and 2009, the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC) claims that the presence of the Hungarian Guard and Jobbik has had a definite influence in the country, arguing that they contribute to a climate in which people feel more empowered to act out on negative feelings that they might hold and frustrations that they might have.59 [99] In Gyöngyöspata, the location of Hungarian Guard demonstrations in the past, several far-right vigilante groups have surfaced claiming that local police are unable to protect white Hungarians from Gypsy crime. Some of the groups include the Defence Force, the New Hungarian Guard, the Vagabonds for the Protection of Hungarians, and the Civil Guard Association for a Better Future. According to the Parliamentary Commissioner for National and Ethnic Minority Rights (the Minorities Ombudsman), these civil guardian patrols have been frightening the minority community of Roma. Further the Minorities Ombudsman states that because of its small and ineffective police force (in Gyöngyöspata) political forces selected the village as an ideal location for its increasing dissatisfaction over the police inefficiency, provoking conflict between Roma and non-Roma inhabitants. Perhaps the most important confrontation of 2011 was in Gyöngyöspata, where, following altercations and alleged criminality by some local Roma, paramilitary groups threatened the Roma population for weeks. The crisis ended with the deployment of police forces and the holding of a mayoral election.60 [100] In August 2012 400-500 Hungarian Guard marched through the Roma settlement on the outskirts of Cegled (southeast of Budapest) shouting slogans such as, "Dirty Gypies, we will kill you." The news items indicate that police were there in force to prevent violence and ultimately forced the withdrawal of the Guard from the area.61 [101] In April 2011 the para-military group Defence Force organized a military training camp near Gyöngyöspata's Roma neighbourhood and Hungarian Red Cross evacuated approximately 270 Roma women and children from the village. The police dissolved the training camp following a government decree that imposes stricter punishments to uniformed people that organize unauthorized patrols. The Defence Force commander, Tamas Eszes, was detained along with some of his associates although they were subsequently released when a court found there were no grounds to detain them.62 [102] In July 2009, police banned a demonstration that was to be held in Budapest. The police indicated that they would take firm action against any private individual or member of any organization, with special regard to the Hungarian Guard, who want to demonstrate. In August 2009, police initiated legal proceedings against 16 members of the Hungarian Guard for violating the ban on participation in any event of a legally disbanded civil organization. The Hungarian Guard members were standing in front of the Budapest police headquarters while the Guard's founder, Gábor Vona, was in a hearing inside the building accused of being the chief organizer of a ceremony to induct new members into the banned group.63 [103] In April 2010 charges were filed with the public prosecutor against the chief of the Hungarian Guard and two of his associates on suspicion of illegally maintaining the uniformed organization. In July 2010, police arrested more than 70 members of the Hungarian National Guard at demonstrations in Budapest that were attended by around 600 members of the organization to commemorate the banning of the Hungarian Guard. Police also arrested another 20 Hungarian National Guard members attending the same rallies because they were in possession of minor firearms. During an April 2011 anti-Roma rally, at which 600 Jobbik supporters demonstrated against "Gypsy terror" in Hejoszalonta, police in riot gear installed metal barriers to keep the Jobbik supporters away from a counter-rally by human rights groups. While this shows the pervasiveness and determination of these para-military groups, it also shows the police can and do act to protect the Roma.64 [104] The Criminal Code criminalizes incitement to hatred against any national, ethnic, racial group or other groups of population, which is punishable with imprisonment up to three years. The use of symbols of despotism, including a swastika, an arrow-cross, hammer and sickle, are punishable with a fine. Article 174/B criminalizes assaults committed because of a victim's nationality, race, ethnicity or religion and is punishable by imprisonment for up to five years.65 Police are reluctant to apply hate crime laws although there is evidence they apply other laws instead. [105] On some occasions leaders in authority, including the Parliamentary Commissioner for Civil Rights, made some disparaging remarks with respect to the Roma. In each case there was investigation and consequences. There has been effort to bolster public relations efforts to garner support for tolerance such as efforts to encourage public personalities to speak out against racism. [106] In August 2010 a member of the far-right Jobbik faction in parliament, Gyula Gyorgy Szabolcsagyva, allegedly harassed and threatened two journalists of the weekly paper Hetek during the Magyar Sziget music festival in Veroce. According to reports, Szabolcsagyva, carrying a whip, told the journalists "you should be glad that you were not beaten up." He reportedly also said it was a sign of "Jewish arrogance" that the journalists turned on their tape recorder and that he wanted to "stamp out their guts." Szabolcsagyva denied the reports. The Central Investigative Chief Prosecutor's Office launched an investigation on the basis of harassment accompanied by the threat of physical violence. On June 29, parliament waived the parliamentary immunity of Szabolcsagyva, and the prosecutor's office pressed charges against him on September 6. The case remained pending at year's end.66 [107] Police are clearly not successful in every case and in some cases still do not act in cases involving crime against Roma, or still may be unprofessional, but there are also several incidents when authorities do act to investigate and prosecute in cases of crimes against Roma. In August 2010, the National Bureau of Investigation announced the completion of its investigation into a series of physical attacks against Roma in 2008 and 2009. As a result of the investigation, the Pest County Prosecutor charged four suspects with the murder of six persons, the serious injury of five others, and threats to the safety of an additional 55.67 [108] From January 2008 until July 2011 there were a number of attacks committed against Roma which were reported by the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC).68 Although the report is not exhaustive and does not address state response to the attacks in great detail, several of the incidents mention action taken by authorities. For the period reported to March 201169 the ERRC provides information on the progress of 22 cases in Hungary in which Roma were victims of violent attacks between 2008 and 2009, with the following results: In six cases the police investigation was suspended because no suspect was identified; in one case the police investigation was suspended for lack of crime, and an investigation against the alleged victims for false testimony was opened; in 12 cases prosecution was pending; In one case the perpetrator was convicted, resulting in 11-years imprisonment; and in two cases no information was available. In more than half the cases police took some action, are investigating, and there has been a conviction. [109] While the murder of some Roma is troubling, the actions of the state clearly show serious effort to bring forward adequate protection, with more than just some results. It is clear, police can and will take action in cases involving crimes against Roma. What is clear also is that the protection is not perfect and police are sometimes unwilling or unable to assist the Roma.70 [110] The claimants complain that police hate the Roma and they are just like the rest of white Hungarians. If the claimants are not satisfied with the response of some police they have some recourse. In the first nine months of the year (2012), authorities found 3,022 police officers responsible for breaches of discipline, 766 guilty of petty offenses, 283 guilty of criminal offenses, and 10 unfit for duty. In the same period, courts sentenced four police officers to prison terms, gave suspended sentences to 39, fined 106, and dismissed 12. In the same period, courts convicted 37 officers of corruption. No information was available on the number placed on probation. [111] Victims of lesser police abuses may complain either to the alleged violator's unit or to the Independent Police Complaints Board (IPCB), which investigated violations and omissions by the police that affected fundamental rights. The five-member body, appointed by a two-thirds majority of parliament, functions independently of police authorities. At the end of 2011, the board had received 805 reports from the public. It reviewed 458 complaints (including some cases filed in 2010) and found serious legal violations in 67 and minor legal violations in 33. The board forwarded the 67 cases to the national police chief, who agreed with the findings in two cases, partially accepted the findings in three, and rejected the findings in three. The rest remained pending. The IPCB's authority is limited to making recommendations to the NPH and reporting its findings to parliament,71 with court action an option if recommendations are not followed. [112] There is the Roma Police Officers' Association in Hungary and Roma are able to file a complaint to the association. The complaints mostly concern discrimination in employment, discriminatory treatment, and discrimination by law enforcement authorities or police officers.72 [113] Of course there are allegations of police brutality made by Roma. Most of the complaints filed with the Office of the Chief Public Prosecutor are filed by Roma victims. [114] To help Roma with any problems they may encounter with discrimination, the Ministry of Public Administration and Justice (formerly the Ministry of Justice and Law Enforcement) operated an anti-discrimination legal service network that provides free legal aid to Roma in cases where they encountered ethnic discrimination.73 The expansion of the Roma Anti-Discrimination Customer Service Network in 2008 made available 30 lawyers at 47 municipalities in the country and the coordination of the program in the Ministry is ensured by a desk officer for Roma affairs. Clients can obtain information on the customer service contact details of Network lawyers in 24 hours a day. There was a nation-wide campaign promoting the network and toll-free number. Since 2004 the Network has handled more than 7,200 cases.74 However, there is criticism that the lawyers' offices were located in the larger cities, inaccessible to those Roma living in deep poverty in small villages, and there were some reports that the network's lawyers rejected some Romani cases, although we do not know on what grounds.75 In this case the claimants were from a large city. [115] In a March 2010 report to the United Nations several NGOs agreed that Hungary has one of the most advanced anti-discrimination laws and a system for minority protection in the Central and Eastern European region. A number of mechanisms have been developed to ensure that minority groups enjoy civil and political rights. However, the central government's general failure to maintain strong and effective control mechanisms over rights violations takes its toll on Hungary's largest minority, the Roma.76 They go on to say that law enforcement authorities systematically fail to provide effective protection to Roma, although there is no comment on what they mean by "effective protection". Despite the mechanisms in place to make complaints against police misconduct, clearly the Roma are not assisted well. In a September 2010 shadow report to the United Nations several NGOs agree that the Roma minority still experiences discrimination by the police during investigations and criminal proceedings including lack of information and legal help, and reluctance from the police from the very first moment when they want to report a crime. [116] However, in a March 2011 ERRC report on anti-Roma violence in Hungary, which looked at 22 cases of violence against Roma, the organization noted that police misconduct and procedural errors were documented during the investigation of one of the violent crimes against Roma, as raised by NGOs and later confirmed by the Independent Police Complaint Body and by the Head of Police. Misconduct by the National Security Service was also revealed.77 [117] Although there are instances of the failure of the state to provide protection to the Roma, there are also many instances where the state has responded to provide protection. This combined with the serious efforts to affect the lives of Roma in Hungary, lead me to conclude that the evidence is mixed. The evidence is not clear or convincing that the state would not, on a balance of probabilities, be reasonably forthcoming with adequate protection. [118] The claimants experienced mistreatment in various aspects of life. All of the above shows that there is recourse for Roma who face mistreatment in Hungary. It is reasonable for the claimants to expect the state would be reasonably forthcoming with serious efforts at protection. Unfortunately, as noted by the Council of Europe's European Commission against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI), it is a failure of the local authorities in implementing centrally enacted legislation that is at the heart of much of the mistreatment experienced by Roma in daily life.78 However, others note that Hungary has one of the most advanced systems for minority protection in the region. Despite these efforts funding routinely fails to reach groups with the greatest needs.79 [119] On the other hand, Hungary is a democracy. There is no persuasive evidence that Hungary is in a state of collapse. Hungary has fully functioning state apparatus. Although slipping slightly in its overall "Democracy Score" Hungary still scores relatively highly on all major democracy indicators.80 The burden is on the claimants to provide clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, the state would not be willing or able to provide adequate protection. Although the burden may be reduced somewhat in the situation of the Roma, the burden remains higher rather than low. [120] Evidence shows that state's efforts are serious demonstrating an ongoing willingness, and that while progress is slow, there are signs of progress. There is reliable evidence that the state does intervene to protect the Roma, but there are examples that the state also fails in some respects to provide protection that is effective at the operational level. On balance, the country documentation does not provide sufficient evidence that the protection offered would not be adequate. It is reasonable to expect the claimants to make more effort to seek protection of the state and there is sufficient evidence to indicate the state would be forthcoming with protection. The claimant has not satisfactorily rebutted the presumption of state protection. [121] Therefore, I find the claimants are not Convention refugees and their claims under section 96 of the IRPA fail. The rest of the analysis of this decision will be under section 97 of the IRPA. Section 97 - Persons in Need of Protection [122] There was no residual information that was not considered in the assessment above upon which a claim under section 97 could be assessed separately or differently. Not all Roma are persecuted, they do not all face a risk to their lives or risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a danger of torture. The claimants have not rebutted the presumption of the state's ability to protect its nationals. [123] Therefore, I find the claimants are not persons in need of protection and their claims under section 97 of the IRPA fail. Compelling Reasons s.108(4) [124] Counsel submits that if I find that there has been a change of circumstance by which the claimants would not now be refugees, I should consider whether there has been such serious harm that they should not have to return to Hungary. [125] Since I have not found the claimants were refugees when they left Hungary, and I not found that there has been a change in circumstances since the claimants left Hungary, I cannot apply the compelling reasons section of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [126] Having failed to provide any reliable persuasive evidence of personal persecution or efforts to seek protection the applicants have failed to establish that merely because they are Roma they are at risk and that there is inadequate state protection in Hungary for Roma. [127] There is not a serious possibility the claimants would face persecution in Hungary or that they would be subjected personally to a risk to their lives, a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a danger of torture. Therefore, the claimants are not Convention refugees and the claimants are not persons in need of protection. [128] The claims are rejected.
