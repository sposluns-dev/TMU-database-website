Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXXXX XXXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of Hearing June 26, 2009 Date(s) de l'audience Place of Hearing Toronto, Ontario Lieu de l'audience Date of Decision January 5, 2010 Date de la décision Panel Martin Ginsherman Tribunal Counsel for the Claimant(s) Geraldine Macdonald Conseil(s) du / de la / des demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister Conseil du ministre [1] The claimant, XXXXX XXXXX, alleges a well-founded fear of persecution in Russia and Israel. In Russia, he fears the state as he is wanted as a witness to testify against Russians who posed as Chechen terrorists. In Israel, he fears persecution because he refuses to serve in the Israeli armed forces. I will analyze his claim against Israel because I find that he will not experience persecution nor cruel or unusual treatment or punishment if returned there. ALLEGATIONS [2] The claimant alleges he married his Jewish wife and moved to Israel in 1997 to escape his problems in Russia. He and his wife lived for three years in suburban XXXXX. He experienced what can be considered harassment because people always questioned him on why he immigrated to Israel. After a while, his wife started to miss her mother and wanted to return to Russia and she never felt 'at home' in Israel. At the end of 2000, he and his wife and daughter returned to Russia. [3] The war in Chechnya had started. In 2001, he and his wife divorced. In 2003, he was called to testify in court against the Chechens who were on trial for the 1995 hostage taking. He started to receive threatening phone calls because of the evidence he wanted to give about a conspiracy. He believed that Russians had helped the Chechens in this hostage taking and state officials did not want this evidence presented in court. In late 2005, the claimant was attacked and he believes this was due to his appearance in court. Later in the year, the driver of a car tried to run him over. The hearing resumed in the summer of 2006 and the claimant was again called to testify. Instead of attending at court, he fled to Israel in XXXXX 2006. [4] He remained in Israel until XXXXX 2006. He tried to get work but his documents had expired. His bank accounts too had been closed. He had no money to live on. There was a war then and he feared being conscripted because he no longer had the exemption to military service that he had when he first arrived Israel in 1997. He borrowed money and flew to Germany. He stayed there for two months but did not institute an asylum claim. The claimant arrived in Canada on XXXXX, 2006 and made his asylum claim the same day. DETERMINATION [5] I find that the claimant is not a Convention refugee as he does not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Russia. I also find that the claimant is not a person in need of protection in that his removal to Russia would not subject him personally to a risk to his life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, and in that there are no substantial grounds to believe that his removal to Russia will subject her personally to a danger of torture. My reasons follow. ANALYSIS [6] The determinative issues are whether the claimant has established that he is a Conscientious Objector (CO) to military service in Israel. Whether the Claimant is a Conscientious Objector [7] The case law is clear that countries have the right to conscript citizens into military service1 Fear of combat is insufficient to justify a fear of persecution 2. The panel also has to look at the treatment a claimant could face if returned to their country.3 Zolfagharkhani indicates that it is not the claimant's motivation for serving that is relevant, rather it is the principal or intent of the conscription law. I have no information before me that the Israeli military draft is not a law of general application. Therefore, the claimant cannot say the conscription laws in Israel caused him a prejudice. [8] An alternative analysis posits that a conscientious objector is expressing a religious opinion 4 Counsel did not provide submissions on this aspect of the claim. I do not find that he is a conscientious objector as he alleges for his religious opinion. [9] Counsel's submissions deal with the existence of Conscientious Objection (CO) to military service in Israel. It is her submission that CO status does not exist and refers to documents in our Index for Israel March 30, 2009 to make her argument. I suggest that she is using the documents to make her argument and does not submit the documents that refute her position. By so doing, counsel is advocating for her client, which is her role. She is also arguing that the Israeli forces have acted with impunity and are the target of international condemnation. The Israeli forces are not on "trial" here, rather the matter before me is whether the claimant fulfilled his obligations to seek CO status in Israel before seeking Canada's surrogate status. [10] The claimant was conscripted into the Russian army according to his military book.5 The panel questioned the claimant why his military book stated that he is fit for military service with restrictions according to entries for 1994 and 1995. He testified that he obtained a medical deferral through a doctor and is unfit to serve. I give this answer no weight given the information in his military book. The information in the military book is that he is fit to serve, but with restrictions. This is different from 'unfit', which the claimant alleges was his status. I prefer the information in his military book that he was fit to serve in the Russian army, but with restrictions. [11] The claimant's status for military service changed in 2003 to "found fit for military service." He was given the same status in 2006. Based on this information, I find that the claimant was fit to serve and whatever medical deferment he had from 1994 to 1995 was removed. [12] The claimant served in the Russian army in similar though not identical circumstances if he were to be conscripted into the Israeli army. The circumstances are similar because Russian soldiers were fighting in Chechnya for part of the time he was a soldier. Many conscripts were sent to fight there, but not all of them. The claimant's allegation that he would fight in Lebanon, or other front if conscripted into the Israeli army is less speculative. However, there are ways for him to address his fear of serving in the Israeli armed forces, which will be discussed below. [13] CO cannot select which armies they will fight in. It's either one is willing to fight or one is not. Having served in the Russian army negates his status as a CO in the Israeli armed forces. [14] Counsel submits that there is no alternative service in Israel or a way for CO to legally not serve in the Israeli armed forces. As I have found the claimant not to be a CO, there is no need to explore the consequences of his actions in Israel. However, to provide a complete analysis, I will review the country condition information before me. [15] The following exemptions are made for males in Israel. Not all of them apply to this claimant. These exemptions are being presented to refute counsel's submissions and are laws of general application to those that belong to the defined class. * Tal Law: renewed law for 5 years from 2007: Haredi Jews (ultra orthodox) are exempt from military service to pursue religious studies. Those, over age 22 can also interrupt studies to work for 1 year, and then have a shortened military service or return to religious studies. Reassessed yearly.6 * Sahar Law: sports prodigies are exempt from military in exchange for performing service in their communities.7 * Profile 21: medical classification leading to exemption from service as unsuitable due to medical issue.8 * Profile 41: 'Adjustment difficulties diagnosis"-not serious enough to be discharged but given a lighter service.9 * New immigrants over age 30: are exempt from military service and reserve duty 10 * Israeli male citizens: over age 30 are exempt from military service11, not reserve duty, it continues until age 50.12 * Foreign military service: those serving in foreign armies are entitled to shortened service(see Military booklet) 13 * Pacifists: see Conscientious Objection, package 2, page 4, paragraph 12 Consequences of Delay to Enlist and Notify of Authorities: * AWOL: Absent without leave: failure to show for enlistment after receiving order. are considered deserters. Military police will get a profile and can be arrested, tried and imprisoned for 14-35 days (ISR 41569. E, June 10, 2003) * Objectors: Those refusing to enlist repeatedly are sentenced repeatedly. Repeated incarceration ceases when: 1) person gets a discharge 2) military decides, after 3-6 terms to send person to 'incompatibility committee' which may result in discharge.(see conscientious objectors below) Israelis Living Abroad * If Israelis living abroad do not apply for an exemption (for religious or medical grounds, may be considered absent without leave, or in contravention of Israeli law.14 Israelis Living Abroad who return to Israel15 * those who left under the age of 16, or born abroad to Israeli parents are exempt as long as they are resident abroad. If they return they are subject to conscription according to age. * those who left Israel, 16 or older, are subject to conscription at age 18. * "returned to Israel" someone who visits for more than 1 year, or whose separate cumulative visits total more than 120 days Alternatives to Military Service: ISR36779, April 17, 2001 * Alternative service: generally not available, some exceptions Conscientious Objectors:16 Two types of objection: * Total objection: pacifism=exemption * Selective objection: political objection to specific policies or duties have no exemption * decided by the Conscience Committee if you fall under Total or Selective * In law only women and girls can be exempt for conscientious objection, so men have to establish themselves as pacifists in front of the Conscientious Objection Committee Refusers Solidarity Network NGO assists objectors Military Prisons 17 * The military prisons, #4, and #6, are detention centres where individuals are detained after refusing to serve, and following sentencing by the military tribunal. Description of facilities varies with conditions identified as difficult to bad. No evidence that condition breach Article 3. * New Immigrants and Service * Evidence suggests that all new potential immigrants are notified of military service issues. Ministry of Absorption website, also available in Russian, sets out military requirements; and JAFI, Jewish Agency for Israel, prepares new immigrants for their move, including identifying their military service options. Offices include Russia, Ukraine, Azerbajan, Belarus, Georgia and Kazakstan.18 * Consular officials say that all Soviet immigrants who emigrate to Israel know of military requirements. 19 [16] In summary, there were avenues available to the claimant to not serve in the Israeli military, however, he did not avail himself of them. For example, he could have applied for a reduced length of service having served in a foreign army 20 As counsel submitted, there is the Conscientious Objection Committee. I agree the Committee does not grant many CO status and people are jailed for not serving in the armed forces. These jails maybe harsh, but do not violate international standards. However, this is a law of general application. The claimant did not apply to such a committee as is his onus. [17] Another point to consider is whether the claimant had a well-founded fear when he left Israel as Israel is a democracy. Therefore, the claimant has a higher test to meet in showing that the state is unable or unwilling to help them. In the case of this claim, it would use the state apparatus to keep him out of the armed forces. As can be seen above, there were at least two approaches that he could have used to not serve in the Israeli armed forces; the National Service and applying to the Conscientious Objection Committee. Counsel may submit that this Committee is arbitrary and grants very few applicants their desired outcome. However, that is not the test that I have to apply in assessing whether the claimant exhausted their access to state protection. In this case, the claimant made no attempts to seek the state's protection in a democratic state. GENERAL CREDIBILITY FINDINGS [18] The claimant did find that his bank accounts were frozen and some documents expired as he had been out of the country for three years. However, the claimant did not make serious attempts to reopen his bank accounts or renew his documents. He did contact an agency that helps Russians in Israel. He was told by an Israeli official that he would have to wait eight months to be issued an identity document. The official asked him why he returned to Israel. The claimant testified that he did not recall what he told the official. I find that the claimant is withholding information from me and I make a negative finding about his credibility. The claimant was asked how much his airfare cost him versus using the same amount of money to stay in Israel. He estimated that he could live with his savings for three months. He also testified that he was working as a security guard. I find that he could have stayed in Israel while reacquiring his expired documents. [19] The claimant testified that he was unaware that he would have to serve in the military when he returned to Israel. He alleges that he had an exemption due to having a young child and assumed that he would still be exempt. I find that the claimant made a wrong assumption, in fact, one that he should have not made. He no longer had a young daughter as he had when he arrived in Israel the first time. It is not logical that some one returning to Israel and knowing that conscription was inevitable, would not have verified his draft status before returning. In the alternative, people make poor choices. In this case, the claimant's return without first investigating his draft status is one example of making a poor choice. [20] In the alternative, the claimant testified that he obtained a medical document that allowed him to serve in the Russian armed forces, but with restrictions. The claimant did not testify how he obtained this medical document. However, he testified that he did not have an Israeli doctor who could issue a similar letter. Having done many claims about military service in Russia, I know that people fearing military service purchase letters from doctors to get exemptions from military service. These range from not having to serve at all to serving with restrictions as with this claimant. I have reasons to believe from the claimant's responses that more likely than not he purchased such a letter. As well, these restrictions were removed when he served between 2003 and 2006. That is, his military service restrictions were the result of a letter rather than injuries allegedly incurred when held a hostage. I make a negative finding about his credibility because he was not truthful with the panel on a material fact of how he obtained his restricted military designation. SUMMARY [21] The claimant made his claim against both Russia and Israel. I made findings just against Israel because the claimant failed to establish that he is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection in Israel. There are two main reasons for coming to this conclusion. The first is that he failed in his onus to seek the Israel's protection by establishing that he is a CO from serving in the Israeli armed forces as he alleges. Allied to this is that he did serve in the Russian army, which disqualifies him from applying to be a CO in another country. The second reason is the general credibility findings that cause me to find the claimant was not being truthful. CONCLUSION [22] For the above reasons I find that XXXXX XXXXX is not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. The Division rejects his claim.
