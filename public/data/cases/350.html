Introduction [1] These Reasons deal with an application by the defendants to strike the entirety of the notice of civil claim of the corporate plaintiff, 311165 BC Ltd. ("311165"), under R. 9-5(1)(a), (b), (c), or (d) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules. The other plaintiff, Stephen Ambrose, has discontinued against the defendants. Mr. Ambrose is the sole shareholder, director, and president of 311165. The representative of 311165 in this proceeding was its secretary, Padraig Mac Roibeaird. [2] The relief sought by 311165 is against: (a) Constable Gardiner for damages for abuse of process, injurious falsehood, and negligent investigation; (b) the Attorney General of British Columbia for a declaration of liability for those damages and costs sought against Cst. Gardiner and for damages sought against the RCMP; (c) the RCMP for damages for conspiracy to engage in malicious prosecution; and (d) the Attorney General of Canada for damages for abuse of process, malicious prosecution, gross negligence, and slander of title including for the loss of property value and decline in profits. [3] The claims set out in the notice of civil claim arise from the discovery of a large and sophisticated marihuana grow operation on a 20-acre property, having the civic address of 3661 Northway Road, Malakwa, BC ("3661 Property") on July 6, 2010. The discovery arose from a search warrant authorizing the search of three properties: the 3661 Property; the adjacent property having the civic address of 3621 Northway Road, Malakwa ("3621 Property"), owned by the plaintiff 311165; and Lot 4, Northway Road, Malakwa. The focus of the claims is a Restraint and Management Order ("RMO") obtained against the 3621 Property. [4] Colin Martin and Jason Airey were charged with the offence of production of marihuana and possession for the purpose of trafficking. On March 25, 2014, Mr. Airey was convicted of production of marihuana and possession of marihuana for the purpose of trafficking. He was sentenced that same day. On March 19, 2014, Mr. Martin entered guilty pleas for the same two offences and was sentenced on November 7, 2014. II. Background [5] 311165 owns the 3621 Property, a 6.25-acre property adjacent to the 3661 Property. It was purchased in 2005 from Ms. Cahill, the spouse of Mr. Martin. On the 3621 Property is a 6,700 square foot custom built home which Mr. Martin had built. Mr. Martin and Ms. Cahill resided in that house and continued to do so after the purchase by 311165. [6] 311165 says that immediately after the July 6, 2010 search, the RCMP released inaccurate and misleading information to the media regarding the investigation into charges arising from the search of the 3661 Property. The plaintiff says that the source of this information was the Information to Obtain, sworn by Constable Faulkner in support of a search warrant for the aforementioned three lots. The information complained of was that Mr. Ambrose was under investigation for production of marihuana and referenced in three local newspapers. [7] On October 7, 2010, the plaintiff listed the 3621 Property for sale for $1.75 million. [8] On October 22, 2010, the Director of Public Prosecutions applied ex parte and was granted the RMO over the 3621 Property and the 3661 Property under s. 14 of the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act, S.C. 1996, c. 19. The application was supported by an affidavit sworn by Cst. Gardiner ("Gardiner Affidavit"). The RMO was filed in the BC Land Title Office. The RMO directed the Seized Property Management Directorate ("SPMD"), a department of the Ministry of Public Works of Canada, to carry out certain supervisory functions. [9] On or about January 26, 2011, an official of the SPMD ordered the real estate agent to take down the listing of the 3621 Property. [10] The plaintiff applied to have the 3621 Property removed from the RMO. [11] The supporting affidavit on that application was sworn by Mr. Ambrose on March 8, 2011. He refuted statements of fact that Cst. Gardiner had provided. 311165 argued that the 3621 Property was not offence-related property and that Mr. Ambrose was neither complicit in the offences committed on the 3661 Property, nor did he collude with any of the offenders convicted of offences on the 3661 Property. It is asserted that Cst. Gardiner made a number of assumptions, drew wrong conclusions, and made false and misleading statements to the issuing judge. [12] Associate Chief Justice MacKenzie (as she then was) heard the application to vary the RMO on the 3621 Property on April 12, 2011. She dismissed 311165's application: Canada (Attorney General) v. 31165 BC Ltd., 2011 BCSC 592 [Variation Application]. In doing so she noted that in order to obtain a restraint order, the proponent had to establish reasonable grounds to believe that the subject property is offence-related property; that the reasonable grounds standard connoted a reasonable probability or a credibly based probability; and that the standard did not require the proponent to establish a prima facie case, nor did it require proof on a balance of probabilities or proof beyond a reasonable doubt. She stated the following: [7] I have concluded this is an application to set aside the Order; the applicant does not seek to change its terms, but instead seeks that the Order not apply to him. [8] The applicant has approached this application as a full hearing on the merits, disregarding the need to obtain leave to apply to set aside the Order. I refuse to grant leave for the following reasons: - the applicant has not established a reasonable likelihood that granting leave would assist in a determination of the relevant issues; - the Ambrose affidavit only raises further questions about the applicant's knowledge of and/or complicity in the marihuana grow operation, which are properly determined at a forfeiture hearing; and - the applicant has not demonstrated that the Court should exercise its inherent jurisdiction to grant relief to an innocent third party. [9] Even viewing the applicant's evidence in the manner most favourable to the applicant, the applicant has not demonstrated by the affidavit of Mr. Ambrose or submissions of counsel that there were insufficient grounds to grant the Order. [13] The Court of Appeal declined to hear the appeal for want of jurisdiction in reasons indexed as 2011 BCCA 409. [14] In November 2012, the RMO was varied to allow the 3621 Property to be listed for sale with the SPMD retaining the proceeds of any sale. The asking price was dropped to $850,000. In October 2015, 311165 accepted an offer of $715,000, subject to mortgage approval; however, the deal collapsed as a result of the purchaser's circumstances. The 3621 Property was recently relisted at $750,000. [15] The forfeiture hearing with respect to the 3621 Property and the 3661 Property was held in March 2015 before Justice Beames. Mr. Ambrose testified in the hearing. Justice Beames issued her decision in December 2015, dismissing the application in relation to the 3621 Property and allowing the application in relation to the 3661 Property: R v. Martin, 2015 BCSC 2243. The following extracts from her reasons are relevant here: [37] While the offender, Mr. Martin, lived in a house on Lot 5 [3621 Property], with his family, the marihuana grow operation was entirely contained on Lot 7. The evidence does support the conclusion that Mr. Martin used his computer, located in the residence on Lot 5, to conduct business associated with the marihuana grow operation. There were a modest number of documents found in the residence as well that appear to have related to the marihuana grow operation. I am not persuaded that anything else found in the residence related to the marihuana grow operation. [40] Crown relies on a number of cases in which only part of a property was used for a marihuana grow operation in support of the argument that Lot 5 was offence-related property, including R. v. Ford 2010 BCCA 105 and R. v. Van Kessel 2010 BCSC 257. However, Crown has been unable to provide any case authority in which the real property the Crown sought to have forfeited was a distinct and separate parcel of land from the land on which the offence was committed. [41] I am not prepared to find that the use of a computer (or for that matter a telephone) or the storage of documents related to a marihuana grow operation on a parcel of land separate from the land actually being used for the grow operation is a sufficient basis for finding that the land where the computer and documents were situate was offence-related property. [42] Consequently, I find that Lot 5 was not offence-related property. Even if I had concluded that it was, I am satisfied that the registered owner was not complicit or in collusion with the offender. [43] The Crown's application for forfeiture of Lot 5 is dismissed. III. Rule 9-5: Striking Pleadings [16] The provisions under the relevant rule relied upon by the applicant read as follows: Rule 9-5 -- Striking Pleadings Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. Admissibility of evidence (2) No evidence is admissible on an application under subrule (1) (a). [17] When considering whether to strike the pleadings the court is to consider the pleadings as they currently stand, as well as how they could be reasonably amended. The facts pleaded are assumed to be true unless they are manifestly incapable of being proven. Though evidence is not admissible in relation to an application under subrule (1)(a); evidence is admissible in relation to subrule (1)(b) through (d). [18] The applicant faces a high threshold. In order to have the pleadings struck, it must be "plain and obvious" that they offend one of the subrules in R. 9-5(1): Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959 at 980. IV. Parties' Submissions A. Defendants' Position [19] The defendants submit that "[n]o cause of action arises" at all because there was a valid court order authorizing the RMO and therefore "[a]n allegation that the Restraint Order was unlawful cannot succeed". [20] With respect to the claims of injurious falsehood and slander of title, made respectively against Cst. Gardiner and the Attorney General of Canada, the defendants submit that the claims are bound to fail pursuant to R. 9-5(1)(a) because: (a) no particulars are provided with respect to what information or allegation was false or misleading and, therefore, "[i]t is unknown what words or information provided or used by the RCMP or contained in the affidavits or Restraint Order are impugned." This is contrary to the requirements of R. 3-7(21) which apply where slander is alleged; (b) the statements complained of were contained in the Gardiner Affidavit and in Cst. Faulkner's affidavit, both of which are absolutely privileged, citing Hung v. Gardiner, 2003 BCCA 257 at para. 6; (c) registering the RMO in the Land Title Office cannot amount to slander of title because it was issued as part of the court process and is absolutely privileged, citing Tersigni v. Fagan, [1959] O.J. No. 65 (C.A.); and Pete & Martys (Front) Ltd. v. Market Block Toronto Properties Ltd., 5 C.P.C. (2d) 97, [1985] O.J. No. 564 (High C.J.); and (d) the statements that there were reasonable grounds to believe that Lot 5 was offence-related property were true. [21] With respect to the plaintiff's claim for malicious prosecution, made against the Attorney General of Canada, the defendants say that the plaintiff has not pleaded any facts to support the required element of lack of reasonable and probable cause and therefore, these pleadings must be struck under R. 9-5(1)(a). Further, the defendants seem to assert that there was reasonable and probable cause. [22] The defendants also submit that because the RMO was a lawful act, the remainder of the pleadings are unnecessary and vexatious pursuant to R. 9-5(1)(b). [23] It is further argued that the remainder of the pleadings are embarrassing and therefore offend R. 9-5(1)(c). [24] Lastly, the defendants say that there is a basis for striking pleadings as an abuse of process under R. 9-5(1)(d). [25] This is so, they submit, because the civil claim is a collateral attack on the validity of the RMO, as the plaintiff is asking the court to find that the RMO was invalid and to award damages as a result. They contend that the plaintiff has already challenged the RMO in the criminal proceeding and to permit it to do so again would be an abuse of the court's process. [26] Further, the defendants submit that a portion of the notice of civil claim merely duplicates the allegations made in a defamation action against Black Press ("Black Press Litigation"). Therefore, the defendants say those portions of the pleadings should be struck as an abuse of process and as limitation-barred. B. Plaintiff's Position [27] The plaintiff contends that the defendants have not established that it is plain and obvious that the claims should be stuck. [28] The plaintiff submits that, at a minimum, the facts establish that something was very wrong in the way in which the 3621 Property was "alienated for over five years", that the plaintiff suffered serious financial harm, and that any reasonable view would lead to the conclusion that the facts form the basis for an action or actions in tort. [29] The plaintiff argues that the action has a public interest dimension. In this regard, it submits that the "defendants partially acted to expand the previous limits of property seizure under the provisions of the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act" and that "it suffered great harm from being used as a sort of lab rat in an experiment to see if the Courts could be rooked into ordering forfeiture of property on grounds never before attempted." [30] The plaintiff disputes the defendants' submission that the civil proceeding is a collateral attack on the RMO. It says that its claim does not allege that the RMO was unlawful, but rather, that the RMO was obtained through false and misleading claims contained in the Gardiner Affidavit and that the defendants used the RMO to harm the plaintiff. V. Rule 9-5(1)(a): No Reasonable Claim [31] At the outset, I do not agree with the defendants that "[n]o cause of action arises" because there was a valid court order authorizing the RMO. Some of the torts alleged (e.g. negligent investigation, abuse of process) may be made out whether or not the forfeiture proceedings against the 3621 Property had merit. [32] Thus, I will analyze the individual tort claims against the requirement in R. 9-5(1)(a) in the following order: (a) the claims against Cst. Gardiner; (b) the conspiracy to engage in malicious prosecution claims against the RCMP; and (c) the claims against the Attorney General of Canada. A. Claims against Cst. Gardiner [33] As noted, 311165 claims against Cst. Gardiner for injurious falsehood, abuse of process, and negligent investigation. 311165 also seeks a declaration that the Attorney General of British Columbia is liable for the damages and costs sought against Cst. Gardiner, should they be awarded. [34] In my view, all of these claims are bound to fail and should be struck pursuant to R. 9-5(1)(a). 1. Injurious Falsehood [35] Injurious falsehood and slander of title are different names for the same tort. As noted in Raymond E. Brown, Brown on Defamation, 2nd ed. (Toronto: Carswell, 1999) (loose-leaf updated 2016, release 3), vol. 10 at s. 28.1(1): Actions for injurious falsehood originally arose under such names as slander of title, slander of goods, trade libel, interference with prospective advantage, disparagement of goods, title or property and commercial disparagement. The term "injurious falsehood" was coined by Sir William Salmond and has gradually been accepted as a more accurate characterization of the interests being protected. [Citations omitted.] [36] The elements of a claim for injurious falsehood are: (1) a false statement disparaging the plaintiff's business, goods, or property; (2) published to a third person; (3) maliciously and without just cause or excuse; and (4) resulting in special damages in the form of pecuniary loss: Brown on Defamation at s. 28.1(1). [37] Essentially, the plaintiff complains that Cst. Gardiner swore a false and misleading affidavit for improper purposes and that he was motivated by malice. [38] While I agree with the defendants that the claim lacks particulars with respect to the injurious falsehood claim, that deficiency could be remedied by an order for further particulars. [39] In spite of this, the claim remains fatally flawed, as the only allegations that could support it all relate to the contents of the Gardiner Affidavit. [40] The contents of the Gardiner Affidavit are absolutely privileged by witness immunity. As stated by Justice Cromwell (as he then was) in Elliott v. Insurance Crime Prevention Bureau, 2005 NSCA 115: [112] The witness immunity rule is part of a larger immunity which applies to participants in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings. As Raymond E. Brown puts it in 2 The Law of Defamation in Canada, looseleaf updated to 2004 Rel. 4, (Toronto: Carswell, 1999) at ¶ 12.4(1) and 12.4(4)(a), "An absolute privilege or immunity attaches to those communications which take place during, incidental to, and in the processing and furtherance of, judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings. No action for libel or slander will lie for words spoken or written during the ordinary course of those proceedings. ... The protection of this privilege extends to all the participants in the judicial or quasi-judicial proceeding including the judge, jury witnesses, parties and their counsel." See also Watson v. M'Ewan, [1905] A.C. 480 (H.L. (Sc.)); Halls v. Mitchell, [1928] S.C.R. 125. [41] The immunity extends to affidavit evidence (McDaniel v. McDaniel, 2009 BCCA 53 at para. 40) and to evidence given falsely and maliciously (Workum v. Olnick et al, 2005 BCSC 1262 at para. 11, aff'd 2006 BCCA 528 at para. 7). [42] Further, the immunity extends beyond defamation actions. As Cromwell J. stated in Elliott at para. 113, "[t]he protection is against actions of any sort, and is not limited to actions for libel and slander". In other words, "[w]hatever form of action is sought to be derived from what was said or done in the course of judicial proceedings must suffer the same fate of being barred ": Hung v. Gardiner, 2003 BCCA 257 at para. 34, citing Marrinan v. Vibart, [1962] 3 All E.R. 380 at 383 (C.A.). Justice Burnyeat in Workum, also referred to a number of cases in which the privilege has operated: [11] I am satisfied that the absolute privilege attaches to evidence given at judicial and quasi-judicial proceedings and that it makes no difference that the words may be totally false, spoken mala fide or without actual malice. I am also satisfied that the Rule has been applied in British Columbia to stop actions commenced against those said to have perjured themselves or those said to have conspired to give or to get others to give false statements or testimony: (a) Geyer v. C.C.I. Merritt [1979] B.C.J. (Q.L.) No. 640 (B.C.S.C.) affirmed (1980) 26 B.C.L.R. 374 (B.C.C.A.) (a claim for civil remedy against the defendants for perjury, false statement and for misleading a justice); (b) Redman v. Fisher [1986] B.C.J. (Q.L.) No. 2675 (B.C.S.C.) (an action relating to the Residential Tenancy Act where it was alleged that a false affidavit had been sworn); (c) Carnahan v. Coates (1990) 47 B.C.L.R. (2d) 127 (B.C.S.C.) (an action against a psychologist alleging negligence and abuse of process arising out of alleged unethical conduct in preparing a report dealing with the right to access where the report was provided to the Court); (d) Boerner v. Bruhaug [1992] B.C.J. No. 1926 (B.C.C.A.) (a defamation action against a lawyer arising out of what the lawyer had said in the courtroom); (e) Kosendiak v. Parsons [1993] B.C.J. (Q.L.) No. 1579 (B.C.S.C.) affirmed [1994] B.C.J. (Q.L.) No. 1529 (B.C.C.A.) (a defamation action against a lawyer who defended an employer in a grievance proceeding where the plaintiff claimed that certain statements in the submissions made by the lawyer were defamatory); (f) Mayer v. Mayer, supra; (g) Turner v. Cuttler [1997] B.C.J. (Q.L.) No. 2289 (B.C.S.C.) (an action against a lawyer for alleged perjury during the assessment of a professional account); (h) Zanetti v. Bonniehon Enterprises Ltd. [2002] B.C.J. No. 1906 (B.C.S.C.) (an action based on libel, negligence, or attempted obstruction of justice relating to alleged perjury in arbitration proceedings); and (i) Hung v. Gardiner supra. [43] I see nothing in the plaintiff's case which displaces the application of the immunity. Accordingly, I conclude that this claim should be struck. 2. Abuse of Process [44] The tort of abuse of process has two elements: (1) a wilful misuse or perversion of a court process for an extraneous or improper purpose and (2) some damage resulting: Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083 at para. 54. There may be an additional element of some act or threat made in furtherance of the legitimate legal process, but it is unclear if that is the law in British Columbia: Smith v. Rusk, 2009 BCCA 96 at para. 34. [45] Once again, the only allegations that could possibly support this claim relate to the contents of the Gardiner Affidavit. This is apparent from the portion of Part 3 of the notice of civil claim, which states: 1. The Plaintiff says the Defendant Scott Gardiner abused the process of the law by using the protection of absolute privilege to publishing false and defamatory statements and falsely stating reasonable belief in the Gardener affidavit. The plaintiff cannot circumvent witness immunity by framing the action in abuse of process. I therefore conclude that this claim should also be struck. 3. Negligent Investigation [46] The elements of negligence are well-known. To succeed in its negligent investigation claim 311165 must show: (1) that Cst. Gardiner owed it a duty of care; (2) that Cst. Gardiner failed to meet the appropriate standard of care; (3) that 311165 has suffered a compensable loss or injury; and (4) that the loss or injury was caused by Cst. Gardiner's negligent act or omission. [47] The only facts relating to Cst. Gardiner in Part 1 of the notice of civil claim essentially state that he swore a false and misleading affidavit for improper purposes and was motivated by malice. To the extent, if any, that the plaintiff seeks to rely on the contents of the Gardiner Affidavit for the negligent investigation claim, I conclude that it is bound to fail by reason of witness immunity. [48] However, in Part 3 of the notice of civil claim the plaintiff states: 3. that Constable Gardiner was negligent in his investigation leading up to the swearing of his affidavit. 4. that Constable Gardiner owed a duty of care to both Plaintiffs. [49] These are conclusions for which the plaintiff offers no material facts in support. In particular, the plaintiff has not pleaded material facts to support the conclusion that Cst. Gardiner owed it a duty of care or to say how he breached the standard of care. [50] In my view, as pleaded this claim is bound to fail. [51] It follows then that the claims against the Attorney General of British Columbia for the action against Cst. Gardiner cannot succeed. Accordingly, those claims are struck except that the plaintiff will have 30 days to file amended pleadings to address the deficiencies if possible, without prejudice to the defendant to bring on a further application to strike. B. Claims against the RCMP and the Attorney General of British Columbia [52] The plaintiff claims against the RCMP on the basis that it conspired to engage in malicious prosecution. 1. Conspiracy [53] There are two types of conspiracy in tort law: predominant purpose conspiracy and unlawful means conspiracy: Pro-Sys Consultants Ltd. v. Microsoft Corporation, 2013 SCC 57 at para. 73. [54] The elements of predominant purpose conspiracy are: (1) an agreement or concerted action between two or more persons; (2) with the predominant purpose of causing injury to the plaintiff; and (3) overt acts committed that cause damage to the plaintiff: Watson v. Bank of America Corporation, 2015 BCCA 362 at para. 125. [55] Like predominant purpose conspiracy, unlawful means conspiracy requires an agreement or concerted action between two or more persons. The additional elements of unlawful means conspiracy are that: (1) the conduct of the defendants is unlawful; (2) the conduct is directed towards the plaintiff (alone or with others); (3) the defendants should know that injury to the plaintiff is likely to result; and (4) injury to the plaintiff does occur: Watson at para. 56. [56] Pleadings alleging conspiracy must be as specific as possible: Can-Dive Services Ltd. v. Pacific Coast Energy Corp. (1993), 96 B.C.L.R. (2d) 156 at para. 9 (C.A.), leave to appeal ref'd [1993] 3 S.C.R. viii (note). In Can-Dive Services Ltd. at para. 8, Chief Justice McEachern quoted with approval the following excerpt from J.I.H. Jacob & Iain S. Goldrein, eds., Bullen & Leake & Jacob's Precedents of Pleadings, 13th ed. (London, U.K.: Sweet & Maxwell, 1990) at 221-22: The statement of claim should describe who the several parties to the conspiracy are and their relationship with each other. It should allege the conspiracy between the defendants giving the best particulars it can of the dates when or dates between which the unlawful conspiracy was entered into or continued, and the intent to injure It should state precisely the objects and means of the alleged conspiracy to injure and the overt acts which are alleged to have been done by each of the alleged conspirators in pursuance of the conspiracy, and lastly, the injury and damage occasioned to the plaintiff 2. Malicious Prosecution [57] To succeed in a claim for malicious prosecution, a plaintiff must prove that the prosecution was: (1) initiated by the defendant; (2) terminated in favour of the plaintiff; (3) undertaken without reasonable and probable cause; and (4) motivated by malice or a primary purpose other than that of carrying the law into effect: Miazga v. Kvello Estate, 2009 SCC 51 at para. 3. 3. Application [58] It is not entirely clear whether the plaintiff is alleging unlawful means conspiracy or predominant purpose conspiracy. In the Relief Sought section of the notice of civil claim, 311165 seeks damages for "conspiracy to engage in malicious prosecution". However, in para. 11 of Part 3, the plaintiff pleads that "known and unknown members of the [RCMP] conspired to smear the reputation of Stephen Ambrose and harm him through causing economic harm to the corporate Plaintiff". [59] In any event, the claim is bound to fail because it is framed only in vague and general terms. The only specific allegation is found in para. 15 of Part 3: 12. The known RCMP conspirators were Constable Gardiner and Constable Faulkner. The Plaintiffs say that these two Defendants did not act alone in the conspiracy, and acted wrongfully for RCMP objectives, rather than personal gain. 15. An explicit example of the conspiracy in action is found in the action of Constable Faulkner preparing a false and misleading affidavit in the Black Press litigation, followed by PPSC lawyer Phillipe Alma forwarding a copy of the false and misleading affidavit to counsel for Black Press Ltd. [60] Even so, the Faulkner affidavit, referred to in paras. 15-17 of Part 1 of the notice of civil claim, is unrelated to any "prosecution" initiated by any of the defendants; rather, it is related to Mr. Ambrose's Black Press Litigation. Further, its contents are absolutely privileged due to witness immunity. [61] On this basis, the claims made against the Attorney General of British Columbia and the RCMP regarding conspiracy to engage in malicious prosecution must be struck. C. Claims against the Attorney General of Canada [62] As noted, 311165 claims against the Attorney General of Canada for abuse of process, malicious prosecution, gross negligence, and slander of title. 1. Abuse of Process [63] The elements of abuse of process were discussed earlier at para. 44. [64] Part 3 of the notice of civil claim addresses this claim as follows: 18. The Plaintiff says that the Director of Public Prosecutions and the PPSC abused the process of the law in respect to the provisions of the [CDSA] in regard to seizure of alleged offense related property. In that regard, the Plaintiff says: a. by the time of the April 12, 2011 application to vary the terms of the RMO, counsel for the Director of Public Prosecutions had a clear warning that much of the facts and premises of Constable Gardiner's affidavit were false and misleading, and that a deceptive tactic of conflation was being used by the RCMP; b. the Director of Public Prosecutions acted for ancillary or collateral and improper purpose in maintaining the RMO after becoming aware of the failings of the Gardener affidavit; i. the first improper purpose was to extend the scope of offense related property seizure legislation to restrain without legal justification property that had no reasonable connection to the offense elsewhere, and in the absence of complicity on the part of an innocent owner; ii the second improper purpose became, once this action had been filed in 2012, to attempt to carry out the first improper purpose as a means of avoiding liability of the parties named herein, and to avoid paying damages; c. during all of this time, the corporate Plaintiff was suffering economic harm, and Stephen Ambrose was suffering harm to his reputation, for which general and punitive damages are claimed herein; [65] In my view, it is not plain and obvious that this claim will fail on the ground that it discloses no reasonable claim. The plaintiff pleads that the defendant abused the process of the court and specifies the improper purposes. The plaintiff pleads that it suffered damage in Part 1 and at para. 18(c) of Part 3 of the notice of civil claim. Given that the law is uncertain as to whether an additional element of some act or threat made in furtherance of the process is required in British Columbia, I cannot conclude that this claim is bound to fail on the grounds set out in R. 9-5(1)(a). 2. Malicious Prosecution [66] The elements of the tort of malicious prosecution are set out above at para. 57. [67] The notice of civil claim sets out the following legal basis with respect to this tort: 21. The corporate Plaintiff says that proceedings for obtaining a restraint and management order and seeking forfeiture are criminal law proceedings. The Plaintiffs were, while not charged, both subjected to no less a prosecution than persons under some form of indictment. 22. This was an action against the corporate Plaintiff, and indirectly against Stephen Ambrose, in which the PPSC sought to obtain a severely punitive outcome against the corporate Plaintiff in criminal proceedings, and for a purported criminal law objective. All along the way, the PPSC in concert with the RCMP falsely painted Stephen Ambrose as a businessman living a shadow criminal life. 23. The PPSC proceeded with malice in this prosecution. It knew within a month of Constable Gardiner's affidavit that something was very wrong with the manner in which the RMO came to be put in place. It carried on, for the improper purposes enumerated above, on an entirely false foundation in a five year process that severely harmed the corporate Plaintiff and Mr. Ambrose [68] In my view, it is at least arguable that the prosecution was initiated by the defendant and terminated in favour of the plaintiff, 311165. [69] The defendants may be correct that the plaintiff has not pleaded facts to support the element of lack of reasonable and probable cause. Indeed, the plaintiff does not specifically say that prosecutors lacked reasonable and probable cause. However, in my view, it is apparent from the whole of the pleadings that this is what the plaintiff is alleging. [70] I conclude that this claim should not be struck under R. 9-5(1)(a). 3. Gross Negligence [71] The plaintiff addresses this claim in the Legal Basis section of its notice of civil claim as follows: 19. The corporate Plaintiff says the Director of Public Prosecutions was under a duty of care at all times, and at a minimum after the corporate Plaintiff pointed out, and the PPSC acknowledged, serious errors in the Gardiner affidavit. 20. The corporate Plaintiff says that the agents of the Director failed in that duty, were grossly negligent, and harmed the corporate Plaintiff, for which general and punitive damages are claimed herein; [72] The underlying fatal flaw in this claim is that there is no cause of action against prosecutors for recklessness, negligence, or even gross negligence. [73] In Henry v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2012 BCSC 1401, Justice Goepel, as he then was, concluded that a prosecutor did not owe a duty of care to a particular accused, and therefore could not be found liable in negligence. Such a duty would impinge upon the wide breadth of prosecutorial discretion and upset the balance between Crown immunity and Crown liability. The Supreme Court of Canada referenced these same policy concerns in Henry v. B.C. (A.G.), 2015 SCC 24, in addressing the liability threshold in cases of wrongful non-disclosure by a prosecutor. Justice Moldaver, writing for the majority at paras. 91-92, determined that the threshold should "be set near the high end of the blameworthiness spectrum" as a lower standard would implicate "a duty of care paradigm that ignores the basic realities of conducting a criminal prosecution." [74] For these reasons, I conclude that this claim is bound to fail and should be struck. 4. Slander of Title [75] The elements of slander of title are the same as those of injurious falsehood: (1) a false statement disparaging the plaintiff's business, goods or property; (2) published to a third person; (3) maliciously and without just cause or excuse; and (4) resulting in special damages in the form of pecuniary loss. [76] As noted in Brown on Defamation at s. 28.1(15): Where a plaintiff is pursuing an action for slander of title or slander of goods, the publisher must be identified, the words constituting the slander must be pleaded clearly and precisely, and there must be an express allegation that the plaintiff has suffered some particular special damage as a result of the slander, unless there is some special statutory provision foregoing an allegation of special damages where the words are calculated to cause pecuniary damage with respect to an office, profession or trade. It must be alleged and proved that the words constituting injurious falsehood were followed as a natural and legal consequence by pecuniary damage to the plaintiff. If the plaintiff is claiming special damages as a result of a loss to an intending purchaser of his or her property, particulars must be provided as to the name of the intended purchaser, the date on which he or she received notice of the publication, and the amount of damages claimed to be lost as a result of the slander of title. The plaintiff does not have to allege, as in malicious prosecution, that the defendant acted without reasonable and probable cause. If the plaintiff claims that his title to property was slandered by the defendant shortly before its sale by auction which prevented some persons from bidding on the property, particulars should be provided of the occasions when and where the words complained of were spoken, the persons present on those occasions, and the best particulars that can be given of the persons who were desirous of purchasing the property and were prevented from doing so by reason of the words spoken by the defendant. It is not sufficient to allege that shares in which the plaintiff has an interest have been depreciated and lessened in value, or that others have been led to believe that the plaintiff has no right to or interest in the shares, or that he or she has been hindered and prevented from selling or disposing of the shares. Nor is it sufficient to allege generally in an action for slander of title that the plaintiff lost the sale of his lands. [Citations omitted.] [77] The Legal Basis section in the notice of civil claim states this about the slander of title claim: 25. Slander of title occurs when the placement of the caveat places a cloud of suspicion or doubt over the owner's claim to title. The result is economic loss to a plaintiff when it becomes difficult to sell the property except by lowering the price, and when other damage causing issues arise as a result of the cloud of suspicion. In this case, the Property was very stigmatized as a result of the entirety of false allegations which came to be widely distributed and cannot be retracted. [78] I agree with the defendants that the pleadings lack the requisite particulars. [79] In any case, the RMO was issued as part of the court process and, therefore, the statements within the RMO are absolutely privileged. In this respect the RMO is analogous to a lis pendens or a certificate of pending litigation: see Tersigni at para. 4; Brown at s. 28.1(12)(a). [80] Therefore, the slander of title claim is bound to fail and should be struck. VI. Rule 9-5(1)(b): unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous, or vexatious [81] The defendants say that the RMO was lawful and therefore the remainder of the plaintiff's pleadings are unnecessary and vexatious. [82] Justice Fisher in Willow set out when a pleading will be struck under R. 9-5(1)(b): [20] Under Rule 9-5(1)(b), a pleading is unnecessary or vexatious if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action, if it does not advance any claim known in law, where it is obvious that an action cannot succeed, or where it would serve no useful purpose and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v Canadian Jewish Congress, [1999] BCJ No. 2160 (SC); Skender v Farley, 2007 BCCA 629. If a pleading is so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is pleaded, it may also be unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious. An application under this sub-rule may be supported by evidence. [83] The mere fact that the RMO was "lawful" does not necessarily mean that the remainder of the claims are unnecessary and vexatious. In my view they should not be struck under R. 9-5(1)(b). VII. Rule 9-5(1)(c): prejudice, embarrass, or delay the fair trial or hearing [84] As is the case for an application to strike under R. 9-5(1)(a), (b), or (d), the test for R. 9-5(1)(c) requires that it be plain and obvious that the pleadings may prejudice, embarrass, or delay the fair trial or proceeding: Lougheed v. Wilson, 2012 BCSC 169 at para. 60. [85] The defendants say that the pleadings are embarrassing as the pleaded facts "confuse the real foundation of the Plaintiffs' complaint". The true issue, according to the defendants, is that the plaintiff is embarrassed by the police scrutiny of its relationship with Mr. Martin, but that this does not give rise to a cause of action. [86] As already discussed there are other difficulties with the plaintiff's pleadings and to that extent they have been struck. I do not need to address them further under this head. VIII. Rule 9-5(1)(d): abuse of process [87] The defendants essentially make three arguments in relation to R. 9-5(1)(d), which I will address separately: (a) this tort claim for damages arising from the effects of the valid RMO is a collateral attack on the validity of that order; (b) the plaintiff seeks to relitigate the issue of whether the RMO was valid or proper; and (c) the portion of the claim addressing the Black Press Litigation should be struck because it duplicates the allegations from that litigation and is limitation-barred. A. Discussion of R. 9-5(1)(d) [88] The doctrine of abuse of process arises in many legal contexts. In Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E., Local 79, 2003 SCC 63, Justice Arbour, writing for the majority, described abuse of process in that context: 37 In the context that interests us here, the doctrine of abuse of process engages "the inherent power of the court to prevent the misuse of its procedure, in a way that would . . . bring the administration of justice into disrepute" (Canam Enterprises Inc. v. Coles (2000), 51 O.R. (3d) 481 (C.A.), at para. 55, per Goudge J.A., dissenting (approved [2002] 3 S.C.R. 307, 2002 SCC 63)). Goudge J.A. expanded on that concept in the following terms at paras. 55-56: The doctrine of abuse of process engages the inherent power of the court to prevent the misuse of its procedure, in a way that would be manifestly unfair to a party to the litigation before it or would in some other way bring the administration of justice into disrepute . One circumstance in which abuse of process has been applied is where the litigation before the court is found to be in essence an attempt to relitigate a claim which the court has already determined. [89] Abuse of process is used in the same sense in R. 9-5(1)(d), as Justice Fisher discussed in Willow: [21] Abuse of process under Rule 9-5(1)(d) or the court's inherent discretion is a flexible doctrine. It allows the court to prevent a claim from proceeding where to do so would violate principles of judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice. A claim may be struck where it is a collateral attack on an administrative decision that is subject to appeal or judicial review: Cimaco International Sales Inc. v British Columbia, 2010 BCCA 342; Stephen v HMTQ, 2008 BCSC 1656; Varzeliotis v British Columbia, 2007 BCSC 620; Gemex Developments Corp. v City of Coquitlam, 2002 BCSC 412; Berscheid v Ensign, [1999] BCJ No. 1172 (SC). A claim may also be struck as an abuse of process where it is an attempt to re-litigate an issue that has already been decided: Toronto (City) v Canadian Union of Public Employees (CUPE), Local 79, 2003 SCC 63. [90] This flexibility does not, however, lower the high bar set for the applicants. The test to be satisfied remains, whether it is plain and obvious that allowing the claim to proceed would violate the "principles of judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice": Willow at para. 21. B. Collateral Attack [91] In my opinion, it is not plain and obvious that the plaintiff's claim is a collateral attack on the validity of the RMO. [92] The rule against collateral attack was stated in Wilson v. The Queen, [1983] 2 S.C.R. 594 at 599, as follows: It has long been a fundamental rule that a court order, made by a court having jurisdiction to make it, stands and is binding and conclusive unless it is set aside on appeal or lawfully quashed. It is also well settled in the authorities that such an order may not be attacked collaterally--and a collateral attack may be described as an attack made in proceedings other than those whose specific object is the reversal, variation, or nullification of the order or judgment. [93] In my opinion, the plaintiff's claim is not a collateral attack because the plaintiff does not seek to set aside the RMO itself. In this regard the claim has some parallels to that in Toronto (City). In that case, the Court held that it was an abuse of process, but not a collateral attack, for a man's privies (in that case the union) to seek to relitigate the material facts supporting a conviction for sexual assault in a subsequent dismissal grievance arbitration. The majority stated: [34] in the case at bar, the union does not seek to overturn the sexual abuse conviction itself, but simply contest, for the purposes of a different claim with different legal consequences, whether the conviction was correct. It is an implicit attack on the correctness of the factual basis of the decision, not a contest about whether that decision has legal force, as clearly it does. Prohibited "collateral attacks" are abuses of the court's process. However, in light of the focus of the collateral attack rule on attacking the order itself and its legal effect, I believe that the better approach here is to go directly to the doctrine of abuse of process. [94] It seems the plaintiff's focus is not on the validity of the RMO, but on the fact that the authorities did not do anything based on the assertion that Mr. Ambrose was involved and that the property was offence-related. The strength of the argument is furthered by the finding that the RMO encompassing the 3621 Property was not upheld. [95] I turn then, to abuse of process. C. Abuse of Process [96] The plaintiff submits at para. 6(h) of its application response, that it is not seeking a declaration that the RMO is invalid, but rather: that the RMO was obtained through the presentation of false and misleading material to the issuing judge, and that the officer claiming to have reasonable beliefs did not have those beliefs, and manufactured legless assertions by which he purported to inform himself to the extent necessary to be able to make his contested claims. [97] In my view, it is an abuse of the court's process to allow the plaintiff to relitigate the issue of whether there were reasonable and probable grounds for the RMO. [98] 311165 had the opportunity to challenge the basis for the RMO at the hearing before MacKenzie A.C.J. The plaintiff (or its privy, Mr. Ambrose) did so. As MacKenzie A.C.J. noted at para. 8 of the Variation Application, "[t]he applicant has approached this application as a full hearing on the merits, disregarding the need to obtain leave to apply to set aside the Order." She concluded at para. 9 that "[e]ven viewing the applicant's evidence in the manner most favourable to the applicant, the applicant has not demonstrated by the affidavit of Mr. Ambrose or submissions of counsel that there were insufficient grounds to grant the Order." Later at para. 81, she agreed "with the Attorney General that the applicant has not demonstrated an absence of reasonable grounds". [99] The plaintiff raises no new arguments here; rather, it advances the same arguments as it did, or could have, before MacKenzie A.C.J. While the plaintiff does not seek to set aside the RMO itself, it is effectively relitigating what was already decided at the variation hearing: that there were reasonable grounds for the RMO. Therefore, it is an abuse of process to reargue this issue. [100] One consequence of this finding is that the claim against the Attorney General of Canada for malicious prosecution must fail and be struck under R. 9-5(1)(d). A claim for malicious prosecution requires proof of the absence of reasonable and probable grounds; however, I have determined that it is an abuse of process for the plaintiff to revisit this issue. [101] Lack of reasonable and probable grounds is not an element of the abuse of process claim that the plaintiff makes against the Attorney General of Canada. In setting out the legal basis for its abuse of process claim at Part 3, para. 18 of its notice of civil claim, the plaintiff pleads, in part: a. counsel for the Director of Public Prosecutions had a clear warning that much of the facts and premises of Constable Gardener's affidavit were false and misleading, and that a deceptive tactic of conflation was being used by the RCMP; b. the Director of Public Prosecutions acted for ancillary or collateral and improper purpose in maintaining the RMO after becoming aware of the failing of the Gardener affidavit; [102] While these allegations refer to the Gardiner Affidavit as false and misleading, it is not, in my view, plain and obvious that the abuse of process claim is an attempt to relitigate the question of whether there were reasonable and probable grounds for the RMO. The thrust of the pleading appears to focus on the conduct of the Director of Public Prosecutions in light of the assertion that the Director had become aware of the failings of the Gardiner Affidavit in relation to Mr. Ambrose and the 3621 Property. Accepting that there were such reasonable and probable grounds, the pleadings still offer some basis on which to argue the claim for abuse of process. [103] Thus, this claim should not be struck under R. 9-5(1)(d). D. Black Press Litigation [104] The Black Press Litigation is a defamation claim. The plaintiff does not advance a defamation claim in this proceeding. I have also determined that the related claims in injurious falsehood and slander of title should be struck under R. 9-5(1)(a). [105] The Black Press Litigation facts included in the notice of civil claim are unnecessary to the rest of the claims and as a result should be struck under R. 9-5(1)(b). IX. Summary [106] The abuse of process claim against the Attorney General of Canada should not be struck. [107] The malicious prosecution claim against the Attorney General of Canada should be struck under R. 9-5(1)(d) as an abuse of process. [108] The remaining claims should be struck under R. 9-5(1)(a) except for the proviso regarding negligent investigation. [109] The facts related to the Black Press Litigation are struck under R. 9-5(1)(b).
