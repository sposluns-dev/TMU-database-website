[17] I have, therefore, first examined the independent documentary evidence provided by the RPD14, documents and DVDs submitted by the claimant15 and counsel's written submissions16 with regards to the present situation for Roma in Hungary. Hungary has a population of approximately ten million [2010].17 According to the United States Department of State (DOS) report18 the Romani community remains the largest ethnic minority in Hungary accounting for two percent of the population (approximately 200,000 persons). However, unofficial estimates varied widely and suggested the actual figure was much higher, ranging between 500,000 and 800,000 persons.19 [18] An Amnesty International (AI) article20 reports that since 2006 Hungary has been going through political upheaval and a deepening economic crisis. The Roma are more susceptible to becoming scapegoats and blamed for the country's socio-economic problems as a larger proportion of Roma are dependent upon state economic support. The AI article mentions that eighty percent of adult Hungarians believe that the problems of the Roma would stop if the Roma started working and sixty-two percent agree that criminal tendency is in the blood of Roma.21 I find that the documentary evidence indicates that the attitude of some Hungarian people, including some in positions of authority, toward the Roma is discriminatory and prejudicial. The effect has been to marginalize the Roma people. The AI document22 and the DOS report 23 as well as evidence provided by the claimant24 state that Roma are discriminated against in almost all fields of life, particularly in employment, education, housing, penal institutions, and access to public places. [19] Based upon the latest RPD documents25 and documents provided by the claimant26 the situation for Roma individuals in Hungary has not improved, but rather worsened. According to the Society for Threatened Peoples (STP), an independent human rights organization based in Germany, Roma in Hungary are consciously despised by the majority population and pushed to the edge of society, while previously hidden anti-Roma attitudes are becoming more open.27 [20] According to the latest United States DOS report; Among the most important human rights problems during the year were societal discrimination and exclusion of the Romani population and violent right-wing extremism. Discrimination against Roma exacerbated their already limited access to education, employment, health care, and social services. Right-wing extremism, including public campaigns by paramilitaries to intimidate and incite hatred against Roma and other minorities, increased. Also the government began implementing a new law that restricts media freedom by increasing government influence over the media in general. The government also adopted a new Fundamental Law to replace the 1949 constitution, as well as more than 20 cardinal laws. New laws concerning the judicial system, religious organizations, and media freedom gave rise to concerns that the new legislation could undermine the country's democratic institutions by removing key checks and balances. The Fundamental Law and most cardinal laws were to come in to force on January 1, 2012.28 According to the European Commission against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI) Report on Hungary29 Roma children experience high drop-out rates at the secondary level of education as well as correspondingly limited future choices and employment prospects. The claimant's efforts to seek State Protection [27] Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, I find that the claimant has not rebutted the presumption of state protection. [28] One of the determinative issues in the case at hand is the presumption that countries are capable of protecting their citizens, and this underscores the principle that international protection comes into play only when a refugee claimant has no other recourse available. [29] The Immigration and Refugee Board (Board) is not obliged to prove that Hungary can offer the claimant effective state protection, rather, the claimant bears the legal burden of rebutting the presumption that adequate state protection exists by adducing clear and convincing evidence which satisfies the Board on a balance of probabilities.47 [30] In other words, to rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide "clear and convincing" confirmation of the state's inability to protect its citizens.48 A claimant who alleges that state protection is inadequate must persuade the Board that the evidence establishes that the state protection is, in fact, inadequate. The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities.49 [31] A claimant must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.50 I considered whether or not the claimant took all reasonable steps in his circumstances to seek protection. [32] The claimant was questioned as to what efforts he made to seek state protection. The claimant testified that on a number of occasions he sought the assistance of the police but generally the perpetrators were not found. I have previously found that the claimant failed to provide evidence regarding police reports. [33] The claimant testified that in 2010 their property was set on fire and he called the fire department and police. He testified that the police arrived and waited for the fire department while the fire continued to burn. He testified that his family suspect it was XXXX XXXX, a man who was formerly married to one of the claimant's sons, who is responsible for the fire. I asked the claimant if this was not a case of a personal vendetta. The claimant testified that there had been two personal issues when there was a fight which involved Mr. XXXX. In this particular incident, it may be that it is not motivated because of the claimant's ethnicity, but rather a personal issue involving one or two family members. In any case, I take note that the police did respond to the claimant's call and, although it may have been frustrating for the claimants, the police cannot be faulted for the lateness of the fire department. The claimant testified that he and his family members were able to get the fire under control. The claimant testified that he asked the police to provide security by regularly patrolling their area and he was disappointed that they came infrequently. He testified that they did not hear from police regarding those responsible for the fire. The fact that the claimants did not hear from the police is not persuasive evidence that the police were doing nothing in regards to an investigation. I appreciate the family's frustration of not finding the arsonists. However, I do not think that the police's inability to find unidentified perpetrators of the fire, before the claimants had left Hungary in XXXX 2010, indicates that state protection is not adequate. [34] The claimant testified that in late 2009 their animals were poisoned but they did not call the police because by this time they were focusing on leaving the country. The police cannot be faulted if the claimant chose not to request police assistance in this matter. [35] In view of these principles outlined above, I find that the claimant has not provided the requisite clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, state protection in Hungary is inadequate. State Protection Efforts for the Roma in Hungary [36] The Court has indicated that it is not enough to say that steps are being taken that may in the future result in adequate state protection. It is the state protection that is actually provided at the present time that is relevant.51 Regard must be given to what is actually happening and not what the state is endeavouring to put in place.52 Any efforts must have "actually translated into adequate state protection" at the operational level.53 [37] I have, therefore, reviewed the documentary evidence with regard to the Court's instructions that efforts concerning state protection must have actually translated into adequate state protection. [38] A fair reading of the documentary evidence indicates that the central government is motivated and willing to implement measures to protect the Roma, but these measures are not always implemented effectively at the local or municipal level. The documentary evidence relating to government efforts to protect the Roma and to legislate against broader forms of discrimination and persecution is mixed. [39] I acknowledge that there are some inconsistencies among several sources within the documentary evidence; however, the objective evidence regarding current country conditions suggests that, although not perfect, there is adequate state protection in Hungary for Roma who are victims of crime, police abuse, discrimination or persecution, that Hungary is making serious efforts to address these problems, and that the police and government officials are both willing and able to protect victims. Twenty regional police departments are directly subordinate to the NPH; city police are subordinate to the regional police and have local jurisdiction.54 [41] According to Amnesty International: [T]here were nine attacks against Romani communities in 2008 and 2009 which bore similar characteristics. Although the victims of the attacks lived in various places across the country, their houses were mostly located on the peripheries of settlements close to motorways. [51] I find that the evidence, thus, indicates that police do still commit abuses against people, including the Roma, but there is also evidence that indicates it is reasonable to expect authorities to take action in these cases and that the police are capable of protecting Roma. For example, on August 9, the National Bureau of Investigation announced the completion of its investigation into a series of physical attacks against Roma in 2008 and 2009, which was referred to earlier in my reasons. As a result of the investigation, the Pest County Prosecutor charged four suspects with the murder of six persons, the serious injury of five others, and threats to the safety of an additional 55. The case was scheduled to go to trial in 2011.64 This was one of the more high-profile series of attacks against the Roma. In light of the criticism relating to Hungary's initial investigations into these crimes, Hungarian officials, in November 2008, created a special investigation unit with the National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) consisting of fifty members, established specifically to investigate the series of attacks. The unit was then strengthened in April 2009 to one hundred members, resulting in the arrest of the aforementioned suspects. 65 [52] The claimant testified that he and members his family were victims of violence by racists. The claimant testified that they did not get adequate protection from the police. He testified that it was usual for the police to close any investigation by stating that the perpetrators were unknown. Considering the totality of the evidence in this case, including the claimant's testimony regarding his interactions with the police and those of similarly situated persons, I find that there may be situations and circumstances where some police may act in a discriminatory manner, and in other situations and circumstances the police may not have sufficient evidence to conclude an investigation such that they would charge/arrest/convict the perpetrators. Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, however, I find that the claimants had and would have recourse to seek remedies against institutional and non-institutional discrimination and racist practices, to obtain state protection against violence, and that there are numerous mechanisms in place to seek redress if the claimants were to be denied protection or if their complaints were not to be taken seriously by the police due to their Roma ethnicity. [53] For example, the European Commission Against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI) notes with satisfaction that, in 2007, the Hungarian authorities enacted legislation creating the Independent Police Complaints Board (IPCB), which began functioning in spring 2008. The ECRI was established by the Council of Europe. It is composed of independent and impartial members, who are appointed on the basis of their moral authority and recognized expertise in dealing with racism, xenophobia, anti-Semitism and intolerance. The IPCB was created to "investigate concrete complaints against the actions of police." This Board is composed of five legal experts elected by Parliament for six years. The IPCB is an independent body that offers viewpoints to the police on decisions taken and provides "recommendations to improve the work of the police". The IPCB investigated violations and omissions by the police that affected fundamental rights. As of December 8, the IPCB had received 285 complaints from the public. It reviewed 501 (including some cases filed in 2009) and found legal violations in 157. The organization forwarded 157 cases to the national police chief, who agreed with the findings in one case, partially accepted the findings in twenty-seven, and rejected the remainder. Information in the NDP shows that penalties for police officers found guilty of wrongdoing include reprimand, dismissal, and criminal prosecution. As stated previously, a legislative amendment that took effect on March 24th mandates the automatic dismissal of police officers convicted of criminal acts committed while on duty.66 [54] The Budapest-based NGO European Roma Rights Center (ERRC) describes the IPCB as a credible and independent watchdog to ensure the accountability of the police and has called for the government to ensure the IPCB's independence and strengthen its mandate.67 [55] According to the documentary evidence, where a Roma victim of violence or threats has made a complaint to the local police which has not been handled adequately or fairly, there is recourse to different complaint mechanisms. According to the Minorities Ombudsman, the Police Act of 1994 allows for a complaint against an "unlawful action" of a police officer to be submitted to the local captain of the police, with a higher ranking captain having the option to appeal the decision. The resulting decision can be reviewed by the court. The HHC stated that complaints shall be submitted within 30 days of the alleged violation by the concerned person or his or her representative. Complaints may also be represented by human rights NGOs or foundations, minority self-governments or certain law professors. The complaint is decided on in an administrative procedure by the head of the respective police unit within 30 days after receiving the complaint. The HHC notes that this "first instance decision" can be appealed "on any ground" to the "superior of the concerned police unit". If the appeal is forwarded to it, the superior body shall deliver a reasoned decision, affirming, modifying or annulling the first instance decision. The subsequent "second instance" decision may be challenged before the court by the complainant. However, the Court "may not change the decision: it may only send the case back to the police and order a new procedure. The police are obliged to follow the guidelines set by the court in the new procedure recommendations to the NPH and reporting its findings to Parliament.68 [56] There is also a complaints office at the National Police headquarters. Police action, or inaction, can be challenged in the civil courts; victims can file lawsuits for damages to remedy personal rights violations. There is no single independent institution in Hungary to investigate corruption cases, but there are a number of governmental departments mandated to fight different types of corruption. The Defence Service of Law Enforcement Agencies, which falls under the Ministry of Justice and Law Enforcement, investigates if a member of law enforcement is involved in a corruption case; citizens can turn to them with information. High-level corruption and organized crime cases are under the jurisdiction of the Central Investigation Department of the National Office of the Prosecutor.69 The ECRI, in its report covering the year of 2008, notes that incidents of police brutality towards Roma continue to be reported. It also notes that Prejudice against Roma still permeates the police force, as other sectors of society. According to this report, at the end of 2006, a number of anti-Roma postings were reported by two police officers to have been made on an internal web site of the national police. This web site was immediately suspended and an investigation launched. Several police officers found responsible for the postings were later sent to tolerance training.70 [57] Taking into account the foregoing, the documentary evidence indicates that criticism of Hungary's treatment of the Roma is warranted, especially in the context of the standards followed by other European Union member states in relation to human rights. Despite that, it is the claimant's responsibility in this particular case to rebut the presumption that adequate state protection exists in Hungary. I find that the Hungarian government is motivated and willing to implement measures to protect the Roma, and I have provided specific examples of how this is effective at the operational level. I find that there are recourses available to the claimant if he is not satisfied with police response at the first level or in the first instance. [58] While I acknowledge that the documentary evidence is mixed, in the circumstances particular to this case, the claimant has not demonstrated that state protection in Hungary is inadequate or that he need not have taken all reasonable efforts to seek state protection in Hungary before seeking international protection in Canada. [59] Therefore, regarding the totality of the evidence before me, while there is evidence to indicate that police do still commit abuses against people, including the Roma, the evidence also demonstrates that it is reasonable to expect authorities to take action in these cases and that the police are both willing and capable of protecting Roma, and that there are organizations in place to ensure that the police are held accountable. Therefore, in the circumstances of this case, the presumption that adequate state protection exists in Hungary is not rebutted. Principles and Jurisprudence Applied [85] In assessing the issue of state protection, I am guided by the jurisprudence from the Supreme Court of Canada and the Federal Court. The courts have established a number of principles relating to state protection and I have applied those principles to the case at hand. [86] There is a presumption that, except in situations where the state is in complete breakdown, the state is capable of protecting its citizens. The presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens underscores the principle that international protection comes into play only when a refugee claimant has no other recourse available. Having canvassed the country conditions documents, I find that Hungary is in effective control of its territory and has in place a functioning security force to uphold the laws and constitution of the country.94 [87] To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide "clear and convincing" confirmation of the state's inability to protect its citizens.95 A claimant who alleges that state protection is inadequate must persuade the Board that the evidence establishes that the state protection is in fact, inadequate. The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate.96 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact that the state's efforts are not always successful will not rebut the presumption of state protection.97 A claimant cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state.98 Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection.99 [88] Refugee protection is meant to be a form of surrogate protection to be invoked only in those situations where the refugee claimant has unsuccessfully sought the protection of their home state. The onus is on the claimant to approach the state for protection in situations where state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.100 To qualify for refugee status, a claimant must satisfy the Board that he or she sought, but was unable to obtain, protection from their home state, or alternatively, that their home state, on an objective basis, could not be expected to provide protection.101 In the absence of a compelling explanation, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim, at least where the state is a functioning democracy with a willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens.102 [89] A claimant's burden of proof is directly proportional to the level of democracy in the state in question: the more democratic the state's institutions, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them.103 In a functioning democracy, a claimant will have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status. The documentary evidence104 before the Board indicates that Hungary is a democracy, and there are free and fair elections. There is a relatively independent and impartial judiciary. Therefore, in countries such as Hungary, the claimant must do more than merely show that he or she went to see members of the police force and that those efforts were unsuccessful. A claimant must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.105 Local failures by authorities to provide protection do not mean that the state as a whole fails to protect its citizens, unless the failures form part of a broader pattern of the state's inability or refusal to provide protection.106 No government is expected to guarantee perfect protection to all of its citizens at all times, and the fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens, is not enough to justify a claim, especially where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authorities in place and is making serious efforts to protect its citizens.107 Less than perfect protection is not a basis to determine that a state is either unwilling or unable to offer reasonable protection.108 [90] I determine that the claimant is not a Convention refugee given that state protection is available to him in Hungary.
