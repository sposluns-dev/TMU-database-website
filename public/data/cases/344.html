Mohebbi v. North Vancouver RCMP

2015 BCSC 2083 Mohebbi v. North Vancouver RCMP IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Mohebbi v. North Vancouver RCMP, 2015 BCSC 2083 Date: 20151117 Docket: S153574 Registry: Vancouver Between: Farideh Mohebbi Plaintiff And North Vancouver RCMP, Superintendent J.C. Kennedy and Guild Yule LLP Defendants Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Williams Reasons for Judgment Plaintiff Self-Represented: No Appearance Counsel for Superintendent J.C. Kennedy: Counsel for Guild Yule LLP: S. Arden S.G. Cordell Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. August 12, 2015 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. November 17, 2015 [1] This application arises in the context of an action involving multiple claims against the North Vancouver detachment of the RCMP, Superintendent J.C. Kennedy, and the law firm of Guild Yule LLP ("Guild Yule") by the plaintiff, Farideh Mohebbi ("the Action"). [2] In this application, Guild Yule seeks an order that it be struck from the Notice of Civil Claim and the claim against it be dismissed pursuant to R. 9-5(1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009. [3] The North Vancouver RCMP and Superintendent Kennedy seek a similar order to what Guild Yule seeks. In the alternative, however, they request that: 1. the Minister of Justice of the Province of British Columbia (the "Minister") be substituted for the defendant North Vancouver RCMP; 2. the action against Superintendent Kennedy be dismissed; 3. the style of proceeding be amended accordingly; 4. the time for service and filing of the Minister's Response to Civil Claim be extended until 21 days after the service and filing of the Amended Notice of Civil Claim or the date of issuance of the Order made on this application, whichever is applicable; and 5. costs be awarded. Relevant Facts [4] On May 22, 2013, constables from the North Vancouver detachment of the RCMP, acting pursuant to the Mental Health Act, RSBC 1996, c. 288, attended at Ms. Mohebbi's home and removed her to Lion's Gate Hospital in North Vancouver, British Columbia, where she was detained. Ms. Mohebbi was handcuffed during the incident. [5] Ms. Mohebbi later filed two lawsuits against the Lions' Gate Hospital and others in relation to her arrest and detention (the "Other Actions"). Guild Yule represents several of the defendants in the Other Actions. [6] On March 6, 2014, Ms. Mohebbi lodged a formal complaint with the RCMP, alleging that the constables who detained her used improper force by handcuffing her and escorting her in front of her neighbours. Ms. Mohebbi also alleged that a constable neglected his duty by failing to properly investigate a complaint of sexual assault that she made against a physician who had treated her. [7] On February 27, 2015, Superintendent Kennedy, as the officer in charge of the North Vancouver RCMP detachment, wrote a letter to Ms. Mohebbi. The letter was in accordance with the public complaint process overseen by the Commission for Public Complaints Against the RCMP. That letter advised that the allegation of improper use of force had been found to be unsupported and that no action would be taken. However, the second allegation, Superintendent Kennedy reported, was found to be supported. The constable responding to the assault complaint had failed to follow procedure for such incidents. As a consequence, he received further training for future investigations, and the investigation into the sexual assault was reopened and assigned to the Sex Crimes Unit. [8] On April 13, 2015, Ms. Mohebbi attended the office of Guild Yule where she met with Mr. Timothy Wedge, an associate with the firm, in order to discuss the Other Actions. According to affidavit evidence, during that meeting Ms. Mohebbi demanded that Mr. Wedge seek settlement instructions from his clients. When he declined to do so, Ms. Mohebbi refused to leave the office. Eventually, building security was called, followed by the Vancouver Police, who escorted Ms. Mohebbi from the building. [9] On May 3, 2015, Ms. Mohebbi filed a Notice of Civil Claim - the Action. In her pleadings, Ms. Mohebbi alleges the following claims against the respective defendants: · North Vancouver RCMP - discrimination, racism, and bullying; · Superintendent Kennedy - intentional and negligent infliction of emotional distress; and · Guild Yule LLP - "Taking [Advantage] of [a] Person with Disabilities." Ms. Mohebbi seeks damages of $7 million. [10] Guild Yule argues that the Notice of Civil Claim should be struck under R. 9-5(1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules as it discloses no reasonable claim and is unnecessary and vexatious, embarrassing, and an abuse of process. [11] I note that Ms. Mohebbi is a self-represented litigant. Evidence adduced in the course of the hearing of this application satisfies me she was aware of the hearing and that she consented to the date before it was booked; however, for reasons she has provided, she elected not to attend. Discussion and Analysis [12] Rule 9-5(1) reads as follows: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [13] In Dempsey et al. v. Envision Credit Union et al., 2006 BCSC 750, Garson J. summarized the jurisprudence on R. 9-5, at para. 17: In summary, a pleading will be struck out if: (a) the pleadings are unintelligible, confusing and difficult to understand (Citizens of Foreign Aid Reform [Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, 1999 CarswellBC 2111 (S.C.)]); (b) the pleadings do not establish a cause of action and do not advance a claim known in law (Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, supra); (c) the pleadings are without substance in that they are groundless, fanciful and trifle with the Court's time (Borsato v. Basra, [2000 BCSC 28, rev'd on other grounds 2000 BCSC 1898]) (d) the pleadings are not bona fides, are oppressive and are designed to cause the Defendants anxiety, trouble and expense (Borsato v. Basra, supra); (e) the action is brought for an improper purpose, particularly the harassment and oppression of the Defendants (Ebrahim v. Ebrahim, 2002 BCSC 466). [citations added] [14] A court has the power under R. 9-5 to stay the proceedings and allow an amendment to the pleadings or to dismiss the action in its entirety. The latter should only occur in "plain and obvious" situations: Herbison v. Canada (Attorney General), 2013 BCSC 2020, at para. 18, rev'd in part on other grounds 2014 BCCA 461. [15] Discussing a judge's role in a motion to strike, the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42, stated the following at para. 23: [23] . It is not about evidence, but the pleadings. The facts pleaded are taken as true. Whether the evidence substantiates the pleaded facts, now or at some future date, is irrelevant to the motion to strike. The judge on the motion to strike cannot consider what evidence adduced in the future might or might not show. To require the judge to do so would be to gut the motion to strike of its logic and ultimately render it useless. [Emphasis added.] [16] Where a claim is brought by self-represented litigants, the court should consider whether defective pleadings can be corrected by way of an amendment. The court should not, however, provide legal advice on how those deficiencies can be cured: Ahmed v. Assu, 2014 BCSC 1768, at para. 19. [17] The test for striking pleadings is that the court may do so if, assuming the material facts stated in the notice of civil claim can be proved, it is "plain and obvious" the plaintiff's pleadings disclose no reasonable cause of action or have no reasonable prospect of success: Imperial Tobacco, at para. 17; Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959, at 980. That said, though material facts must be taken as true, there is no such requirement for allegations based on assumption and speculation: Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083, at para. 19. [18] In Borsato, at para. 24, Master Baker stated that "[a] pleading is frivolous if it is without substance, is groundless, fanciful, 'trifles with the court' or wastes time." A pleading is vexatious or unnecessary where it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or fails to advance any known legal claim: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, at para. 47. [19] Where pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what claim is being pleaded, they are "embarrassing": Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, at para. 47. Pleadings are also embarrassing where they are so irrelevant that allowing them to stand would impose useless expenses and prejudice the trial by involving parties in a dispute tangential to the main claim: Keddie v. Dumas Hotels Ltd. (Cariboo Trail Hotel), [1985] 62 B.C.L.R. 145, at 147 (C.A.). [20] In addition, the defendant Guild Yule argues that Ms. Mohebbi's pleadings are an abuse of process. Considering that topic in Babavic v. Babowech, 1993 CarswellBC 2950 (S.C.), this court noted at para. 18 that: [t]he categories of abuse of process are open. Abuse of process may be found where proceedings involve a deception on the court or constitute a mere sham; where the process of the court is not being fairly or honestly used, or is employed for some ulterior or improper purpose; proceedings which are without foundation or serve no useful purpose and multiple or successive proceedings which cause or are likely to cause vexation or oppression. Analysis Guild Yule LLP [21] Ms. Mohebbi cites as the legal basis for her claim against Guild Yule, "Taking [Advantage] of Person with Disabilities with NO Lawyer and still Homeless!!!" The statement of facts pertaining to this defendant states, "Guildyule Lawyers called the police on (April-2015) and rudely send me out off their office while I had an appointment with them!!!" [22] To my mind, Ms. Mohebbi's pleadings do not establish a reasonable claim known in law against Guild Yule, despite my assuming her material facts are true. No material facts have been pleaded in Ms. Mohebbi's Notice of Civil claim that could establish a relationship between her and Guild Yule sufficiently proximate so as to give rise to a duty of care. A solicitor generally does not owe a duty of care to an opposing litigant: Lawrence v. Sandilands, 2003 BCSC 211, at para. 79. [23] Based on the jurisprudence referenced above, Ms. Mohebbi's pleadings, with regard to Guild Yule, are unnecessary, frivolous, and vexatious. As it is difficult to understand what is being pled, they are also embarrassing. I am unable to guess what claim could possibly be raised here with even the most generous of interpretation. However, I am not prepared to agree with Guild Yule that Ms. Mohebbi's pleadings rise to the level of an abuse of process. That said, I do not believe the pleadings can be remedied by amendment. As a result, I would strike the pleadings as they relate to Guild Yule and dismiss the claim against that party. Superintendent Kennedy [24] In the "Legal Basis" section of the Notice of Civil Claim, Ms. Mohebbi alleges "intentional infliction of emotional distress," "negligent infliction of emotional distress," and that Superintendent Kennedy was "engaged in UNLawful [Activities] with" two doctors. Under "Statement of Facts," Ms. Mohebbi states the following: Since (June-2014) which I made my complain to the NV RCMP I have [been] sending: documents and letters and emails to: Superintendent Kennedy so far no result yet ..??? Mr. Kennedy did not give Permission to the other Officers to investigate on time and bring out the Facts. Abusing the Person with Disabilities with Mental Health Act. [25] Section 21 of the Police Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 367, protects police officers such as Superintendent Kennedy from personal liability absent "dishonesty, gross negligence, or malicious or wilful misconduct." [26] Ms. Mohebbi's pleadings do not disclose a reasonable cause of action against Superintendent Kennedy. Moreover, given the standard required to pierce the statutory protection afforded to an investigator under the Police Act, it is unlikely that there is a reasonable prospect for success. There is no mention of anything in the pleadings apart from assumption and speculation that amounts to "dishonesty, gross negligence, or malicious or wilful misconduct." [27] Ms. Mohebbi's claim that Superintendent Kennedy was "engaged in UNLawful [Activities] with" two doctors, without the required material facts, amounts to mere assumption and speculation. Therefore, I do not have to take it as true. It is unclear whether what is alleged here is a claim of criminal or civil liability. I also find that, with regard to Superintendent Kennedy, the pleadings are embarrassing due to their lack of clarity. I do not believe that these defects can be cured by amending the pleadings. [28] Accordingly, I would strike those portions of the pleadings with regard to Superintendent Kennedy and dismiss the claim against him. It is therefore not necessary to consider, as counsel for Superintendent Kennedy argues, that Ms. Mohebbi's claim is a collateral attack on an administrative decision. RCMP [29] Ms. Mohebbi alleges "Racism - Bullying - Corruption" as the legal basis for her claim against the RCMP. Under the "Statement of Facts," she notes: On (May/22/2013): North Vancouver RCMP acted on (Mental Health Act). 3 Officers handcuffed me & send me to the hospital ([Lions' Gate Hospital])??? Only based on A phone call from a NURSE (cps) !!! I have (PTSD) Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder after that day... [Emphasis in original.] [30] Like Ms. Mohebbi's claims against the other defendants, the pleadings against the RCMP do not seem to express a reasonable claim. They are confusing and unintelligible, though to a lesser extent. However, I do not believe they are frivolous or unnecessary as are the pleadings against the other defendants. Here, Ms. Mohebbi describes some material facts that could potentially form the basis of a claim, though I make no comment on the potential for success or failure of that claim. Also, taking into consideration Ms. Mohebbi's self-representation and the principle set out in Ahmed, I conclude it is appropriate to allow Ms. Mohebbi to amend her pleadings with regard to the RCMP. [31] Ms. Mohebbi will have 60 days from service upon her of these reasons and the entered judgment to amend, file, and serve her amended pleadings. In the event that Ms. Mohebbi has not served her amended pleadings within 60 days, her action will stand dismissed. No further court application will be necessary to secure the order for dismissal. That order will be available upon proof of service on Ms. Mohebbi of these reasons and the entered order and upon proof by affidavit evidence that there has been no compliance with the orders made herein. [32] Counsel for the defendant RCMP points out that the "North Vancouver RCMP" identified in the Notice of Civil Claim is not a legal entity. Instead, the correct defendant when tortious conduct is alleged against provincial constables is the Minister: Police Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 367, ss. 11, 21. Counsel requests the Minister be substituted as defendant. I would agree and order that substitution. Summary [33] In summary, I would order that: 1. the claim against Guild Yule LLP be struck from the Notice of Civil Claim and the action against that party be dismissed; 2. the claim against Superintendent J.C. Kennedy be struck from the Notice of Civil Claim and the action against him be dismissed; 3. the Minister of Justice of the Province of British Columbia be substituted for the defendant, North Vancouver RCMP; 4. the style of proceeding be amended accordingly; 5. Ms. Mohebbi have leave to amend her pleadings in accordance with the timeline set out above; and 6. The Minister will have 21 days from service to file a response to Ms. Mohebbi's amended Notice of Civil Claim. [34] I would dispense with the requirement that Ms. Mohebbi endorse the order. [35] Given the circumstances of this action, I will make no order as to costs at this time. "J. Williams J."
