October 23, 2015 The Application [1] In this breach of contract action, the defendants seek the following orders: 1. An order staying the plaintiff's action until the plaintiff posts security for costs in the amount of $55,000.00, or any other amount the Court may order. 2. In the alternative, an order staying the entire action until the plaintiff posts security for costs in the amount of $55,000.00, or any other amount the Court may order, and that if the plaintiff fails to post security for costs within 60 days, the plaintiff's claim will be dismissed as abandoned and the defendants will be at liberty to proceed on their counterclaim. 3. Costs of this application forthwith, in any event of the cause. [2] The claim for $55,000 as security is based on the defendants being awarded special costs if successful at trial. However, in oral submissions, the defendants suggested that the sum of $25,000 would be adequate security. Facts [3] The business dealings which resulted in this proceeding date back to January 2010 and centre around the operation of a Salty's Fish & Chips outlet in Langford, B.C. According to the pleadings, the defendant Salty's Fish & Chips Ltd. is a franchisor and grants licenses to third parties to operate individual outlets. Krista Enslow was an employee of the defendant franchisor between 2001 and 2007. Ms. Enslow became involved in the operation of the Langford outlet in 2010. [4] The original licensing agreement dated January 18, 2010, is between 0863196 B.C. Ltd. ("086") as licensee and the franchisor defendant. Krista Enslow is a director and shareholder in 086. In 2010, the other shareholder in 086 was Ms. Enslow's then romantic partner, Christopher Bradbury. Ms. Enslow, Mr. Bradbury and Bradbury Enterprises Ltd. were named as indemnitors under the licensing agreement. The agreement had a five year term, subject to renewal periods of two years. [5] In conjunction with this agreement, Bradbury Enterprises purchased all of the shares and assets in 086 for the sum of $215,000. [6] On December 11, 2012, 086 was dissolved. The company was recently restored to the Registrar of Companies on application by the Canada Revenue Agency. [7] By late 2012, Ms. Enslow and Mr. Bradbury had parted ways. According to the pleadings, 086 did not effectively manage the business. Nevertheless, Ms. Enslow apparently remained in the good books of the defendant franchisor as an arrangement was put in place to allow the continued operation of the outlet without Mr. Bradbury's involvement. [8] On February 18, 2013, Ms. Enslow incorporated the plaintiff. At about the same time, the licensing agreement with 086 was terminated pursuant to a letter from the defendants' solicitors. The letter outlines numerous breaches of the agreement by 086. [9] Next, on March 28, 2013, a new agreement was entered into between the defendant franchisor and the plaintiff as franchisee. The agreement names Krista Enslow and her father, Wayne Enslow, as guarantors of the franchisee's obligations under the agreement. The agreement also refers to an Asset Purchase Agreement. That agreement is not in evidence. Ms. Enslow says that there was "supposed to be" an assignment of assets from 086 to the plaintiff. This asset assignment, from a by then defunct company, was never perfected. However, Ms. Enslow says that the plaintiff continued to have the benefit and use of these assets which are essentially the equipment and supplies required to operate the outlet. [10] Subject to any breaches resulting in termination, the new agreement was to remain in force until 2016. The plaintiff also entered into a sublease with the second named defendant. [11] The plaintiff's operation of the business was not profitable. Ms. Enslow blames both the defendants' interference and a general economic downturn for this circumstance. The defendants say that the plaintiff mismanaged the business and failed to comply with the terms of the franchise and sublease agreements. [12] In early December 2014, the defendants demanded payment of royalties and rent from the plaintiff. The plaintiff made the rent payment but cheques for the royalties bounced due to insufficient funds. The plaintiff says that the royalty payment demands were premature as these monies were not due until January 2015. [13] A further letter of termination was issued by the defendants' solicitors such that the plaintiff vacated the premises leaving all equipment and supplies behind. The plaintiff says that those items remain in the possession of the defendants; there is no evidence to the contrary. [14] The plaintiff commenced this action on April 27, 2015. The notice of civil claim was amended shortly before this application was heard. The primary allegation brought against the defendants is for breach of the franchise agreement, the sublease and a collateral oral agreement made between the parties. Among other remedies, the plaintiff claims the return of $230,000, being monies which the plaintiff allegedly invested and lost in the operation of the outlet. The plaintiff also asserts a claim for punitive and aggravated damages. [15] The defendants' response pleads the plaintiff's various breaches of the franchise and sublease agreements. These allegations are repeated in a counterclaim brought against the plaintiff and the guarantors of the franchise agreement. In their counterclaim, the defendants seek judgment for breach of contract, a declaration that the royalties owed to Salty's Fish & Chips Ltd. are held in trust by each of the defendants by counterclaim, specific performance of the guarantee, and a declaration that the defendants by counterclaim must indemnify the defendants for all of the losses described in the pleadings. [16] A reply and response to counterclaim have also been filed. The more relevant pleading to this application is the counterclaim to counterclaim brought by Ms. Enslow against the defendants and their principal, Mr. Wass. In again seeking the return of $230,000, Ms. Enslow pleads the torts of intimidation, unlawful interference with economic relations and conversion. The conversion plea relates to the defendants' retention of personal items, business records and chattels following the termination of the franchise agreement and the plaintiff's eviction from the outlet. [17] Ms. Enslow says that her physical health has deteriorated since the events in question. She has made application for employment insurance sickness benefits. Currently, Ms. Enslow is employed part-time as a pizza cook. That part-time employment provides Ms. Enslow with a monthly income of about $1,200. A single parent of one child, Ms. Enslow receives child support of $500 and is borrowing monies from an unidentified source to make ends meet. Ms. Enslow has collapsed her only asset, an RRSP valued at $3,000. The circumstances of Wayne Enslow are also touched upon in the evidence. Mr. Enslow is said to be the recipient of a monthly disability benefit "from the government", the amount of which is not disclosed. Mr. Enslow and his spouse own a fully encumbered mobile home worth about $20,000. [18] The defendants present a bill of costs that includes preparation for and attendance at a five-day trial. The bill estimates the defendants' fees and disbursements necessary to conduct the proceeding at just over $24,000. Analysis [19] The parties agree on the general principles of law to be applied by the court. Those principles, including specific factors that are relevant to this particular application, are nicely summarized in Intergrated Contractors Ltd. v. Leduc Developments Ltd., 2009 BCSC 965 ("ICL"): [9] This application is governed by the provisions of s. 236 of the Business Corporations Act, S.B.C. 2002, c. 57, which reads as follows: If a corporation is the plaintiff in a legal proceeding brought before the court, and if it appears that the corporation will be unable to pay the costs of the defendant if the defendant is successful in the defence, the court may require security to be given by the corporation for those costs, and may stay all legal proceedings until the security is given. [10] The court also has inherent jurisdiction to order a party to post security for costs. [11] The first stage of the legal test on an application for security for costs is the requirement that the applicant make out a prima facie case that the respondent would be unable to pay the applicant's costs if the respondent's claim fails: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 B.C.S.C., 91 A.C.W.S. (3d) 362; Kropp v. Swaneset Bay Golfcourse Ltd. (1997), 29 B.C.L.R. (3d) 252, 90 B.C.A.C. 170; and Fat Mel's Restaurant Ltd. v. Canadian Northern Shield Insurance Co. (1993) 76 B.C.L.R. (2d) 231 (C.A.), 25 B.C.A.C. 95. [12] If the applicants do meet this requirement, the respondent may resist an order for security for costs by showing that it has sufficient exigible assets to satisfy an award of costs: Scopeset Technology Inc. v. Astaro Corp., 2004 BCSC 830. [13] As well, the respondent to a security for costs application can resist an order for security for costs if it can show there is no arguable defence to its claim: Scopeset at para. 15. [14] In addition, there are other factors which may touch upon the exercise of the court's discretion in determining whether or not to grant an order requiring a party to post security for costs. Potentially relevant on the facts of this application are the following factors: a) Where the court is satisfied that ordering security for costs would unfairly stifle a valid claim, the court may refuse to order security: Kropp at para. 17; b) Where the security for costs application is brought against a defendant advancing a counterclaim, and the counterclaim is sufficiently intertwined with the defendant's defence of the main claim: Gray v. Powerassist Technologies Inc., 2001 BCSC 1208, 10 C.P.C. (5th) 148 (B.C.S.C.), and Scotford Electrical & Technical Services Ltd. v. Blue Mountain Log Sales Ltd., 2005 BCSC 538, 10 C.P.C. (6th) 237 (B.C.S.C., Master); and c) Where the financial hardship that may give rise to the respondent's inability to pay costs is due to the very actions of the applicants at issue in the respondent's claim: Tour-Mate Technologies Corp. v. Syntronix Systems Limited et al., [1993] B.C.J. No. 599 (S.C.); and Middlekamp v. Fraser Valley Real Estate Board, [1993] B.C.J. 2965 (S.C.). [15] Finally, if the court concludes a security for costs order is warranted, the court has discretion as to the quantum of security. [20] As this matter comes before a master of the court, the applicant may only rely on the statutory authority for the order: Emperor Oil Ltd. v. Panorama Petroleum Inc., 2015 BCSC 1498 at para. 4. [21] The plaintiff concedes that the defendants have met the first stage of the test. The plaintiff also acknowledges that it has no exigible assets to satisfy a costs award. [22] However, the plaintiff submits that there is no arguable defence or, alternatively, the defence is weak. I disagree. While the court has considered the evidentiary merits of defences on these types of applications, the threshold for establishing an arguable defence is quite low. This is not like the situation described in ICL where the defence was a mere denial without material facts being pled: paras. 25-27. The statement of defence here asserts positive allegations which, in turn, are relied on in the counterclaim. [23] The onus lies with the plaintiff to demonstrate that ordering security would unfairly stifle this action. That onus had not been met on the evidence presented. The plaintiff must do more than show that it has no assets; it must also persuade the court that there is no ability to raise funds: Kropp v. Swaneset at para. 22. The courts expect some evidence from the shareholder, director or operating mind of the impecunious corporation to address this inability to raise funds. The evidence must be by way of affidavit, not statements from counsel or mere speculation: Kropp at para. 2; Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, 1999 CanLII 5860 at para. 22. While Ms. Enslow has a very modest income and no assets, she acknowledges borrowing monies from an unidentified source to meet her day to day needs. She does not say that these borrowings are limited either in time or amounts or could not be accessed to assist the plaintiff. It is not clear to me whether the plaintiff's failure to fully address this particular factor was deliberate or simply an oversight. Nevertheless, the court should not be asked to shore up the plaintiff's case. [24] Nor has the evidence persuaded me that the plaintiff's financial hardship is due to the very actions of defendants. The plaintiff suggests that the hardship is due to the wrongful termination of the franchise and sublease agreements together with the defendants' wrongful retention of the chattels used in the outlet's operation. On this application, the plaintiff acknowledges that it has never owned any assets. The evidence is vague at best as to why the asset purchase agreement did not result in the transfer of the chattels' ownership to the plaintiff. Blame for the failure to perfect the asset purchase agreement should not be laid solely at the door of the defendants. The plaintiff was apparently a party to the agreement and could have ensured compliance with all terms. Banking records show that the plaintiff operated in the red throughout its operation of the Langford outlet. [25] Where the scales of justice start to tip in favour of the plaintiff, however, is with respect to the remaining factor mentioned in ICL. In ICL, the court decided that security for costs ought not to be ordered against a party whose counterclaim was sufficiently intertwined with the main claim. The court adopted the following passage from Gray v. Powerassist Technologies Inc., 2001 BCSC 1208: [29] In my opinion, the claim and counterclaim are so intertwined that it would constitute an injustice to permit the claim to proceed while stifling the ability of the defendants to pursue a counterclaim is [sic] founded on the same facts upon which the defence is dependent. While the claim and counterclaim clearly have some distinct features, particularly with respect to the nature and quantum of damages, both proceedings have their genesis in the same agreement and its termination. Both proceedings depend on a determination of which of the two parties was at fault in the termination of the agreement. [26] In the case at bar, the plaintiff is a defendant by counterclaim. The counterclaim by the defendants has resulted in the counterclaim to counterclaim. Under Rule 3-4 of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, the defendants' counterclaim may proceed even if the plaintiff's claim is stayed. Thus, the plaintiff could be left in the position of having to defend a claim without the ability to prosecute its own action where both actions involve essentially the same business dealings and agreements. The alternative remedy sought by the defendants on this application is that the entire action be stayed until the plaintiff posts security. That is not a fair result to all of the parties involved. In particular, it would be unjust to stay Ms. Enslow's counterclaim to counterclaim as she is not being asked to post security for costs. Like the court in ICL, I find this factor to weigh heavily against ordering the plaintiff to provide security for the defendants' costs. [27] While it remains uncertain as to whether the plaintiff could raise security for the defendants' costs, the court must be mindful that the objective of s. 236 is to provide "some protection for the community against litigious abuses by artificial persons manipulated by natural persons": Pearson v. Naydler, [1977] 3 All E.R. 531 at p. 533, cited with approval in Kropp at para. 16. [Emphasis added.] And, that the court should be concerned not to allow the power under s. 236 to be used to "stifle a genuine claim by an indigent company against a more prosperous company, particularly when the failure to meet that claim might in itself have been the material cause of the plaintiff's impecuniosity": Keary Developments Ltd. v. Tarmac Construction Ltd., [1995] 3 All E.R. 534 at p. 540. The plaintiff's claim may not ultimately succeed but the proceeding does not, on the evidence, amount to "litigious abuse". Here, the plaintiff's use of the corporate veil and continued involvement in the franchise relationship was at least acquiesced to if not promoted by the business savvy defendants. [28] In the result, the defendants' application is dismissed. The application may be renewed if the counterclaims are resolved or severed such that the only action to be tried is that between the plaintiff and the defendants. [29] Costs of the application will be in the cause. "C.P. Bouck" Master C.P. Bouck
