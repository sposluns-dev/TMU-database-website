Johnson v. Smith

2018 BCSC 836 Johnson v. Smith IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Johnson v. Smith, 2018 BCSC 836 Date: 20180522 Docket: S178037 Registry: Vancouver Between: David Eugene Johnson Plaintiff And Ellen Smith and Colin Smith Defendants Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Butler Reasons for Judgment The Plaintiff: Appearing In Person Counsel for the Defendants: Gordon T. Behan Place and Date of Trial/Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. January 26, 2018 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. May 22, 2018 [1] The defendants, Ellen Smith and Colin Smith, bring this application to strike the notice of civil claim filed by the plaintiff, David Johnson. The action arises from the plaintiff's relationship with Alice Hastings Janisch and the appointment of the defendant Ms. Smith as the committee of her mother, Ms. Janisch. The defendant Mr. Smith is the husband of Ms. Smith. Background [2] The plaintiff met Ms. Janisch in September 2014. He began a relationship with her and moved into her home in late 2014. At that time, Ms. Janisch was experiencing the onset of dementia. In May 2012, Ms. Janisch had granted an enduring power of attorney ("EPOA") to Ms. Smith, her only child. After receiving a medical opinion that Ms. Janisch was incapable of managing her affairs, Ms. Smith signed the EPOA on June 16, 2016. In early August 2016, Ms. Janisch was admitted to hospital in Sechelt and subsequently into a care home. At the same time, Ms. Smith, acting under the EPOA and with the assistance of the RCMP, arranged for the removal of the plaintiff from Ms. Janisch's home. [3] On September 9, 2016, Ms. Smith applied under the Patients Property Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 349, to be appointed committee over the person and estate of Ms. Janisch. The plaintiff appeared at the hearing of that application and opposed the orders sought by Ms. Smith. In reasons indexed as Janisch (Re), 2016 BCSC 2003, Madam Justice Griffin granted the orders sought (the "Griffin Reasons"). She found that Ms. Janisch was incapable of managing her financial affairs and personal care decisions and appointed Ms. Smith as committee. The plaintiff did not appeal that decision. [4] On September 1, 2016, the plaintiff commenced an action against Ms. Smith in Provincial Court. He claimed damages for his "unlawful eviction by use of an invalid power of attorney" from Ms. Janisch's home, for intentional infliction of financial and emotional distress, and for "the forced separation" from Ms. Janisch using an invalid power of attorney. The plaintiff subsequently discontinued the small claims action. [5] The notice of civil claim ("NOCC") in this action was filed on September 22, 2017. The plaintiff is a self-represented litigant. The NOCC is confusing, difficult to understand, and portions of it are unintelligible. It sets out the circumstances that form the basis of the claims under a number of headings: "Forced Breach of Agreements", "Defamatory Expressions", "Malicious Intent", "Elder Abuse/ Theft", "Fraud", "Hate Crime/ Discrimination" and "Surveillance Placed in Home/ Computer". The plaintiff claims damages for defamation, racial profiling, breach of contract, conspiracy to commit fraud, intentional infliction of financial and emotional distress and implementation of surveillance and computer monitoring. He also asks that the "committee be vacated and placed in the hands of the Public Guardian and Trustee". He seeks a declaration that he be recognized as Ms. Janisch's "Common Law Partner" and that he be recognized as an experienced caregiver. He also claims aggravated and punitive damages. [6] For the reasons that follow, I conclude that the whole of the NOCC should be struck. Rather than quoting from that document in any length in this judgment, I will refer to the summary of the plaintiff's claims set out in a table at paragraph 23 of the defendants' notice of application. The summary sets out the general nature of the claims and identifies the paragraphs in the NOCC that appear to plead the facts and law relevant to each cause of action. The Griffin Reasons [7] A fundamental problem with the NOCC is that it is a collateral attack on the order appointing Ms. Smith as committee. While the plaintiff advances other claims, all of his claims stem from his assertion that the EPOA was somehow obtained or used fraudulently and that Ms. Smith should not have been appointed committee for Ms. Janisch. The essence of his claim is that he and Ms. Janisch should have been permitted to continue in their relationship living in Ms. Janisch's home. He challenges the suggestion that Ms. Janisch was incapable of managing her financial affairs and personal care decisions. However, the issue of Ms. Janisch's capacity was conclusively determined in the Griffin Reasons and no appeal was taken from that order. [8] Given the importance of the Griffin Reasons, I will set out the relevant findings and observations made by Madam Justice Griffin, beginning at para. 5: [5] The Act requires there be two affidavits from medical practitioners setting out their opinion in support of these conclusions. There are two such affidavits in evidence. In short they are from Dr. John Sloan, a family physician, who is well qualified as the past president of the BC Association of Geriatric Care Physicians; and Dr. Williard Johnston, a family physician with considerable experience conducting capacity assessments. [6] Each medical doctor has provided the opinion that Mrs. Janisch is by reason of mental illness, disease or age incapable of managing her financial affairs and personal care decisions and that improvement is unlikely. I note that both physicians provided very detailed examples of the cognitive difficulties they observed on the part of Mrs. Janisch when they assessed her. These detailed examples were compelling. [20] The evidence is uncontradicted that Ms. Smith has had a strong relationship with her mother throughout her entire life. Ms. Smith has provided her petition materials to her uncle, who is Mrs. Janisch's only sibling, Mr. Arthur. He has reviewed the materials and signed a consent of next of kin consenting to Ms. Smith's appointment as committee. [21] The Public Guardian and Trustee, who has been served with these materials, takes no position but has indicated that the medical evidence does indeed indicate that Mrs. Janisch is incapable of managing herself and her affairs, that the Public Guardian and Trustee does not oppose the appointment of Ms. Smith as committee without bond and that Ms. Smith should receive her reasonable costs payable from the estate of Mrs. Janisch as special costs. [22] There is one party who has come to court opposing the application, and that is David Johnson. He did not file materials in time, but I allowed him to present his materials and make submissions. Mr. Johnson's evidence is that he met Mrs. Janisch in September 2014. According to the uncontradicted evidence of the petitioner, Mrs. Janisch was already experiencing signs of dementia by that time. [24] Mr. Johnson appears to have three points to make in his submissions. One, that he was in a loving relationship with Mrs. Janisch and that she would be better off to live with him than to live in a care facility. Two, he mistrusts the petitioner's motives and doubts that she has had a historically strong and loving relationship with her mother. Three, he appears to blame the petitioner for Mrs. Janisch's steep decline in her mental abilities. [25] Let me deal with the first point. Mr. Johnson is not seeking to be appointed the committee of Mrs. Janisch. Presumably he knows that if he was seeking to be her committee he would have to provide a judge of this court with evidence that he is capable of taking care of Mrs. Janisch's needs and that it would be in her best interests to appoint him as a committee rather than her daughter. He avoids having to prove his ability to be a committee this way and instead concentrates on attacking Mrs. Janisch's daughter. [26] It is not enough that someone loves a patient to qualify to be a committee. A person has to be trustworthy, financially responsible, have an ability to provide or supervise the provision of medical care, and show willingness to submit themselves to judicial oversight. Mr. Johnson avoids putting in evidence on all of these points. [27] There is some evidence filed by Ms. Smith which raises serious questions about Mr. Johnson's capability to be a committee. It is not necessary to decide those issues, but just to point out that Mr. Johnson's position is disingenuous. He wants to have the right to look after Mrs. Janisch, but he does not want to be subject to any rules or oversight and does not want to go to the trouble of proving his abilities. This position raises red flags in and of itself. [29] I turn to Mr. Johnson's suspicions regarding Ms. Smith's motives. His suspicions are not grounded in evidence. He scandalously attacks the petitioner, including publicly defaming her on the internet. [32] I will next address Mr. Johnson's third focus in his submissions. He seems to believe that Mrs. Janisch only deteriorated because of the petitioner's interference. I have to say I find this argument to be malicious, hurtful and unfair. One cannot really expect that Mrs. Janisch, if she was mentally capable, would be happy about her daughter being put through these attacks and through these suggestions that her daughter caused her to fail in her health. [33] There is absolutely no medical opinion to support Mr. Johnson's notion. The fact is that Mr. Johnson understood soon after he met Mrs. Janisch that she had health problems, she had dementia and it was progressive. Mr. Johnson's view of things fails to appreciate that the very fact of having this condition and being confused about the future is extremely stressful on the patient and on the patient's loved ones, including of course her daughter. [34] The materials set out in extensive detail the steps taken by Ms. Smith to arrange for proper care for her mother. I note Ms. Smith's evidence that she places considerable importance on having her mother nearby so that her children can visit with her regularly. It is important to her that her mother remains an important part of her family no matter how poor her mental condition is or becomes. Ms. Smith is hoping that through this experience her children will be able to learn the importance of standing by a family member even when this situation is difficult. [35] I am satisfied that the steps taken by Ms. Smith to date with respect to her mother have been in the best interests of her mother. I have no doubt whatsoever on the material that Ms. Smith will continue to act in her mother's best interests and has only her mother's best interests in mind in seeking to be appointed as committee. [9] I have set out the findings of Madam Justice Griffin in some detail, as the allegations in this action are similar to those made against Ms. Smith in the committeeship application. By advancing the claims alleged in the NOCC, the plaintiff is attacking the appointment of Ms. Smith as committee and attempting to re-litigate the issues that were determined in that proceeding. Law on striking pleadings [10] The defendants' application is brought pursuant to Rule 9-5(1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules: (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [11] In Dempsey v. Envision Credit Union, 2006 BCSC 750, Madam Justice Garson, as she then was, summarized the wide variety of circumstances in which a pleading will be struck out pursuant to what is now Rule 9-5, at para. 17: [17] In summary, a pleading will be struck out if: (a) the pleadings are unintelligible, confusing and difficult to understand [Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 (B.C.S.C.)]; (b) the pleadings do not establish a cause of action and do not advance a claim known in law (Citizens for Foreign aid Reform, supra); (c) the pleadings are without substance in that they are groundless, fanciful and trifle with the Court's time (Borsato v. Basra, [2000 BCSC 28]; (d) the pleadings are not bona fides, are oppressive and are designed to cause the Defendants anxiety, trouble and expense (Borsato v. Basra, supra); (e) the action is brought for an improper purpose, particularly the harassment and oppression of the Defendants (Ebrahim v. Ebrahim, 2002 BCSC 466). [12] In Huang v. Silvercorp Metals Inc., 2016 BCSC 278 at para. 19, Madam Justice Warren set out the test under Rule 9-5(1): whether it is "plain and obvious that the action is certain to fail because the pleading contains a radical defect". She also explained at para. 20, the different evidentiary rules that apply depending on the subsection involved: [20] The defendant relies on subparagraphs (a), (b) and (d) of Rule 9-5(1). The "plain and obvious" standard applies to all branches of Rule 9-5, but different evidentiary rules apply. For the purpose of an application to strike under subparagraph (a) on the basis that the pleading discloses no reasonable claim, no evidence is admissible and the facts asserted in the Notice of Civil Claim must be taken as proven: International Taoist Church [of Canada v. Ching Chung Taoist Association of Hong Kong Limited, 2011 BCCA 149,] at para. 21. While evidence is admissible on applications grounded upon subparagraphs (b) and (d), it is only admissible for the purpose of showing whether it is plain and obvious that the claim ought to be struck as unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious under subparagraph (b), or an abuse of process under subparagraph (d). [13] As in Huang, the defendants rely on subsections (a), (b) and (d). [14] Under sub-rule 9-5(1)(a), the analysis must proceed on the basis that the facts pleaded are true, unless they are manifestly incapable of being proven: R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42 at para. 22; Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 at paras. 34, 37 (B.C.S.C.). A notice of civil claim must set out clearly the material facts on which the plaintiff relies to support a cause of action known to law: Stoneman v. Denman Island Local Trust Committee, 2010 BCSC 636 at para. 27. The power to strike out claims that are bound to fail as a matter of law is a "valuable housekeeping measure essential to effective and fair litigation": Imperial Tobacco at para. 19. As the Court noted in Imperial Tobacco, it is beneficial to litigants and the justice system as a whole to weed out unmeritorious claims. [15] Applications to strike based on sub-rules 9-5(1)(b) and (d) do not proceed on the basis that the facts pleaded are true. As explained in Krist v. British Columbia, 2017 BCCA 78 at para. 24: In contrast, applications under subrules 9-5(1)(b), (c) and (d) permit, and arguably require, external facts or evidence. That is because applications under those subrules are contextually driven and context must be provided to establish the required findings. Those findings require applying a legal standard or principle to a set of facts. [16] Applying subsections (a), (b) and (d) to the claims set out in the NOCC, I conclude that some of the claims should be struck because the pleading discloses no cause of action and the remainder of the claims should be struck because they are scandalous, frivolous, vexatious and are an abuse of the Court's process. In arriving at these conclusions, I have taken into account the fact that the plaintiff is a self-represented litigant but have concluded that no amendment of the NOCC could cure its deficiencies. Application to strike claims pursuant to Rule 9-5(1)(a) [17] As I have indicated, I will use the defendants' summary of the claims as set out in the table at paragraph 23 of the notice of application. In doing so, I will refer to the type of claim and the paragraphs in the NOCC as set out in the table. The defendants argue that the following claims can be struck pursuant to this subsection. Breach of contract (Part 1, paragraphs 1 to 12 and 25; and Part 2, paragraphs 5 and 10) [18] The plaintiff alleges that he and Ms. Janisch entered into an agreement to live together as a couple. He says the breach of contract occurred when the defendants placed Ms. Janisch in a care home and removed him from her house. [19] There are two primary reasons why a breach of contract claim has no prospect of success. First, the plaintiff does not allege that he entered into any agreement with the defendants. Second, even if the plaintiff and Ms. Janisch agreed to live together as a couple, no rights accrued to him at common law or under the Family Law Act, S.B.C. 2011, c. 25; a spousal relationship does not arise at law unless persons have lived together in a marriage-like relationship continuously for two years. The allegation here is that the relationship lasted approximately 21 months. [20] I must consider whether it might be possible for the plaintiff to amend the NOCC to allege a claim of inducing breach of contract. It is doubtful that the law recognizes such a claim given the nature of the alleged agreement. However, the plaintiff advances no allegation in the NOCC that the defendants knew of the alleged agreement. Any claim for inducing a breach of contract must allege that the defendants knew of the existence of the contract. Accordingly, there is no prospect the NOCC could be amended to plead a claim for inducing breach of an agreement to live together in a marriage-like relationship in Ms. Janisch's house. The breach of contract claim is struck as disclosing no reasonable claim. Defamation (Part 1, paragraphs 13 to 22 and 30 to 31; and Part 3, paragraphs 6 to 7) [21] The plaintiff alleges the defendants made defamatory statements about him in the Sechelt community. Some of the allegations are incomprehensible, for example the following is pled at Part 3 (mislabelled as Part 2 in the NOCC), paragraph 6: As a result of the wrongful actions by [the defendants] and their premeditated actions against the plaintiff, where the plaintiff understood their action(s) to be defamatory and to mean that [the defendants] did set out to deliberately harm the plaintiff, the plaintiff does not know and has no means of ascertaining the exact terms and initial circumstances of the Defamatory Statements. [22] The clearest allegation is set out at Part 1, paragraph 17: The defendants have for over (2) years made claim throughout Sechelt that the plaintiff is an "Arsonist" and a "Thief" and spoke the same words to the plaintiff in the presence of Provincial Court Judge Merrick. [23] In a defamation action, the plaintiff must prove that the impugned words were defamatory, that they referred to the plaintiff, and that they were published: Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61 at para. 28. A plaintiff must plead the actual words that are alleged to give rise to defamation: Nazerali v. Mitchell, 2016 BCSC 810 at paras. 144-145 (varied on other grounds in 2018 BCCA 104); and Shannon v. King, [1931] 2 W.W.R. 913 (B.C.C.A.). [24] There are significant difficulties with the NOCC. Other than the reference to the terms "arsonist" and "thief", the actual words allegedly used by the defendants have not been set out in the NOCC. The pleadings do not attribute specific words to the defendants. Some of the allegations clearly refer to statements (again without specific words) made by other, unnamed individuals. Further, the plaintiff does not say (other than in the reference to Judge Merrick) to whom the alleged statements were made. Of course, to the extent that a statement was made in a settlement conference in the context of the plaintiff's Provincial Court action, the defence of absolute privilege would apply. [25] The defendants submit that the pleading in its present form could not succeed. I agree. However, I am of the view that it might be possible for the plaintiff to amend the NOCC to raise a claim in defamation. Accordingly, I cannot strike the claim under subrule (1)(a). As I will set out later in these reasons, I am of the view that the action as a whole has been brought for an improper purpose and is an abuse of process. Accordingly, while I cannot strike the claim in defamation under this subsection, I will do so under subrules (1)(b) and (d). Alleged hate crime/ discrimination and racial profiling (Part 1, paragraphs 25 to 28; and Part 2, paragraph 7) [26] The plaintiff alleges the defendants have acted in a racist manner towards him. He is black and says that Ms. Smith committed a "crime of Hate under the Criminal Code of Canada" when she said she would never let her mother "be cared for by someone like" the plaintiff. He further alleges that the "case revolves around discrimination and the attitude of South Africans". He asserts that a message must be sent that the kind of behaviour displayed by Ms. Smith "will not be allowed to go unnoticed here in Canada". [27] These allegations are poorly drafted but they appear to raise an allegation of some form of discrimination or harassment based on race. Any such allegation must be pursued through a human rights proceeding. There is no tort of discrimination: Seneca College of Applied Arts and Technology v. Bhadauria, [1981] 2 S.C.R. 181. These allegations disclose no reasonable claim. Conversion (Part 2, paragraph 12) [28] The NOCC contains a single sentence in which this allegation is made. The plaintiff says the defendants should be held responsible for insurmountable damage to possessions removed to a locker facility. No particulars are provided. There is no prospect the claim could succeed as set out in the NOCC. [29] This claim is similar to the defamation claim; there is a possibility that the plaintiff could amend the NOCC to advance a claim for conversion that could withstand an application to strike. However, as I have set out below, the NOCC should be struck entirely given my conclusion that the claim has been brought for an improper purpose and is an abuse of process. Fraud (Part 1, paragraphs 11, 23 and 25; Part 2, paragraph 10; and Part 3, paragraph 8) [30] The plaintiff alleges the defendants conspired together to use a forged or fraudulent EPOA to remove Ms. Janisch from her home three months before she would have become the plaintiff's legal common law partner. He alleges the defendants "hid behind" the fraudulent EPOA even though the Ministry of Justice has deemed their actions to be fraudulent. [31] Pursuant to Rule 3-7(18), full particulars of an allegation of fraud or fraudulent misrepresentation must be set out in a notice of civil claim. The plaintiff has failed to provide full particulars; the allegations are vague. It is not clear from the NOCC who the plaintiff alleges was the maker or forger of the EPOA or how it was used to "hide behind". It is also unclear how the plaintiff could suffer any loss from the use of the EPOA which has nothing to do with him. The allegation that the defendants acted fraudulently to deny him the opportunity to become Ms. Janisch's legal common law partner cannot form the basis of a claim in damages given the circumstances: Ms. Janisch was removed from the home by medical authorities and the actions taken by Ms. Smith after September 9, 2016 were made pursuant to the committeeship order. [32] The claims alleged disclose no possible cause of action. Intentional infliction of financial/ emotional distress (Part 1, paragraph 25) [33] The NOCC does not make specific factual allegations to support this claim. It appears that the plaintiff included this allegation to describe how he feels about the overall circumstances. [34] To establish a claim for intentional infliction of mental suffering, a plaintiff must plead the following material facts: flagrant or outrageous conduct by the defendants that was calculated to harm the plaintiff; and which caused the plaintiff to suffer a visible and provable medical illness: Frame v. Smith, [1987] 2 S.C.R. 99 at 127-128. [35] The tort has received limited consideration in Canada but is in the nature of a personal injury claim: Edgar v. The British Columbia Institute of Technology, 2015 BCSC 710 at para. 38. The defendants' conduct must be shown to be intentional; reckless indifference is insufficient to establish liability: Piresferreira v. Ayotte, 2010 ONCA 384 at para. 75. [36] The NOCC does not contain any material facts to support a claim for negligent infliction of mental distress. The plaintiff does not allege any "flagrant or outrageous" conduct. He does not allege or even suggest that he is suffering from any medical illness, let alone one caused by actions of the defendants. It is plain and obvious that the NOCC discloses no reasonable claim for negligent infliction of mental suffering. Implementation of surveillance or computer monitoring (Part 1, paragraph 29; and Part 2, paragraph 13) [37] These allegations are difficult to understand. The plaintiff seeks an order that "the defendants be held libel (sic) for the overwhelming amount of (COMPUTER SURVEILLANCE CODE) which was designed to keep the plaintiff from making this case and also the (OBVIOUS) (AND WITNESSED) attempt to place images into the computer as well as extract images from the computer to a nearby mobile unit." He also alleges that the claim includes "what appears to be an attempt to cause a fire". [38] In order to establish a claim for intrusion upon seclusion, a plaintiff must establish intentional or reckless conduct by the defendants by which they invaded without lawful justification the plaintiff's private affairs or concerns; and that a reasonable person would regard the invasion of privacy as highly offensive, causing distress, humiliation or anguish: Lewis N. Klar et al., Remedies in Tort (Toronto: Thompson Reuters, 1987) (loose-leaf 2015 supplement), ch. 24 at 34. [39] A number of decisions of our courts have determined there is no common law cause of action for breach of privacy in British Columbia: Mohl v. University of British Columbia, 2009 BCCA 249 at para. 13; Turkson v. TD Direct Investing, A Division of TD Waterhouse Canada Inc., 2016 BCSC 732 at para. 180, aff'd 2017 BCCA 147; and Tucci v. Peoples Trust Company, 2017 BCSC 1525 at para. 49. Breach of privacy is a statutory tort governed by the Privacy Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 373: Ari v. Insurance Corporation of British Columbia, 2015 BCCA 468 at para. 8. The relevant section provides: 1 (1) It is a tort, actionable without proof of damage, for a person, wilfully and without a claim of right, to violate the privacy of another. (2) The nature and degree of privacy to which a person is entitled in a situation or in relation to a matter is that which is reasonable in the circumstances, giving due regard to the lawful interests of others. (3) In determining whether the act or conduct of a person is a violation of another's privacy, regard must be given to the nature, incidence and occasion of the act or conduct and to any domestic or other relationship between the parties. (4) Without limiting subsections (1) to (3), privacy may be violated by eavesdropping or surveillance, whether or not accomplished by trespass. [40] The plaintiff does not plead the Privacy Act as a legal basis for the alleged breach of privacy. While this could be remedied by amendment, the NOCC contains no allegation of fact supporting the suggestion that the defendants started a fire or conducted surveillance. Rather, he says that they should be responsible ("libel") for such acts. Therefore, it is plain and obvious that this pleading discloses no reasonable claim. It is also frivolous and vexatious. Vacating committeeship order (Part 2, paragraph 3) [41] The plaintiff seeks an order that "the Committee be vacated and that [Ms. Janisch's] case be placed in the hands of the Public Guardian and Trustee". [42] The plaintiff did not appeal the order appointing Ms. Smith as committee. This claim is a collateral attack on that order and should be struck on that basis alone. [43] The NOCC does not invoke s. 6(2) of the Patients Property Act, which provides that the court may rescind the appointment of a committee on the application of the Attorney General, the Public Guardian and Trustee or any other person. The section does not establish criteria for removal of a committee but s. 18 provides that a committee must exercise her powers for the benefit of the patient and the patient's estate. Accordingly, the circumstances in which the appointment of a committee might be rescinded include where the committee is no longer able to act or is not exercising her powers in accordance with the Act. [44] The NOCC does not allege any material facts to support a potential claim that Ms. Smith has failed as committee to exercise her powers for the benefit of Ms. Janisch or her estate. The allegations advanced are the same ones the plaintiff relied on at the hearing before Madam Justice Griffin. In other words, the allegations predate the appointment and are an attempt to re-argue that appointment. [45] The NOCC discloses no reasonable claim for vacating the committeeship order. Application to strike claims pursuant to Rule 9-5(1)(b) and (d) [46] As set out in Krist, it is necessary to examine the factual context when considering whether to strike a pleading pursuant to either of these subsections. That context is succinctly set out in the affidavit of Ms. Smith and in the Griffin Reasons. The plaintiff filed a brief affidavit that attached three exhibits: lengthy documents he wrote describing his version of events and attaching photographs. He swears that "all information and documents included in this affidavit are true and to the best of my ability." The information in the affidavit is not properly admissible. The very lengthy documents are full of hearsay and are, in the main, argument or opinion. I have nevertheless read most of the attached material. However, for the purpose of considering the application to strike the NOCC under these subsections, the plaintiff's material adds nothing of value to the contextual information in the Smith affidavit. [47] In considering the defendants' application under these subsections, I have also considered the plaintiff's oral submissions. He made the following statements that illuminate his motive or purpose in bringing this action: · I am not trying to sue anyone or anything. I just want to get the Supreme Court to look at this so [Ms. Janisch] won't die. I am not coming here for money. I'm not here looking for anything particularly, really. I'm trying to show that something really nasty and really dirty and underhanded took place here. To the point where the lady of the house was thrown out. · I told Judge Merrick at the Provincial Court settlement conference I only want to sue for one dollar. · I am not trying to sue [Ms. Smith] for anything. And [Ms. Janisch] begged me not to sue her daughter. · I am not looking for anything to do with money. · If you throw me out of here I will sue Mr. Behan (counsel for the defendants). I am not going to stop. · Mr. Behan is the criminal here. · I want to give the message that a woman is being beaten to death for her money. [48] The essence of the plaintiff's claim is his attempt to set aside the committeeship order. He wants to restore the situation that existed before Ms. Janisch was moved into a care home and he was removed from her house. The claim is thus a collateral attack on the order appointing Ms. Smith as committee. As noted by Madam Justice Warren in Huang at para. 36: [36] The rule against collateral attack is properly viewed as a particular application of the broader doctrine of abuse of process: Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E., Local 79, 2003 SCC 63 at para. 22. The collateral attack rule has been described as follows: "a judicial order pronounced by a court of competent jurisdiction should not be brought into question in subsequent proceedings except those provided by law for the express purpose of attacking it" [49] The collateral attack on the committeeship order is clearly an abuse of process. Here, the abuse of process can be said to taint the other causes of action advanced by the plaintiff. To the extent that the plaintiff raises other issues, he is doing so to oppress the defendants by causing them anxiety, trouble and expense. He accused Mr. Behan of committing crimes when all he has done is fulfil his role as counsel for the defendants. He threatened to commence an action against Mr. Behan if his NOCC is struck. He advised that he would continue to pursue his attempt to set aside the committeeship order and attack the defendants, notwithstanding any order this Court might make. [50] In these circumstances, the NOCC should be struck in its entirety. This includes the two claims (defamation and conversion) that might in some other context be capable of amendment. In the circumstances here, it is clear that these allegations have no merit but are advanced in furtherance of the plaintiff's attempt to challenge the committeeship order and cause the defendants trouble and expense. The allegations are frivolous and vexatious. [51] In summary, it is plain and obvious that the NOCC discloses no reasonable cause of action and is an abuse of process. Even if the NOCC is read "as generously as possible" or the plaintiff's case is "put at its highest", there is no basis on which it could possibly succeed. Costs [52] The defendants say this action should never have been commenced and ask that costs be awarded as special costs and be fixed in a lump sum. I agree that the action should never have been commenced. The real issue raised by the plaintiff was dealt with in the Griffin Reasons. This action had no prospect of success. The allegations of fraud were made without any basis in fact or law. At the hearing of the application, the plaintiff made outrageous allegations of criminal activity on the part of Mr. Behan. The plaintiff's conduct in commencing and pursuing the action was reprehensible and is deserving of rebuke. [53] I am inclined to order special costs, however, it is not possible to do so in a fixed amount. There is no provision in the Rules that permits the Court to award special costs on a rough and ready lump sum basis: Gichuru v. Smith, 2014 BCCA 414. I understand that the plaintiff is without income or assets of substance. It is likely that the expense of assessing special costs is not justified. [54] In these circumstances, the order I make is that the defendants are entitled to special costs. However, if they wish to have costs assessed summarily, they may make written submissions to the Court in that regard. If the defendants seek to have costs assessed summarily, the costs will not be awarded on a special costs basis. The written submissions of the defendants must be provided to the Court and the plaintiff within 14 days. The plaintiff will have 14 days to provide written submissions in response. "Butler J."
