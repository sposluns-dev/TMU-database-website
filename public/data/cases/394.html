The claimant is a forty-seven year old citizen of Peru and Israel who claims refugee protection as a Convention refugee for reasons of religion and membership in a particular social group, namely, the family. She also claims refugee protection for a risk to life and a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment and danger of torture. The evidence included the claimant's Personal Information Form (PIF), her examination under oath, and the exhibits provided by both claimant's counsel and the Refugee Protection Officer (RPO). ALLEGATIONS The claimant's allegations are as follows: She was born and raised in Lima, Peru. She comes from a Jewish family. Her mother's family was Orthodox. Her family and her three siblings still reside in Lima. She has completed over twenty years of education and has principally worked in administrative jobs but more recently as a caregiver. Her father was abusive. When she was twenty years of age, she met a local Pentecostal pastor. She began to learn about the Christian faith. As her interest in Christianity increased, her father became more aggressive towards her. The claimant visited Israel for the first time in 1994; she liked the country. In 1995, she was able to obtain her immigration papers and ultimately her Israeli citizenship. Soon after her arrival in Israel, the claimant became immersed in the various Christian communities in Jerusalem. By the end of 1996, she had fully converted to Christianity. This put her into difficulty with individuals within the Israeli society and more particularly the Orthodox community. The claimant faced regular incidents of harassment and discrimination both in the workplace as well as when looking for lodging. She returned on regular occasions to Peru to visit her family. On one occasion when the claimant returned home, her mother found dead animal blood at the door of her house. In XXXXX, 2003 the claimant's home in Lima was burglarized and her passports were stolen. The claimant believed that this was the work of SOGNUT, the international Jewish agency that is responsible for assisting Jewish citizens of foreign countries to emigrate to Israel. The claimant did not want to return to Israel. She felt she could no longer remain in Peru as she was afraid of both her family members as well as the SOGNUT organisation. As the authorities and police in Peru are corrupt, she could not get the protection of the police. The claimant came to Canada on February 13, 2004. Soon thereafter, she attempted to remain in Canada as a part of a live-in caregiver programme. When she was refused a visa to enter the United States (Buffalo) in order to complete her immigration application, the claimant made this present refugee application on April 18, 2005. DETERMINATION The panel determines that, since the claimant has not provided credible or trustworthy evidence, she is neither a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection" by reason of a risk to life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or danger of torture. ANALYSIS The panel is satisfied, based on the passports seized at the time of making the present refugee application, that the claimant is a citizen of both Peru and Israel.1 In the present context, while the panel recognized dual citizenship with the present claimant, the panel believes that the evaluation of the claimant's well-founded fear of return began with her citizenship in Peru. The claimant was born and spent the majority of her life in that country. In Peru, the claimant fears two specific groups - the SOGNUT organisation and its members - who she alleges harassed her and committed acts of violence against her when she returned to Peru from Israel. Secondly, the claimant fears her family members and the members of the Jewish community in Lima. In particular, she fears her father, who has never accepted her conversion. Overall, the panel does not believe the claimant has provided credible, plausible or even consistent evidence with respect to her fear of harm in Peru. With respect to the SOGNUT organisation, although based in Lima, it has offices throughout Peru. By the claimant's own admission, its mandate is to assist members of the Jewish community internationally to emigrate to Israel. The claimant alleges that subsequent to her conversion to Christianity while in Israel (1996) and on her return visits to Peru, members of this organisation would have somehow been responsible for involvement in a break-in at her home and leaving dead animal blood outside her door. When the claimant was asked why this organisation would commit these acts against her the claimant had no explanation. The claimant does not produce any objective evidence that this organisation, known worldwide as assisting members of the Jewish community to emigrate to Israel, would be involved in acts of intimidation and harassment of this nature. The claimant has contradictory behaviour with respect to her status as a citizen of Israel. Despite returning to Peru and alleging fears of discrimination and persecution in Israel, not only did the claimant not renounce her Israeli citizenship that she acquired in the 1990s but she even went to the extent of renewing her passport in Lima prior to her departure to Canada in 2004. When asked why, the claimant had no explanation except to say that she knew that with an Israeli passport she could easily come to Canada. Yet if agents of the Israeli government (SOGNUT) were a principal agent of persecution for her, why would she seek the protection of the Israeli state? The claimant had no explanation. The panel does not believe that the claimant's actions are consistent with her allegations. Coupled with the overall lack of objective evidence to support her allegations, this leads the panel to the conclusion that the claimant is simply not telling the truth. This is not a believable story. Even if the panel were to accept that these agents of a foreign government (Israel) would have committed acts of harassment against her, the issue of state protection arises. The claimant only produces one police report2 dated just prior to her departure to Canada (in XXXXX, 2004) regarding the break-in at her residence. When the claimant was asked whether or not she could garner the protection of the Peruvian authorities, she said that they were all corrupt and would not assist her. Yet she made only one detailed report and left soon after. The panel does not believe that that is credible or plausible. While corruption may be an issue with respect to the police in general, the panel does not believe that the claimant has established with clear and convincing evidence an absence of state protection. With respect to the claimant's second agent of persecution, namely her family and members of the local Jewish community, here again the panel does not believe that the claimant has provided credible evidence. Despite alleging an abusive relationship with her father, which necessitated her departure and immigration to Israel in the mid-1990s, the claimant continued to return to Peru and, more particularly, to her father's residence for visits. Why would this be the case? The claimant alleges that as a female in Peru she would not be able to live elsewhere other than with her family. The panel again does not believe this is credible or plausible. The claimant produced no details of whom, if anyone, she fears in the Jewish community. The claimant is forty-seven years of age, single and well-educated. Moreover, she has lived abroad on her own for many years, and even in an extremely dangerous part of the world. The panel believes that the claimant would be able to reside on her own and thus avoid the members of her family whom she alleges are agents of persecution. The panel believes that her allegation is not credible, plausible, or objectively based. Overall, the panel does not believe that the claimant has provided a credible story. With respect to Israel, the tribunal must believe that the claimant used the law of return of the state to Israel to emigrate there and then convert to Christianity. While the claimant alleges that she faced serious harassment amounting to persecution in Israel, she remained there for a period of over ten years. Again while the panel does note that the state of Israel may provide preference to members of the Jewish faith, the majority of the documentation3 recognizes that full social and political equality regardless of religious affiliation is respected in the state of Israel. Thus, here again, the panel does not believe that the claimant has provided credible or plausible evidence of persecution. Finally there is the issue of the delay in claiming refugee protection. Despite alleging the fear of return to both Peru and Israel which necessitated her departure, the claimant arrived in Canada in February, 2004 as a visitor. She continued to extend her visitor's visa and applied for a live-in caregiver program throughout that period. She only made the refugee application following the refusal for her to attend an interview at the Canadian consulate in the United States. When confronted, the claimant stated that she was not aware of the refugee program. What is clear is that the claimant did make inquiries into permanency in Canada, which could have included a refugee application. Thus the panel does not accept the claimant's explanation and considers that her behaviour is not consistent with someone fearing persecution or serious harm. CONCLUSION The panel determines that the claimant, XXXXX XXXXX, is neither a "Convention refugee" within the meaning of section 964 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (the Act) nor a "person in need of protection", as defined in subsection 97(1)5 of that Act.
