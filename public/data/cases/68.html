Volpentesta Tribunal Counsel for the Claimant(s) Steven Beiles Conseil(s) du / de la / des demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister Conseil du ministre REASONS AND DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX (the "male claimant") and his spouse, XXXX XXXX (the "female claimant") claim to be citizens of Russia and request protection under sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA).1 These claims were joined according to Rule 49(1) of the Refugee Protection Division Rules. ALLEGATIONS [2] The following is a summary of the claimant's allegations as set out in the Personal Information Forms2 (PIF) containing their shared narrative (narrative).3 [3] The claimants suffered various forms of mistreatment because the male claimant is Jewish on his father's side. Although they were precluded from practicing their faith or holding their tradition during the Soviet era, from the time he was in elementary school the male claimant recalls being called names, ostracized and assaulted because he is part Jewish. This continued throughout his education. The family home was vandalized with graffiti a number of times. The male claimant was prevented from studying at university of his choice and had to settle for the local town college. While doing his compulsory military service he was called names and pushed around; he was assigned the less favourable assignments more often than others; he was assaulted. [4] After the fall of the Soviet Union there was an increase in nationalism. As a result he found it difficult to find work and was mistreated at work. The male claimant lost employment opportunities because he was Jewish. The male claimant tried to start a support group for others from mixed Jewish backgrounds and as a result suffered threats and an assault. His grandfather's tombstone was vandalized, their home was vandalized with graffiti and he received threatening calls warning him to leave Russia. [5] After the male claimant was beaten by nationalists in XXXX 2010 and then again in XXXX 2010, the claimants arranged for a visit to Canada. They arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2010 and requested protection a week later. The fear if they return to Russia the will suffer persecution because the male claimant is part Jewish. DETERMINATION [6] I find that the claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. My reasons are as follows. ANALYSIS Identity [7] The claimants' oral testimony and documentary evidence establish that they are citizens of Russia.4 Credibility [8] Some of the claimant's evidence was not reliable. For example the male claimant says he was attacked on XXXX XXXX, 2010 while on his way home. They called him names, pulled his bag and hit him causing him to black-out. The male claimant said he did not recognize the men; they called him "kike" and told him to get out. The male claimant confirmed that nothing else was said. However, in the narrative the claimants state the male claimant was attacked by nationalists who told him this was his last warning. While it is possible that the agents of harm had sent other men to attack the male claimant and thus he did not recognize his attackers, I think it is reasonable to expect he would have mentioned at the hearing that they were nationalists. More importantly, the male claimant left out the purpose of the attack being the issuance of a last warning. The male claimant explained that the men most likely did say that it was the last warning, but he was trying to forget. I reject this explanation. The claimants completed a detailed narrative describing a number of incidents. The male claimant said this was the most serious incident. This was the last incident and precipitated the claimants' departure from Russia. Given these circumstances and the nature of the threat, it is reasonable to expect the claimants to recall this. [9] Further, related to the same XXXX 2010 incident, the male claimant states in the narrative that he suffered a concussion and went to clinic for treatment. The male claimant said at the hearing that he did not get any medical treatment and only knew from experience that he had suffered a concussion. The male claimant explained that he must be confused. I reject this explanation. As noted, the male claimant said this was the most serious incident; it was the most recent incident; it was the incident that caused the claimants to leave Russia. It is reasonable to expect the claimant to recall whether he received medical treatment. [10] Similarly, the claimant said that police were not called regarding the XXXX 2010 incident, but in the narrative the police were called by doctors, but they could not come as they were short staffed. [11] Based on all of the above I find the evidence with respect to the assault of XXXX 2010 is not reliable and this incident did not occur as the claimants would have me believe. Further, this infects the chain of attacks related to this incident. I note that the claimants state in their narrative that this was their last warning. I therefore also doubt there was a serious of related attacks trying to force the claimants to leave Russia. [12] The male claimant said he suffered a severe attack in 1995 while at school. The male claimant was dating a Russian girl and during a dance he was harassed by some of the other boys. The girl was upset and wanted to go home. On the way, the male claimant was circled by three boys who attacked him. He was taken by ambulance to a hospital. Police were called and they took a report. While the claimant provided documentary evidence to support a hospital visit at this time where he suffered a concussion and wound to the head,5 the claimants did not provide a copy of the police report which would substantiate how the injury was caused. The claimants were vague and evasive in explaining their effort to obtain a copy of the police statement. At first they said that no case was open. Then they explained that authorities could not find it. The claimants explained that the male claimant's parents tried to obtain a copy over the phone some time last year and his parents were told nothing was registered. I do not believe this is a reasonable effort to obtain a copy of a document that should be available that could support the allegations. There are any number of ways in which the male claimant could have suffered a head injury while at in school, the police statement would have gone a long way in describing exactly how the male claimant was injured. [13] The claimants were vague with respect to the alleged threatening phone calls as well. The male claimant said he had made some effort to start a group for Jews of mixed marriages and placed an ad on the radio. The male claimant also tried to register the group. Somehow, the registration was always refused. The claimants allege that they started receiving threatening phone calls after the city administrator went on a television program and said that certain groups who engaged in Zionist propaganda mentioning the male claimant by name. [14] The claimants had no documentary evidence to support their allegation that they attempted to register a group. The claimants explained that they simply did not keep it. I reject this because the male claimant states he developed a deep interest in his Jewish background and did a lot of research into his grandfather. This prompted him to go further to place an ad on the radio in an effort to attract others with a similar background, and then this group tried to register formally. This all seems like a rather organized effort and planned. It is likely there should be some documentary evidence that would indicate they attempted to start a group. [15] Furthermore, when explaining the radio advertisement the male claimant was evasive and unclear. The male claimant could not describe the content of the ad, not even whether the ad mentioned Jew or Jewish. It seems to me that if he were advertising to attract people like him (Jewish or mixed Jewish) there would be no point to the ad at all unless it mentioned this. The fact that the male claimant could not recall the ad or whether it even mentioned the focus of the group is incredible. I draw an adverse inference and find that the evidence with respect to the male claimant trying to establish a group is not reliable. [16] The claimants could not estimate the number of calls they received after the city administrator went on TV in 2009. However, they said it would be once or twice per day and then nothing for a while. The callers were unknown. They could not specify over what period of time they received the calls and could not specify when the calls stopped. After repeated attempts to determine when the calls stopped the claimants said alternately that the interested decreased; that the calls probably stopped. When pressed again to estimate when the calls stopped they finally said approximately three months before leaving. The vague responses and lack of detail lead me to believe that the claimants did not receive threatening phone calls as they would have me believe. If they did, whatever interest there was seems to have stopped much prior to their departure from Russia. [17] The male claimant says he lost his job in XXXX 2008 as a result of his being Jewish. However, in the narrative the male claimant was told by his employer that some influential people in the town did not want him in the company anymore because he should have been more flexible. It seems the male claimant was unwilling to succumb to the extortion efforts of a certain XXXX manager. Although the XXXX manager did hurl racial insults at the male claimant, the essence of the problem was the unwillingness to pay the extortion. [18] The male claimant would like that I conclude he lost his job in XXXX 2010 as a result of his being Jewish. However, his workbook indicates that he left employment of his own will. The male claimant explained that this is what his employer wanted because the order came from above to get rid of him. However, the male claimant never clearly states why the order was given to get rid of him. The male claimant wants that I tie together the vandalism to his grandfather's tombstone and his subsequent report to the chief of police to the loss of his job. This is a leap I cannot logically make. [19] In XXXX 2008 the male claimant said he was waylaid by three people at night. He recognized these people from the XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX. The three people hit him in the face and he was only saved when the people fled as a passerby was walking a dog. The claimants moved as a result of this attack. The claimants said this attack was not reported to police because one of the attackers had a brother who was in the police service. However, this detail is not contained in their narrative. The claimants explained that they did not know why this was not contained in the narrative. I reject this explanation. The narrative instructions ask claimants to describe efforts to obtain protection from the state or why they did not make an effort. The fact that one of the attackers had a relative in the police is significant as it raises the profile of an agent of harm. It is reasonable to expect this information would have been in the narrative. Furthermore, the narrative in fact states that the incident was reported to police. The evidence with respect to this incident is not reliable. The incident did not occur as the claimants would have me believe. [20] Based on all of the above, I find the claimants have fabricated or exaggerated several of the central allegations of serious harm in an effort to advance or embellish a claim for protection in Canada. Section 96 - Convention Refugee Grounds Persecution? [21] Much of the claimants' evidence with respect to serious harm has been found to be unreliable. What remains is list of mistreatment including insults, vandalism to his grandfather's tombstone and graffiti on the apartment door or car (some of which is unreliable because it relates to threatening calls related to starting his group). [22] While the male claimant believes he lost jobs due to his Jewish background, this is not supported by the evidence. Furthermore, the male claimant lists several employers of varying duration. It appears that the male claimant was indeed able to find a job even after losing one. Since the male claimant said the only way anyone would know he was Jewish was by his look and his last name, it is reasonable that any employer would have immediately known about his Jewish background. The female claimant worked in human resources. While she is not Jewish, it appears she did not suffer adverse conditions in employment due to being married to a Jew. In these circumstances, it is difficult to say the claimants suffered employment discrimination because of the male claimant's Jewish background. [23] Similarly, in education, while the male claimant was not allowed to go to the university he wanted because one administrator had certain views, the male claimant was able to complete a college education. Even if the male claimant suffered discrimination and mistreatment in Russia because he is Jewish, this does not appear to have risen to the level of persecution. None of the claimants' fundamental human rights were denied. Even cumulatively, while it must be difficult to endure such insults and discriminatory attitudes, over the course of their lives the claimants were able to enjoy their fundamental human rights and none of the mistreatment experienced was serious. State Protection? [24] Even if I am incorrect and the mistreatment does amount to persecution, and I do not believe that it does in this case, the determinative issue in this section 96 analysis is whether the claimants have rebutted the presumption of state protection. I find that they have not for the following reasons. [25] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens except in situations where the state is in a state of complete breakdown.6 To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect absent an admission by the national's state of its inability to protect that national.7 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration, the test is whether the protection offered is adequate.8 The evidence that state protection is not adequate must be reliable and probative and it must also satisfy me, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.9 The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.10 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not be enough to justify a claim that the victims are unable to avail themselves of protection.11 The fact that state protection is not perfect12 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability to provide protection.13 The burden of proof that rests on the claimant increases with the level of democracy of the state in question. The more democratic a state is, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all course of action open to them to demonstrate state protection was or would not be forthcoming.14 [26] In this case the male claimant experienced mistreatment from his childhood wherein he feels that he was let down by the authorities in Russia when he and his family needed protection. Growing up he felt that Jews were outcasts; he remembers peers calling him derogatory names; being ostracized; humiliation in the classroom; and being assaulted. With the rise of nationalism after the fall of the Soviet Union so increased anti-Semitism; Jews were viewed as intruders. [27] As noted much of the evidence of harm has been found to be unreliable. What remains is that the male claimant is part Jewish and the female claimant is married to a man who is part Jewish. [28] In this case the male claimant states in his narrative that he reported to the chief of police when his grandfather's tombstone was vandalized during neo-Nazi commemoration of Hitler's birthday. The claimants did not appreciate the police chief's attitude when he said they should bring in the perpetrators by hand so he could prosecute the perpetrators. While his attitude may have been disturbing, the reality or practicality the chief was conveying was that there was no way to know who had caused this terrible disgrace and therefore not much the police could do. This might show a lack of professionalism or courtesy, but it is unreasonable to draw a conclusion that authorities would not be willing or able to provide adequate protection. [29] Even if I were to believe the claimants' allegations on one occasion in XXXX 2008 when he could identify the perpetrators he did not report the incident to police because the brother of the perpetrator worked for police. Subjective fear of the consequences is not a good basis for rebutting the presumption of state protection. [30] Then in another attack in XXXX 2010, if I were to believe that account, the male claimant could not identify or describe attackers as it was dark and he blacked-out very quickly. Even if he reported this to police, how could he reasonably expect authorities to provide protection? In any event, the claimants say they did not report this to police. [31] The claimants say they reported the damage to their car. The police told them there was not much they could do. Again, without knowing who may have caused this damage this admission is more a matter of reality or practicality than a discredit to the protection offered by the state. The police told the claimants to contact their insurer for the damage to their vehicle. [32] The claimants own experience with authorities and their ability to address their concerns was disappointing no doubt, but I find it would be unreasonable to conclude from this experience that the state would not be forthcoming with serious efforts at adequate protection to address the threat. [33] While documentary evidence supports that police are sometimes slow to recognize the racial elements of crimes, they do prosecute under ordinary criminal offenses which are easy to prosecute and there is growing evidence of increased law enforcement efforts that has been responsible for reducing racially motivated attacks as is discussed below.15 Documentary evidence indicates that the state is making serious efforts to combat anti-Semitic rhetoric, publications, crimes, and to change society attitudes. While progress is slow in some areas, it is clear the state is committed and there has been progress. [34] The Jewish population of Russia is estimated to be between 250,000 and 1,000,000 with 286 registered Jewish organizations to support them. Jewish leaders reported significant improvements in official attitudes towards Jews, but anti-Semitism remained a problem at the societal level. The SOVA Centre, an NGO that seeks to combat extremism and nationalism, monitored acts of anti-Semitism in 2010. From July to December 2010, the SOVA Center registered eight acts of anti-Semitic vandalism. There has been a reduction in vandalism due to a decrease in the activities of the nationalist groups Russian Way and Resistance, which had been very active in these crimes. According to the NGO Moscow Bureau for Human Rights, law enforcement officials are investigating vandalism in Voronezh, where 20 Jewish gravestones in a local Jewish cemetery were knocked down on July 27, 2010. On October 7, 2010 anti-Semitic inscriptions appeared on a Jewish synagogue in Barnaul and the local police are investigating that.16 This indicates that the state's effort over the years has dramatically improved the circumstances for Jews in Russia. Obviously not perfect, but clearly positive signs of progress. [35] Violent attacks against Jews were infrequent with only a few episodes occurring during 2010. The government has publicly criticized nationalist ideology and expressed support for legal action in response to anti-Semitic acts. While incidents still occurred and sometime authorities did not consider the racial motive, authorities continued to investigate and prosecute. In September 2009 skinheads in Khabarovsk threw Molotov cocktails into a synagogue and into the house of a policeman who had been investigating cases of extremism. Khabarovsk Anti-extremist Department police detained the group, and criminal proceedings were opened against two of the suspects. They faced up to five years' imprisonment for the synagogue attack and up to life imprisonment for the attack on the police officer. On October 28, 2010 a Moscow Court sentenced a 22-year old neo-fascist with links to the Nationalist Socialist Society to life imprisonment for killing 15 persons, some of whom were Jewish.17 This indicates authorities do take action in crimes against Jews. It also indicates that authorities are sacrificing their own lives to stem extremism. [36] Anti-Semitism on television or in other mainstream media was infrequent and was more likely to appear in low-circulation newspapers or in pamphlets. However, according to the Moscow Bureau of Human Rights (MBHR), anti-Semitic material on Russian-language Internet sites increased during the year. However, the state made serious efforts to combat this and there were several instances in which the government successfully prosecuted individuals for anti-Semitic statements or publications. [37] On March 12, 2010 a court in Izhevsk gave a one-year suspended sentence to neo-Nazi Russian National Unity member Andrey Mokrushin for painting swastikas and anti-Semitic threats on the walls of a local Jewish community center, according to the Union of Councils for Jews in the Former Soviet Union. On February 3, 2010 a court sentenced the editor of the anti-Semitic newspaper Orthodox Rus to three years in a prison colony for inciting ethnic and religious hatred for distributing an anti-Semitic film, Russia with a Knife in the Back.18 This indicates that the authorities are taking action to combat the propagation of a culture of racism and anti-Semitism. [38] Sources indicate that there has been a revival of the Jewish religion and culture in Russia and that some Russian Jews who emigrated from Russia to Israel, USA, or other Western countries, are returning to Russia for job opportunities.19 Federal authorities, and in many cases regional and local authorities, facilitated the establishment of new Jewish institutions. The Federation of Jewish Communities reported that federal officials and some regional officials had been cooperative in the community's efforts to seek restitution of former synagogues, although some Jewish groups asserted that the government had returned only a small portion of the total properties confiscated during the Soviet period. A governmental pilot program in religious education that began in April 2010 continued in 19 regions, including the Muslim-majority North Caucasus and the Jewish Autonomous Region in the Far East.20 [39] Despite these efforts to combat extremism including anti-Semitism and the resultant improvements, it cannot be denied that anti-Semitism remains a serious societal problem. Also, while Russian government does not sanction anti-Semitism and there are laws aimed at punishing criminal acts involving nationality, race or religion more seriously, it is clear that authorities do not always investigate the racial motives for crimes properly which perpetuates the perception of impunity.21 However, according to SOVA, counteraction to anti-Semitism in Russia has substantially improved in Russia over the past few years, and promoting anti-Semitic propaganda was hardly prosecuted from 2004 to 2006, but from 2007 to 2008, 22 of 70 hate-speech convictions were for inciting anti-Semitism, although in 9 of the 22 cases, offenders received only probation sentences, and many high-profile ultra-nationalists who promote anti-Semitism were not prosecuted. The results of the efforts are apparent through to 2011 as noted above. (SOVA reports 30 acts of anti-Semitic vandalism in 2007 and 24 acts in 2008,22 and by 2010 there were only 8.) [40] The state is making serious efforts to combat anti-Semitism and extremism and there are positive signs of progress. The claimants may have experienced local failures or lack of professional decorum, but the preponderance of evidence indicates that they could reasonably expect state protection would be forthcoming if he were to require it, particularly in the larger centres such as Moscow and St. Petersburg where the majority of Russia's Jews reside.23 [41] The claimants have not satisfied me with clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, the state would not be forthcoming with adequate protection. Changing societal attitudes is not an easy function, but there is no doubting the states dedication and there is clear evidence of the state taking action against anti-Semitic and extremist criminal activity. [42] Therefore, I find the claimants are not Convention refugee and their claims under section 96 of the IRPA fail. The rest of the analysis of this decision will be under section 97 of the IRPA. Section 97 - Persons in Need of Protection [43] While the male claimant was sometimes mistreated and bullied at school, he was able to continue through school and was not denied an education. The claimants were not denied health care. The claimants were not denied a means of existence or otherwise hindered in any way that would be life threatening. [44] The assessment above with respect to state protection applies here as well. There was no reliable residual information upon which a claim under section 97 could be founded. [45] Therefore, I find the claimants are not persons in need of protection and their claims under section 97 of the IRPA fail. CONCLUSION [46] The claimants do not have a well-founded fear since there is not a serious possibility of persecution, and they would not be subjected personally, on a balance of probabilities, to a risk to life, a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a danger of torture, should they return to Russia. Accordingly, the claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. [47] The claims are rejected. (signed) "B. Volpentesta" B.
