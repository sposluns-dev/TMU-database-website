Motifs et décision Person who is the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX Personne en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, ON Appel instruit / entendu à Date of decision November 30, 2020 Date de la décision Panel H. Shepherd Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Sabine Venturelli Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] I dismiss the appeal. The appellant, XXXX XXXX XXXX is a citizen of Israel. Summary of the Basis of Claim Form [2] The appellant was born in Ukraine, but lost this citizenship when he acquired Israeli citizenship. He fears persecution in Israel by reason of his faith as an Orthodox Christian, his Ukrainian origins, and his refusal to perform compulsory military service in Israel. Refugee Protection Division conclusions [3] The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) concluded that Israel was the country of reference. It rejected the claim for the following reasons: i) The appellant suffered discrimination, not persecution, by reason of his ethnicity and religion. ii) The appellant is a deserter and he failed to demonstrate that military service in Israel constitutes persecution under international law or that the punishments stipulated for desertion amount to persecution. iii) The appellant did not rebut the presumption of state protection. Grounds of appeal (summary of arguments) [4] The appellant argues that the RPD made the following errors: i) The appellant's responses were consistent; there were no contradictions. ii) The RPD erred in its assessment of the facts and did not take into account all the evidence provided. iii) The RPD should have taken into account the documentary evidence regarding the situation in Israel. However, the RPD did not conduct an adequate analysis. Summary of the Refugee Appeal Division decision [5] Although the RPD erred in concluding that the appellant's political convictions were not authentic, it did not err in concluding that the potential treatment of the appellant does not amount to persecution or serious mistreatment within the meaning of sections 96 and 97 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). DETERMINATION [6] I dismiss the appeal. The RPD correctly determined that the appellant is neither a Convention refugee under section 96 of the IRPA, nor a person in need of protection within the meaning of section 97 of the IRPA.1 ANALYSIS [7] My role is to examine all of the evidence and determine whether the RPD's decision is correct.2 [8] The appellant speaks Russian and he is an Orthodox Christian. Discrimination or persecution [9] The RPD did not raise any credibility issues. Although he experienced discrimination, its cumulative effects did not reach the threshold required to be considered persecution. I agree with this conclusion. The Israel consul granted entry to the appellant in 2015. Following his arrival in Israel, he was granted an internship as an XXXX XXXX. He was not subsequently hired, for unknown reasons. The appellant moved to Haifa, where he was able to find work as an XXXX XXXX XXXX. His neighbours insulted him and his Jewish girlfriend's parents were against their relationship. However, the cumulative nature of the treatment the appellant endured does not amount to persecution, namely, the sustained or systemic violation of basic human rights. Conscientious objector [10] The appellant visited the Nafah military base in the Golan Heights during his integration program in XXXX 2015. In XXXX 2016, he returned to the base to undergo basic military training (tironut) for two months. Subsequently, on XXXX XXXX, 2016, he was summoned to report on XXXX XXXX, 2016, for four weeks of reserve duty (milouim). Because of how the Israel army was treating Palestinians, he was not willing to serve. A non-governmental organization, XXXX XXXX, informed him that he was not eligible for an exemption on the grounds of political objections. He travelled to Canada on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2016, where he filed a claim for refugee protection in August 2017. [11] The RPD did not believe that the appellant was bound by any ethical or moral convictions which prevented him from performing his military service. Otherwise, he would have demonstrated this before. The RPD was unaware of a reasonable explanation. He arrived at this conviction after having spoken with a Palestinian in Tel Aviv. If it is true that he saw Palestinian villages with civilians present at Nafah, it is unlikely that they were used to train soldiers to intentionally perpetrate war crimes. The appellant is not credible on this issue. In spite of this finding, the appellant is most likely opposed to military service for the reasons stated. [12] However, according to the RPD, the appellant is not a conscientious objector.3 This term applies to people who oppose military service in all circumstances, not those who selectively object.4 In this case, it is a question of selective conscientious objection. The first type calls for a subjective analysis, whereas the second requires both a subjective and an objective assessment of the facts. [13] According to the RPD, personal convictions do not constitute sufficient grounds to obtain refugee protection, [translation] "unless the military action is condemned by the international community...or the punishment for desertion is so excessive that it constitutes persecution."5 [14] First and foremost, the law governing compulsory military service is a law of general application that does not apply to individuals for reasons prohibited by the Convention. The RPD examined the typical duration of prison sentences, as well as the maximum prison sentence, and concluded that they are not cruel and unusual since they would not shock Canadians. The appellant did not demonstrate that he would face persecution on account of his refusal to perform his military service. [15] According to the RPD, the Israeli authorities have a procedure for exempting conscientious objectors. However, the appellant did not avail himself of this procedure. He filed a letter from a physician dated August 20, 2016, which states that he has XXXX disorder with mixed features.6 He testified that he developed psychological issues after his two months of military service. The RPD was of the opinion that this does not justify failing to enquire about his military service beforehand or to apply for an exemption. He failed to provide clear and convincing evidence that he would not be able to obtain adequate protection from the authorities. The appellant's objections [16] The appellant expresses general objections without specifying how they apply to the particular facts of this appeal. For instance, quite a few submissions deal with credibility, although the RPD did not make any negative credibility findings on this issue. [17] In paragraph 34, the RPD concluded that the appellant was not a conscientious objector because he should have demonstrated this earlier. The appellant is of the opinion that this conclusion is unreasonable since it is absurd to impose a time constraint on this issue.7 His explanation was reasonable. I agree with the appellant on this matter. [18] The appellant maintains that the RPD should have taken into account the fact that he had been traumatized by events that occurred during his military training. The RPD did not err. It rightly noted that no expert evidence had been provided to explain his mental state. [19] The appellant alleges that the RPD erred in concluding that he had not rebutted the presumption that state protection was available. The Response to Information Request (RIR) of March 10, 2010, demonstrates that applications for an exemption on political grounds would not be accepted. He will not be able to obtain an exemption from military service.8 [20] The appellant maintains that the RPD did not conduct an adequate analysis of the evidence. The Refugee Appeal Division's assessment The discriminatory nature of conscription or punishment tainted by a reasonable apprehension of bias on one of the grounds in the Convention [21] The first issue is whether there is a law of general application, and whether it would be applied in a fair and impartial manner with respect to prosecution and punishment.9 [22] The Israel Defense Forces (IDF) operate an internal judicial system that is governed by the Military Justice Law.10 Pursuant to Article 92, desertion from the army is punishable by up to 15 years of imprisonment. Article 94 provides that a soldier who is absent without leave may be sentenced to a maximum of 2 years in prison. Article 46 establishes that a soldier who fails to fulfill a legal obligation may receive a prison sentence of up to 2 years. However, this calls for a court martial. In general, the accused is tried by an officer and sentenced to a certain number of weeks in prison. [23] Unmarried men who arrive in Israel in accordance with the Law of Return, and who are between the ages of 26 and 29, are exempt from regular military service but must perform reserve duty.11 Those aged 30 and over are fully exempt from military service.12 The appellant is obligated to perform his reserve military duty because he arrived in Israel 11 days prior to his 30th birthday. Following the integration and selection of new immigrants, the recruits begin their basic military training (tironut).13 The number of days of annual reserve duty generally varies between two and five weeks, depending on a number of factors. A reserve soldier is entitled to receive a benefit.14 [24] The Minister of Defense can exempt any soldier from military service under Article 36 of the Israeli Security Service Law. According to the website of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs:15 The IDF will respect the views of a conscientious objector, provided that it is satisfied that these views are genuine. To this end, a special military committee, headed by the IDF's Chief Recruitment Officer, or his deputy, hears the application of those who wish to be exempted from the army on the basis of conscientious objection. Among the members of this committee are an officer with psychological training, a member of the IDF's attorney's office and a civilian expert on conscientious objection. [25] This source makes reference to examples of prison sentences of 10, 14 or 20 days for those who oppose a prison sentence in the Palestinian Territories. The evidence filed by the appellant further states that this may recur several times. [26] The appellant filed an RIR on March 10, 2010, on the treatment of conscientious objectors.16 A sociology professor states that the military verifies whether the person's motives are conscientious or political. The RIR states: Furthermore, sources indicate that those who do not wish to serve in the military because they are opposed to the IDF's actions in the Occupied Palestinian Territories are not likely to be granted an exemption on the grounds of conscientious objection. [27] The evidence establishes that Israel is a democratic state that has enacted military service laws that are laws of general application. The appellant was treated in accordance with the law. There is no evidence that he was treated differently on the basis of one of the reasons outlined in the Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees as regards military service. The distinction between principled conscientious objectors and selective objectors is not discriminatory. The Federal Court makes the same distinction.17 The evidence does not establish that those with political objections based on the treatment of Palestinians are treated differently from other deserters. [28] Incarcerating conscientious objectors who refuse to perform their military service does not on its own constitute persecution.18 No persecution exists when the punishment inflicted is not severe. The RPD rightly concluded that the sentences are not excessively severe. This is in accordance with previous RPD decisions that were confirmed by the Federal Court.19 Military service and international law [29] The second issue is whether Israeli reserve duty is fundamentally illegitimate with respect to international law. Pursuant to paragraph 171 of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees Handbook:20 Not every conviction, genuine though it may be, will constitute a sufficient reason for claiming refugee status after desertion or draft-evasion. It is not enough for a person to be in disagreement with his government regarding the political justification for a particular military action. Where, however, the type of military action, with which an individual does not wish to be associated, is condemned by the international community as contrary to basic rules of human conduct, punishment for desertion or draft-evasion could, in the light of all other requirements of the definition, in itself be regarded as persecution. [30] The appellant filed documents establishing Israel's failure to comply with a number of resolutions by the United Nations General Assembly and Security Council regarding the Occupied Palestinian Territories.21 Evidence was also filed alleging that Israel committed war crimes during two Gaza invasions in 2008 and 2014. [31] According to a 2018 Amnesty International report:22 Israeli soldiers and police and Israel Security Agency officers subjected Palestinian detainees, including children, to torture and other ill-treatment with impunity, particularly during arrest and interrogation.... Israeli soldiers, police and security guards killed at least 75 Palestinians from the OPT, including East Jerusalem, and five Palestinians with Israeli citizenship. [32] Isolated violations do not constitute a violation condemned by the international community.23 Moreover, the issue of complicity is relevant. The Court noted the following in Hinzman in paragraph 129: In Radosevic v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), [1995] FCJ No. 74, the Court dismissed an application for judicial review on the basis that, on the evidence, it was unlikely that the applicant would personally have been called upon to commit atrocities. [emphasis added] [33] Pursuant to the notice to appear, the appellant was required to report to a military base in Negev (Hatzerim) for service in Gaza for a period of four weeks. The documentary evidence refers to two IDF invasions of Gaza in 2008 and 2014. Although some soldiers posted near the fence could have fired shots at people within Gaza during the protests in breach of international law, the appellant would probably not have been obligated to act in this manner. Isolated crimes perpetrated by some soldiers are insufficient to justify the appellant's refusal to serve. It is unlikely that the appellant would be obligated to commit an act that would be condemned by the international community. State protection [34] The RPD did not make any errors in concluding that the appellant did not rebut the presumption that adequate state protection had been available to him. According to the evidence, the appellant would likely not have obtained an exemption based solely on the ground that he objected to service in the Palestinian Territories. However, the appellant's situation encompassed other factors. He provided medical evidence showing that he has XXXX disorder with mixed features.24 He developed the disorder after his military training and prior to his reserve service. He testified that he acquired the disorder in the wake of his military training. One of the committee members responsible for assessing his exemption application would be a psychologist. The appellant had non-political reasons for applying for an exemption from annual reserve service. The appellant was required to exhaust all domestic courses of action before seeking international protection, unless it would have been futile or jeopardized his life.25 The appellant did not do so. Given that this is not a determinative issue, the claim would have failed for the other reasons. [35] Although the RPD erred in concluding that the appellant's political convictions were not authentic, my independent assessment leads me to the conclusion that the appellant is not a person in need of protection, pursuant to sections 96 and 97 of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [36] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, I dismiss the appeal and confirm the determination of the RPD, according to which the appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection.
