MacLean appearing for the (Crown) Respondent [1] MCEACHERN, C.J.B.C.: Mr. Klein has said everything that can properly be said in support of the appeal, but he has not persuaded us that it will be necessary to hear from the Crown. Mr. Justice Donald will give the first judgment. [2] DONALD, J.A.: Lorna Gean Johnson appeals from her conviction for impaired driving causing bodily harm recorded after a trial before a Judge of the Provincial Court at 100 Mile House. [3] She was charged following a head on collision which occurred when she drove her car across the centre line of Highway 97 near 100 Mile House and struck an oncoming vehicle. The accident caused serious injury to the other motorist. [4] Ms. Johnson failed a roadside breath test and later gave two samples of her breath pursuant to a breathalyzer demand, each of which produced readings of 140 milligrams of alcohol per 100 millilitres of blood or .14. The readings were taken slightly taken more than two hours after the accident. An expert for the Crown worked the readings back to the time of the accident and arrived at an opinion that Ms. Johnson had a blood alcohol concentration of between .16 -.18 at that time. The expert also said that everyone with a reading over .1 is impaired in the ability to drive a motor vehicle. [5] The trial judge accepted the blood alcohol evidence and considered it along with the circumstances of the accident which in his words, "showed impaired judgment by the accused" in order to reach his decision that Ms. Johnson was impaired. [6] The decision is attacked in several ways on this appeal but the principal argument runs along the following lines. The trial judge said that if the breathalyzer readings were inadmissible he would have found a reasonable doubt. The readings would be inadmissible if the tests were not taken pursuant to a proper demand. In order for the demand to be proper the officer making the demand must have had reasonable and probable grounds to believe that the accused had committed an offence under s. 253 of the Code within the preceding two hours. Since, it is argued, the indicia of impairment at the accident scene were so slight (mild odour of alcohol on Ms. Johnson's breath, slightly slurred speech and swaying) the reasonableness of the officer's belief depended on the roadside test. The ultimate contention is that the Crown did not prove the validity of the test beyond a reasonable doubt. [7] In support of this argument, counsel for the appellant submits that it was not shown that the officer used an "approved screening device" as defined in s. 254(1) of the Code or that the instrument was properly calibrated before the test was administered. The officer testified that he used what he described as a "SL2". The Regulations containing the Approved Screening Device Order refer in s. 2(a) to "Alcolmeter S-L2". Further, the officer testified that the device was calibrated to .1 milligrams. The relevant part of the transcript of this testimony reads as follows: Q And did you have any knowledge at that time as to what the roadside device was calibrated to read a "fail" at? A Yeah. It's calibrated at .1 milligrams. So it's above the .08 a couple of " [Another question is posed.] [8] At .1 milligrams of alcohol per 100 millilitres of blood even a trace of alcohol would produce a fail result. The so- called legal limit is 80 mg./ It is argued that no reasonable belief can rest on the result produced by a wrongly calibrated and unapproved instrument. [9] As Sopinka J. said in the leading case of R. v. Bernshaw (1995), 95 C.C.C. (3d) 193, whether an officer has reasonable and probable grounds on which to base a demand is a question of fact. At para. 46 [214] he said: ... The decision as to whether a peace officer believes on reasonable and probable grounds that an offence is being committed and, therefore, that a demand is authorized under s. 254(3) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46, must be based on the circumstances of the case. It is, therefore, essentially a question of fact and not one of pure law. [10] In Bernshaw, the view taken by Sopinka J. for a plurality (La Forest, McLachlin and Major JJ.), concurring in the result proposed by Cory J. (with whom Lamer C.J. and Iacobucci J. agreed) but for different reasons, was that where the result of the roadside screening device test forms the sole basis of the office's belief and the officer is aware that the test may be faulty, it cannot be said that his belief was reasonable: ... However, a "fail" result per se is insufficient to furnish reasonable and probable grounds where circumstances exist as discussed above, such that the police knew that the test would yield faulty results. [11] The problem with the roadside test discussed in Bernshaw was that it was administered within 15 minutes of stopping the accused. The manufacturers of the test device recommended waiting 20 minutes to allow any residue of alcohol in the subject's mouth from a recent drink to pass, otherwise the residue could skew the result. There was no evidence in that case that the accused had consumed alcohol within 15 to 20 minutes of the test. Sopinka J. held that the mere possibility of contamination was not sufficient to vitiate the officer's honest belief in making the breathalyzer demand. [12] The trial judge in the present case focused on the officer's belief. There are two crucial findings in this regard. The first relates to the nature of the screening device and the second to the calibration of the device. [13] As to the first, the trial judge held that the officer believed that he used an approved screening device within the meaning of the Code: ... In the case at bar, Cst. Cox testified that he had produced the roadside screening device from the back of the police car. He testified of having training with the use of the device. He did not state whether the device he used was an "approved" screening device, and he was never asked whether it was an "approved" device. He did not state the model of the device with precision. He referred to it as an "SL2" as opposed to an "Alcolmeter SL2". I find beyond a reasonable doubt that Cst. Cox believed the device he was using was an approved screening device, thus he made the roadside screening demand under section 254(2) which refers to an "approved" screening device. The test is not whether an approved instrument was used, but whether Cst. Cox held a subjective belief that it was an "approved" device, and whether that belief was objectively reasonable. If those criteria are met, and if a "fail" reading results, then the results of that reading can form part of the reasonable and probable grounds for making the subsequent breathalyzer demand. The formal training of Cst. Cox with the roadside screening device, the fact that it was produced from the police vehicle and the fact that the roadside screening demand read to the accused referred to "an approved screening device", in the absence of any evidence to the contrary other than referring to the device as an "SL2" as opposed to an "Alcolmeter SL2", all leads me to have no doubt that Cst. Cox reasonably believed he was using an approved screening device. [Emphasis added] [14] Although I agree with the result, I disagree with two aspects of this reasoning: (1) the standard of proof applied to the analysis; and, (2) the implication that the officer's belief is the only thing that matters. The question of belief was just one factual item in the case, it was not an essential element of the charge and as such it ought not to have been determined on the standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt. A piecemeal approach like this is contrary to R. v. Morin, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 345. The second aspect with which I disagree is the test enunciated in the passage underlined above. If it had been shown that the device was not approved and that the officer relied solely on the test for his belief supporting the breathalyzer demand, then in light of Bernshaw I do not know how the demand could be valid regardless of the reasonableness of the officer's belief that the device was approved. But in any event that was not the evidence in this case. [15] The officer testified that he used a "SL2" roadside testing device. If it was necessary for the Crown to prove that he used an approved device, and I assume without deciding that it was, the officer's reference to SL2 sufficiently identified the instrument as one listed in the approving order as "Alcolmeter SL2". Counsel for the appellant argues for a strict rule of proof that the instrument was approved and properly calibrated, but that argument depends on an application of the reasonable doubt standard to each item of the evidence which I have rejected. [16] Counsel for the appellant referred the case of R. v. Nyman (1998) 131 C.C.C. (3d) 125 (Ont. C.A.), where the officer making the demand referred to grams rather than milligrams in describing the results of a breath test. In respect of that evidence, Goudge J.A. for the court said this: ... The language used by the officer to describe the results of the first test -- "1.45 grams percent in 100 millilitres of blood" -- is nonsensical. While it may be that the officer just made a mistake, and meant to say simply ".145 grams in 100 millilitres of blood", the Crown did not choose to view his evidence in this way and recall him to correct his evidence. Rather, it argues that the court should accomplish this task by using judicial notice or simply by inferring the correct meaning. I disagree. Judicial notice cannot be used to correct this evidence. It is not a notorious fact that this officer, when he uses the phrase ".145 grams percent in 100 millilitres of "blood" means ".145 grams in 100 millilitres of blood". Nor can the court simply infer with the required confidence that what the witness meant was not what he said but something different. There is simply no basis for doing so. [17] The present case is to be distinguished from Nyman because here the officer went on to make reference to .08. This then called for an interpretation by the trial judge and gave rise to an inference not possible in the circumstances of Nyman. [18] The second finding went to the reliability of the test and here the problem was the officer's evidence that the device was calibrated to .1 milligrams. Once again, the trial judge applied the reasonable doubt standard and focused solely on the officer's belief. He said: ... Section 254(3) of the Code requires that the police officer subjectively have an honest belief that the suspect has committed the offence and objectively there must exist reasonable grounds for this belief. I accept beyond a reasonable doubt in the case at bar that Cst. Cox subjectively believed the roadside screening device was working properly. There was no evidence given by Cst. Cox to suggest he thought the device was not working properly. Even from an objective point of view, his belief was reasonable. I agree that there was no evidence the screening device was calibrated accurately, but I reject that there is an onus on the Crown to prove the device was reliable when there is no evidence to the contrary. [Emphasis added] [19] The foregoing manifests a piecemeal approach to the evidence and unduly concentrates on the officer's belief about the test when what really matters is the belief regarding the commission of an offence against s. 253. [20] Any suggestion that the test result may have been faulty because of improper calibration was laid to rest by the trial judge's interpretation of the officer's evidence, namely, that when he said .1 milligrams he meant 100 milligrams: Counsel for the accused submits there was no evidence the device was calibrated accurately. Cst. Cox incorrectly referred to it being calibrated at ".1 milligrams" for a "fail" reading, as opposed to "100 milligrams". This evidence of Cst. Cox must be viewed with the other comment made by Cst. Cox about the calibration being higher than the legal limit for driving. Clearly Cst. Cox meant "100 milligrams" and not ".1 milligrams" when he referred to the calibration of the roadside screening device. There was nothing in the evidence of Cst. Cox that would suggest that he thought the device was not working properly. [Emphasis added] [21] Counsel for the appellant argues that the trial judge added to the evidence to reach this conclusion but I cannot agree. As I have said, it is part of a trial judge's task to interpret evidence. There was a reasonable basis for this interpretation and in my opinion it cannot be disturbed. [22] In summary then, I find no merit in the argument that the readings were inadmissible because of a demand based on a faulty roadside test. There was evidence that the officer used an approved device and there is no evidence that the fail result was unreliable. [23] Counsel for the appellant also argues that the indicia of impairment presented by Ms. Johnson were too slight to support a belief in reasonable and probable grounds for making a demand. This assumes that the fail result should be ignored, leaving only the accident, a mild odour of alcohol, slightly slurred speech, swaying and an admission of drinking as the grounds for the officer's belief. [24] Counsel then went through each item on the list in an endeavour to show that there was no reasonable basis for the belief. [25] First of all, the fail result of the roadside test cannot be ignored for the reasons I have already given. Secondly, the circumstances have to be considered as a whole and not in isolation as counsel has treated them. Thirdly, this argument is nothing more than a re-hashing of the evidence which is not our function. [26] Finally, counsel for the appellant alleges that the verdict was unreasonable in that the defence provided cogent and credible evidence contradicting the expert evidence that Ms. Johnson had a blood alcohol concentration of 160-180 milligrams at the time of driving. [27] The gist of the defence evidence provided by Ms. Johnson and the friend with whom she spent the evening putting up preserves was that she did not consume enough wine to produce such a high reading, and that having consumed only four glasses of wine throughout the evening, her blood alcohol level would have been under the legal limit of .08. This is to be considered with the mild symptoms exhibited at the accident scene. The trial judge gave this evidence careful consideration and rejected it for the reasons he gave in his decision. In my opinion, no valid ground has been given for interfering with the trial judge's assessment of the evidence. [28] Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal. [29] MCEACHERN, C.J.B.C. I agree. [30] ESSON, J.A. I agree. [31] MCEACHERN, C.J.B.C. Thank you counsel. The appeal is dismissed. "The Honourable Mr. Justice Donald"
