The RPD found that the appellant is neither a Convention1 refugee nor a person in need of protection pursuant to sections 96 and 97 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (the "Act" or "IRPA").2 DETERMINATION OF THE APPEAL [2] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the Act, the Refugee Appeal Division (the "RAD") confirms the determination of the RPD, namely, that the appellant is neither a Convention refugee pursuant to section 96 of the Act nor a person in need of protection pursuant to section 97 of the Act. [6] The appellant is not requesting that a hearing be held under subsection 110(6) of the IRPA if any documentary evidence is submitted. BASIS OF CLAIM [7] According to the allegations set out in the appellant's Basis of Claim form (BOC),4 the appellant was born in Ethiopia and moved to Israel in 2007 pursuant to the Law of Return. His mother is Jewish and the appellant was a Christian in Ethiopia. The appellant converted to Judaism in Israel and acquired Israeli citizenship. The appellant later converted to Christianity. [8] The appellant submits that he encountered problems due to his Ethiopian ethnicity. In 2012, the appellant was approached by some men who took him to a dark place and made him take his clothes off. They laughed at him and touched his genitals. The appellant reported the incident to an organization called Machash but they did not respond. [9] In 2013 the appellant was stopped by police in Haifa and beaten and interrogated. They wanted to know if he had participated at an Ethiopian protest in Haifa. The appellant reported this incident to Machash but they did not respond to the complaint. [10] The appellant also received threats from unknown individuals who warned him that if he went to the police again they would rape him. The appellant travelled to Canada on a Canadian Visitor Visa on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2016 and made a claim for refugee protection on August 11, 2016. [11] The RPD member found the appellant to be neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection on the basis that he has state protection in Israel. GROUNDS OF APPEAL [12] The appellant has not clearly set out the grounds of appeal in his memorandum.5 According to RAD Rule 3(3)(g) of the Refugee Appeal Division Rules (the "RAD Rules"),6 the AR must contain a memorandum that includes full and detailed submissions regarding: (i) the errors that are the grounds of appeal; and, (ii) where the errors are located in the written reasons for the Refugee Protection Division's decision that the appellant is appealing or in the transcript or in any audio or other electronic recording of the Refugee Protection Division hearing. [13] I have tried to summarize and identify the grounds that the appellant has argued in his memorandum.7 After a careful review of the memorandum, I identify the following grounds the appellant has focused on in this RAD appeal: * Did the RPD member fail to analyze or address the issue of whether the discrimination experienced by the appellant amounts to persecution? * Did the RPD member err in her state protection analysis in finding that the police protection is forthcoming for the appellant in Israel? CONSIDERATION OF NEW EVIDENCE [14] Subsection 110(4) of the Act provides that the person who is the subject of the appeal may present only evidence that arose after the rejection of their claim or that was not reasonably available, or that they could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented, at the time of the rejection. [15] The appellant is required to provide full and detailed submissions as part of his memorandum and provide explanations for how the proposed new evidence meets the requirements of subsection 110(4), and how it relates to his claim.8 [16] Once the "new evidence" has been accepted by the RAD, the RAD is also guided by the recent Federal Court of Appeal (the "FCA") case, Singh.9 In Singh, the FCA has said: [64] In conclusion, I am of the view that there is no valid reason not to apply, for the most part, the implicit criteria established by this Court in Raza to subsection 110(4) of the IRPA. The wording of that provision is almost identical to the wording of paragraph 113(a), and the context in which it was adopted as well as the underlying judicial policy considerations support an identical approach despite the fact that they apply to separate proceedings and different decision-makers. In any case, the issue seems rather academic to me, to the extent that the implicit criteria from Raza do not truly add to the wording of subsection 110(4) but are necessarily implied. Except for the materiality of evidence, which does not lend itself to the same analysis in an appeal and which subsection 110(6) already considers in determining whether a new hearing should be held, it is not necessary to interpret subsection 110(4) and paragraph 113(a) differently. It goes without saying that the RAD always has the freedom to apply the conditions of subsection 110(4) with more or less flexibility depending on the circumstances of the case. [17] In Singh,10 the FCA also said: [54] . . . The role of the RAD is not to provide the opportunity to complete a deficient record submitted before the RPD, but to allow for errors of fact, errors in law or mixed errors of fact and law to be corrected. [18] The RAD will assess the evidence on "newness" as outlined in subsection 110(4). If it passes this test, the RAD will go on to assess the "new evidence" on credibility and relevance before admittance of such evidence for the RAD appeal. REMEDY SOUGHT [24] The appellant has requested that the RAD, under paragraph 111(1)(b) of the Act, set aside the determination of the RPD and substitute a positive determination. ROLE OF THE RAD [25] Although the Act sets out grounds for appeal as well as possible remedies, it does not specify the standard of review to be applied by the RAD. [26] In the Federal Court of Appeal (FCA) decision for Huruglica13 Justice Gauthier states: [103] I conclude from my statutory analysis that with respect to findings of fact (and mixed fact and law) such as the one involved here, which raised no issue of credibility of oral evidence, the RAD is to review RPD decisions applying the correctness standard. Thus, after carefully considering the RPD decision, the RAD carries out its own analysis of the record to determine whether, as submitted by the appellant, the RPD erred. Having done this, the RAD is to provide a final determination, either by confirming the RPD decision or setting it aside and substituting its own determination of the merits of the refugee claim. It is only when the RAD is of the opinion that it cannot provide such a final determination without hearing the oral evidence presented to the RPD that the matter can be referred back to the RPD for redetermination. No other interpretation of the relevant statutory provisions is reasonable. [27] In Youkap,14 the Federal Court confirmed that the RAD may show a certain level of deference towards the RPD's finding when credibility issues are involved, and that the RAD must conduct an independent assessment of the evidence as a whole. Furthermore, the RAD has a duty of deference regarding the credibility findings of the RPD as the RPD has had the opportunity to hear the witness.15 This has also been stated by the FCA decision in Huruglica16 where Justice Gauthier states: [11] [. . .] These respective roles suggest deference is owed to findings of fact, or findings of mixed fact and law, that can be traced back to evidence given at the RPD hearing. Where the RAD has new evidence before it, either through documents or from an oral hearing, less deference may be owed, as the RPD will not have considered this evidence. [. . .] [14] Turning to the expertise and advantageous position of the RPD versus that of the RAD, the RAD underlined that the RPD always has the advantage of seeing and questioning refugee claimants, while the RAD will unfrequently have this opportunity. This "suggests that the RAD show deference to the RPD on findings of fact and particularly in respect to credibility, other than in situations where the RAD holds an oral hearing and therefore has opportunity to consider evidence first hand" . . . .17 [28] In this appeal, and taking into consideration the recent FCA decision in Huruglica,18 with respect to findings of fact (and mixed fact and law), which raise no issue of credibility of oral evidence, the RAD is to review RPD decisions applying the correctness standard. Otherwise, where an issue concerning credibility of oral evidence is evident, I will be applying the points set out in Huruglica19 which is to conduct an independent assessment of all the evidence to determine whether the appellant is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection, while giving certain deference to the RPD's findings regarding the appellant's credibility and any other issues where the RPD enjoys a particular advantage. As well, it is clear from the many recent Court cases that have been previously mentioned, all agree that the standard of correctness should be used for any error of law found in the RPD decision on appeal to the RAD. ANALYSIS OF THE MERITS OF THE APPEAL Discrimination vs persecution [29] The appellant submits that the RPD member failed to analyze or address the issue of whether the discrimination experienced by the appellant amounts to persecution. I will focus on the appellant's arguments written in his AR in assessing this issue. [30] I have reviewed the evidence on the record and find that there were no credibility issues related to the appellant's allegations. Therefore, I have no reason to disagree with the RPD on credibility of the appellant. I note the RPD member finds20 the appellant to be a credible witness. Overall, I have found you to be a credible witness and, therefore, I believe what you have alleged in support of your claim. Although there were some omissions in your Basis of Claim Form,21 such as being tasered by the police and that two of the attackers in 2012 were police officers, I find, overall, your evidence has been consistent. You explained that you were frustrated and mad and also in a hurry when you completed the Basis of Claim Form on your own. Also I note that you have been self-represented throughout your refugee claim process. [31] The RPD then moved on to assess human rights by stating:22 Claimants must establish that they will face persecution if returned to their country of nationality. In some cases, cumulative discrimination can reach the threshold of persecution. I'm just going to refer to the UNHCR Handbook, paragraph 54, about discrimination. Differential treatment of various groups exists to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, For example, there are serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practise his religion or his access to education.23 To be considered persecution, the mistreatment suffered or anticipated must be serious. Mistreatment is serious if it involves the denial of a core human right. [32] The appellant submits that his freedom of religious belief is one of the core human rights. However, the RPD member finds:24 There is little evidence of persecution against you as a Christian or Christians in general in Israel. There is little evidence in the country condition documents that there are any legal or other barriers to Christians practising their faith in Israel. I just want to refer to the U.S. Department of State Report on International Religious Freedom for Israel for the year 2014. It's item 12.1 in the National Documentation Package.25 There's certainly legal protection for the practice of Christianity. Page 4 says that the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the basic law on human dignity and liberty protects freedom to practice religious beliefs, including freedom of conscience, faith, religion and worship, regardless of an individual's religion. I recognize that there is a reference on page 21 to societal attitudes towards missionary activities and conversion to other religions as being negative. Pages 2 and 9 also refer to "price tag" attacks against various religious sites, including Christian ones. "Price tag" attacks refer to property crimes and other violent acts often, but not exclusively, committed by settler groups primarily against Muslim and Christian Palestinians and Israeli Arabs, their religious sites and cemeteries. You had testified that you went to church in Israel, albeit on Fridays rather than Sundays, because you sometimes had to work on Sunday. There isn't sufficient evidence before me to find that you have been or would be persecuted in Israel because you're Christian. [33] I find that the RPD member has adequately addressed the issue relating to the appellant's practise of Christianity in Israel. The same article26 also states that Christianity is recognized by the laws in Israel. I also do not find that the appellant has provided persuasive evidence to indicate that he does not have a right to practise Christianity in Israel. [34] The appellant also refers to the RPD member's decision where she makes a finding on his right to live in certain places in Israel. The RPD member finds:27 You have stated that you do not wish to live in a place where you cannot donate blood or purchase or rent housing in certain neighbourhoods because you are Ethiopian. I understand that 12.5 percent of land in the public domain in Israel is owned by an NGO called the Jewish National Fund. Its statutes prohibit the sale and lease of land to non-Jews. This is a relatively small percentage of the land that non-Jews do not have access to in Israel, so I find that this is not a widespread issue. [35] The appellant submits this finding as an error in the RPD decision. I find the appellant has not provided any arguments to indicate where the RPD member erred in this finding. [36] The appellant also submits that the RPD erred in her finding that the assaults he experienced do not, cumulatively, amount to persecution. The appellant submits that he believes that "physical assault based on racial discrimination is persecution". I do not agree with the appellant. The appellant must establish that there are serious grounds to believe that there is such well-founded fear and he will have the fear everywhere in Israel and that the state is unwilling or incapable of protecting him. It is not sufficient for the appellant to establish that the harm in question is inflicted commonly in his country or that the danger would happen randomly. It has been established since at least the early 1980s that cumulative acts of harassment, which on their own may be no more than acts of common criminality, may constitute persecution.28 It is the panel's duty to determine whether or not the evidence in this case displays a pattern of serious and systematic harassment, criminality, or abuse that it would consider as persecution. The RAD agrees with the RPD member that it does not amount to persecution. [37] However, I find that the experiences of the appellant amounts to discrimination and harassment at most but does not rise to the level of persecution. I accept, as did the RPD member, that the appellant has suffered discrimination and has been a victim of random attacks. However, I do not find that this behaviour the appellant was subjected to amounts to sufficient and credible evidence that his problems took place in a systematic fashion. The RAD concurs with the RPD member that although the appellant may be discriminated against due to his ethnicity, this does not amount to persecution. At best, this demonstrates random attacks, spread to a few times in his life while living in Israel, and in a non-systematic fashion. [38] Furthermore, as the RPD member acknowledged, the appellant has attended university and has been employed. The RPD member finds: While some Ethiopians have limited access to education and employment, your education and work history is not consistent with someone who has been discriminated such that it would amount to persecution. [39] Thus, I do not find that the appellant has adequately established that his experiences in Israel due to his ethnicity, on a balance of probabilities, amount to persecution. [40] The Federal Court has said that "where state protection is available for the types of events alleged as discriminatory, the cumulative assessment is not necessary".29 The Federal Court has also said, "It is appropriate to consider both the actions of the government against the individual claimant and the overall atmosphere created by the state's intolerance."30 I find that this argument made by the appellant is assessed sufficiently through the RPD member's finding that state protection is available for the appellant in Israel. State protection [41] The appellant submits that since the RPD member agrees that the Machash failed in protecting him then it is most likely that the police will also fail to protect him in Israel and that is why he did not seek protection from the police. The RPD member finds that the determinative issue is this claim is state protection. [42] The RPD member finds:31 In your case, there is a presumption that Israel being capable of protecting its citizens. Israel is not a state in a state of complete breakdown. Israel is a multi-party parliamentary democracy. This is how it is described in the U.S. Department of State Reports on Human Rights Practices for 2015. This is NDP item 2.1.32 There is not clear and convincing evidence that the Israeli Government is unwilling or unable to provide state protection. The objective documents confirm that Israel has a functioning police force and provides adequate protection to its citizen, including oversight of the police. [43] The RPD member recognized that there are complaint mechanisms in place which the appellant could have utilized to make formal complaints against the people he dealt with at the Machash. The appellant did not do so. The RPD member finds:33 In your case, it appears that Machash did fail you. However, you did not approach the state for protection beyond going to Machash. Given that Israel is a democracy, it is reasonable to expect that you would challenge the police misconduct and inaction within Machash to other organizations such as the Police Complaints Unit, the State Comptroller or NGOs. You explained your failure to approach these other organizations because you need to have a documented response from Machash of their investigation before these other organizations would investigate your complaint. I do not find this explanation to be satisfactory. There is no indication in the country condition documents that you would have to wait for Machash to conclude its investigation and/or to produce a record of its investigation before going to these other organizations. These organizations are all separate and apart from each other. They are concurrent avenues of recourse for Israeli citizens against state actions. They are not processes that follow one another in a hierarchy. [44] The RPD member finds that the appellant did have state protection in Israel. I have reviewed all the evidence before the RPD and the RAD. There is no persuasive evidence before the RAD to find, on a balance of probabilities, that the appellant made any attempts to seek state protection at a higher level at any time for his problems with discrimination or his fear of the threats and attacks. I also cite Federal Court jurisprudence referring to state protection and the responsibility of a refugee claimant to pursue it in their country. The RPD is not obliged to provide proof that the state can offer the claimant effective protection, rather, the claimant [appellant] bears the legal burden of rebutting the presumption that state protection exists by adducing clear and convincing evidence which satisfies the RPD, or the RAD, on a balance of probabilities.34 [45] Particular to the appellant's situation, he experienced threats from non-state agents who were unknown to him. He also experienced a situation with the police who thought the appellant had participated at an Ethiopian protest. Both incidents were reported to Machash but the appellant did not receive a response. However, the appellant did not make any efforts to pursue these complaints at a higher level and stated that he needed "documentary response" from Machash to pursue the matter further is unsatisfactory. Not only is protection offered by the police to be considered but also other state agencies, e.g. those that investigate police conduct.35 Country reports also note: The government took some steps to prosecute and punish officials who committed abuses within Israel regardless of rank or seniority. The government proceeded with structural reforms to reduce impunity and increase accountability.36 [46] A claimant [appellant] must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.37 The appellant testified38 that there are no other organizations where he can bring further his complaints and that he can only do this with the Machash. I find the appellant has not provided persuasive evidence that he made any efforts to pursue treatment experienced by the the Machash. Thus, I do not find the appellant, on a balance of probabilities, has rebutted the presumption of state protection given his situation in Israel. [47] Furthermore, the appellant's main argument is that state protection is not forthcoming due to his ethnicity. This is the appellant's main reason for seeking refugee protection in Canada. I note that the RPD member recognized that ethnic tension with Ethiopians does exist in Israel. However, I also note that the RPD member finds that the government is making efforts to protect this ethnic group. [48] With respect to the appellant's ethnicity, the RPD member finds:39 The question remains as to whether or not you would be able to obtain state protection as an Ethiopian in Israel. The country condition documents do not provide clear and convincing evidence that the state is unwilling or unable to protect you by reason of your ethnicity. There is objective evidence of societal discrimination against Ethiopians in Israel and some instances of violence against Ethiopians and police brutality. The United States Department of State Report talks about the treatment of Ethiopians. It states: According to the Central Bureau of Statistics, based on figures at the end of 2014, an estimated population of 135,500 Ethiopian Jews face persistent societal discrimination, although officials and citizens quickly and publicly criticize discriminatory acts against them. Following the disappearance of Avera Mengistu, who independently and of his own free will entered Gaza and was believed to have been apprehended by terrorist groups in September 2014, the military initially imposed a gag order on reporting on the case that lasted until July. Family and friends of Mengistu alleged his case received inadequate attention from the government because he was Ethiopian; however, the prime minister subsequently visited the family. In April, Israeli-Ethiopian citizens protested against what they perceived to be discriminatory treatment in society. The galvanizing event for the protest was the publication of a video from April 26 that showed two police officers in Holon stopping and beating uniformed Ethiopian-Israeli soldier Demas Fekadeh. Members of the Ethiopian community emphasized the widespread nature of such discrimination and abuse. Protestors in Jerusalem and Tel Aviv demanded the police officer be arrested and charged; however, as of September 9, such action had not taken place. Prime Minister Netanyahu, President Reuven Rivlin and many ministers and Knesset members condemned the attack against Fekadeh, praised his call to avoid violence, and promised to work to lessen socioeconomic gaps between sectors of society.40 There is some evidence that the Israeli Government is making some efforts to improve the situation of Ethiopians in Israel. The government maintained several programs to address social, educational and economic disparities between Ethiopian Israelis and the general population. For instance, there's mention of a program operated by the Ministry of Welfare that supports Ethiopian integration into the workforce with education and social support for families with a budget of NIS three million, which is $770,000. The Ministry's total budget was expected to reach six billion shekels or 1.5 billion dollars for the year. I have also reviewed the documents you provided with your refugee claim in Exhibit 1. There are a number of articles that refer to unequal treatment of Ethiopians in housing, jobs, military service and other sectors of Israeli society. There is certainly some level of stratification in Israel based on race. However, the evidence on the Israeli Government's inability to unwillingness to protect its citizens, including ones of Ethiopian descent, is not clear and convincing. There appears to be a robust criminal justice system in Israel and various avenues of recourse within the police, other independent governmental bodies and NGOs in the event that the police do not respond to a complaint or behave abusively or improperly. [49] The RPD member also finds:41 The government maintained several programs to address social, educational and economic disparities between Ethiopian Israelis and the general population. For instance, there's mention of a program operated by the Ministry of Welfare that supports Ethiopian integration into the workforce with education and social support for families with a budget of NIS three million, which is $770,000. The Ministry's total budget was expected to reach six billion shekels or 1.5 billion dollars for the year. [50] Isolated cases of persons having been victimized may not reverse the presumption of state protection. A state of profound unrest with ineffective protection for the claimant may, however, have reversed it.42 This is not the case for the appellant. Therefore, the appellant's submission that the RPD member failed to recognize the cumulative effects of discrimination amounts to persecution fails as, on a balance of probabilities, it is clear from the evidence that state protection is forthcoming for people such as the appellant in Israel. [51] I, again, note that the RPD member recognizes the ethnic tension in Israel and the police response to such tension. However, the RPD member finds:43 There is objective evidence of societal discrimination against Ethiopians in Israel and some instances of violence against Ethiopians and police brutality. The United States Department of State Report talks about the treatment of Ethiopians. It states: According to the Central Bureau of Statistics, based on figures at the end of 2014, an estimated population of 135,500 Ethiopian Jews face persistent societal discrimination, although officials and citizens quickly and publicly criticize discriminatory acts against them. Following the disappearance of Avera Mengistu, who independently and of his own free will entered Gaza and was believed to have been apprehended by terrorist groups in September 2014, the military initially imposed a gag order on reporting on the case that lasted until July. Family and friends of Mengistu alleged his case received inadequate attention from the government because he was Ethiopian; however, the prime minister subsequently visited the family. In April, Israeli-Ethiopian citizens protested against what they perceived to be discriminatory treatment in society. The galvanizing event for the protest was the publication of a video from April 26 that showed two police officers in Holon stopping and beating uniformed Ethiopian-Israeli soldier Demas Fekadeh. Members of the Ethiopian community emphasized the widespread nature of such discrimination and abuse. Protestors in Jerusalem and Tel Aviv demanded the police officer be arrested and charged; however, as of September 9, such action had not taken place. Prime Minister Netanyahu, President Reuven Rivlin and many ministers and Knesset members condemned the attack against Fekadeh, praised his call to avoid violence, and promised to work to lessen socioeconomic gaps between sectors of society.44 [52] Through my own independent evidence of the country reports, I note that there is no persuasive evidence before me that there is a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection. Country reports note that "Communities with a large concentration of African migrants were occasionally targets of violence. The Tel Aviv municipality dedicated a special police unit to combat violence and crime in the migrant community."45 Therefore, efforts are being made to protect people like the appellant. More importantly, no state can guarantee perfect protection.46 [53] Taking into consideration the country reports, I find the appellant has not provided clear and convincing evidence that state protection was not available for him in Israel. Nor is there any information before the RPD Member, or the RAD, that state protection is ineffective in Israel. A claimant [appellant] who alleges that the state protection is inadequate must persuade the Immigration and Refugee Board that the evidence establishes that the state protection is inadequate.47 [54] In summary, having considered all the evidence, I find that I agree with the RPD that the appellant has state protection in Israel, and I also find there is no persuasive evidence that the appellant would not have state protection on the basis of his fear of cumulative acts of discrimination amounting to persecution, or for his ethnicity or religion. REMEDY [55] For all these reasons, I confirm the determination of the RPD in accordance with subsection 111(1) (a) of the Act, namely, that the appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. This appeal is therefore dismissed.
