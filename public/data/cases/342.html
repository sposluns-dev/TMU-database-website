Aujla Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia January 21, 2022 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, British Columbia January 21, 2022 Summary: The appellant applies for: an order removing his appeal from the inactive list; an order that no court fees be payable; copies of disks of the hearings below; and permission to serve the respondents by email. Held: Applications dismissed. It is obvious that the appellant's appeal is bound to fail, as he has not identified any legal errors in the judgment below. It is thus not in the interests of justice to reactivate the appeal. The other applications are therefore moot, although they would be dismissed in any event. The no fee status application would fail for lack of merit; the application for recordings in the court would fail because that is a matter for the Supreme Court of British Columbia; an order for service by email would not be required as any further documents would not need to be served personally and all parties have provided email addresses for service. [1] FENLON J.A.: The appellant, Robert Grosz, seeks the following: 1) an order removing his appeal from the inactive list; 2) an order that no court fees be payable; 3) to be provided with disks of the hearings below from which to make transcripts for his appeal; and 4) permission to serve the respondents by email. Background [2] This matter arises from a real estate transaction and its lengthy subsequent litigation. The appeal is from an order of Justice Forth made February 4, 2020, striking Mr. Grosz's claim against all of the defendants: 2020 BCSC 128. [3] On February 7, 2020, Mr. Grosz filed his notice of appeal, along with his notice of motion for an order that no fees be payable, which was originally set to be heard on April 28, 2020. All of the respondents, with the exception of Ms. Jones, filed motions for security for costs. All of the security for costs motions were set to be heard on March 18, 2020. [4] On March 17, 2020, in response to the COVID-19 pandemic, Chief Justice Bauman issued a direction adjourning all matters scheduled between March 18 and May 1, 2020; this adjourned all of the above motions. Court hearings resumed in mid‑September 2020. No further steps were taken by Mr. Grosz once court hearings again became available. [5] On August 17, 2021, a letter was sent from the Court of Appeal Registry to Mr. Grosz advising him that his appeal had been placed on the inactive list, pursuant to s. 25 of the Court of Appeal Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 77, and that if no steps were taken, the appeal would stand dismissed as abandoned on February 7, 2022. [6] On January 13, 2022, Mr. Grosz filed a notice of motion seeking the relief I identified at the outset of these reasons: · removal of his appeal from the inactive list; · no-fee status; · disks from the hearing below; and · the right to serve the respondents by email. [7] A single justice in chambers can remove an appeal from the inactive list: s. 25(2), Court of Appeal Act. The test is that identified by Mr. Grosz in his submissions. There is no rigid test for when to reactivate an appeal; rather, the overriding concern is whether reactivation is in the interests of justice: Deline v. Kidd, 2003 BCCA 170 at para. 11. [8] The factors which this Court has considered include the following, as set out at para. 24 of Kar Recovery, Ltd. v. KDA, 2004 BCCA 503 (Chambers): a) The extent of the delay; b) The explanation for the delay; c) Any prejudice arising from the delay; and d) The likelihood of success on appeal. [9] Mr. Grosz explained the delay between September 2020 and the fall of 2021 as due to a period of hospitalization and illness. He says that it was not until October 2021 that his medications were changed and that his condition stabilized to a point where he could pursue his appeal. Even with that explanation, however, the likelihood of success on appeal remains the predominant consideration in this case. [10] Some background is necessary to assess that factor. On June 19, 2014, Mr. Grosz, through his company, The Matryx Corporation ("Matryx"), entered into a contract of purchase and sale (the "Contract") with the Royal Trust Corporation of Canada ("Royal Trust") for a property in Surrey (the "Property"). The Property was a condominium unit which had been owned by the respondent, Eleanor Bird, prior to her death in 2013. Royal Trust was the executor of her estate. Matryx has since assigned its rights under the Contract to Mr. Grosz. [11] Mr. Grosz first viewed the Property on June 18, 2014, along with the respondent, realtor Ronald Elliott, who is the husband of the listing agent, the respondent, Catherine Elliott. Mr. Elliott was a realtor with the respondent, West Coast Realty Ltd. Mr. Grosz alleged that Mr. Elliott told him that Ms. Bird did not die in the Property. [12] The next day, the Contract was entered into by Mr. Grosz to purchase the Property, and it included a clause that the "Property is an estate sale" and the sale was "as is" without legal warranties or representations from the seller. [13] The Contract also included certain subject conditions for the sole benefit of the buyer (Mr. Grosz), which were required to be removed--either waived or declared fulfilled--by July 4, 2014 (the "Subject Removal Date"). These included that the buyer was to "receive and approve certain documents with respect to information that could reasonably adversely affect the use or value of the Property" and "obtain, approve, or waive an inspection report against defects which might adversely affect the Property's use or value": at para. 9. [14] On June 24, 2014, Mr. Grosz learned that Ms. Bird had indeed died in the Property and that because her body had not been found immediately, there was decay that caused defects to the Property, which had required remediation. The remediation work had been carried out in the summer and fall of 2013, without the necessary building permits from the City of Surrey. [15] On June 30, 2014, four days before the Subject Removal Date, Mr. Grosz commenced an action in the Supreme Court of British Columbia against West Coast Realty Ltd., Seasons Real Estate Services Corporation, and Catherine and Ronald Elliott (collectively, the "Realtor defendants"), as well as Royal Trust and the Estate of Ms. Bird (the "First Action"). [16] On July 4, 2014, at Mr. Grosz's request, the Subject Removal Date was extended to July 14, 2014. Mr. Grosz's further request to extend the Subject Removal Date was refused by Royal Trust, which claimed it had met its disclosure obligations. [17] Mr. Grosz contacted the City of Surrey, which informed him that no building permit had been obtained, but that one could be required to do future work. Mr. Grosz also claimed he was told that the City of Surrey would retroactively enforce the building permit requirement against him if he purchased the Property, which he believed would cost more than the fair market value of the Property. [18] In July 2019, Mr. Grosz learned that the City of Surrey had not retroactively enforced the building permit requirement against the subsequent purchaser, the respondent Tammy Esther Jones, who purchased the Property in March 2015. [19] As Matryx had failed to waive or fulfill the subject conditions by the Subject Removal Date, on July 15, 2014, Royal Trust took the position that the Contract had been terminated. It communicated this to Mr. Grosz, along with its intention to relist the Property for sale. [20] No steps were taken in the First Action between July 2014 and July 2019, so on July 22, 2019, Royal Trust filed an application to have the action dismissed for want of prosecution (the "Dismissal Application"). In response, Mr. Grosz filed a notice of intention to proceed, and brought an application to strike the Dismissal Application (the "Strike Application"). Moreover, he secured a trial date for the First Action for ten days in January 2021, without consulting the defendants. [21] The parties to the First Action attended before Justice Forth in chambers on August 28, 2019. At that time, Mr. Grosz indicated an intention to add further parties to that action. Justice Forth ordered that the parties were not to serve or file any additional materials without seeking her leave on September 24, 2019--the date set for the Dismissal Application to be heard--and were also not to bring any chambers applications between August 28 and September 24. [22] On September 3, 2019--during the period in which he was prevented from bringing an application to add parties to the First Action--Mr. Grosz commenced another action (the "Second Action"); it is the Second Action which forms the basis for this appeal. In the notice of civil claim for the Second Action, Mr. Grosz appended the notice of civil claim for the First Action, and re-pleaded and incorporated it into the Second Action. [23] In addition to the parties to the First Action, Mr. Grosz added the following new parties to the Second Action (at para. 28): a) several senior Royal Trust employees, being: Michael Van Der Kooy, Vice President; Jacqueline Eddy, Senior Trust Officer; and Pauline Savoy, Regional Client Service Manager (together with Royal Trust, the "Royal Trust defendants"); b) Boughton Law Corporation, the law firm representing Royal Trust in the First Action, and several of its lawyers and a staff member, being: James D. Baird, estate solicitor; Heather Craig, lawyer for Royal Trust in the First Action; Gregg E. Rafter, lawyer for Royal Trust in the First Action; Marcia C. Pedersen, legal assistant; and the law corporations of Baird, Craig and Rafter (the "Boughton defendants"); c) the strata corporation for the complex in which the Property is located (the "Strata defendant"); d) the City of Surrey; and e) Ms. Jones, the current owner of the Property. [24] On September 24, 2019, Justice Forth heard the Dismissal and Strike Applications; she issued reasons for judgment indexed at 2019 BCSC 1993 on November 21, 2019, dismissing the First Action for want of prosecution. [25] On December 3-4, 2019, Justice Forth heard several applications in the Second Action. The Royal Trust, Realtor and Strata defendants all brought applications to have the Second Action dismissed on the basis that there was no genuine issue for trial as the claims were statute-barred by the expiry of the limitation period. As well, each of the applicant defendants--being all the defendants, except Ms. Jones--in the action applied to have the Second Action dismissed pursuant to Rule 9-5(1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg 168/2009 on the basis that: the pleadings disclosed no reasonable claim; the action was frivolous, vexatious, and embarrassing; and the action was an abuse of the court's process. [26] In comprehensive reasons for judgment, the judge found all of these applications had been made out. In doing so, she said in part: [102] I do not think it is necessary to go into detail about the deficiencies in the claims as they relate to each of the applicant defendants as I accept that the pleadings offend R. 9-5(1)(a) on the basis that, as a whole, they are prolix, convoluted, at times contradictory, and lacking in material facts and law. Rather than pleading material facts, the notice of civil claim is written as a lengthy narrative and [contains] many extracts from letters, emails, contracts, reports, and other evidence, which are not properly included in a notice of civil claim: R. 3-7(1). ... [104] The fact that the notice of civil claim makes highly inflammatory allegations of fraud, conspiracy, and criminal conduct against the applicant defendants globally, several of whom Mr. Grosz has never met or even communicated with, which are not sufficiently particularized and are based on pure speculation, is also relevant to my finding that this action is vexatious and an abuse of process. [105] I am persuaded that Mr. Grosz is using the court process in an abusive manner. He continues to use the threat of a lawsuit as a means to achieve his personal goals, including to attempt to extract settlements from the applicant defendants in circumstances where it is obvious that the action cannot succeed and no reasonable person could expect a court to grant relief. [106] I am concerned by the use of judicial resources to fuel Mr. Grosz's speculative theories at an inordinate cost to the applicant defendants and to the detriment of other litigants awaiting hearings. To allow the Second Action to continue would be to allow the court's process to be misused and to allow an oppressive and vexatious action to continue against the applicant defendants. [27] Turning to whether Mr. Grosz should be given the opportunity to amend his pleadings, the judge said: [116] I am not convinced that allowing Mr. Grosz the opportunity to amend his pleadings will cure the defects, nor would it be fair to do so in the circumstances. He has always had the ability to make amendments to his pleadings, but has chosen not to do so despite claiming to have discovered the last of the allegations in July and August of 2019, before the Second Action was commenced. Furthermore, his proposed solution to cure the defects is to include three to four times the volume of material in his pleadings. This would likely make the pleadings even more prolix and convoluted rather than assisting the applicant defendants in understanding the claims against them. [117] I also find that amending the pleadings will not assist Mr. Grosz with respect to the question of damages. The Contract is clear that the purchase of the Property was on an "as is" basis with no representations or warranties made with respect to its condition. The subject conditions were included solely for Mr. Grosz's benefit to allow him to walk away from the deal if he found the Property to be unsatisfactory. That is precisely what happened in this case, and, as I held in my November 21, 2019 Reasons at para. 139, the fact that Mr. Grosz did not purchase another property is not the fault of the applicant defendants. [28] Ultimately, the judge concluded the claims should be struck out, saying: [121] I conclude that Mr. Grosz's claims against the Royal Trust defendants, the Boughton defendants, and the Strata defendant be struck, pursuant to R. 9-6 as time barred under the Limitation Act. [122] I also conclude that the pleadings offend R. 9-5(1)(a), (b), (c), and (d) as disclosing no reasonable claim and being vexatious, embarrassing, and an abuse of process. As such, the pleadings are struck and the proceeding dismissed against all of the applicant defendants. [29] Mr. Grosz made articulate submissions today explaining why he was of the view that the appeal should be reinstated. But, with respect, Mr. Grosz has not identified any legal errors in the judge's reasons for judgment. He says that the judge was biased and cherry-picked the facts, did not realize he had discovered new claims against the original defendants that were not barred by the Limitation Act, S.B.C. 2012, c. 13, and failed to exercise her discretion fairly in not allowing him to amend his pleadings, choosing instead the draconian remedy of dismissing his claims. These criticisms reflect disagreement with the judge's conclusions and reasons but do not identify arguable grounds of appeal. [30] I conclude it is plain that an appeal would be bound to fail. I would note parenthetically that it is not in the interests of Mr. Grosz to reinstate an appeal that would almost certainly result in costs being added to the other financial challenges which he has identified in his submissions. Having concluded that the appeal is bound to fail, I also conclude it is not in the interests of justice to reinstate the appeal and decline to do so. [31] There being no appeal on foot as a result of that decision, it is unnecessary for me to consider the other three applications but I will do so for completeness. [32] I would deny Mr. Grosz's application for no-fee status. He meets the first part of the test as someone with insufficient means to retain counsel but he cannot meet the second part of the test, which is establishing a meritorious appeal. [33] I would also, as I said earlier in the hearing, dismiss Mr. Grosz's application for recordings in the court below. That is not an order this Court would make. Whether Mr. Grosz is entitled to copies is a matter for the Supreme Court of British Columbia under its policies, as Justice Chiasson observed in Maughan v. The University of British Columbia, 2009 BCCA 326 at para. 10 (Chambers). [34] Finally, the order to serve by email would have been unnecessary even if the appeal was reinstated. Under Rule 39, the only documents that must be served personally are a notice of appeal or leave to appeal. Once a party files a document containing an address for service which includes an email address, a document may be served using that email address. I understand that all of the parties have provided email addresses in their responses. It therefore would not have been necessary to make an order to that effect. [35] I repeat, in any event, that these last three applications are moot because the appeal has not been reinstated. I would therefore dismiss all of the applications. The respondents are entitled to their costs of this application. "The Honourable Madam Justice Fenlon"
