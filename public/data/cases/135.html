Maughan v. UBC

2003BCSC1367 IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Maughan v. UBC et al, 2003 BCSC 1367 Date: 20030908 Docket: S025856 Registry: Vancouver Between: Cynthia Maughan Plaintiff And The University of British Columbia, Lorraine Weir, Susanna Egan, Anne Scott and Judy Segal Defendants Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Brown Reasons for Judgment Counsel for the plaintiff G. D. Chipeur Counsel for the defendants T. A. Roper, Q.C. M. Cooperwilliams Date and Place of Hearing: June 23, 2003 Vancouver, B.C. [1] Ms. Maughan seeks damages from the University of British Columbia and four professors who she says breached her civil rights pursuant to the Civil Rights Protection Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c.49 (the "Act"). [2] On this application, the defendants ask to have Ms. Maughan's statement of claim dismissed as disclosing no cause of action. [3] In an application such as this, the court considers only whether the statement of claim pleads a cause of action. It is the form of the pleadings, not the substance of the cause of action which is in issue. The court does not weigh the evidence to determine whether the claim is valid. The court must assume, for the purposes of the application, that the facts pleaded are true. [4] I am satisfied that the statement of claim meets this very limited threshold test. However, there are portions of the claim which otherwise offend the pleadings rules and those portions of the claim will be struck. The Plaintiff's Claim [5] The test which I am to apply is: as long as the pleadings disclose a triable issue, either as it exists, or as it may be amended, then the issue should go to trial. The mere fact that the case is weak or not likely to succeed is no ground for striking it out under the provisions of Rule 19(24) (Minnes v. Minnes and Rees-Davies (1962), 34 D.L.R. (2d) 497). [6] The thrust of the plaintiff's claim is that the defendants breached her civil rights, entitling her to damages pursuant to the Act. [7] The Act provides that a prohibited act is conduct or communication which has as its purpose interference with the civil rights of a person by promoting hatred or contempt or inferiority of a person on the basis of religion, ethnic origin or place of origin. A prohibited act is a tort for which a person may receive damages as provided by the Act. [8] I have carefully reviewed the statement of claim. I am satisfied that the plaintiff has pleaded the necessary elements of the tort set out in the Act. [9] Specifically, at para. 29, the plaintiff pleads, with respect to the defendant Weir, that: The June emails promoted hatred and contempt toward the Plaintiff because of her religion. [10] With respect to the defendant Scott, at para. 41, the plaintiff pleads: The Scott letter was conduct designed to expose the Plaintiff to hatred and contempt because of her religion. [11] With respect to the defendant Segal, the plaintiff pleads at paras. 44 and 45: Through the Segal letter, Segal indicated discrimination or an intention to discriminate. [It]subjected the plaintiff to contempt and suggested she was inferior. The effect of such a statement is to expose the plaintiff to ridicule, hatred and contempt. [12] Discrimination is a defined term in the statement of claim. Discrimination is defined at para. 7 as: conduct or communication promoting hatred or contempt of the Plaintiff or a class of persons, or the inferiority of the Plaintiff or a class of person in comparison with others on the basis of religion or ethnic origin or place of origin. [13] The claim against the defendant Egan is set forth in paragraphs 7 and 46 to 49. At paragraph 7, the plaintiff pleads that the Defendants by conduct or communication promoted hatred or contempt of the Plaintiff on the basis of religion or ethnic origin or place of origin. The defendant Egan's behaviour, which the plaintiff says offends the Act, is described in paragraphs 46 to 49 and includes being a "partisan, aggressive and officious advocate against the Plaintiff, particularly at the Plaintiff's final appeal before the U.B.C. Senate." [14] Further particulars may be required to determine what the plaintiff says was done to promote hatred or contempt, but the pleading is sufficient to disclose a cause of action against the defendant Egan. [15] With respect to the defendant, UBC, there is no allegation that the university is liable for the acts of its professors on the basis of vicarious liability. However, there is a pleading at para. 70.1 specifically involving the defendant UBC. [16] In that paragraph, the plaintiff alleges that UBC placed a link in its website to a publication intended to generate contempt for the plaintiff because of her religion. Although the defendant UBC asserts that the website referred to is not its official website, on this application, I must assume that the allegations contained in the statement of claim are true. Hence, para. 70.1 is sufficient to set forth a cause of action against the University of British Columbia. [17] At para. 36 of the statement of claim, the plaintiff asserts that the defendants were negligent. The defendants say that there is no cause of action for educational negligence known to the law. They ask to have the claim of negligence struck. [18] The claim of negligence has not been adequately pleaded. It is not possible to determine the duty of care which the plaintiff asserts the defendants owed to her, nor to determine the particulars of the negligence. Negligence is, however, a claim known to law. On an application of this sort, any doubt should be resolved in favour of permitting the pleadings to stand. It may be that if the claim were amended, with particulars, it would be possible to appreciate the claim which the plaintiff proposes to bring against the defendant Weir and the other defendants in negligence. Accordingly, I will not strike this aspect of the claim. Offending Portions of the Statement of Claim Rule 19(24) sets out four bases on which the court may strike a statement of claim, in whole or in part: (a) it discloses no reasonable claim; (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious; (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay a fair trial; or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court. [19] Portions of the statement of claim offend Rule 19 in the sense that they are irrelevant and legally embarrassing. As Mr. Justice Romilly has noted in Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, [1997] B.C.J. No. 2160, a pleading is unnecessary or vexatious if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or does not advance any claim known in law. For this reason, I am of the view that paras. 57 - 65 and 68 - 70, must be struck. [20] At paras. 57 - 61, the plaintiff asserts that she has not received full disclosure of documents from UBC, that the Freedom of Information Office has not disclosed all information as required by the University of British Columbia policies. These allegations do not advance the plaintiff's claims pursuant to the Act, nor do they disclose any other claim known to law and must be struck. [21] At paras. 62 - 65, the plaintiff refers to the findings of the Senate Committee of the University of British Columbia on her grade appeal. This, too, is an embarrassing pleading in the sense that it is irrelevant to claims advanced by the plaintiff in this action. [22] At paras. 68 - 70, the plaintiff asserts that the defendants violated UBC's standards of non-discrimination. There may well be processes within the university to address this issue. However, assuming that UBC did not follow its policies, as the plaintiff alleges, this failure alone would not be a breach of the Act or otherwise constitute a cause of action. For this reason, these paragraphs must be struck. [23] Finally, the defendants have asserted that in determining whether material facts required to support the claim have been pleaded, it is appropriate for the court to consider the documents referred to in the statement of claim, as did the Supreme Court of Canada in Monenco Ltd. v. Commonwealth Insurance Co., [2001] 2 S.C.R. 699. In that case, the Supreme Court of Canada looked to documents referred to in the pleadings to determine whether there was a claim pleaded which could come within an insurance policy and trigger an insurer's duty to defend. Here, the defendants asked me to look to the documents to determine whether those documents are capable of forming the material facts necessary to support the plaintiff's claim. In doing so, it seems to me that I would be weighing the evidence, rather than merely looking to see whether facts have been pleaded to support a claim. In my view, on an application of this nature, the court must give the plaintiff the benefit of the doubt. In other words, rather than looking to the documents to which the plaintiff has referred to assess whether the facts in those documents support the plaintiff's claim, the court can only look to see whether allegations have been made which, if true, are sufficient to set forth a cause of action. [24] In summary, paragraphs 57 - 65 and 68 - 70 of the statement of claim are struck. [25] Costs in the cause. "B.J. Brown, J." The Honourable Madam Justice B.J. Brown
