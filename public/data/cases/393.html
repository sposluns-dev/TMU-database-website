Associated with the claim is that of her son, XXXX XXXX who is XXXX years old. The claimants are both citizens of Israel who are seeking protection in Canada citing sections 96 and 97 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). Ms. XXXX XXXX is named designated representative of XXXX XXXX. ALLEGATIONS [2] The principal claimant alleges that she and her son face a risk of persecution that arises from several sources. Specifically, she alleges that if she returns to Israel she will be "persecuted for my social activity, political and religious views, for criticizing of the management of the factory, which was selling expired goods, [and] for the support of the Palestinian minority." The principal claimant also alleges that she has faced gender-related persecution at the hands of her ex-boyfriend. [3] The principal claimant alleges that she criticized the Rabbis in her Synagogue in XXXX, Israel, for essentially XXXX XXXX. As a result, the principal claimant alleges that she was criticized and harassed, and was attacked and strangled by an "extremist." The principal claimant alleges that her son was also assaulted by fellow students defending the Rabbis, which resulted in psychological distress. [4] The principal claimant alleges that her neighbors in her building are prejudiced against her because she in a Russian who is not viewed as Jewish, such that she is tense and concerned for her child who at one point was struck by a woman of Moroccan Jewish ethnicity who also allegedly called him a "dirty goy." [5] The principal claimant alleges that as a member of a social and political aid group identified as "Imun" which was created in 2009, she was targeted and her apartment ransacked because she supported tolerance and harmony among diverse peoples. In XXXX 2010, the principal claimant alleges that the office of Imun was attacked by 10 people while she was there, and she alleges that she was arrested in XXXX 2010 while protesting the Israeli capture of a Turkish ship seeking to aid Palestinians. The principal claimant alleges that the agent of persecution in this case was a political party identified as Our Home is Israel (OHI). [6] The principal claimant also took a principled stand against the sale of outXXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX where she worked. As a result, she was fired from work in XXXX 2010. The principal claimant alleges that her former employer made sure she would not be hired again in XXXX. The principal claimant alleges that she continued to protest and was told privately that she was at risk of being killed because of her stand on this issue. [7] The principal claimant alleges that her boyfriend of the time identified as Mr. XXXX XXXX was fired from his job as a result of the principal claimant's activities. According to the principal claimant, this caused Mr. XXXX to become resentful and begin to abuse the principal claimant. [8] The principal claimant alleges that the police did not protect her when she phoned them. She was told, "You Russians will settle your problems yourselves. You always fight and complain". The claimant alleges that her relationship with Mr. XXXX deteriorated further and she was horrendously abused. The principal claimant alleges that she finally forced Mr. XXXX out of her apartment in XXXX 2010 and then by selling jewelry and borrowing money from her bother in Canada, she traveled to Canada with her son arriving on XXXX XXXX, 2010. The claimants lodged a refugee claim on XXXX XXXX, 2010. DETERMINATION [9] The panel determines that Ms. XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX are not "Convention refugees" under section 96 of IRPA. The claimants are also not "persons in need of protection" within the meaning of section 97(1)(a) or (b) of IRPA. ANALYSIS [10] The panel is satisfied as to the identity of the claimants. A passport was provided the panel for each claimant. [11] In this case, the panel determines that the claim of the principal claimant must be determined based on an analysis of section 96 in that the claimants allege fear of persecution based on several sources including religion, politics and gender related issues. The principal claimant, on a balance of probabilities, also faces a risk to her life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if she returns to Israel at the hands of her boyfriend (section 97(1)(b)). [12] With respect to credibility, on a balance of probabilities, the panel believes that the claimant is at the very least embellishing her story because the principal claimant is involved in so many activities at the same time that it is difficult to believe that all of these overlapping events could have occurred all at the same time with relatively the same level of risk. For example, in the XXXX XXXX months of 2010, the principal claimant looses her job, is actively protesting against the firing of Russian workers, is lodging complains against her former employer, facing conflict with the OHI group, being attacked by extremists because of her concerns related to Rabbis, dealing with the emotional problems and fights involving her son while at the same time her relationship with her boyfriend was falling apart leading to violence. [13] The panel is also concerned that so little of her allegations are documented. With respect to protests and break-ins, the panel would expect news articles and police reports. Regardless, there is very little aside from testimony that provides corroborative evidence of a risk to the claimants. [14] Even if the panel were to believe the allegations, the panel must deny these claims because the panel concludes that the claimant has failed to provide clear and convincing evidence that the protection provided by the State of Israel is inadequate to protect the claimant given her specific circumstances. [15] With respect to the risk from the Rabbis, the claimant states that after she stopped attending Synagogue, she was left alone. With respect to her activities in Umun, there is evidence that she participated in this organization.1 The claimant alleges that because of this participation, her apartment was ransacked. The panel notes that the principal claimant has no evidence of this attack in the form of a police complaint. The principal claimant alleges that the police arrived and looked but she was not given any document to show this had occurred. The panel notes that there is documentation related to a fight involving her son2 and a complaint made against her employer.3 This implies that documentation can be secured; yet, in this claim there is no police documentation of the various attacks, including those of her boyfriend. The claimant was asked about this and various reasons were provided for their absence including that she was afraid of her boyfriend. Regardless, according to testimony, the claimant alleges that she changed the locks in XXXX 2010 and then called the police when Mr. XXXX tried to break in. The principal claimant alleges she has since been threatened but again, she has not reported these threats. [16] With respect the assaults of her boyfriend that she has reported, the principal claimant alleges that her first phone call was rejected by the police. The claimant alleges making a second phone call which was equally unsuccessful, but she never went to a police station. With respect to the "extremists," the claimant's door was splashed with black paint, yet again there was no police complaint. The panel concludes that given that Israel is a democracy with significant security resources, the panel views the absence of this type of documentation negatively, especially given the surfeit of evidence in other areas. The panel has some familiarity with Israeli claimants and in other claims, the police can be relied on to both show up and provide detailed supporting documentation. [17] With respect to State protection, being a strong democracy, the panel notes that the State of Israel provides protection to political protesters, guarantees for due process of the law and has worked to address intolerance and racism in society. [18] With respect to the documentation related to efforts to address gender related violence, although some rights organizations raise concerns, for example, according to one RIR, In correspondence with the Research Directorate, the ARCCI Representative stated that "Israel is a country with a strong, Western legal system and therefore violence is treated as a criminal offense" (4 Feb. 2010). According to Country Reports 2008, "rape is illegal and the law doubles the penalty if the perpetrator assaults or rapes a relative" (US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 5). Country Reports 2008 mentions the following laws with respect to violence against women: -the Equality of Women Law, which "provides equal rights for women and protection from violence, harassment, exploitation and trafficking" (ibid.); -the Prevention of Stalking Law (ibid.; see also Kayan 2008); and -the Prevention of Family Violence Law (US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 5; see also Kayan 2008). Furthermore, the Rights of Victims of Crimes Law addresses the rights of victims through the various stages of a criminal case (Israel n.d.a; see also Kayan 2008). With respect to legal recourse, WAV questions the capacity of the judicial and police system to address "repetitive cases" of domestic violence, citing an incident in which a repeat domestic violence offender assaulted his 13-year-old daughter (20 Aug. 2009). Response by government authorities The Israeli Knesset Committee on the Status of Women, a permanent committee, addresses a number of women's issues, including violence against women (Israel n.d.b). Country Reports 2008 states that the Ministry of Social Affairs operates a hotline and a shelter for victims of violence (US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 5). According to a report submitted by Israel to the UN Human Rights Committee in July 2008, social workers are employed at eleven police stations throughout the country to assist with domestic violence complaints (Israel 21 Nov. 2008, 29). Country Reports 2008 notes that a police call centre was available to communicate information to victims regarding their cases (US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 5). According to the Israeli government report submitted to the UN Human Rights Committee, the Ministerial Committee to Combat Domestic Violence "operates an internet portal for women, children, and male victims of domestic violence" which contains relevant websites and pertinent information (21 Nov. 2008, 29). In correspondence with the Research Directorate, the ARCCI Representative stated that the ARCCI receives 30 percent of its budget for its RCCs from Israeli government grants (4 Feb. 2010). L.O. Combat Violence Against Women, an organization that provides shelters, hotlines and legal assistance related to domestic violence issues, notes that government funding to its programs has not increased at the same rate as has the cost of living in Israel (n.d.). Services available According to Country Reports 2008, women's groups provided services such as counselling, crisis intervention, legal assistance and shelters (US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 5). Kayan Feminist Organization, an organization that advocates for Palestinian-Israeli women, provides legal assistance and works on policy issues relevant to Arab women, such as the upgrading of police emergency phone lines to include an Arabic language option (n.d.). L.O. Combat Violence Against Women operates shelters for women and children in Herzliya, Hadera and Rishon as well as hotlines in Hebrew and Russian (n.d.). In the Israeli government report submitted to the UN Human Rights Committee, the government of Israel provides the following information on shelters available to victims of domestic violence: Protection from violence is provided by 14 shelters for battered women, established in different locations throughout the country. Due to their distinct cultural and religious needs, two shelters have been specially designated for Arab women, and one for ultra-Orthodox Jewish women. Furthermore, two shelters are accessible to the physically disabled, one of those serving the Arabic population. Together, these shelters provide emergency intervention for nearly 1,600 women and children annually. (Israel 21 Nov. 2008, 26)4. [19] Overall, the panel must conclude that the State of Israel exceeds any minimum requirement that might be required to conclude that State protection is not inadequate with specific reference to the claimant. [20] The panel concludes that the claimants have not met their burden of establishing, on a balance of probabilities, that their allegations are true. Even if the panel were to accept that the allegations are valid, the panel determines that the claimants have not met their burden of establishing with "clear and convincing" evidence that with respect to their specific circumstance, State protection in Israel is inadequate. CONCLUSION [21] For all of the reasons set out above this panel determines that the claimants are not "Convention refugees" nor are they "persons in need of protection." Therefore, their claims are denied. Stephen J. Gallagher Stephen J.
