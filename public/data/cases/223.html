Fountain v. BC College of Teachers

2007 BCSC 830 Fountain v. British Columbia College of Teachers IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Fountain v. British Columbia College of Teachers, 2007 BCSC 830 Date: 20070608 Docket: S38336 Registry: Vernon Between: Michael William Fountain Appellant And British Columbia College of Teachers Respondent Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Ross Reasons for Judgment Counsel for the Appellant In person Counsel for the Respondent Ian Hayward Date and Place of Trial/Hearing: February 6 and 7, 2007 Vernon, B.C. Introduction [1] This is an appeal of a decision of the respondent, British Columbia College of Teachers (the "College"), finding the appellant, Michael William Fountain, guilty of conduct unbecoming a member of the profession. The appeal concerns Mr. Fountain's conduct while off-duty and involves an incident in which he fired a shot over the heads of his sons in the aftermath of a domestic dispute. [2] The errors alleged are that the Hearing Panel (the "Panel"): (a) failed to ensure that Mr. Fountain knew the case he had to meet; (b) failed to consider relevant circumstances; and (c) failed to apply the appropriate standard of proof. Background [3] Mr. Fountain graduated from the University of British Columbia in 1975 with a B.Sc. degree. He worked for a number of years in the oil industry as a drill operator and on an oil drilling ship. He also has work experience as a bartender and bouncer. He returned to university and received his Professional Certificate in 1988. Since receiving his professional certification, Mr. Fountain has taught in Granisle, Prince Rupert and Burns Lake. [4] At the time of the incident, Mr. Fountain had considerable experience with firearms and had qualified for a Firearm's Acquisition Certificate. He had received training for the non-violent handling of situations including a one-day seminar directed to defusing violent situations and not hitting back. [5] In August 2001, Mr. Fountain was living on a farm property located approximately 16 kilometres from the town of Burns Lake. The property fronted onto Highway 16. The main farmhouse was located at the top of a circular driveway. A cabin was located down the driveway. There were some old railway ties piled on the ground beside the cabin. [6] Mr. Fountain has three sons from a previous relationship, Michael, Tyler and Rhett. He had been raising his sons as a single parent for many years prior to 1997 when his relationship began with Susan Fountain. Ms. Fountain also has three children from a previous relationship. Mr. Fountain and Susan married in 1999 and have a son, Angus, who was born in April 2001. [7] There were five children living at the farmhouse in the summer of 2001; Ms. Fountain's three children from her previous relationship, Rhett and Angus. Rhett was then 17 years old. There had been some earlier conflicts between Rhett and Ms. Fountain. [8] The Panel found that Mr. Fountain regarded the home as his preserve and considered that the children who resided there were subject to his rules and direction. [9] At the time of the incident, Tyler Fountain was 20 years old. There was a history of conflicts between Tyler and his father, and between Tyler and Ms. Fountain. On earlier occasions, Tyler had been allowed to stay in the cabin away from the main farmhouse. He asked to stay at the farm in August 2001. As on earlier occasions, he was allowed to stay at the cabin, and understood that he would have to leave in the event of conflict between himself and his father or Ms. Fountain. [10] On August 22, 2001, the day of the incident, Mr. Fountain had gone into Burns Lake. Rhett and Tyler were in the kitchen of the farmhouse preparing some macaroni. Ms. Fountain overheard a conversation that she perceived was disrespectful. Holding young Angus, she confronted Rhett and Tyler, and told them to leave the house. Tyler refused to leave until he had finished preparing his food. A verbal confrontation ensued and escalated. [11] Mr. Fountain returned home to find Ms. Fountain, holding the baby, screaming at Tyler, who was holding a pot of boiled macaroni. Mr. Fountain perceived a real threat from Tyler to Ms. Fountain and Angus from the pot of boiling macaroni and got in between the two. He told them both to "shut-up". Tyler complied, but Ms. Fountain did not. Eventually Mr. Fountain told Ms. Fountain to take the baby and leave the kitchen, and she did so. [12] Mr. Fountain told Tyler to leave the house. Tyler agreed to leave, but told Mr. Fountain to back up so that he could put on his shoes. Tyler then backed up against the kitchen cabinet and put on his shoes. He left the house, taking the pot of macaroni with him. It was his evidence before the Panel that he feared an attack from Mr. Fountain. Rhett left the house with Tyler. [13] Tyler and Rhett walked down the driveway to the railroad ties beside the cabin where Tyler was staying. Tyler sat down on the ties to eat his food. Rhett was standing near him. Mr. Fountain came out of the farmhouse, walked down the driveway to where Tyler was sitting and demanded that Tyler get off his property. Tyler remained sitting and replied that he would leave once he was finished eating and that he would not come back. Mr. Fountain then grabbed the pot out of Tyler's hands and threw it some distance away saying that now Tyler was finished and could leave. [14] One of the boys then launched a physical attack on Mr. Fountain. The other joined in. Rhett held Mr. Fountain in a headlock, punching his head while Tyler held his lower body, repeatedly punching his back and ribs. The beating was serious. Mr. Fountain suffered significant pain and fear. He was injured. [15] At some point in the attack, Mr. Fountain threatened the boys with retaliation if he was able to get up. At some point Tyler offered to stop. Tyler said that if Mr. Fountain would let them go, they would leave immediately. Mr. Fountain responded that this was not good enough and that if he got up, he would "kick your ass". [16] By this point, Ms. Fountain arrived on the scene. She was able to pull Rhett off his father and tried to pull Tyler off. Mr. Fountain asked her to get an axe handle and then asked her to get a gun. [17] She went into the house and returned with a 30:06 rifle and a bullet. Mr. Fountain testified that when Ms. Fountain returned with the gun, Tyler said something to the effect of "for God's sake Dad, let's be done", or "Stop this," or "for God's sake Dad, I am your son, let me go". Mr. Fountain responded with words to the effect of "perhaps you don't know who was hitting who. I can't stop nothing." Mr. Fountain acknowledged that he might have said words to the effect of "you have done your best, there is nothing broken, when I get up, I am going to kick your ass." The boys ran down toward the bottom of the driveway. [18] The two boys ran, and then walked toward the edge of the property. At some point, just outside the gate to the farm, Tyler stopped and looked back to see that Mr. Fountain was following them. Tyler ripped off his shirt, which was torn. Mr. Fountain yelled at them to keep going. [19] Just outside the gate to the farm, some 150 to 200 yards away from Mr. and Ms. Fountain, the boys stopped and faced each other. They then resumed walking and heard a shot. They turned to look up the driveway. [20] Mr. Fountain fired the shot from the rifle after Ms. Fountain had handed him the rifle and bullet, and he had loaded and aimed. The shot was fired upwards at a 45 degree angle, and given the trajectory of the bullet, there was no actual danger to Tyler, Rhett or others. [21] The Panel found that at the point the shot was fired, the boys had no shirts. Rhett's belongings were at the house and they had limited options as to where they might go. The Panel found further that both Mr. Fountain and Ms. Fountain believed that the boys might return to the farm; however neither of the boys had given any physical or verbal sign of that at the material time. [22] Mr. Fountain testified, and the Panel accepted, that his intention in firing the weapon was to communicate a message to his sons to leave the property, likening it to scaring off rogue bears. [23] Tyler and Rhett walked to a nearby residence and Tyler called the RCMP at Burns Lake. As a result of the police investigation, Mr. Fountain was charged that on or about August 22, 2001, he did: 1. use a firearm in a careless manner pursuant to s. 86(1) of the Criminal Code of Canada; and 2. store a firearm in a careless manner pursuant to s. 86(1) of the Criminal Code of Canada. [24] On December 16, 2002, in Burns Lake Provincial Court, Her Honour Judge Kranz convicted Mr. Fountain of careless use of a firearm and acquitted him of unsafe storage of a firearm. [25] On April 14, 2003, after concluding a preliminary investigation, the College issued a citation (the "Citation") against Mr. Fountain alleging that while employed as a teacher with School District #91 (Nechako Lakes), Michael William Fountain, 1. on or about August 22, 2001, at or near Burns Lake, British Columbia, did engage in the careless and reckless use of a firearm by firing it over the heads of his sons, Tyler Fountain and Rhett Fountain. 2. on or about December 16, 2002, at or near Burns Lake, British Columbia, was convicted of a criminal offence under s. 81(1) of the Criminal Code, that being the careless use, carrying, handling, shipping, transportation of a firearm, prohibited weapon, restricted weapon, prohibited device, ammunition or prohibited ammunition. [26] Mr. Fountain appealed his conviction. On April 13, 2004, Madam Justice Koenigsberg overturned the conviction and acquitted Mr. Fountain of careless use of a firearm: R. v. Fountain (13 April 2004), Smithers 17634 (B.C.S.C.). Her reasons concluded: Thus, in my view, the learned trial judge in coming to the conclusion that the Crown had proved beyond a reasonable doubt both the actus rea and the mens rea of this offence, she failed to consider the totality of the circumstances. Most particularly, by putting as much reliance as she did on the fact that the reason for shooting into the air was that Mr. Fountain simply wished to finish the fight on his own terms, that is, not letting his sons go without any show of strength or bravado on his part she failed to consider that there was cogent evidence providing a reasonable different inference. The Crown submitted that the Crown had proved a marked departure from the actions of a reasonable person in all the circumstances by evidence of a combination of Mr. Fountain's anger at the time at his sons and the fact that he shot the rifle in order to continue the fight. And not in self-defence or in protection of his property. It is the Crown's position that no reasonable person in the circumstances of Mr. Fountain would have shot the gun for any reason. Thus, the only reason was to make a statement and scare the boys. However, given Mr. Fountain's uncontradicted evidence of a perception that the boys had paused in the road talking, and evidence corroborated by medical evidence that indeed he was "sore" and vulnerable, it is equally reasonable to infer that he shot the gun in the way he did for the reasons he said he did. Thus in my view, there is simply insufficient evidence to support some of the essential findings of fact made by the trial judge as I have indicated above. Secondly, in my view, in the totality of the circumstances I do not believe that the trial judge applied the appropriate test. That is, that the Crown must prove that there was a marked departure from a reasonable person in the circumstances in which the accused found himself beyond a reasonable doubt. As indicated above, the most telling piece of evidence not considered by the trial judge was the actual injury which had been sustained by Mr. Fountain, which injury and its obvious consequence, that is, significant pain and a sense of diminished capacity to defend himself or his wife more than raises a reasonable doubt as to whether Mr. Fountain's actions met the test of a marked departure from that of a reasonable person in all the circumstances. On a different view and one which is supported by objective evidence Mr. Fountain's act, while perhaps a matter of questionable judgment was in fact, reasonable in all the circumstances. Nowhere in her reasons for judgment does the trial judge remind herself that, the standard of proof which the Crown must meet in demonstrating a marked departure from the actions of a reasonable person in all the circumstances is beyond a reasonable doubt. While the failure of a trial judge to state the proper standard is not definitive as to whether an error has been made, in my view, there has been a failure to actually apply such a test in the face of the totality of the evidence. If such a standard had been applied to all the evidence before her the trial judge would have been led to acquit Mr. Fountain. In the result, the conviction cannot stand. [27] A hearing was conducted by the College with respect to the Citation on June 14-17, 2005. In view of Madam Justice Koenigsberg's decision, the College did not proceed with para. 2 of the Citation. [28] The Panel concluded that after Mr. Fountain defused the situation in the kitchen, matters escalated. The Panel concluded that this subsequent escalation was due in part to Mr. Fountain's conduct in confronting Tyler, refusing to let Tyler finish his food, and throwing away the pot of food. The Panel concluded, "his confrontation of Tyler at this point unnecessarily provoked the situation and contributed to the further events." [29] The Panel found that Mr. Fountain fired that shot after the beating had ended and the boys had withdrawn in circumstances in which there was no immediate threat and no need to fire the weapon in self-defence. [30] The Panel accepted Mr. Fountain's evidence that he used the gun to send a message. However, the Panel concluded that a reasonable teacher would not have communicated with a gun shot in the circumstances, found Count 1 of the Citation proven and concluded that Mr. Fountain was guilty of conduct unbecoming of a member of the College. Standard of Review [31] In determining the standard of review, the court is to apply the pragmatic and functional approach as set out in Pushpanathan v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), [1998] 1 S.C.R. 982 and Dr. Q v. College of Physicians and Surgeons of British Columbia, 2003 SCC 19, [2003] 1 S.C.R. 226 [Dr. Q]. There are four factors to be considered when applying this approach: 1. the presence or absence of a privative clause, or a statutory right of appeal; 2. the relative expertise of the administrative decision-maker vis-à-vis the court, with regard to the specific issue in question; 3. the purpose of the statute as a whole and of the provision in particular; and 4. the nature of the problem i.e. whether it is a question of fact, law, or mixed fact and law. [32] The standard to be applied in relation to an appeal of a decision of the College in relation to a finding of professional misconduct or conduct unbecoming a member is reasonableness; see Kempling v. British Columbia College of Teachers, 2005 BCCA 327, 255 D.L.R. (4th) 169 [Kempling No. 2], Stuart v. British Columbia College of Teachers, 2005 BCSC 645, 40 B.C.L.R. (4th) 360 [Stuart], Mitchell v. British Columbia College of Teachers, 2003 BCSC 1636, 17 Admin. L.R. (4th) 237 [Mitchell], except for questions of law, in which case, the standard is correctness; see Stuart at para. 41. Failure to Disclose the Case to be Met [33] Mr. Fountain submits that the Citation alleged that the misconduct at issue was the careless and reckless use of a firearm by firing it over the heads of his sons. The Panel concluded that he had used the firearm to send a message and concluded that the discharge of the weapon was neither necessary nor justified. Mr. Fountain submits that by finding him guilty of conduct unbecoming a member of the College by discharging a gun in circumstances where it was not necessary or justified as opposed to what was set out in the Citation, the College found him guilty of an offence with which he had not been charged, and thereby acted in excess of its jurisdiction. [34] The respondent submits that the conduct that was alleged to have amounted to professional misconduct was disclosed with sufficient precision in the Citation. Mr. Fountain understood the essence of the complaint was whether his firing of the gun on the day in question fell below the standard of behaviour expected of a teacher in the circumstances. The respondent submits, in addition, that Mr. Fountain was not found guilty of conduct that did not fall within the scope of the Citation. [35] An allegation of professional misconduct need not have the precision required in a criminal prosecution. However, the charge must give reasonable notice of the allegations that are made and must contain sufficient particulars so that the person charged can properly prepare his defence; see Re Golomb and College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario (1976), 68 D.L.R. (3d) 25 (Ont. Div. Ct.). The person accused of professional misconduct must know with reasonable certainty what conduct is alleged to have amounted to professional misconduct. [36] In this case, it was clear what conduct was being alleged to have amounted to misconduct. There is no suggestion that Mr. Fountain's ability to defend was in any way compromised. I conclude that Mr. Fountain was aware of the case he had to meet. [37] In my view, nothing turns on the fact that the schedule of the Citation refers to careless and reckless use of a firearm and the Panel concluded that the use of the firearm was neither necessary nor justified. The Panel was conducting a hearing to determine whether the conduct as referred to in the schedule of Citation constituted professional misconduct or conduct unbecoming a member. That was the essential inquiry. The conduct at issue in relation to that inquiry was identified. [38] Moreover, I agree with the submission of the respondent that unnecessary or unjustified conduct falls within the scope of conduct that is careless. Black's Law Dictionary 8th ed. (St. Paul, MN: Thomson/West, 2004) defines careless as: Careless. 1. The fact, condition, or instance of a person's either not having done what he or she ought to have done, or having done what he or she ought not to have done. 2. A person's general disposition not to do something that ought to be done. An action that was neither necessary nor justified would fall within the scope of this definition. [39] In the result, I find that the Citation did disclose the case that was to be met and that Mr. Fountain has not established any error in relation to this ground of appeal. Failure to Consider Relevant Circumstances [40] Mr. Fountain submits that the Panel concluded, mistakenly, that his acquittal was based on technical evidence with respect to how and when he aimed the weapon. The Panel's reasons with respect to this issue state: Thus, for instance, while the Provincial Court Judge found the Respondent angry at the time of the weapon's discharge, and the Appeal Court did not appear to disagree, the Panel might consider this as proven as it was not successfully rebutted. Also the acquittal was based on more technical evidence as to how and where the Respondent aimed the weapon (emphasis added). [41] I agree that in this respect the Panel was mistaken. However, the decision of the tribunal must be reviewed as a whole; see Law Society of New Brunswick v. Ryan, [2003] 1 S.C.R. 247 [Ryan]. A mistake that does not affect the decision as a whole is not sufficient to justify intervention. I am satisfied that the Panel's error, in this regard, was not material to the decision it reached. [42] Mr. Fountain submits further that the Panel ignored, or did not take sufficient cognisance, of the totality of the circumstances including his subjective belief and thus fell into error. Having reviewed the Panel's decision, I am satisfied that it considered the evidence regarding Mr. Fountain's state of mind, made findings of credibility, and found facts based on the totality of the evidence. [43] The jurisprudence makes it very clear that it is not the function of the court hearing the appeal to re-evaluate the evidence and substitute its opinion for that of the Panel. Rather, the question is whether the Panel's assessment of credibility and the application of the standard of proof was unreasonable, in the sense of not being supported by any reasons that can bear somewhat probing examination; see Dr. Q at para. 39. From that perspective, it is my conclusion that the Panel's consideration of the evidence and findings of fact was not unreasonable. Failure to Apply the Appropriate Standard of Proof [44] Mr. Fountain alleges that the Panel failed to apply the proper standard of proof. This is a question of law and the appropriate standard of review is correctness; see Stuart. [45] The authorities establish that the appropriate standard of proof is less than the criminal standard of beyond a reasonable doubt, but higher than the balance of probabilities. "The case against a professional person on a disciplinary hearing must be proved by a fair and reasonable preponderance of credible evidence"; see Jory v. College of Physicians and Surgeons of British Columbia, [1985] B.C.J. No. 320 at para. 14 (B.C.S.C.) (QL). This is the standard of proof specifically identified and acknowledged by the Panel. Thus, in this regard, no error has been identified. Failure to Address the Standard Against which the Conduct is to be Measured [46] Mr. Fountain submits that in finding him guilty of conduct unbecoming by firing a gun the Panel did not address or apply the standard against which the conduct should be measured - the standard of the reasonable teacher. This is a question of law for which the appropriate standard of review is correctness. However, the Panel did specifically identify and acknowledge that the standard to be applied was that of the reasonable teacher. Accordingly, the Panel did not fall into error in this respect. Application of the Standard to this Case [47] Mr. Fountain contends that the Panel erred in concluding that based upon the facts it had found, the conduct constituted conduct unbecoming a member. Mr. Fountain has submitted that this amounts to a question of jurisdiction or law and that the standard of review is correctness. However, in my view, the authorities indicate that the standard of review with respect to the question of whether the conduct was unbecoming a member is reasonableness; see Kempling No. 2 at para. 24, and Mitchell. [48] Mr. Fountain submitted that in order to constitute conduct unbecoming, the conduct must be shown to have an adverse effect on the school system to which the teacher owed a duty of responsibility, or upon the teacher's ability to carry out his professional obligations. In other words, there must be evidence before the Panel to support such a conclusion. He cited the following in support of this proposition: 1. Dickinson, G.M., "Out of School Misconduct Merits Teachers' Termination" (1996-1998) 8 Educ. & L.J. 239; 2. La Forest, Hon. Mr. Justice J.V., "Off Duty Conduct and the Fiduciary Obligations of Teachers," (1996-1998) Educ. & L.J. 119; 3. Eggertson v. A.T.A. (2002), 2002 Carswell Alta 334 (C.A.); 4. Re Board of School Trustees of School District No. 34 (Abbotsford) and Shewan, 26 D.L.R. (4th) 54 at pp. 3, 8-11, 14-18, 20-21 (B.C.S.C.); 5. Shewan v. Abbotsford School District No. 34, [1987] B.C.J. No. 2495 (B.C.C.A.) at p. 4; and 6. Kempling v. British Columbia College of Teachers, 2004 BCSC 133 [Kempling No. 1]. [49] Mr. Fountain then submitted that the Panel failed to address how the impugned conduct had any effect on the school system or any effect upon the teacher's ability to carry out his professional obligations. [50] Dealing with the later submission first, the respondent submits that the Panel did address the risk of harm to the integrity of the school system, albeit briefly. The respondent submits further that evidence of harm is not required. The respondent submits that the cases listed above reflect a general principal that harm to the education system in the form of a loss of the community's confidence in the school system flows from the conduct itself and may be reasonably inferred from that conduct. It is enough if the conduct creates a risk of harm to the school system in the form of a loss of public confidence in teachers and the system. Direct evidence of harm to an individual or to the school system is not a prerequisite to a finding of misconduct. [51] The conduct giving rise to the Citation occurred when Mr. Fountain was not performing his duties as a teacher. It is well settled that such off-duty conduct can give rise to discipline when the conduct has a negative impact on the teacher's ability to carry out his obligations as a teacher or where the conduct has a negative impact on the school system, for example, where the activities conflict with core values of the education system; see Ross v. New Brunswick School District No. 15, [1996] 1 S.C.R. 825 [Ross] at para. 44. In this case, the appellant does not dispute that off-duty conduct can give rise to discipline, but submits that this is so only where there is direct evidence of harm. [52] This issue has received considerable attention in the jurisprudence. In Fraser v. Public Service Staff Relations Board, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 455 [Fraser], a public servant had been discharged from his employment for public criticism of the government. One issue on appeal was whether the Adjudicator had erred in law by inferring, without any direct evidence, that the appellant's effectiveness as a public servant was impaired. Chief Justice Dickson, speaking for the court, noted that there are two senses of impairment that are relevant. The first, direct impairment is impairment in the effective performance of the specific job. With respect to direct impairment, the court concluded that the general rule is that direct evidence is required, but that this rule is not absolute. The second type of impairment is impairment in a wider sense. The court concluded that, in appropriate circumstances, an inference of this wider impairment could be drawn without direct evidence. At para. 45-48 of the judgment, the court said: Mr. Fraser further contends the Adjudicator erred in law by finding that his effectiveness as a public servant was impaired by his public statements, without any evidence to that effect. It is true the Adjudicator found Mr. Fraser's effectiveness as a public servant was impaired. It is also true there was no direct evidence to this effect before the Adjudicator. There was not, for example, testimony from so-called "clients" of Revenue Canada (i.e., persons subject to a tax audit) establishing that in their eyes Mr. Fraser's conduct placed his impartiality and judiciousness in doubt. In spite of this, the Adjudicator concluded that Mr. Fraser's activities were job-related in that they led to "impairment" of his ability to do his job properly. Indeed he found impairment in two senses: first, impairment to perform effectively the specific job because of the inferred effect on clients; secondly, and in a wider sense, impairment to be a public servant because of the special and important characteristics of that occupation. I do not think the Adjudicator erred on either count. As to impairment to perform the specific job, I think the general rule should be that direct evidence of impairment is required. However, this rule is not absolute. When, as here, the nature of the public servant's occupation is both important and sensitive and when, as here, the substance, form and context of the public servant's criticism is extreme, then an inference of impairment can be drawn. In this case the inference drawn by the Adjudicator, namely that Mr. Fraser's conduct could or would give rise to public concern, unease and distrust of his ability to perform his employment duties, was not an unreasonable one for him to take. Turning to impairment in the wider sense, I am of opinion that direct evidence is not necessarily required. The traditions and contemporary standards of the public service can be matters of direct evidence. But they can also be matters of study, of written and oral argument, of general knowledge on the part of experienced public sector adjudicators, and ultimately, of reasonable inference by those adjudicators. It is open to an adjudicator to infer impairment on the whole of the evidence if there is evidence of a pattern of behaviour which an adjudicator could reasonably conclude would impair the usefulness of the public servant. [53] The Supreme Court of Canada returned to this issue in Ross. In that case, a teacher had published and disseminated his racist and anti-Semitic views outside of the classroom. The Board of Inquiry had concluded that the respondent's off-duty conduct constituted discrimination which poisoned the educational environment at the school and created an environment in which Jewish students were forced to confront racist sentiment. One issue at the appeal was the submission of the respondent that while there was direct evidence of anti-Semitic incidents in the School district, there was no evidence linking his writings with these incidents and hence no proof of harm. [54] Mr. Justice La Forest, speaking for the Court, cited Fraser with approval on the issue of permissible inferences of harm, stating at para. 46-47: The next question is whether a finding of discrimination may be supported by an inference on the basis of what is reasonable to anticipate as an effect of the off-duty conduct. In Fraser v. Public Service Staff Relations Board, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 455, a public servant was discharged for publicly criticizing the government. Dickson C.J. observed two forms of impairment: impairment to perform the specific job and impairment in a wider sense. With respect to impairment of the first kind, the general rule, he stated, should be that direct evidence of impairment is required. He qualified this rule, however, as not absolute and stated that when the nature of the occupation is important and sensitive, and when the substance, form and context of the employee's comments are extreme, an inference of impairment may be sufficient. In that case, Dickson C.J. accepted the finding of the adjudicator that the public servant's off-duty conduct "could or would give rise to public concern, unease and distrust of his ability to perform his employment duties" (pp. 472-73). Similarly in this case, the Board found that the respondent's off-duty comments impaired his ability to fulfil his teaching position. The teaching occupation is uniquely important. This, combined with the substance of the respondent's writings and statements and the highly public media through which they were disseminated, i.e. television and published works, supports the conclusion that this finding of the Board is correct. [55] With respect to whether an inference could be drawn that there was impairment on the broader scale, La Forest J. concluded at para. 49: Pursuant to a television interview given by the respondent in 1989, the School Board itself characterized the effect produced by the respondent's conduct in this manner: ... the climate created by this aggressive approach creates hostility that permeates and interferes with the desired tolerance required by the school system to show respect for the rights of all students and their families to practice their religious faith. As to whether there is impairment on a broader scale, I conclude on the authority of Fraser, supra, that a reasonable inference is sufficient in this case to support a finding that the continued employment of the respondent impaired the educational environment generally in creating a "poisoned" environment characterized by a lack of equality and tolerance. The respondent's off-duty conduct impaired his ability to be impartial and impacted upon the educational environment in which he taught. [56] Kempling No. 1 concerned a finding by the College that Mr. Kempling committed professional misconduct or conduct unbecoming a member by virtue of publications in which he expressed views concerning homosexuality that were found to be discriminatory and derogatory. There was no evidence of a "poisoned" school environment or specific complaints against the appellant. Mr. Justice Holmes noted at para. 39 that non-discrimination is a core value of the education system, and therefore, a finding that writings were of a discriminatory and derogatory nature can properly form part of the basis of a determination of conduct unbecoming. [57] Homes J. concluded that inferences of harm could be drawn from the conduct in an appropriate case and that the inferences drawn by the Panel were reasonable, stating at para. 42: The harm visited on the public school system by the appellant's published writings is of two types: harm per se, and harm that could be inferred as the reasonable and probable consequences of that conduct [Ross, supra, at 859-60]. In my view, the appellant's published writings were harmful to the public school system per se, not only because of their discriminatory content, but also because he explicitly linked that content to his position as a teacher and counsellor. He continued at para. 46 to 50: By publicly linking his private, discriminatory views of homosexuality with his status and professional judgment as a teacher and secondary school counsellor, the appellant called into question his own preparedness to be impartial in the fulfilment of his professional and legal obligations to all students, as well as the impartiality of the school system. That in itself is a harmful impact on the school system as a non-discriminatory entity. As for harms that could be inferred as reasonable and probable consequences of the appellant's off-duty conduct, these were, in the Hearing Panel's reasons: a loss of public confidence in the appellant as a teacher and in the public school system, a loss of respect by the students for the teacher involved, and other teachers generally, and controversy within the school and the community which disrupts the proper carrying on of the education system. It was reasonable for the Panel to draw these inferences. From the appellant's published writings and his publicly linking them to his teaching and school counselling position, a negative inference could reasonably be drawn as to the appellant's ability to be impartial as a teacher. It would be reasonable to expect that student and public confidence in the appellant and the public school system would be undermined. It would also be reasonable to anticipate that homosexual students would generally be reluctant to approach him for guidance counselling, which would impair his ability to fully carry out his professional duties in fact. As well, there was some evidence of controversy in the community stemming from the appellant's published writings, such as letters to the local newspaper branding his views as intolerant and discriminatory, and letters from the Presidents of the Quesnel School Counsellors' Association and Quesnel District Teachers' Association disassociating themselves from the appellant's views. The harm, whether per se or inferred, to the school system, the teaching profession, and student and public confidence in them, resulting from the appellant's writings published off-duty warrant a finding that his conduct was unbecoming a BCCT member. [58] An appeal was taken [Kempling No. 2]. Mr. Kempling submitted on appeal that the College did not have the right to regulate his off-duty conduct in the absence of direct evidence of harm to the school system. Mr Justice Lowry, speaking for the court, concluded that it was not necessary to determine whether an inference of harm was sufficient to sustain a finding of conduct unbecoming because there was direct evidence that Mr. Kempling's writing had caused harm. This evidence was that Mr. Kempling had stated in his writings his belief that homosexuality is immoral, perverse and perhaps the product of mental illness. Moreover, he made it clear that these views will inform his actions as a teacher. The Court concluded at para. 45 that by these statements Mr. Kempling demonstrated that he was committed to fulfilling his public and professional responsibilities in an intolerant and discriminatory manner. Hence, proof that he had actually discriminated against a particular student, for example, was not required to prove that the system suffered harm. His statements damaged the integrity of the school system by undermining the core value of non-discrimination. [59] In summary, the case law establishes that in appropriate circumstances it is permissible to draw an inference of direct impairment or of impairment in the wider sense in the absence of direct evidence. Relevant factors to be considered include: (a) the nature of the conduct at issue; (b) the nature of the position; (c) whether there is evidence of a pattern of conduct; (d) evidence of controversy surrounding the conduct; (e) evidence that the private conduct has been made public; and (f) evidence that the private conduct has been linked by the member to the professional status of the member. [60] However, the case law also establishes that not all off-duty conduct will serve as a basis for discipline. In Shewan v. Abbotsford School District No. 34 (1987), 21 B.C.L.R. 2d 93 (C.A.) [Shewan], the Court of Appeal considered a suspension of two teachers for misconduct pursuant to the provisions of the School Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c. 375. The teachers were husband and wife. The conduct at issue was that Mr. Shewan had taken a nude photograph of Ms. Shewan that was then published in Gallery magazine. At trial, (1986), 26 D.L.R. (4th) 54 (B.C.S.C.), Mr. Justice Bouck had concluded that the key question is "whether the act of misconduct affects the teacher in his or her educational capacity". If it does not, then it is not an offence under the School Act. [61] The Court of Appeal concurred: The question in this case is not whether the photograph is obscene, but whether the publication of such a photograph of a teacher in such a magazine will have an adverse effect upon the educational system to which the teacher owes a duty to act responsibly. (Shewan at 97) [62] In Ross, Justice La Forest described the type of off-duty conduct that will give rise to discipline in the following way at para. 45: It is on the basis of the position of trust and influence that we hold the teacher to high standards both on and off duty, and it is an erosion of these standards that may lead to a loss in the community of confidence in the public school system. I do not wish to be understood as advocating an approach that subjects the entire lives of teachers to inordinate scrutiny on the basis of more onerous moral standards of behaviour. This could lead to a substantial invasion of the privacy rights and fundamental freedoms of teachers. However, where a "poisoned" environment within the school system is traceable to the off-duty conduct of a teacher that is likely to produce a corresponding loss of confidence in the teacher and the system as a whole, then the off-duty conduct of the teacher is relevant. [63] Justice La Forest returned to this theme in an article entitled "Off Duty Conduct and the Fiduciary Obligations of Teachers" (1996-1998), 8 Educ. & L.J. 119 in which he stated the following at 136-137 (footnotes omitted): While the conduct of Mr. Bhadauria was obviously extreme, an examination of the case law from the lower courts reveals that the range of outside conduct that has been subjected to discipline is quite broad, running from seemingly less serious conduct such as participating in a topless photo contest, to more serious behaviour such as engaging in sexual relations with students and involvement in criminal activity such as indecent exposure, theft, possession of stolen goods and fraud. As I see it, the reason these activities were held to constitute cause for discipline is not because they fell below some subjectivity defined threshold of community morality. Rather, it was because such conduct was perceived to have the potential to destroy the trust and confidence the community places in teachers because of their unique role. The following passage from the British Columbia Court of Appeal's decision in Shewan is apposite. The reason why off-the-job conduct may amount to misconduct is that a teacher holds a position of trust, confidence and responsibility. If he or she acts in an improper way, on or off the job, there may be a loss of public confidence in the teacher and in the public school system, a loss of respect by students for the teacher involved, and other teachers generally, and there may be controversy within the school and within the community which disrupts the proper carrying on of the educational system. Certain kinds of outside activity, in other words, can result in demonstrable harm to the educational system. In her dissenting reasons in Cowichan, McLachlin J.A., as she then was, identified the various harms that can accrue from the retention of a teacher who has engaged in off-duty misconduct, including the risk that the misconduct may recur with resultant injury to students, the danger that students may be influenced by inappropriate role models, diminution of teaching effectiveness caused by loss of respect from students and the community and the public's loss of confidence in the educational system. It is these specific harms, and not the violation of a state-imposed moral code, that the prohibition of off-duty misconduct seeks to redress. [64] In Trinity Western University v. British Columbia College of Teachers, [2001] 1 S.C.R. 772 the Supreme Court of Canada returned to this theme. After noting that holding a discriminatory belief will not be a cause for discipline, Justices Iacobucci and Bastarache, writing for the majority, stated at para. 37: Acting on those beliefs, however, is a very different matter. If a teacher in the public school system engages in discriminatory conduct, that teacher can be subject to disciplinary proceedings before the BCCT. Discriminatory conduct by a public school teacher when on duty should always be subject to disciplinary proceedings. This Court has held, however, that greater tolerance must be shown with respect to off-duty conduct. Yet disciplinary measures can still be taken when discriminatory off-duty conduct poisons the school environment. [65] In summary, the framework for the analysis of off-duty conduct that arises from the case law is: (a) some, but not all, off-duty conduct can give rise to discipline for professional misconduct or conduct unbecoming; (b) in considering whether the particular conduct at issue is such as to give rise to discipline, the Panel should consider whether the conduct evidences direct impairment of the ability to function in the professional capacity or impairment in the wider sense as described in the case law; and (c) direct evidence of impairment is not always required. In an appropriate case, impairment can be inferred. In the absence of direct evidence of impairment, the Panel will need to consider whether it is appropriate to draw on inference of impairment in the circumstances. [66] The next issue is the analysis of the reasons of the Panel in light of the standard of reasonableness as described in Ryan. Justice Iacobucci noted at para. 50 of Ryan that "when deciding whether an administrative action was unreasonable, a court should not at any point ask itself what the correct decision would have been." He then set out the approach to be adopted as follows at paras. 54-56: How will a reviewing court know whether a decision is reasonable given that it may not first inquire into its correctness? The answer is that a reviewing court must look to the reasons given by the tribunal. A decision will be unreasonable only if there is no line of analysis within the given reasons that could reasonably lead the tribunal from the evidence before it to the conclusion at which it arrived. If any of the reasons that are sufficient to support the conclusion are tenable in the sense that they can stand up to a somewhat probing examination, then the decision will not be unreasonable and a reviewing court must not interfere (see Southam, at para. 56). This means that a decision may satisfy the reasonableness standard if it is supported by a tenable explanation even if this explanation is not one that the reviewing court finds compelling (see Southam, at para. 79). This does not mean that every element of the reasoning given must independently pass a test for reasonableness. The question is rather whether the reasons, taken as a whole, are tenable as support for the decision. At all times, a court applying a standard of reasonableness must assess the basic adequacy of a reasoned decision remembering that the issue under review does not compel one specific result. Moreover, a reviewing court should not seize on one or more mistakes or elements of the decision which do not affect the decision as a whole. [67] In Mitchell, Madam Justice Humphries described the approach as follows at para. 31: This case, and the others cited by the respondent that have followed that approach, pre-date Ryan. To put it in the terms of that case, "after a somewhat probing examination," is the decision reasonable; is the explanation for the decision tenable or basically adequate? While the applicable standard of review is not one of correctness, and it does not matter if I find the reasons compelling, a decision will be unreasonable if there is no line of analysis within the given reasons that could reasonably lead the tribunal from the evidence before it to the conclusion at which it arrived. [68] The Panel's reasons first identify the correct standard of proof. The Panel then addresses the standard of conduct, stating that: As noted in Shewan, supra at page 3, the standard is not that of the reasonable man but, for a teacher, is higher. This of course includes off the job conduct. The Panel then states: The question for this Panel is whether, in discharging the weapon in the circumstances as demonstrated by the evidence, the respondent breached that standard so as to be guilty of conduct unbecoming a member. In resolving that issue the Panel has considered all of the evidence presented and the arguments of Counsel offered and, particularly, have considered the events preceding the discharge which serve as the context for what followed. [69] The Panel concluded that the discharge of the weapon, in the circumstances, was neither necessary or justified, and that the conduct fell below the standard of the reasonable teacher. The reasons conclude: But the boys were not wildlife and there was no need to send a message that way, if at all - by firing the gun at that time. In essence, the Panel finds that the Respondent was angry, was getting the last word, and was showing his sons that he indeed was master of his property and that his orders were to be followed. The discharge of the weapon was not done from fear of immediate threat or from a need of self-defence but from the Respondent's need to show his sons who was in charge. In the view of the Panel, the discharge of the weapon as demonstrated by the evidence was neither necessary nor justified. A reasonable teacher cannot be expected, in the circumstances of this case, to have communicated with a gun shot. The confidence the public interest invests in members of the teaching profession requires a commensurate standard of conduct of those members, as noted in Shewan, supra, and the Respondent breached that standard wit his action in firing the gun. Accordingly, the Panel finds that Count 1 of the Citation is proven and that the Respondent, Michael William Fountain, is guilty of conduct unbecoming a member of the College. [70] It is my conclusion having reviewed the reasons of the Panel that, in the words of Humphries J., there is no line of analysis within them that reasonably leads from the evidence before it to the conclusion arrived at. There is nothing in the reasons to suggest that the Panel took what is, in my view, the necessary next step and considered whether this off-duty conduct that fell below the standard was such as to constitute impairment of Mr. Fountain's ability to fulfill his responsibilities as a teacher, or whether the conduct would harm the education system. [71] The Panel appears to have overlooked the fact that not all off duty conduct, even if it falls below the standard of the reasonable teacher, serves as a basis for discipline. The reasons do not address what it is about this conduct in the circumstances that leads the Panel to the conclusion that discipline is warranted. The reasons do not disclose, for example, if the Panel concluded that the conduct was such as to impair Mr. Fountain's ability to function as a teacher (direct impairment), or whether it constituted impairment in the wider sense described in the case law. As these questions were not addressed, it follows that no reasons for reaching the conclusion were provided. The reasons do not disclose what inferences the Panel was drawing. [72] While I agree with the respondent that direct evidence of harm is not required, it was in my view necessary for the Panel to address these issues in order to arrive at the conclusion that the conduct constituted conduct unbecoming a member. The Panel had to address the question of whether this particular off-duty conduct was such as to attract disciplinary consequences and the basis for this conclusion, the Panel stopped short, and accordingly, its verdict is unreasonable. [73] In the circumstances, I have concluded, not without some reluctance, given the length of time this matter has already been outstanding, that the appropriate course is to refer the matter back to the College for reconsideration in light of these reasons.
