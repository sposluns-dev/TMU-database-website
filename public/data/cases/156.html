Andrews Tribunal Counsel for the persons who are the subject of the appeal Aleksei Grachev Conseil des personnes en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX (the Principal Appellant), and his wife XXXX XXXX XXXX (the female Appellant) claim to be citizens of China. They appeal a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) denying their claims for refugee protection. DETERMINATION [2] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) confirms the decision of the RPD and dismisses the appeal. BACKGROUND [3] The Appellants allege that they are at risk in China based on their religious beliefs. They are Pentecostal Christians. On XXXX XXXX, 2017, their house church was interrupted by Public Security Bureau (PSB) officers and they were taken to the police station for conducting a cult gathering. The Appellants were detained and tortured for approximately thirty days. Upon release they were threatened that if found at a similar gathering in the future they would be put in jail. Fearful of what would happen to them and given the Principal Appellant's mother's past experience of persecution by the PSB for her religious beliefs that led to her death, they decided to leave China. They contacted a snakehead to assist them and applied for refugee protection upon arrival in Canada. [4] The Appellants' application for refugee protection was heard on XXXX XXXX, 2017 and XXXX XXXX, 2018. In a decision dated February 13, 2018, the RPD rejected the Appellants claims finding that core allegations of their claim were not credible. [5] On appeal the Appellant argues that the RPD erred in its credibility findings. ROLE OF THE RAD [6] The standard of review is correctness as established in Huruglica1, where the Federal Court gives guidance in regard to the role of the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD). The RAD will defer to findings of the RPD where the RPD had a meaningful advantage in assessing the credibility of oral testimony. ANALYSIS [7] The determinative issue on appeal is credibility. Evidence submitted on Appeal [8] The Principal Appellant is not relying on any new evidence referred to in subsection 110(4) of the IRPA and he does not request an oral hearing under s.110 (6) of the IRPA. Findings related to credibility Practice of Christianity in China [9] The RAD has considered the Appellants arguments related to whether they would be able to practice their faith in China and whether the RPD accounted for the objective evidence about problems for Christian adherents. The RAD finds that the RPD did not selectively engage with the National Documentation Package (NDP), which will be discussed later in this decision. However, the RAD has conducted its own independent analysis of the country documents and finds that in addition to being applicable to the forward-looking analysis or sur place claim, the documents also do not support that incidents such as the ones alleged were occurring in Fujian in 2007. [10] The RAD has looked at the country condition evidence and considered the Appellant's circumstances. They are Pentecostal Christians, which falls under the broader category of Protestant churches. [11] Although designated as a country of particular concern by the United States (US) Commission on International Religious Freedoms, documents specify that out of the country's approximately 1.4 billion persons, the State Administration for Religious Affairs (SARA) estimates that there are more than 200 million religious' followers. [12] Many experts, however, believe that official estimates underestimate the total number of religious adherents. The US government estimates that there are 657 million religious' believers in the country, including 250 million Buddhists, 70 million Christians, 25 million Muslims, 301 million observers of folk religions, and 10 million observers of other faiths, including Taoism (July 2010 estimate). According to 2014 data from the Jewish Virtual Library, the country's Jewish population is 2,500 people.2 [13] While difficult to quantify, the various sources within the NDP specify that the number of Protestant adherents is anywhere between 23 to 100 million persons.3 The estimates are so divergent because of the fact that there are official and unofficial Protestant churches in China. [14] Religious groups are required by law to register with the government and only those affiliated with one of the five state-sanctioned "patriotic religious associations" (Buddhist, Taoist, Muslim, Catholic, and Protestant) are permitted on paper to hold services. For Protestants, they must register under the officially recognized umbrella organization for Protestant churches known as the Three-Self Patriotic Movement (TSPM) and register with the previously mentioned SARA. [15] Registered religious organizations are allowed to possess property, publish approved materials, train staff, and collect donations. According to regulations, religious organizations must submit information about the organization's historical background, doctrines, key publications, minimum funding requirements, and a government sponsor. Due to the difficulty of fulfilling these requirements, many religious organizations either remain unregistered or register as commercial enterprises.4 [16] As such, the number of registered churches is a known figure (between 23 and 29 million persons). However, the number of unregistered or underground house churches is unknown and the NDP specifies that as many, if not more Protestants choose to worship at unregistered churches rather than official churches.5 [17] The RAD notes that the documentary evidence concerning the treatment of Christians is mixed. Christianity is growing rapidly6 in China and persons move freely between State-registered churches and the unregistered churches7. In fact, according to one source, "no great theological differences exist between registered churches and unregistered churches and many Chinese Christians attend both."8 The differences are said to be primarily political. [18] Documents also indicate that official and unofficial Christian groups alike operate within a grey or uncertain area. According to the United Kingdom (UK) home office: The Chinese constitution guarantees freedom of religion for 'normal religious activities' but does not define 'normal'. The Chinese authorities seek to control all aspects of religious worship. Restrictions on religious freedoms and activities are widespread, but there are also widespread regional variations in severity. [19] There is an indication that in many parts of the country cooperative or at least tolerant relations developed between local officials and churches, both registered and unregistered".9 Hence, many groups meet quietly with tacit approval of local authorities and the treatment received is dependent on a number of factors including the region in which they operate, the size of their church and whether their activities are seen to promote social or economic development interests.10 [The] tens of thousands of underground congregations (both Catholic and Protestant but mostly Protestant), while technically illegal, are often tolerated by local officials and sometimes even allowed to operate openly as long as they are not explicitly 'political'.11 According to the Australian Refugee Tribunal: [U]nregistered groups should not be equated with groups which have been declared 'evil cults.' While, strictly speaking, both groups are illegal (as they operate outside the registration process), in practice unregistered religious groups are sometimes treated with a certain level of tolerance, while individuals belonging to 'cults' are ruthlessly pursued.12 [20] The recognized groups that are banned and/or deemed to be "evil cults" by the government are as follows: * Falun Gong * the Guanyin Method religious group (Guanyin Famen or the Way of the Goddess of Mercy), * Zhong Gong (a qigong exercise discipline) * And Christian groups: Shouters, Eastern Lightning, Society of Disciples (Mentu Hui), Full Scope Church (Quan Fanwei Jiaohui), Spirit Sect, New Testament Church, Three Grades of Servants (San Ban Puren), Association of Disciples, Lord God religious group, Established King Church, the Family Federation for World Peace and Unification (Unification Church), Family of Love, and South China Church.13 [21] The RAD notes that Pentecostal Christians are not included on the above list of banned groups, which appear to be the most sought after and whose members have a higher likelihood of being detained, arrested and harassed. [22] The UK Home office states that in general the treatment faced by Christians in China, including those from unregistered churches, is unlikely to amount to persecution and the risk for Christians expressing and living their faith in China is very low. [Despite] a variety of official and social sanctions against it, the majority of Christians are able to worship and practice without facing serious difficulties. Millions of Chinese Christians manifest their beliefs openly, and senior government officials have praised religious communities' positive role in society, and urged approved religious groups to promote 'economic and social development' and 'socialist principles'.14 [23] The RAD finds that while many groups meet without interference, documents also discuss that there is uneven enforcement dependent on the region in question and some house churches are raided or evicted from their meeting locations. As such, there may be a risk of persecution for bishops and pastors (with a higher profile) or persons who worship at unregistered churches and conduct themselves in a way that garners public attention "to them or their political, social or cultural views." Those persons do face a risk of harassment, detention and fines15 and the documents provide various examples of Protestants who have been incarcerated and charged with a number of crimes. However, the RAD finds that while there are examples of authorities harassing congregants, it often does not rise to the level of persecution. [24] In addition, the panel notes that documents discuss a series of incidents that have escalated tensions between the government and Christian churches in recent years that began in March 2013. The authorities in Zhejiang Province launched a three-year campaign called 'Three Rectifications and One Demolition" which began to dismantle what they term as "illegal structures" and in practice focused mainly on church buildings.16 They began with the Sanjiang church in Wenzhou and numbers vary, but according to the UK Home office 400 churches were torn down or had crosses forcibly removed and /or demolished in 2014, while The US Department of State estimates it was over 2000 structures including 600 crosses which were destroyed or demolished by the end of 2014. [25] Most of these incidents occurred in Zhejiang province and both underground and state-sanctioned churches were impacted. In response, "[m]any Zhejiang pastors and congregants openly resisted the campaign, resulting in the detention, prosecution or conviction of several church leaders and activists."17 And, inevitably there was a spike in the numbers of Christians who were being arrested during the years that followed this campaign. [26] Freedom house references the fact that while not as systematic as in Zhejiang, church demolitions were also reported in Fujian, Henan, and Anhui Provinces.18 [27] A World Watch Monitor report of 22 September 2015, 'Analysis: What to make of China's demolition of crosses', noted: Some Christians fear that the Chinese central government is testing how Christians in Zhejiang respond to this pressure. They are concerned that this is an early stage of a new, nationwide wave of discrimination against Christians. Other Christians disagree. While it's true that a small number of "house churches" were closed down recently, most church leaders outside Zhejiang don't report any difference in government attitude towards them. Meetings and gatherings for training have continued as usual. There's no evidence that points to an increase in discrimination on a national level.19 [28] The RAD finds that in reviewing the documents, the majority of the incidents are specific to Zhejiang Province, which is not where the Appellants were living. While there is a note that some demolitions occurred in Fujian, the Appellants' home province, there is no indication of how many churches were impacted. It only alludes to the fact that structures were dismantled and there is no indication that this was widespread. The documents note that one perhaps unintended impact that official actions/the campaign in Zhejiang Province has had is that it has increased solidarity between China's Christian community (registered and unregistered) and between Catholic and Protestant groups, which is likely one reason why the removal campaign has not spread from Zhejiang to other provinces.20 [29] The RAD finds that the documentary evidence taken as a whole confirms that the government is generally tolerant of groups that meet in homes or small groups. This is supported by the sheer number of adherents and the pace at which Christianity is growing in China. However, the government clearly remains suspicious of religious groups with foreign ties, fast growing membership, or popular leadership. [30] The Appellants are from XXXX city, Fujian Province. As such, the RAD has looked at the NDP specifically in relation to incidents involving Christians in Fujian Province. Having reviewed the recent documentary evidence contained in the NDP, the RAD has found that there was little recent evidence of persecution involving house church Protestants in Fujian Province. [31] Documents suggest that Fujian has traditionally been fairly tolerant and liberal in its treatment of Christians.21 The Australian Migration Review Tribunal in its 2013 background paper on Protestants in China22 states that although the province contains relatively large numbers of Protestants, there are few reports of repression of house-church Christians in the province and goes on to quote Lambert, the author of China Christians Millions who describes Fujian as follows: Fujian has a thriving and rapidly growing Christian community. As a coastal province in the south east, it was one of first to be evangelised from the early 19th century. By 1949 there were about 10,000 Protestants. Official estimates of Protestant Christians in 2004 were 1,179,000 - a twelve-fold growth after fifty-five years of Communism...In 2000 the TSPM magazine Tianfeng [indicated]... Fuzhou, the capital, with its six surrounding rural counties and two smaller municipalities had at least 350,000 Protestants in 2002, meeting in 300 registered churches and 2,000 meeting points...In general, the official religious policy has been applied relatively liberally in Fujian, although there have been occasional crackdowns on house churches and underground Catholics. [32] The background paper shows that this finding is supported by a Canadian fact finding mission to the province in 2000 as well as by the executive secretary of the Hong Kong Christian Council in 2005. It cites a 2009 report which confirms there are a large number of independent house churches and the background paper also indicates that Fujian is rarely mentioned in reports on breaches of religious freedom by the US Department of State, the US Commission on International Religious Freedom, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch or the various Christian Non-Government Organizations that report on China. [33] The RAD notes that even within a more recent 2017 document from Freedom house titled The Battle for China's Spirit,23 a map which depicts religious persecution by province indicates that the degree of religious persecution recorded in Fujian province is low. [34] The RAD has looked at the most recent incidents against Protestants in Fujian cited in the NDP. * In October 2010, the authorities reportedly took away a worker and sealed three venues used for church gatherings related to a church in Lianjiang county in Fujian '.24 * In 2006 police closed unregistered places of worship in various provinces including Fujian, according to the US Department of State. The 2006 annual report of the China Aid Association also reports on the demolition of house churches in Jilin and Fujian. The report cites an incident in September 2006 in which a house church was destroyed in Pingtan County, Fujian.25 * As previously mentioned church demolitions were also reported in Fujian in 2014 related to the campaign26 [35] The only other incidents in the NDP relate to actions against two of the banned/ 'evil cult' groups; the arrest of members of the Church of Almighty God/ Eastern Lightning in December 2012 and in 2009 reports from two localities in Fujian province indicate that the Shouters church was a target for public security forces27. [36] The Appellants are from XXXX district, XXXX city, Fujian province. There are no incidents within the NDP reported in that area regarding Christians. The RAD has considered that the documentary evidence regarding Fujian province reveals no house arrests of house church members outside of those who were in banned groups. The incidents reported by reputable sources only discuss the closure of a few churches and the demolition of an undisclosed number of churches, which is not a widespread phenomenon. [37] The RAD has considered the documentary evidence regarding Fujian province and notes that the incidents cited in the NDP occurred in areas unrelated to the Appellants. The RAD also finds that incidents in the province are few, relative to the number of Christians in the country overall and in light of the number of Christians practicing in unregistered churches. [38] The RAD finds on a balance of probabilities, that if there were recent arrests or incidents of persecution of house church Christians in Fujian province, there would be some documentation of these arrests or incidents of persecution by reliable sources. However, there is not and the RAD finds that the Appellants' allegations are inconsistent with the documentary evidence, as the documents do not support that there is a serious possibility of persecution or risk of arrest for Christians practicing at house churches in XXXX city now or in 2017 when their incident allegedly occurred. Corroborative evidence [39] The RAD finds that the RPD did not err in making a negative inference based on the lack of supporting documentation. [40] The RPD found that given that the appellants had sought and provided other documents in support of their claim, it was reasonable to expect that they would provide documents to support their detention allegations and their failure to do so led the RPD to draw a negative inference regarding their credibility. [41] The Appellants allege that unless there are valid reasons to question an appellant's credibility, it is an error to require documentary evidence and they should not be disbelieved simply because they did not present any. [42] The RAD has reviewed the audio recording of the hearing. At the hearing, the female Appellant testified that she was not given any documents on release and she did not have any documents about her time in police detention. She testified that she had no idea of whether there were any documents that they tried to get from China as her husband did everything. When it came to the Principal Appellant's testimony, he was asked about whether he had any documents related to detention and he testified he did not bring them, but there was a receipt showing that during the thirty days in detention he had to pay for their own living expenses. He testified that he did not bring it because he was being smuggled out of the country, so he could not bring the receipt. He testified that when arrested, police accused him of joining an evil cult. [43] The RAD rejects the Principal Appellant's explanation and finds it is not reasonable that he did not present proof of their detention at XXXX police station, which was in their possession in China. This is something that he even discussed at the Port of Entry (POE) with the officer, despite the fact that he maintains there was a language barrier and interpretation problems when he arrived. At the POE, he indicated the same when questioned by the officer. When the officer asked whether he brought any evidence of his story he responded that during the time being detained in China, they made some payments of money to police because if they did not pay, they would not get hot water. The following exchange then occurred: Q: Do you have any proof of your story today about the Chinese police? Did you bring anything with you? A: No, not now, I can ask the family to mail it. Q: What will they mail to us? A: They can mail over the receipts and also the paper showing how many days I was eating. Q: Why didn't you bring it with you today? A: I don't know the reason for that. Whatever document you need, I can get it from my home. [44] The RAD finds that the Appellants were on notice that this was something that would assist their claim given the above conversation which occurred when they initiated their claim. Further, both the RPD rules and the instructions provided to claimants on their BOC specify that they should tender any and all documents in support of their claim. Moreover, they were represented by experienced Counsel in the preparation and presentation of their claims. And, as indicated by the RPD, they furnished other documents including their Hukou and Marriage Certificate in support of their claim knowing the importance of said documents. [45] While they suggest that they should not be disbelieved simply based on the absence of corroborative documents, the RAD finds that one can draw a negative inference from the lack of corroborative evidence where it is reasonable to expect such evidence. Their failure to supply any documentation to indicate that they were incarcerated where they state such documents are available in addition to the credibility concerns leads the RAD to find that on a balance of probabilities, they were not incarcerated as a result of house church attendance as alleged. Further, this finding was not made in isolation. The RPD also found that there was a lack of credibility based on the female appellant's contradictory statements about her fears. And, the RAD has found above that the objective documents do not support that such arrests are occurring at house churches in XXXX, Fujian. Uncontested findings [46] The RPD also made a number of further findings that were not challenged by the appellant as follows and the RAD will not be dealing with them as it has no reason to disturb these findings. 1) The circumstances of the principal appellant's mother's death do not pose a forward-facing risk to the appellants. 2) The female appellant's statements about risk are inconsistent with a subjective fear of persecution. Exit from China [47] The RPD found that given the documentary evidence as well as the Jurisprudential Guide concerning exit controls in China, that the appellants' explanation about how they left the country contradicted the country documents on the matter. The RPD preferred the objective documentary evidence and found on a balance of probabilities that border officials approved the claimants for foreign travel in the normal course. [48] The appellants argue that it was an error to find that it was implausible to depart from China using their own passports. They argue that implausibility findings should only be made in the clearest of cases as supported by case law, that the RPD mentioned the Jurisprudential Guide, but failed to provide an analysis of the situation, failed to explain what evidence it was expecting from the appellants and did not follow Federal Court jurisprudence on the matter. [49] The RAD finds that it is unnecessary to address the above argument given the other findings related to credibility are sufficient to dismiss the appeal. Standard of proof [50] The RAD finds that the RPD did not err in its use of the standard of proof or the legal test to be met. [51] The RPD found that the appellants do not face a serious possibility of persecution on a convention ground in China and that they did not establish on a balance of probabilities, that they would be personally subjected to a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a danger of torture in China. [52] The appellants argue that the RPD applied the wrong legal test in this matter with respect to its section 96 analysis and the test is "more than a mere possibility" rather than a serious possibility. [53] The standard of proof refers to the standard the panel will apply when assessing the evidence adduced for the purpose of making factual findings, which is the balance of probabilities. With respect to the test for Convention Refugee status the evidence must show only that there are "good grounds" for fearing persecution and the test, now known as the Adjei test, is whether there is a "reasonable" or "serious possibility" that the claimant would be persecuted if he or she returned to the country of origin.28 [54] Courts have used various terms to describe this test - "good grounds", "reasonable chance", and "reasonable" or even "serious" possibility. The RAD finds that more than a mere possibility and serious possibility are one and the same and it is clear that the RPD understood and used the correct legal test and standard of proof.29 Credibility summary [55] The RAD has considered that the documents do not support an arrest in XXXX in 2017; that the documentary evidence indicates that the appellant's church would not have been considered an evil cult which would have been dealt with harshly. The RAD has also considered the uncontested findings and found that that the arrest/detention never happened as reasonably expected corroborative documentation regarding the arrest was not provided. As such, the RAD finds that the appellants have not established on a balance of probabilities that they were arrested in 2017 for their participation in a Protestant unregistered church and the objective country documentation does not support a forward-looking fear based on their being Pentecostal while in China. Sur place analysis [56] As stated previously, the RAD has considered the Appellants arguments related to whether they would be able to practice their faith in China and whether the RPD accounted for the objective evidence about problems for Christian adherents. The RAD finds that the RPD did not selectively engage with the National Documentation Package (NDP). While it did not mention each reference, it did acknowledge that there are some problems for practicing Christians. The RPD based its findings on the preponderance of the evidence, and the RAD finds it arrived at the correct conclusion after its own independent assessment. [57] The RPD found that on a balance of probabilities, the Appellants are practicing Pentecostal Christians in Canada and that they would be able to practice their faith upon return to China. [58] The Appellants argue that the RPD came to the conclusion that there is no problem for practicing Christians despite evidence to the contrary within the NDP. They argue that the RPD offered no justification for choosing some documentary evidence over other evidence and offered no global analysis. [59] The RAD has considered that the Appellants have been attending the Living Stone Assembly in Canada. There is no evidence that it is connected to any church in China (a church with a similar name in China referenced within the NDP). The Appellants have also submitted documents30 from the aforementioned church in Canada to indicate that they have been attending services and have been baptized. As such, the RAD has considered whether there is a serious possibility that they would be persecuted should they choose to practice Christianity at any number of unregistered churches in China. [60] The RAD has looked at the documentary evidence as assessed in paragraphs 11-37 of this decision. In summary, the documentary evidence taken as a whole confirms that the government is generally tolerant of groups that meet in homes or small groups; that Fujian is one of the more liberal provinces when it comes to its treatment of house church Christians both registered and unregistered; the degree of religious persecution recorded in Fujian province is low and there are no noted arrests of house church members outside of those who were in banned groups. [61] The RAD finds that on a balance of probabilities, given the conditions in Fujian Province and the Appellants' personal circumstances, that the Appellants would be able to practice their religion, worshiping in the Christian congregation of their choice, if they were to return to Fujian Province in China, and that there is not a serious possibility that they would be persecuted for doing so. CONCLUSION [62] Based on all of the above, and on the RAD's own assessment of the evidence including the audio recording, the supporting documents, and the objective evidence, the RAD finds that the Appellants' allegations of being detained by the authorities for having participated in a house church in China are not credible and as Pentecostal Protestants, they can practice without fear of persecution. [63] The RAD finds the appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. [64] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD and dismisses the appeal. (signed) "T. Andrews" T. Andrews September 16, 2019 Date 1 M.C.I. v.
