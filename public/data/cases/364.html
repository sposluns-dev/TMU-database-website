CONCLUSIONS AND CONSEQUENCES................................................. 79 A. INTRODUCTION [1] This is a Judgment with respect to opposing applications under Rule 18A. The plaintiff asks for Judgment declaring that the defendant has breached certain contractual rights of the plaintiff by unfairly suspending him from coaching basketball. He asks that the said suspension be quashed as a result of the contractual breaches. [2] The defendant asks that the action be dismissed because it did comply with the contractual terms that existed between the parties, and provided a fair process and a fair result. [3] At all material times, the plaintiff was a teacher and high school basketball coach at G.P. Vanier Secondary School in Courtenay, British Columbia, ("Vanier School"). [4] At all material times the defendant, B.C. School Sports, ("BCSS"), was a private, self-governed, voluntary organization incorporated under the Society Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c.433. BCSS had no statutory public role, nor any formal affiliation with government or British Columbia school boards. BCSS was governed according to its Constitution and Bylaws, as well as several Operating Policies and Procedures and Competitive Rules and Regulations enacted by the BCSS membership. The membership consists of approximately 400 public and private junior and senior secondary schools ("Member Schools"), from across British Columbia. Vanier School was a Member School. [5] The defendant had limited financial resources and a volunteer Board of Directors with a paid administrative staff consisting of three persons. [6] The BCSS Competitive Rules and Regulations, (the "Rules"), included a Coach's Code of Conduct that Member Schools' coaches must follow ("Code of Conduct"). The Rules provided for a complaint procedure where a violation was alleged against a Member School coach. The process contemplated a hearing before a Discipline Panel (the "Panel"), and a right of appeal to an Appeal Panel. Representatives from the BCSS Management Committee made up the Panel, while representatives from the BCSS Disciplinary Appeal Committee made up the Appeal Panel. [7] Both parties agree that this lawsuit was properly commenced in contract by Writ of Summons, rather than as an application under the Judicial Review Procedure Act, R.S.B.C 1996, c.241, ( "JRPA"). This is because BCSS is a voluntary, self-created and self-governed association. It is not a statutory body. The JRPA is restricted to the latter. [8] Nevertheless, even though these proceedings are founded in contract, many principles of administrative law are applicable. [9] The terms of the contract in question are set out in a book entitled B.C. Schools Sports Handbook & Directory , (the "Handbook"). [10] One of the initial questions that arose was whether or not the plaintiff was a "member" of BCSS, and therefore whether or not he was bound as a party to the contract. [11] The categories of membership of BCSS, as noted in the Handbook, all include schools in British Columbia, not individuals, and not coaches. [12] Although coaches are not referred to in the Handbook as "members" of BCSS, the Handbook purports to bind coaches with its provisions relating to conduct. There are a number of sections that deal expressly with coaches, which are labelled as follows: 1. Coaches Code of Conduct (sic) 2. Community Coach Guidelines 3. Recruiting 4. Penalties for Violations of the Coach's Code of Conduct 5. Appealing Penalties - Coach's Code of Conduct Policies [13] In the plaintiff's affidavit he deposes that he has been teaching for twenty eight years and he has been involved in either coaching, or other supervisory capacities of student athletes, for almost as long. It is clear that he was aware that BCSS was the body which governed high school athletics. At no time in these proceedings was it argued that the Rules of BCSS relating to coaches do not apply to him. At no time was it seriously argued before me that the plaintiff is not bound by these Rules. [14] In James (Guardian ad litem of) v. Pacific Coast Amateur Hockey Assn., [1982] B.C.J. No. 266 (C.A.) (QL), an issue arose as to whether or not a minor hockey player could sue in contract based on a handbook similar to the Handbook in this case. Anderson, J.A. said at para. 16: In my view, James has a contract with the appellant Association and the right to sue upon that contract: Vlahovic v. Teamsters' Joint Council No. 36 et al (1979), 17 B.C.L.R. 277. There is a mutual agreement to pursue a purpose (minor league hockey play) and to abide by certain rules. The argument that only the member clubs have a right to sue the appellant fails. Either on the concept of agency (the member club contracts with appellant as agent for James), or on the basis of a unilateral offer by the appellant Association to permit players to play if they abide by certain rules, a contract exists. [15] Applying the same reasoning, I am satisfied that the plaintiff is bound by the terms of the contract that existed between the schools and BCSS, which terms are set out in the Handbook. [16] The plaintiff was found to have violated provisions in the Rules against recruiting. He was punished by way of a suspension from coaching any high school sports until the end of the 2004-2005 B.C. High School Boys Basketball season. Both counsel are agreed that I have no jurisdiction to review the merits of those findings. The defendant argues that, by virtue of a particular clause in the Handbook, I have no jurisdiction at all, not even a limited jurisdiction to review the process by which the decisions were arrived at. The plaintiff's view is that I have a limited supervisory jurisdiction, along traditional administrative law principles, and that on exercising that jurisdiction, I should conclude that the process was flawed in a number of ways, with the consequence that the plaintiff was denied natural justice and a fair process. [17] The relevant sections of the Handbook are as follows: Section 2 CODES OF CONDUCT Part 1) COACH'S CODE OF CONDUCT ... 2.1.9 Coaches shall not, either directly or indirectly, encourage a student to attend a particular school for the purpose of participating in interschool sport. Section 4 RECRUITING 4.1 Definition: Recruiting is defined as a representative of a school, either directly or through another party, encouraging a student to attend a school other than that in whose catchment area he or she resides, for the purpose of being involved in extracurricular sport. The school representative could be a teacher, administrator, school district employee, school employee, student, parent, alumnus or any person representing themselves as a school contact. 4.2 Statement on Recruiting: In support of the BCSS overriding purpose of the eligibility rules, which is to ensure fair and equitable competition for all students participating in BC SCHOOL SPORTS competition, the BCSS membership strongly condemns any attempt to recruit a student-athlete into a particular member school. 4.3 Indicators of Recruiting: There are many legitimate times that a school representative and/or coach of a school team will be in contact with students from another school. Therefore, indicators of recruiting have been developed to provide parameters of behaviour expectations for coaches and other school representative(s). For brevity, the term "coach" is used, but the policy applies equally to coaches and any other school representative(s). 4.3.1 A coach shall not initiate contact with any student who does not attend, or does not directly feed into the coach's school, in order to suggest, encourage or explain a transfer to a different school. ... 4.3.5 A coach shall not entice a student who is not currently enrolled at the coach's school, or whose present school does not directly feed into the coach's school, into transferring to the coach's school. Examples of some applicable situations are listed below, but are not limited to: ... ... 4.3.5.4 providing transportation or equipment or clothing/shoes or financial inducement in order to facilitate a non-enrolled student's involvement in out-of-session contests, leagues, camps, clinics or related programs. [18] Also contained in the Handbook are a number of Rules under the following heading: "Procedures for Dealing with Incidents Involving Recruiting, Multiple Athletic Associations or General Misconduct". It is not necessary for me to set out all of those procedures here. However, under that heading, there are the following sub-headings, each of which has a set of Rules under them: 8.4.2.1 Filing a Complaint 8.4.2.2 Disciplinary Hearing 8.4.2.3 Discretionary Penalties [19] There is also a section in the Handbook setting out procedures under the heading: "Appeal Process For Disciplinary Action Taken", followed by a number of sub-headings and Rules. THE DOMESTIC CLAUSE [21] Counsel agree that "domestic clause" is a term that describes a clause that purports to oust the Court's jurisdiction to review various aspects of relevant proceedings. [22] During these proceedings, the clause, ("Domestic Clause"), has been referred to variously as: 1. Domestic Clause 2. Privative Clause 3. Exclusionary Clause 4. Waiver Clause 5. Non-Justiciable Rights Clause. [23] In the Handbook, the Domestic Clause is set out at s.8.5.4.4. as follows: 8.5.4.4 BCSS Disciplinary Appeal Committee: for any appeal filed regarding decisions made under policy 8.4.2, the decision of the BCSS disciplinary appeals committee is final and binding on all parties, and no decision, order, direction or ruling of the committee shall be questioned or reviewed in any court, and no order shall be made or process entered or proceeding taken in any court whether by way of injunction, declaratory judgment, prohibition or otherwise to question, review, prohibit or restrain the committee or any of its proceedings. [24] The defendant argues that s.8.5.4.4 of the Handbook is a term of the contract, agreed upon the by parties, and binding on the coaches. Therefore, there is no jurisdiction whatsoever left for the Courts. [25] The plaintiff argues that the Domestic Clause effectively prohibits the Court from reviewing the merits of the proceedings, but that no such clause can have the effect of prohibiting the Court from exercising a limited supervisory jurisdiction, which it has traditionally reserved for itself. [26] The defendant argues that the Domestic Clause is effective in that: 1. It evinces an agreement between the parties that BCSS' disciplinary proceedings will not create legal rights, the breach of which would warrant legal remedies. 2. The general relationship between the parties is contractual. A part of that contract is a mutual agreement that specific aspects of their affairs, such as disciplinary proceedings, do not give rise to justiciable rights. It is an express choice made by those involved in BCSS activities. The defendant argues that the Court should respect that choice. 3. It shows the collective intention of the parties, including the plaintiff who participates in the affairs of BCSS, that the disciplinary proceedings such as those in the case at Bar, be a purely internal, domestic matter, and not be the subject of court proceedings. 4. The plaintiff is asking the Court to ignore the agreement between him and BCSS. The Court should not permit the plaintiff to plead a breach of contract on the part of the defendant, while himself ignoring and acting contrary to the very contract he relies on. 5. The plaintiff is bound by the restrictions in the Handbook, including the Domestic Clause restriction, in exchange for which he is granted the privilege of being allowed to coach a Member School team in BCSS regulated competition. He cannot pick and choose which parts of the Handbook he intends to be bound by. He is bound by all the terms and conditions of participation, including the Domestic Clause restriction on commencing legal proceedings. 6. If the plaintiff is not prepared to accept the terms and conditions of participating in BCSS activities, his remedy is to forego coaching a Member School team. 7. The Domestic Clause operates as a waiver by persons, such as the plaintiff, of their right to sue, over matters such as the subject matter of this action. 8. The Domestic Clause is effective, the defendant argues, not only to prohibit a court review of the merits, but also to prohibit a review of jurisdictional matters, including matters involving rules of natural justice or procedural fairness. The parties can agree to keep these matters out of the Courts, and they have done so. [27] The plaintiff argues that even if all of the defendant's arguments are correct, the Domestic Clause can never, and should never, have the effect of ousting all of the Court's jurisdiction, including what has historically been referred to as its limited supervisory jurisdiction. [28] I have been referred to a number of cases that discuss the Court's jurisdiction where there are domestic clauses. I shall review them in two general categories: 1. Cases involving the usual type of Domestic Clause which purports to prohibit a review on the merits, but does not purport to restrict the Court's limited supervisory jurisdiction; and 2. Cases involving a Domestic Clause which purports to oust all Court jurisdiction, including a review on the merits, but also including a review involving any limited supervisory jurisdiction. (1) The Usual Type of Domestic Clause [29] The modern jurisprudence in this area of the law is generally traced back to the 1919 English Court of Appeal case of Balfour v. Balfour, [1919] 2 K.B. 571, (C.A.) where Atkin L.J. notes the following at p.578: The defence to this action on the alleged contract is that the defendant, the husband entered into no contract with his wife, and for the determination of that it is necessary to remember that there are agreements between parties which do not result in contracts within the meaning of that term of our law. ... And at p.579: ...They are not sued upon, - not because the parties are reluctant to enforce their legal rights when the agreement is broken, but because the parties in the inception of the arrangement, never intended that they should be sued upon. Agreements such as these are outside the realm of contracts altogether. [30] The next case of importance to be decided was the English case of Rose and Frank Co. v. J.B. Crompton & Bros., Ltd., [1923] 2 K.B. 261 (C.A.), aff'd [1925] A.C. 445 (H.L.). [31] In Rose, Bankes L.J. held, inter alia, at p.283, as follows: The intention clearly expressed is that the arrangement set out in the document is only an honourable pledge, and that all legal consequences and remedies are excluded, from it. If this is the true construction of the clause, it must govern the entire arrangement, and there is consequently no room for the principle upon which the learned judge decided this part of the case... . Once it is established that the language of the clause is the bona fide expression of the intention of the parties, the matter is in my opinion, concluded and it becomes manifest that no action can be maintained upon the agreement ... . [32] In the 1954 case of Baker v. Jones, [1954] 2 All E.R. 553 (Q.B.) at p.558, the Domestic Clause stated the following: The decision of the central council in all cases, and under all circumstances, shall be final. The Court of Queen's Bench at p.558 ruled as follows: That contract is contained in, or to be implied from, the rules. The courts must consider such a contract as they would consider any other contract. Although parties to a contract may, in general, make any contract they like, there are certain limitations imposed by public policy, and one of those limitations may be that parties cannot, by contract, oust the ordinary courts from their jurisdiction. [33] In the 1957 case of Charleston et al v. MacGregor and Brother-Hood of Railrioad Trainmen (1957), 11 D.L.R. (2d) 78 (Alta. S.C.), the relevant clause states as follows at p.82: The Grand Lodge has exclusive jurisdiction over all subjects pertaining to the Brotherhood, and its enactments and decisions upon all questions are the supreme law of the Brotherhood. The Court, at pp.81-82, adopted with approval the following comments from Bankes L.J. in Rose, para.30, supra: There is, I think, no doubt that it is essential to the creation of a contact, using that word in its legal sense, that the parties to an agreement shall not only be ad idem as to the terms of their agreement, but that they shall have intended that it shall have legal consequences and be legally enforceable. [34] The most often quoted statement of the law in British Columbia is found in North Shore Independent School Society v. B.C. School Sports Society, [1999] B.C.J. No. 143 (S.C.) (QL), where the Court states at para. 36-37: The narrow scope for judicial review of the decisions of a domestic tribunal were noted by Dohm J. in Vancouver Hockey Club v. 8 Hockey Ventures Inc. (1987), 18 B.C.L.R. (2d) 372 (S.C.) at 375: The review by the Court of orders made by an unincorporated association such as the N.H.L. through its president and chief executive officer (a domestic tribunal as it were) is limited. The power in no way includes the right in the Court to substitute its decision for that of the domestic tribunal. The Court is not the Court of Appeal. Rather, its power is narrow and it may only interfere if the order was made without jurisdiction (or against the rules) or if it was made in bad faith or contrary to the rules of natural justice. In addition, the Courts will be reluctant to interfere with the decisions of a domestic tribunal where it is shown that internal remedies have not been exhausted. And there is even greater reluctance to interfere if the decision is based upon opinions regarding the standards of propriety and conduct appropriate for members of a particular association. ... These well-known principles provide the foundation of the Court's review. (My emphasis added) These cases show that the Courts are prepared to interfere with the decision of a domestic tribunal where it can be shown that the tribunal exceeded its jurisdiction or failed to comply with the rules of natural justice or otherwise acted in bad faith. What these cases also demonstrate is the reluctance of the Courts to intervene by substituting the Court's judgment for the judgment of the tribunal on a matter of substance within the tribunal's jurisdiction. And at para. 55: The Court's role in cases of this nature is to focus on process: that is, was the decision made without jurisdiction or in bad faith or contrary to the rules of natural justice? Matters of substance such as the proper interpretation of phrases such as "extraordinary circumstances" clearly within the jurisdiction of the domestic tribunals in this case are best left to those tribunals. [35] In North Shore the defendant was the same as in the case at bar. However, the Domestic Clause was different. The only reference in that case to a Domestic Clause is found in para. 15 of the Judgment: "The decision of the Jury of Appeal is final and binding on all parties ... ." The Domestic Clause in question did not play a part in the Court's reasoning or decision. In my view, the reasoning would have been the same even if there had been no such clause. [36] In the 1998 case of Peerless (Guardian ad Litem of) v. B.C. School Sports (1998), 157 D.L.R. (4th) 345 (B.C.C.A.), the British Columbia Court of Appeal commented on the Court's limited supervisory jurisdiction. The Domestic Clause simply stated that decisions are "final and binding on all parties". In a split decision, the majority noted at para. 23: ... the courts have been gradually moving from a policy of non-interference with the decisions of "private bodies" to a reluctant willingness to exercise a limited supervisory jurisdiction depending on the nature of the rights or interests at stake ... [37] In determining the nature of its supervisory jurisdiction, the Court approved of the reasoning in Vancouver Hockey Club Ltd. v. 8 Hockey Ventures Inc. (1987), 18 B.C.L.R. (2d) 372 (S.C.), which reasoning was also adopted and approved in North Shore, (para. 34, supra). [38] In the 2000 case of Falk v. Calgary Real Estate Board Co-operative Ltd. (2000), 82 Alta. L.R. (3d) 201, the Court held at para. 25 as follows: I find that it would be against public policy to enforce the contractual term in the membership agreement in a manner which would exclude judicial review of the decisions of the Board's disciplinary tribunals. However, in doing so I reiterate my previous conclusion that the court must be mindful of its limited role in reviewing these decisions, as they emanate from domestic tribunals. [39] The defendant also urged upon me a number of cases, where parties had agreed to waive and/or release another party from their right to sue in negligence. The defendant argues that in those cases, where releases have generally been upheld by the courts, an analogy can be drawn to Domestic Clauses such as the one in the case at bar. The defendant argues that neither situation should be viewed as attempting to oust the Court's jurisdiction, but rather as parties agreeing to limit their own rights. [40] In my judgment, those cases do not provide a useful analogy. They are significantly different because they do not involve a question of reviewing a decision, which purportedly affects a person's rights. Further, the voluntary organization cases must involve considerations of administrative law principles, whereas the release/waiver cases do not. Finally, the voluntary association Domestic Clauses purport to influence a wide variety of unknown occurrences which might arise in the future, whereas the release clauses generally refer to a release of a very specific right, if it arises in the future, in a very specific and predictable way. [41] It is clear that the historical development of the case law with respect to these clauses has resulted in the state of the law being as described in North Shore. It is against that background, that BCSS adopted the Domestic Clause in the Handbook in the case at bar with the express intention of ousting not only the Court's ability to review, but also to oust its limited supervisory jurisdiction as well. (2) The Domestic Clause Which Purports To Oust All Court Jurisdiction [42] Counsel for BCSS advised me, and it is not disputed, that the wording of the Domestic Clause in the case at bar (para. 23, supra) was included in the Handbook with the specific intention of ousting the Court's limited supervisory jurisdiction; in particular, that portion of the Domestic Clause which states: ... or proceeding taken in any court whether by way of injunction, declaratory judgment, prohibition or otherwise to question, review, prohibit or restrain the committee or any of its proceedings. [43] Counsel for BCSS advises that this clause has not yet been considered by any court in British Columbia. He is also unaware of any case, in any common law jurisdiction where a clause with such a broad sweep, whose express purpose is to oust even the Court's limited supervisory jurisdiction, has been considered. [44] With no previous decisions to guide me, I am left to rely upon: 1. Dicta from the cases with clauses that oust everything except limited supervisory jurisdiction 2. Specifically, I remind myself, inter alia, of the comments of the Courts in the more recent cases of: (a) North Shore, (para. 34 supra), (b) Peerless, (para. 36 and 37 supra); and (c) Falk, (para. 38, supra); 3. Policy considerations [45] With the foregoing in mind, I consider that the Court is now faced with competing principles of public policy, all of which the Courts have traditionally considered important. Those principles are: 1. Non-Involvement (a) Courtrooms are not the place for disputes about high school athletics to be resolved. Educational money should be spent on education, not on lawsuits. High School students should see that, in all but the rarest of cases, disputes can be resolved by meeting, by talking, by compromising, and without going to Court over every disagreement. This reasoning is not limited to school and athletic organizations; rather, it is applicable to all manner of voluntary associations. Our Courts will not get involved in the internal affairs of such organizations. (b) Courts have traditionally not permitted their calendars to become clogged with disputes of this sort, involving the internal business of voluntary organizations, and this is a policy which must continue. 2. Limited Supervisory Jurisdiction (a) The Courts are loathe to give up their limited supervisory jurisdiction. There will always be those rare cases where an authoritative decision or guideline from a Court may be necessary and welcome. There are certain basic principles that govern relationships between people, which all people are bound by, and which cannot be contracted out of. The Courts have always retained the jurisdiction to govern those basic principles, and so long as the jurisdiction remains restricted and limited to those rarest of cases, the Courts have jealously guarded it. (b) The Courts have no interest in the day-to-day activities of voluntary associations, but they have traditionally maintained a real and important interest in the processes by which those organizations govern themselves. [46] I conclude that the public policy considerations in favour of the Court retaining its limited supervisory capacity must prevail. By ensuring that the adjective "limited" remains paramount, there is no likelihood that the competing public policy principle of non-involvement will be violated - that is, there is no likelihood that a deluge of voluntary association cases will flood the Courts. The Court will still not conduct a review of the merits, and this Judgment does nothing to diminish the importance of maintaining that public policy principle. [47] Consequently, I conclude the following: 1. Section 8.5.4.4. (the "Domestic Clause") of the Handbook: (a) Is inoperative insofar as it purports to oust the Court's limited supervisory jurisdiction. (b) Remains operative insofar as it prohibits any review of the merits by the Court. 2. The Court's limited jurisdiction and limited interest in cases of this kind remains precisely in the same state it would be in if there were no Domestic Clause at all, that is, as described in North Shore, and Peerless. 3. It follows that I will exercise the limited supervisory jurisdiction which I retain, and for that purpose I now turn to an assessment of the process employed by the BCSS in dealing with the plaintiff in the case at bar. I will restrict myself to assessing those matters of process suggested in North Shore. I have no interest in a review of the merits, and there will be none. D. THE ERRORS [48] The plaintiff complains of three groups of errors in the process which he argues fall within those types of matters that I can assess in my limited supervisory jurisdiction: 1. Notice; 2. Procedural Fairness; and 3. Bias. (1) Notice [49] Proper Notice is a basic tenet of natural justice and is within those categories of issues the Court will consider in the exercise of its limited supervisory jurisdiction. [50] In Lakeside Colony of Hutterian Brethren v. Hofer, [1992] 3 S.C.R. 165, the Supreme Court of Canada states at pp.195-196: A member must be given notice of the cause for which he is to be expelled. It is insufficient merely to give notice that the conduct of a member is to be considered at a meeting. This is what was done in Cohen v. The Congregation of Hazen Avenue Synagogue (1920), 47 N.B.R. 400 (S.C.). The member had been repeatedly disruptive at meetings of the synagogue, and notice was given of a meeting to consider his conduct. The court found, at p.409, that this was insufficient notice for the purposes of an expulsion: Before the plaintiff could properly be adjudged guilty of an offence warranting his suspension for life he should, according to the most elementary principles of right and justice, have been informed of the nature of the charge against him, and given an opportunity to answer the same. [51] And several paragraphs later: ... adequate and timely notice is as important for two reasons. First, it gives the person who may be expelled an opportunity to consider his or her position and either see the error or his or her ways and seek reconciliation, or prepare to defend himself or herself. Second, adequate and timely notice allows the members of the group who are to make the decision an opportunity to ensure that they will be able to attend the meeting and contribute to the discussion, or perhaps to ask for an adjournment if they are unable to attend. [52] In the case at bar, the plaintiff complains that he was entitled to, but did not receive, proper Notice at two different stages of the proceedings: (a) Notice - The Conduct Review stage; and (b) The Penalty stage. (a) Notice - The Conduct Review Stage [53] The plaintiff acknowledges that he received Notice of the hearing involving conduct and concerning recruiting, but asserts that he was not given proper or sufficient Notice, in several different ways. [54] The plaintiff did receive Notice of a complaint and a hearing date relating to a violation of the Code of Conduct, ss.2.1.9 and 4.3.1. However, he was not given Notice of a complaint or hearing under s.4.3.5.4. He did not learn that s.4.3.5.4 was to be considered until it was announced at the commencement of the hearing. The relevant sections of the Handbook are reproduced at para. 17, supra. [55] The defendant's response to this argument is that I need not concern myself with it, because the Panel ultimately ruled that there was not enough evidence to find that the plaintiff violated s.4.3.5.4. Therefore, even if the Notice was inadequate, the plaintiff suffered no consequence as a result. [56] The plaintiff also argues that the only Notice he received about a violation and a hearing was with respect to the student, J.V. While the plaintiff acknowledges that he knew that other complaints, provided as "additional information" might be referred to for corroboration, he did not learn until the day of the hearing that this "additional information" would form the basis of substantive complaints in their own right. This is significant for a number of reasons: 1. The plaintiff and his principal did investigative work (interviewing the parents) with respect to the complaint involving the student, J.V., but did no such investigative work relating to the other complaints, (the "additional information"). If the plaintiff had known that these complaints would be considered in their own right, he would have done similar investigations relating to those students. 2. The correspondence of the plaintiff's principal supports the argument that they believed there was only one actual complaint, and that all of the other instances were merely provided as "additional information". 3. The plaintiff had little to worry about the hearing related to what he believed was the sole complainant, (J.V.). There was a basis for him to be confident about that matter because the student and the student's parents had provided statements saying that they did not wish to take part in the process, and they did not provide any incriminating information about the plaintiff. As a result, that complaint was dismissed, and the only violations that the Panel found to be proven were with respect to the "additional information". [57] The defendant counters that: 1. Notice may not have been perfect, but it was fair. The plaintiff understood in advance that there was to be a hearing concerning alleged recruiting violations. The details of each of those violations were known to him in advance, including the details relating to the "additional information". 2. When told at the outset of the hearing that all of the matters (including the "additional information") would be considered as substantive matters in their own right, the plaintiff did not object, or ask to adjourn. 3. In Lakeside, (para. 50 and 51, supra,) the Court states at para. 81: Likewise in Young v. Ladies' Imperial Club, [1920] 2 K.B. 523 (C.A.), the notice indicated only that the conduct of a particular member would be reported on and discussed. The court held that this was insufficient notice to allow for a decision to expel. Sterndale L.J. agreed that it would be sufficient if the notice made it quite clear to everyone what would happen at the meeting, but decided that the notice in question did not even convey in substance what would happen: I quite agree with what has been said to the effect that one ought not to examine this agenda and these notices of meetings too particularly and too meticulously; if in substance they convey to the members of the committee what is going to be done, that is sufficient, although one might have thought it might have been better done. (b) Notice - The Penalty Stage [58] The plaintiff was suspended from involvement in all competitive high school sports for the duration of the basketball season. He maintains that he was surprised by this result, since it was his first violation, and since, he argues, he was found to have committed only the less severe of the offences of which he had been accused; that is, "encouraging" under s.2.1.9, not "recruiting" under s.4.3.1. [59] Only after his arrival at the hearing did the plaintiff become aware that the allegations involving "additional information" would be considered as substantive complaints in their own right. Each violation is capable of drawing its own penalty. Consequently, the plaintiff had no Notice that he was in jeopardy of a much greater penalty than he thought he was. [60] He further argues, that in those circumstances, the possibility of a suspension was highly unlikely and he was therefore entitled to Notice that a suspension would even be considered at the Penalty hearing. [61] Section 8.4.2.3 of the Handbook notes the following with respect to penalties available for all manner of violations: Discretionary Penalties: the hearing panel will rule on the complaint, with the following options available to them. These are not written as progressive penalties, but should be applied with due care taken regarding the severity of the offence and the discipline history of the "respondent": A) no steps taken; complaint not upheld B) the complaint upheld, but no action taken C) a letter of reprimand to offending coach D) a letter of reprimand to offending coach and instructions to take a coaching certification theory course within 12 months E) a letter of reprimand and a probationary period for the offending coach of up to twelve (12) months F) penalty as in (d) and (e) but with an additional probationary period for the member school of the offending coach G) the suspension of the offending coach from coaching restricted and unrestricted school competition in British Columbia (all sports and all levels) for a minimum period as decided by the athletic association disciplinary committee or the sport commission disciplinary committee, to a maximum permanent suspension from school sport coaching in B.C. H) any coach disciplined under (g) will receive an automatic minimum twelve (12) month suspension for a second instance of a violation of the coach's code of conduct, within a three (3) year period from the first instance. [62] The plaintiff argues that a penalty under sub-section "G" is the harshest penalty permissible under the Rules for a first violation. Since this was a first violation, it was not within the realm of contemplation that he should have received it. Therefore, he should have received Notice that it was being considered. [63] The defendant counters as follows: 1. A suspension until the end of basketball season, under sub-section "G", is not the harshest penalty permissible for a first violation. Sub-section "G" permits suspensions for any length of time up to and including a permanent suspension. That would be the harshest penalty. The plaintiff only received a suspension of a few months. 2. The plaintiff was well aware, in advance, of the options available under s.8.4.2.3 of the Handbook. 3. There is nothing in the disciplinary rules requiring Notice. (2) Procedural Fairness [64] Procedural fairness is a basic tenet of natural justice, and is within those categories of issues that the Court will consider in the exercise of its supervisory jurisdiction. In this case, the plaintiff alleges that there are two fundamental principles of procedural fairness/natural justice which have been violated: 1. The rule that disciplinary decisions have to be based on properly considered evidence, and not on arbitrary whims; 2. The rule that the Panel, and the entire process, must follow and be bound by its own rules. [65] The plaintiff complains that he was entitled to, but was denied, procedural fairness at three different stages of the process: (a) The Conduct Review stage; (b) The Penalty stage; and (c) The Appeal stage. (a) Procedural Fairness - The Conduct Review Stage (i) Decision Must Be Based on Evidence [66] The Panel decides, in its written Reasons, to accept the "evidence" of various students which was given in written form, and as hearsay through the complainant, over the viva voce evidence of the plaintiff. No reasons were given for rejecting the evidence of the plaintiff. The plaintiff acknowledges that the defendant is entitled to make its own procedural rules permitting evidence in written form, and permitting hearsay, as it has in this case. However, he argues that it is simply not fair to reject oral evidence in the face of written evidence, without at least giving reasons, or indicating what was the basis for assessing the credibility of the written statements, and/or the viva voce evidence. Yet, there is nothing in the Reasons except the bald statement of acceptance of the written testimony over the oral testimony. This is a denial of natural justice. So argues the plaintiff. [67] The plaintiff also argues that the Panel made certain findings of fact in its written Ruling which were not supported by the evidence, or even by the written evidence which the Panel apparently accepted and relied upon. (ii) Must Follow Its Own Rules [68] The plaintiff argues that: 1. The Panel considered the written evidence of students, which was not properly signed and/or witnessed by parents as required by s.8.4.2.3(G): If any of the evidence is submitted by a minor, the letter must be co-signed by the minor's parent or guardian. 2. The foregoing breach of the Rules occurred with respect to two of the students, and therefore, two of the alleged incidents. 3. Paragraphs 9 and 10 of the written Ruling are in conflict with each other and incapable of being reconciled. One paragraph says that they accept certain evidence, and the next paragraph says that they do not. 4. The Rules indicate that the plaintiff is entitled to Notice of the alleged violation, and of the hearing, yet the addition of the "additional information" as an alleged violation under s.4.3.5.4 was not raised until the actual commencement of the hearing, (para. 54, supra). 5. The written Ruling concludes that a violation under s.2.1.9 has been proven, and it describes it as "by recruiting or attempting to recruit". The plaintiff argues that s.2.1.9 is not a recruiting violation. It is found in the Rules under s.2 - Codes of Conduct. It is not found in the Rules under the section for Recruiting (s.4). All of the violations under s.4 were considered to be not proven by the Panel and the plaintiff argues that therefore he was not found to have committed any recruiting violations. Rather, s.2.1.9. is simply a violation of "encouraging". Therefore, the Panel violated its own Rules by concluding with words that he had committed a violation, which he had not committed when described by the actual Rule number. (This also has procedural fairness implications at the Penalty stage, infra). [69] With respect to all of the foregoing, the defendant counters that: 1. The Panel may well have made mistakes. It was not perfect. Nevertheless, the fairness with which the Panel conducted itself is obvious in all the correspondence prior to the hearing, and in the written Ruling. An incredible amount of work went into it. The fairness of the process can be seen by virtue of the fact that the plaintiff was found not to have committed several of the alleged violations. The entire process was fundamentally fair, and that is all that can be asked of the Panel and of the defendant. 2. As noted in Dunford (Litigation guardian of) v. Springers Gymnastics Club Inc., [2002] 1 W.W.R. 480, (Man. Q.B.), 239 at paras. 22 and 23: ... Robert's rules were adopted only as a "reference guide" and the Board is not required to follow them slavishly, particularly where the procedure recommended would be overly formalistic and would amount to the "procedural straightjacket" ... The courts have said again and again that the content of procedural fairness depends on the circumstances of each case. What is required is a flexible system adapted to the needs of the organization that provides the person or persons affected with sufficient notice of the Board's concerns and an opportunity to respond. Simply put, the question is whether there has been fundamental fairness in light of the total circumstances... . (b) Procedural Fairness - The Penalty Stage [70] The plaintiff argues that he was denied procedural fairness, and therefore natural justice, when the Panel failed to follow its Rules in the following situations: 1. The complaint was with respect to alleged violations under s.2.1.9 which is listed in the Handbook under the heading "Section 2 - Codes of Conduct", and under s.4.3.1, which is listed in the Handbook under "Section 4 - Recruiting". The Panel found that the offences under s.4.3.1 were not proven because that section requires proof of the coach initiating contact with the student. The plaintiff was found only to have committed the offence of "encouraging" under s.2.1.9, which does not require that the coach initiate contact. Therefore, the Panel erred when it stated in its written Reasons that the violation of Recruiting or Attempting to Recruit had been proven. In other words, the plaintiff argues, he was found to have not committed the more severe offence, but only to have committed the less severe offence. Therefore, it must be an error that he received the most severe of penalties, when he was not found to have committed the most severe of offences. 2. The plaintiff argues that the Panel did not follow its own Rules as required by the preamble to s.8.4.2.3: Discretionary Penalties: the hearing panel will rule on the complaint, with the following options available to them. These are not written as progressive penalties, but should be applied with due care taken regarding the severity of the offence and the discipline history of the "respondent". 3. It is clear from the preamble that the Panel was to consider the severity of the offence. The plaintiff argues that it obviously did not do so because if it had, it would have considered that he was found to have not committed the more severe of the offences (that is, "recruiting"). Further, it is clear that it did not consider the discipline history of the respondent, because if it had done so, it would have determined that there was no discipline history, and therefore would not have provided the most severe of penalties, a suspension. 4. The Panel also denied the plaintiff procedural fairness when it did not consider, as is clear from its written reasons, any other cases at all from any other disciplinary hearings, involving similar violations. 5. The plaintiff was denied procedural fairness because the Board described his behaviour as an "... alarming pattern of behaviour". This must be wrong. There were four findings against him. The Appeal Panel reduced it to three. This could hardly be described as an alarming pattern of behaviour. [71] The defendant responds to all of the foregoing as follows: 1. By repeating those arguments that appear at para. 69 of this Judgment; 2. Section 2.1.9 is simply a different way of recruiting, and it matters not what the heading is in the Handbook. Further, the offence under s.4.3.1 is not really an offence at all, it is simply a "indicator of recruiting". The Panel found that that indicator was not present, but that there was still sufficient evidence to find him guilty of the offence of recruiting in the manner described under s.2.1.9. (c) Procedural Fairness - The Appeal Stage [72] The plaintiff argues that he was denied procedural fairness in the following ways: 1. The Appeal Panel did not deal with his arguments about process. The written Reasons provided by the disciplinary appeal committee are six pages long and deal with a number of arguments, and in the course of doing so, note inter alia, the following: The Appeal Panel also finds that there was a direct linkage between violation 2.1.9 and the definition of recruiting 4.1 in terms of "encouraging" students even though Mr. Street was not found in violation of any of the direct indicators of recruiting as identified in 4.3. It was acknowledged by the Panel that perhaps there was some confusion due to the lack of clarity in the ruling of penalty documentation, but overall the evidence and findings provided were very clear. And: Student H's statement, however, should not have been received as evidence as there was no parental approval ... [73] The defendant responds by pointing to the following words in the Reasons of the Appeal Panel: The panel rejects the submissions regarding improper procedure. All of the requirements were met specifically as they relate to notification required when proceeding with a coach's code of conduct complaint. 3. Bias [74] Bias is within those categories of issues that the Court will consider in its limited supervisory jurisdiction. [75] In this case, the plaintiff argues that there is no reasonable explanation for the numerous errors made, and therefore, the plaintiff asks me to consider that bias is the only logical conclusion. [76] One particularly glaring example of bias, argues the plaintiff, is the Panel's acceptance of the written evidence of students, together with the hearsay evidence of the complainant, over the viva voce evidence of the plaintiff, without providing any reasons for such findings, and without providing any basis for determining the credibility issues one way or the other. [77] The plaintiff argues that the only reasonable explanation is bias. [78] The defendant argues that this is simply too large a leap of logic to make. There may well be errors, but they are simply that: errors, which fall short of being denials of natural justice, and would certainly fall short of establishing bias. E. CONCLUSIONS AND CONSEQUENCES [79] It will be clear that I have not indicated my findings to this point with respect to any of the process arguments raised by the plaintiff. I do so now. [80] I am unable to accept the plaintiff's argument with respect to bias. Even where mistakes were made, I am unable to conclude, or infer, from the evidence, that bias was a factor. [81] I am satisfied that the defendant did commit errors in conducting its process, as alleged by the plaintiff, with respect to the following: 1. Notice at the Conduct Review stage (para. 53-57, supra). 2. Notice at the Penalty stage (para. 58-63, supra). 3. Procedural fairness at the Conduct Review stage - decision must be based on the evidence (paras. 66-67, supra). 4. Procedural fairness at the Penalty stage (para. 70-71, supra). 5. Procedural fairness - at the Appeal stage (para. 72-73, supra). [82] Not all procedural errors necessarily constitute a denial of natural justice, or go to the heart of those jurisdictional matters referred to in North Shore. [83] I turn, therefore, to the question of whether the errors in this case constitute procedural unfairness to the extent that there has been a denial of natural justice. I turn also to the question of what consequences should flow from the errors that I have found. [84] The plaintiff argues that the quashing of the Panel's decisions on culpability and sentence is the only proper remedy. That is the remedy sought by the plaintiff if this Rule 18A application is decided in the plaintiff's favour. [85] In my view, the correct approach is as follows: each single error must be given due consideration in order to determine if it, on its own, is a procedural error/denial of natural justice to such an extent that it renders the process as unfair. If any one such error does have that result, then the plaintiff succeeds with his application. However, if each error, taken individually, does not rise to that level of unfairness, then it is necessary to consider all of the errors collectively. Ultimately, it is a broad view of the entire process which must be considered. Each individual error will have its place within the broad view of the entire process. With this in mind, the Court must ask itself a question concerning the broad view of the entire process, as well as the individual errors that form part of that broad view. [86] The question that I must ask myself is this: "Although imperfect, has this process been fundamentally fair?" [87] I find support in the case law for the proposition that the correct approach is, as I have set out, to ask the general broad-view question about general fairness in the process. [88] In Stachiw v. Saskatoon Softball Umpires Assn., [1985] 5 W.W.R. 651, ((Sask. Q.B.), the Saskatoon Court of Queen's Bench notes as follows at pp.653-654: In my opinion, the applicant's motion must fail for two reasons - one factual and the other legal. On the evidence and based on the apparent method of resolving complaints provided for in the constitution of the association, I am unable to say the applicant did not get a fair hearing. It must be remembered that the association is not a legal body - it is a voluntary association of softball umpires who establish their own rules and procedures to settle disputes that arise within the organization. It is not a court - it is not a lawfully established administrative tribunal bound by rules of procedure and evidence that are understood in advance. Of course, a lawyer (as an example) could criticize the manner in which this complaint against the applicant was handled. However, in my opinion, given the framework of the constitution, the executive gave the applicant a reasonable opportunity to refute the allegation made against him. If he failed to satisfy the executive they were wrong, there is nothing this court can do for him. He is bound by the rules of the association. [89] In Knight v. Indian Head School Division No. 19, [1990] 1 S.C.R. 653, the Supreme Court of Canada notes as follows at p.685: It must not be forgotten that every administrative body is the master of its own procedure and need not assume the trappings of a court. The object is not to import into administrative proceedings the rigidity of all the requirements of natural justice that must be observed by a court, but rather to allow administrative bodies to work out a system that is flexible, adapted to their needs and fair. As pointed out by de Smith (Judicial Review of Administration Action (4th ed. 1980), at p.240), the aim is not to create "procedural perfection" but to achieve a certain balance between the need for fairness, efficiency and predictability of outcome. (My emphasis added). [90] I now turn back to the evidence in the case at bar in order to determine if that "...certain balance between the need for fairness, efficiency and predictability of outcome" has been achieved. On one side of the balance scale, there are those errors listed in para. 81, supra. On the other side of the balance scale are the following factors: 1. The plaintiff had Notice in the sense that he knew exactly what he was being accused of; 2. The plaintiff had a full hearing on the merits, where he was entitled to be heard, and was in fact heard; 3. The plaintiff received written Reasons on the merits; 4. The plaintiff had the opportunity to make full submissions on the question of penalty; 5. The plaintiff received written Reasons on the question of penalty; 6. The plaintiff had a full hearing on appeal; 7. The plaintiff received written Reasons from the Appeal Panel; 8. The plaintiff received the benefit of an incredible amount of hard and time-consuming work by a number of people associated with the defendant; 9. The results of all of the foregoing was that the plaintiff was actually found not to have committed several of the violations alleged. [91] As already noted, when I consider each of the arguments made by the plaintiff, there is no question but that there were errors made in this case. However, when I consider a broad view of the entirety of the process, I can come to no other conclusion but that the process has been fundamentally fair, both in the general sense, and when applied specifically to this plaintiff. [92] I agree with the comments of the Appeal Panel in the case at bar, where it noted in its written Ruling: The panel believes that in accordance with other administrative tribunals of this kind, the simplification of solutions based on information is a practical and less expensive process that is reliable, i.e., contacting families and having the questions be open-ended. To the best of BCSS' abilities this has been a proper and fair investigation process. [93] It follows that the defendant's Rule 18A motion is granted. The plaintiff's action is dismissed. I do not intend, and it is not necessary, to do any detailed calculations with respect to what portion of the argument that covers.
