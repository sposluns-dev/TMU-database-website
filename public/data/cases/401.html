Submissions were received on May 18, 2012, and considered.2 ALLEGATIONS [2] The following is a summary of the claimants' allegations, the full account of which can be found in the male claimant's Personal Information Form narrative.3 In 1998, the claimants decided to move to Israel to start a new life after having many problems in Russia. The female claimant experienced a robbery in 1998, and the police did little to help her. The police checked the claimant's documents and asked why the Russian family lived in Israel and how they had received Israeli documents. The police refused to indicate that the neighbour's assault on the claimant's was because they were not Jewish. After that, the neighbour became very impudent, insulting and threatening the claimant's children. He also threatened the claimant with a gun because he worked for a XXXX department in the XXXX XXXX. XXXX was never penalized for what he did. [5] The claimants had problems obtaining birth certificates for the twins, and they could not receive child support benefits. DECISION [8] I find that the claimants are not Convention refugees and not persons in need of protection as they do not have a fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Israel. I also find that the claimants are not persons in need of protection in that their removal to Israel would not subject them personally to a risk to their lives, nor are there grounds to believe that their removal to Israel will subject them personally to a danger of torture. ANALYSIS [9] This case turns on state protection and information contained in the country documents as well as reasonableness. The claimant was asked if he had a medical certificate from the time he was attacked by his neighbour in 2003. The claimant stated that he did not think it was needed. Question 31 was read to the claimant indicating that medical and police certificates were required. The panel notes that this is not an absolute requirement, but was a requirement if such certificates could be easily obtained, and also notes that the claimant was assisted by counsel known to the board. The claimant was asked if he issued a Power of Attorney to someone in order to obtain the medical report. He then stated that he was told that medical documents would only be issued in person. The panel declared specialized knowledge that she had seen several medical reports issued by hospitals in Israel based on a Power of Attorney. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find that the claimant added that medical reports were issued only in person after he had already stated that he did not try to obtain one. On a balance of probabilities, I find the claimant not to be a credible or trustworthy witness and that he did not have a medical report because there was none. [10] The claimant was asked if, when the police refused to help him when he was attacked by his neighbour, he reported the incident to anyone else. The claimant stated that he did not because he did not want to make matters worse. The Minister of Justice has a division for investigating police complaints composed of 19 civilians and 25 police officers, and in 2008, 15 civilians replaced 15 officers. The unit looked into 1505 complaints, 39% of which were closed because of lack of evidence. 34% of officers were found not guilty and 20% ended in a criminal trial or disciplinary hearing. Complaints can be filed with the ombudsman's office or its website. The Ministry of Absorption's guide for new immigrants provides similar instructions. On the other hand, complaints do not always receive adequate response due to problems within the investigation system in a conspiracy of silence among police officers.4 On a balance of probabilities, I find that the state of Israel has set up a process for complaining against police officers who refuse to do their duty. On a balance of probabilities, I find that the claimants have not rebutted the presumption that the state of Israel can protect its citizens. [11] The claimant was asked if he reported his problems to the Ombudsman. The claimant stated that he never heard of such an organization. The Ombudsman's office assists citizens with complaints against government officials. It has officials who speak Russian in order to assist Russian speakers who complain about government officials, including the police. Israel is a democracy whose last election was considered free and fair. Security forces reported to civilian authorities. National police carried out training with NGOs to promote cultural awareness. There is mention of discrimination against Arabs and Ethiopian Jews. There is no mention of discrimination against Russian immigrants.5 Persons who attempt to rebut the presumption that states cannot protect their citizens face a higher burden of proof commensurate with the level of democracy.6 Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find that it was incumbent on the claimant to seek the assistance of the ombudsman or the public prosecutor before seeking international protection. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find the claimants have failed to rebut the presumption that Israel can protect its citizens. [12] The claimant was asked if he could go and live in an area made up entirely of Russian speakers. Over a million immigrants from the former Soviet Union (FSU) have arrived in Israel since the early 1990s. The Russian Journal noted in 1999 that a Russian speaker can get by in Israel speaking only Russian because of the number of businesses servicing the immigrant community. The FSU immigrant population has retained their distinctly Russian cultural identity and forms their own economic, social, and political infrastructure within Israel. This infrastructure includes Russian language neighborhoods, schools, restaurants, theatres, media, as well as businesses such as non-kosher butchers and Russian language bookstores. However, former Soviet Union immigrants reportedly complained that they faced a hostile bureaucracy and a sense of isolation from the broader Israeli community. [13] In a 2002 Jerusalem Post interview, an official representing FSU immigrant schools noted that Russian-language children are verbally and physically abused and have a higher dropout rate than the average in the Israeli school system. However, within the Russian community mentioned above, the schoolchildren are free from such abuse. The claimant stated that police in Russian communities protect only those who are Jewish and would not protect Christians. There is no mention of this in the documents, stating that this community is composed of only Jewish Russians. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find the claimants can return to Israel and relocate within the Russian community.7 [14] The claimant stated that he was not free to practice his Christian Orthodox religion openly. The Israeli Constitution guarantees freedom of religion and the government protected these freedoms. Although societal relations were strained, 5.7 million Israelis were Jewish, 1.5 million were Christian Arabs and 320,000 were classified as "other," mostly from the FSU. There is no mention of forced conversions. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find the claimants can return to Israel and freely practice their Christian religion. The panel notes that it is common knowledge that Israel relies on tourism for some of its economy, and that tourism is mostly Christians touring the Holy Land. On a balance of probabilities, I find if there is a serious problem for Christians in Israel, this would be mentioned and the government would provide protection. [15] The claimant stated that he was afraid of an ultra-racist organization, funded in part by the government, which campaigns against "miscegenation" and assimilation. The organization, which is visibly Kahanist, received indirect funding from the government. It is funded by the Khemla Association, which in turn receives funds from the welfare Ministry. The leaders of Khemla are prominent Kahanists; one of whom, Levi Hazan, spent six months in prison- a rather severe sentence when it comes to Jewish terrorists- for his part in an attack on a Palestinian bus which wounded six. Others of Khemla's leaders were held in administrative detention.8 The claimant was asked why he included his fear of this organization when nothing was mentioned in his narrative. The claimant was vague in his answers, stating that he didn't know who these people were and some of them might have belonged to this organization. On a balance of probabilities, I find the claimant added this fear on the day of the hearing to bolster his claim. On a balance of probabilities, I find that there is nothing in the claimant's allegations which supports that he may have been attacked by this group. [16] Many of the claimant's allegations are not supported by the documentary evidence because there is no mention of this in the documentary evidence of the Board. For example, there is no mention that police routinely ask members from the Russian community why they are in Israel and tell them to return to Russia. If this was a widespread practice, on a balance of probabilities, I would find this to be mentioned in the documentary evidence. There is no mention of teachers routinely telling children that they must sit apart because they are not Jewish, or who countenance attacks on the playground because children are not Jewish. There is an Ombudsman for children who would support the parents and children who encounter such problems. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find that the state of Israel is making every effort to protect children from abuse.9 [17] The male claimant stated that he received a call a phone call informing him that he was to report for military duties due to the situation at that time. The claimant stated that he did not want to fight against Palestinians. It was put to the claimant that everyone who integrates to Israel was told that they would be required to serve in the military. The claimant stated that he was not told this; however, his wife received the Absorption Basket which every immigrant to Israel receives.10 The claimant stated that he was not a conscientious objector to military service, over all, and had indeed served in the Russian military. However, he stated that he did not want to take up arms against Palestinians. The requirement to serve in the Israeli military is a law of general application. Immigrants are informed of this obligation when they immigrate to Israel. Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find that the claimant cannot refuse to serve in the military simply because he refuses to take up arms against Palestinians who aim rockets into civilian areas in Israel.11 Therefore, on a balance of probabilities, I find the male claimant must do his duty to Israel by serving in the military if so required. [18] I have found the claimants have failed to rebut the presumption that Israel can protect its citizens. This is fatal to the claim. I have also found that the male claimant's refusal to serve in the military is not supported by him being a conscientious objector and that he must fulfill his military responsibilities to Israel. Therefore, I find there is not a serious possibility or reasonable chance that the claimants would face persecution for a Convention ground if they return to Israel. Therefore, the claimants are not Convention refugees. [19] The panel then turned its mind to whether the claimants would be subject personally to a risk to their lives or to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or whether there are substantial grounds to believe that they would be subject personally to a danger of torture if they return to Israel. Based on the above analysis and the claimants' lack of credibility, I find, on a balance of probabilities, that there are no such risks. CONCLUSION [20] Having considered all of the evidence and based on the above analysis, I find the claimants, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX, not to be Convention refugees and not to be persons in need of protection and I reject their claims.
