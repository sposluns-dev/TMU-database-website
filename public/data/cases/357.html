R. v. Topham

2016 BCSC 1895 R. v. Topham IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: R. v. Topham, 2016 BCSC 1895 Date: 20161005 Docket: 25166 Registry: Quesnel Regina v. Roy Arthur Topham Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Butler Oral Ruling on Admissibility of Expert Evidence Counsel for the Crown: Rodney G. Garson Counsel for the Accused: Barclay W. Johnson Place and Date of Trial/Hearing: Quesnel, B.C. October 3 and 4, 2016 Place and Date of Judgment: Quesnel, B.C. October 5, 2016 [1] On November 12, 2015, Arthur Topham was found guilty by a jury of one count of communicating statements for the wilful promotion of hatred against a recognizable group, people of the Jewish religion or ethnic origin, contrary to s. 319(2) of the Criminal Code. Prior to the trial, Mr. Topham filed a notice of application pursuant to the Constitutional Question Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 68, challenging the constitutionality of s. 319(2). In reasons indexed as R. v. Topham, 2015 BCSC 1185, I ruled that the constitutional challenge, should it need to be heard, would proceed after trial. That challenge is now before this Court. [2] By way of background to the current application, the Criminal Code provision in question was considered by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Keegstra, [1990] 3 S.C.R. 697. The Court concluded that s. 319(2) infringed s. 2(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, but found that the provision was saved by s. 1 of the Charter; in other words, the provision was a reasonable limitation on the freedom of expression. [3] One of the questions before me on this application is whether the applicant has met the threshold set out in Canada (Attorney General) v. Bedford, 2013 SCC 72, for reconsideration of the Keegstra decision. As I noted in my 2015 Topham decision, Mr. Topham argues that Keegstra has not settled the question as to whether the legislative provision is a reasonable limit which is demonstrably justified pursuant to s. 1 of the Charter. He says the facts in this case are significantly different from those in Keegstra and says that the law has developed since 1990. In that regard, he refers to the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in Saskatchewan (Human Rights Commission) v. Whatcott, 2013 SCC 11. [4] In the course of this application, Mr. Topham sought to enter in evidence an expert report of Dr. Timothy Jay, a cognitive psychologist from the Massachusetts College of Liberal Arts who has an expertise in offensive speech and language. I ruled that his report and expert opinion was inadmissible and indicated that I would provide reasons for my ruling later in the course of these proceedings. These are now my reasons for the ruling. [5] The report of Dr. Jay is titled "Opinion Regarding Arthur Topham's Israel Must Perish". The first paragraph sets out his opinion and describes the approach he took at arriving at that opinion. It reads as follows: 1. It is my opinion as a cognitive psychologist that a satirical reading of Israel Must Perish by an average adult reader would not result in the satire being considered hate speech. There are several mitigating factors which must be taken into account regarding how people read and comprehend literature, for example, what frame of mind the reader brings to the literature, what the reader thinks the literature is "about" or "means", what impact a satirical reading might have on a reader, and what a reader would ultimately remember about the literature. I also consider the context in which the reader encounters the literature. [6] The Crown objects to the admissibility of the report and challenges Dr. Jay's qualifications as an expert. I will set out the positions of the parties and provide my analysis of the issue before the Court, and the reasons for my conclusion that the report is inadmissible. Position of the Crown [7] The Crown says the report fails to meet all four of the threshold requirements for an expert report as established in R. v. Mohan, [1994] 2 S.C.R. 9. First and most significantly, the Crown says the opinion given by Dr. Jay is not relevant to any issue raised on this application. It is not relevant to the constitutionality of the section of the Criminal Code, and it is not relevant to the Bedford factors, which are involved in any reconsideration of Keegstra. [8] Second, as the opinion is not relevant to any issue raised by this application, there is no necessity for the report. [9] Third, the Crown says that there is an important exclusionary rule which would be violated if the report is admitted in evidence. There is a well-established principle that a party cannot present evidence challenging the findings of fact of a jury after a conviction has been entered. To admit evidence which contradicts or challenges the jury's conclusions would put the trial judge in an invidious position and bring the administration of justice into disrepute. [10] Finally, the Crown says that Dr. Jay is not properly qualified as he is not able to fulfil his duty to the Court. This is because he has demonstrated bias against regulations or rules which place restrictions on offensive language. Position of the Defence [11] The defence argues that Dr. Jay's report should be admitted in evidence and that any concerns about the nature of the report can be dealt with when considering the weight to be given to his opinion. [12] With regard to relevance, the defence says the report discusses the "likely psychological effects" of Israel Must Perish on its audience. This is relevant to consideration of the constitutionality of the Criminal Code provision because of recent developments in the law. The defence says that as a result of the decision in Whatcott, the Court needs to have evidence before it of the effects that the allegedly hateful communications may have on the audience. The defence says the evidence is necessary because there is no other way it can put forward relevant evidence about the psychological effect of offensive expression. [13] The defence says there is no exclusionary rule which would prevent the reception of this evidence. The cases relied on by the Crown deal with sentencing matters and have no application to the present circumstances. Further, the defence argues that where the constitutionality of a statute is in issue, the Court can and should consider a broad scope of evidence in order to elucidate the factual context of the issues before the Court. [14] Finally, the defence says that Dr. Jay was a well-qualified expert who properly approached his role as an expert witness and fulfilled his duty to the Court. Analysis [15] The legal framework for consideration of the admissibility of expert opinion evidence was set out by the Supreme Court of Canada in Mohan. Recently, the Court revisited the issue in White Burgess Langille Inman v. Abbott and Haliburton Co., 2015 SCC 23. Cromwell J., writing for the Court, set out the potential dangers involved in opinion evidence and at para. 19 summarized the two main components of the Mohan approach as follows: To address these dangers, Mohan established a basic structure for the law relating to the admissibility of expert opinion evidence. That structure has two main components. First, there are four threshold requirements that the proponent of the evidence must establish in order for proposed expert opinion evidence to be admissible: (1) relevance; (2) necessity in assisting the trier of fact; (3) absence of an exclusionary rule; and (4) a properly qualified expert Mohan also underlined the important role of trial judges in assessing whether otherwise admissible expert evidence should be excluded because its probative value was overborne by its prejudicial effect -- a residual discretion to exclude evidence based on a cost-benefit analysis: p. 21. [16] While the Crown made submissions on all four of the threshold requirements, I am of the view that item 2 - necessity, and item 4 - a properly qualified expert, are not important to my consideration of the proposed expert report. If the evidence was relevant to an issue before me on this application, then the opinion would be necessary. Further, if the opinion of a cognitive psychologist was required, then Dr. Jay has the necessary qualifications to give such evidence. The fact that he has given expert opinion on only one side of what might be called the "offensive language question" does not render him unqualified. Dr. Jay has appropriate qualifications for a cognitive psychologist. I have read his report and I am satisfied that he approached the preparation of the report in an impartial way. [17] However, I have also reached the conclusion that the proposed expert report does not provide evidence on any issue which is relevant to the application before me. Further, the opinion amounts to a collateral attack on the verdict of the jury and so it should not be permitted in evidence. In other words, there is an exclusionary principle which would be violated if the evidence is admitted. [18] As a result of the failure of the defence to meet these two threshold requirements for the introduction of the expert opinion of Dr. Jay, I conclude it is inadmissible. I will explain why I have arrived at these conclusions. Relevance of the Expert Opinion [19] The defence maintains that the question as to the likely psychological effects of the article "Israel Must Perish" on readers is a relevant issue for the constitutional challenge. This argument is based on statements made by the Supreme Court of Canada in Whatcott. That case was concerned with the constitutional validity of s. 14(1)(b) of the Saskatchewan Human Rights Code. At para. 52 the Court noted: An assessment of whether expression exposes a protected group to hatred must therefore include an evaluation of the likely effects of the expression on its audience. Would a reasonable person consider that the expression vilifying a protected group has the potential to lead to discrimination and other harmful effects? This assessment will depend largely on the context and circumstances of each case. [20] The defence also refers to para. 92 in Whatcott, where the Court concluded as follows: Thus, in order to be rationally connected to the legislative objective of eliminating discrimination and the other societal harms of hate speech, s. 14(1)(b) must only prohibit expression that is likely to cause those effects through exposure to hatred. [21] The defence says that the question of the psychological effect of communications on readers is thus an important issue of fact that is relevant to Mr. Topham's constitutional challenge, such that some evidence about the psychological effect of "Israel Must Perish" on readers should be before the Court. The article, Israel Must Perish was chosen for Dr. Jay's opinion because it is a central work contained in the material from Mr. Topham's website, which formed the basis for the conviction. [22] At trial, Mr. Topham argued that the article was satire and that a reasonable person reading the article would regard it as such. The defence says the article was particularly important to the jury's verdict because it was not included in the material from Mr. Topham's website, which formed the basis for the charge on which he was acquitted. In essence, the defence posits that the Israel Must Perish article must have been central to the jury's conclusions. Accordingly, it says the psychological effects that the article has on a reasonable person is a relevant factual consideration. [23] Mr. Topham's argument conflates the finding of fact made by the jury with the constitutionality of the statutory provision in question. The basis on which the jury arrived at its conclusion that the Crown had proved beyond a reasonable doubt all of the essential elements of the charge under s. 319(2) of the Criminal Code is not a consideration on this application. At trial, the jury was given instructions regarding the law and they were charged with applying that law to the facts they found based on all of the evidence before them. Their conclusion, based on the evidence at trial, is not an issue to be reconsidered or re-examined at this stage of the proceeding. It has no relevance to any possible issue raised by the constitutional question posed. Dr. Jay's opinion is, in substance, a critique of the jury's conclusion. Accordingly, that opinion can have no relevance to the issues that must be considered on this constitutional challenge. [24] In addition, Dr. Jay's opinion has no relevance to any issue raised by the Bedford threshold. In Bedford at para. 42, the Court set out the circumstances under which a court could consider arguments based on Charter provisions in the face of a previous decision: In my view, a trial judge can consider and decide arguments based on Charter provisions that were not raised in the earlier case; this constitutes a new legal issue. Similarly, the matter may be revisited if new legal issues are raised as a consequence of significant developments in the law, or if there is a change in the circumstances or evidence that fundamentally shifts the parameters of the debate. [25] The opinion of Dr. Jay attempts to challenge the jury's verdict in an indirect way by suggesting that a satirical reading of the article by "an average adult reader" would not result in the satire being considered hate speech. This does not raise a legal issue, let alone a new legal issue. Further, it does not relate to any developments in the law since the decision in Keegstra, and does not amount to a shifting of the parameters of the debate about hate speech. Rather, it is a criticism of the factual conclusion arrived at by the jury. Although, I should be careful to note that the jury was not asked the question considered by Dr. Jay: whether Israel Must Perish, on a satirical reading, could be considered hate speech. Rather, the jury was asked to make specific findings based on the essential elements of the offence. [26] Another way of approaching the question as to the relevance of the opinion is to look at the kind of evidence which can properly be considered on an application concerning challenging the constitutionality of legislation. This was canvassed by Madam Justice Saunders, as she then was, in Chamberlain v. Surrey School District No. 36 (1998), 60 B.C.L.R. (3d) 311 (S.C.) at para. 19: In cases concerning the constitutional validity of state action or cases under the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, the Supreme Court of Canada has indicated that a broad scope of evidence should be considered to elucidate the legislative framework, the factual context and purpose of the impugned provision, the ill the Act was intended to remedy, the institutional framework within which the provision will operate and the effect of the provision: Reference re Residential Tenancies Act (Ontario), [1981] 1 S.C.R. 714 (S.C.C.), at 723; Reference re Anti-Inflation Act, 1975 (Canada), [1976] 2 S.C.R. 373 (S.C.C.); R. v. Morgentaler, [1993] 3 S.C.R. 463, 107 D.L.R. (4th) 537 (S.C.C.). [27] It is immediately apparent that the opinion evidence of Dr. Jay does not fall within any of these categories of evidence. It is not evidence which elucidates the legislative framework. The opinion does not deal with the factual context and purpose of the legislation, the ill the Criminal Code provision was intended to remedy, or the institutional framework within which the provision operates. Rather, it is evidence about the theoretical impact on the average person of one part of the evidence presented to the jury. [28] In summary, the opinion evidence of Dr. Jay has no relevance to the application before me. While that is sufficient to deal with its admissibility, I will also consider the third threshold requirement; whether there is any exclusionary rule which would prevent admissibility of the evidence. The Exclusionary Rule [29] The Crown does not point to a particular evidentiary rule which would exclude this evidence. Rather, it says that the evidence should be excluded because it is contrary to basic principles of the criminal law. The rule it relies on is not a rule of evidence but a matter of principle in relation to jury verdicts. The Crown says the principle arises from the position taken by courts on sentencing hearings and that the principle can be applied by analogy to the situation before the Court. [30] I agree with the Crown's position. The fact that Mr. Topham has brought a constitutional challenge does not permit the Court to reconsider or re-examine the findings of the jury. The sentencing decisions relied on by the Crown are instructive in this regard. They establish that once the verdict has been rendered, the Crown and defence have no further right to call evidence regarding the facts of the offence. [31] In R. v. Gauthier (1996), 108 C.C.C. (3d) 231 (B.C.C.A.), the accused was convicted by a jury of sexual assault. At the sentencing hearing, the trial judge refused to permit the accused to call expert evidence to prove that penetration did not occur. The court upheld the trial judge's ruling denying the offender's right to adduce evidence regarding the facts of the crime. In doing so, it quoted from the decision in R. v. Braun, [1995] 3 W.W.R. 402 (Man. C.A.) at para. 36. The court held that although a convicted person has the right to call evidence at the sentence hearing, this right is limited by the nature of the issues before the court. The accused, the court remarked, is normally not able to call evidence aimed at destroying the basis on which the finding of guilt was made. If the accused were allowed to call evidence that attacked the roots the conviction rested upon, it would tend to bring the administration of justice into disrepute. There is, the court said, a requirement for finality in a case once a court has decided an issue. The matter, in effect, is res judicata. [32] The court, in Gauthier, went on to conclude at paras. 30 and 31: [30] The right of the Crown and the defence to call evidence with respect to the facts of the crime ends when the verdict is finally rendered. [31] I see no reason to depart from our established traditions. There are good reasons why trials are structured as they are. All of the evidence surrounding the facts ought to be called as part of the trial. The jury ought to hear all of the evidence surrounding the facts at the time of trial to properly reach their verdict. A conflict in the evidence with respect to the seriousness of an assault might have an effect, in the eyes of the jury, on the credibility of witnesses either for the Crown or the defence. The notion that either the Crown or the defence could call evidence for the purpose of either minimizing or amplifying the facts after a verdict is rendered would lead to unseemly tactics and serious injustices. [33] While the application before me is not a sentencing, there are important similarities between this application and a sentencing hearing. In each situation, the jury has rendered a verdict which is not open to challenge except on appeal. The offender is not entitled to call evidence attacking the factual root of the conviction. The right of Mr. Topham to call evidence is "limited by the nature of the issues before the court". Here, the issues before the Court are the constitutionality of s. 319(2) of the Criminal Code and whether Mr. Topham can meet the threshold for reconsideration of the Keegstra decision. Neither of those issues requires opinion evidence from a psychologist about whether "a satirical reading of Israel Must Perish by an average adult reader would result in the satire being considered hate speech." [34] I agree with the statements made in Braun; if it is permissible for the offender to lead evidence challenging the factual conclusions reached by the jury, the administration of justice would be called into disrepute. In essence, the issue touched upon by the report of Dr. Jay is res judicata. [35] The Crown also referred to R. v. Ferguson, 2008 SCC 6, for the principle that a sentencing judge "is bound by the express and implied factual implications of the jury's verdict", and that the sentencing judge "shall accept as proven all facts, express or implied, that are essential to the jury's verdict of guilty": para. 17. I agree that those principles also apply to a judge considering a constitutional challenge following a jury verdict. [36] In summary, the report of Dr. Jay is inadmissible. It offers no opinion which is relevant to the issues before the Court. Further, the opinion attempts to challenge the jury's conclusions and is thus an impermissible collateral attack on the verdict. "Butler J."
