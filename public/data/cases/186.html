Decision Overview Matter I must consider first Issue Analysis Conclusion

Decision

[1]
The appeal is dismissed. The Tribunal disagrees with the Appellant.
[2]
The Appellant hasn’t shown just cause (in other words, a reason the law accepts) for leaving his job when he did. The Appellant didn’t have just cause because he had reasonable alternatives to leaving. This means he is disqualified from receiving Employment Insurance (EI) benefits.

Overview

[3]
The Appellant left his job as an accounting assistant controller on May 10, 2024 and applied for EI sickness benefits. The Appellant says he left his job because he had medical and mental health issues he needed to address.Footnote 1 The Appellant later converted his claim to regular benefits in December 2024.
[4]
The Appellant says he had just cause for leaving and argues several grounds under subsection 29(c) of the Employment Insurance Act (Act).
[5]
The Canada Employment Insurance Commission (Commission) looked at the Appellant’s reasons for leaving. It decided that he voluntarily left (or chose to quit) his job without just cause, so it couldn’t pay him benefits.Footnote 2
[6]
The Commission says that, instead of leaving when he did, the Appellant could have addressed his concerns with his employer first, requested a transfer or a leave of absence or provided medical documents that supported the need to quit.Footnote 3 The Appellant says he had no reasonable alternatives.
[7]
I must decide whether the Appellant has proven that he had no reasonable alternative to leaving his job.

Matter I must consider first The Appellant requested a hearing by videoconference

[8]
The Appellant requested a hearing by videoconference. However, at the beginning of the hearing, the Appellant said his video camera wasn’t working. The Appellant stated that he wished to proceed without it. The hearing proceeded with my video camera on and the Appellant’s video camera off.

Issue

[9]
Is the Appellant disqualified from receiving benefits because he voluntarily left his job without just cause?
[10]
To answer this, I must first address the Appellant’s voluntary leaving. I then must decide whether the Appellant had just cause for leaving.

Analysis The parties agree that the Appellant voluntarily left

[11]
I accept that the Appellant voluntarily left his job. The Appellant agrees that he quit on May 3, 2024. I see no evidence to contradict this.
[12]
The Record of Employment issued by the employer also indicates that the Appellant quit.Footnote 4

The parties don’t agree that the Appellant had just cause

[13]
The parties don’t agree that the Appellant had just cause for voluntarily leaving his job when he did.
[14]
The law says that you are disqualified from receiving benefits if you left your job voluntarily and you didn’t have just cause.Footnote 5 Having a good reason for leaving a job isn’t enough to prove just cause.
[15]
The law explains what it means by “just cause.” The law says that you have just cause to leave if you had no reasonable alternative to quitting your job when you did. It says that you must consider all the circumstances.Footnote 6
[16]
It’s up to the Appellant to prove that he had just cause. He must prove this on a balance of probabilities. This means that he must show that it’s more likely than not that his only reasonable option was to quit.Footnote 7
[17]
When I decide whether the Appellant had just cause, I must look at all the circumstances that existed when the Appellant quit. The law sets out some of the circumstances I must look at.Footnote 8
[18]
After I decide which circumstances apply to the Appellant, he then must show that he had no reasonable alternative to leaving at that time.Footnote 9

The circumstances that existed when the Appellant quit

[19]
The Appellant says that several of the circumstances set out in subsection 29(c) of the Act existed at the time he quit. I will go through each of them in turn. Specifically, he says that he was harassed by his supervisor, he suffered from discrimination, the working conditions were a danger to his health, he had an antagonistic relationship with his supervisor, and he was under undue pressure by the employer to leave.

Sexual or other harassmentFootnote 10

[20]
The term harassment isn’t defined in the Act. The concept of harassment in the form of workplace bullying is usually seen as actions or verbal comments that could mentally hurt, embarrass or isolate the person in the workplace. It often involves repeated incidents or a pattern of behaviour that is intended to intimidate, degrade or humiliate a particular person or group of people. A review of the jurisprudence where the Umpires have found just cause for harassment under subsection 29(c)(i) of the Act contemplates numerous such incidents and/or a pattern of such behaviour over a period of time.Footnote 11
[21]
The Tribunal’s Appeal Division has considered the issue of workplace harassment and has identified a series of “key principles” that will guide us when we are determining whether a claimant has been harassed. The guiding principles are:

a) harassers can act alone or with others, and don’t have to be in supervisory or managerial positions; b) harassment can take many forms, including actions, comments, intimidation and threats; c) sometimes a single incident is enough to constitute harassment; d) there is a focus on the alleged harasser, and whether the person knew or should have reasonably known that their behaviour would cause offence, embarrassment, humiliation or other psychological or physical injury to the other person.

[22]
The harassment complained of must render the workplace genuinely intolerable.Footnote 12
[23]
Even where harassment has been proven, there may be an obligation to make all reasonable efforts to rectify the situation before quitting.Footnote 13
[24]
The Appellant says he was “bullied and vilified” by his supervisor.Footnote 14 When asked by the Commission to provide examples or details of this, after a long pause, the Appellant said, “I have some homework to do” and said he would contact the Commission the next day.Footnote 15
[25]
I am not aware that the Appellant ever provided the Commission with the particulars of the bullying allegation.
[26]
The employer says the Appellant reported to the head controller and praised him for being “a great supervisor.”Footnote 16
[27]
Shortly before the Appellant left his employment, he sent Human Resources an email saying that he was being bullied at work. The Human Resources manager responded to the email and requested the Appellant attend a meeting to discuss it. The Appellant never responded to the request for a meeting and didn’t provide any details about the alleged bullying. The employer says if the Appellant had reported bullying, they would have taken it seriously. The Appellant resigned a short time later.Footnote 17
[28]
During the hearing, the Appellant explained some of the incidents that led him to feel bullied. The Appellant says that in the beginning, he had a good relationship with his supervisor. But, as time went on, things changed. He testified that in the 4-month period before he quit, there were some issues.
[29]
On January 24, 2024, the hotel occupancy wasn’t good. Management asked all employees to take vacation so they could save on payroll costs. Vacation pay is set aside in the vacation bank, so the money was available to pay people who were on vacation. The Appellant didn’t want to take vacation then, as he had just been off in December. But he wanted to do his part, so he requested a week off at the end of January.
[30]
On the last day before the Appellant was to go on vacation, his supervisor pulled him into a meeting without any notice and said he had to be written up for absences the year before.Footnote 18 Since the Human Resources manager was away at the time, the supervisor had the manager’s assistant present in the meeting.
[31]
The Appellant admits that he was absent for 12 days in the previous year. The employer gives employees Personal Time Off days (“PTO”) and he took the 5 PTO days he was entitled to. He wasn’t sick during those days, but he used them to attend to personal matters, such as car maintenance or court.Footnote 19
[32]
He also had 3 paid sick days. The other days off were unpaid and he took them around the first anniversary of the death of his son. Tragically, the Appellant’s son was killed in a hit and run accident. The Appellant testified that he wasn’t in a good state of mind at the time. The supervisor approved the request for the time off.
[33]
Even though the absences from work were all documented and approved, the supervisor said that if he takes anymore sick time he would be written up or reprimanded. The Appellant testified that he felt threatened when his supervisor said that. He felt like he was being told that he can never be sick again. But the Appellant says, “If you’re sick, you’re sick” and it can’t be planned for.
[34]
The Appellant said it was hypocritical of the supervisor to say that to him because the supervisor had been off work himself for two weeks with COVID-19, which wasn’t planned.
[35]
The Appellant says he has a few health conditions that require treatment, and the supervisor was aware of them. The Appellant has diabetes, arrhythmia, and pain in his feet. He takes medication and is seeing a doctor but sometimes he’s not well and needs time off.
[36]
A short time later, the Appellant’s niece attempted suicide. She lives in Florida. The Appellant was very upset when he heard about her situation and wanted to go and visit her, but he felt that he couldn’t take any time off or he would be fired or disciplined in some way.Footnote 20
[37]
In December 2023, the Appellant’s supervisor emailed him to ask about how many times a week staff was going to the bank to handle deposits and withdrawals. Up to that point, the practice was to go three times a week. But, hotel occupancy was low and there wasn’t a lot of cash flow. The supervisor said that he wanted to reduce trips to the bank to twice a week instead.
[38]
Sometime after that, the Appellant’s supervisor asked him why the staff didn’t go to the bank three times. The Appellant reminded him that it was agreed they would only go twice a week.
[39]
Despite the direction from the supervisor to only do banking twice a week, the supervisor wrote the Appellant up for not going to the bank three times. The Appellant refused to sign the human resources documentation about this. Again, the Human Resources manager was away, and the manager’s assistant was present in the room as a witness. The Appellant felt that he was being treated like a child when the supervisor scolded him in front of a witness (the assistant) and wrote him up for something that he shouldn’t have been.Footnote 21
[40]
Another incident occurred prior to the Easter weekend. It was normal practice to do month-end inventory on the 1st of the following month. On this occasion, the supervisor wanted to do inventory on the Sunday before the 1st of the month. The supervisor told the Appellant about this on the Thursday before Good Friday, after the Appellant had already purchased a ticket to travel and spend Easter with his family.
[41]
The Appellant spoke to the Human Resources manager in confidence (or so he thought) about being excused from participating in the inventory process this time. He said that perhaps someone else could do it. The Human Resources manager said it was part of his job. The Appellant responded that he agreed it was part of his job, but it had always been done on the 1st of the month and this time it’s being done on a different day. The Appellant’s direct reports also had Easter plans and couldn’t come in. The Human Resources manager flatly said, “Do you want this job?”Footnote 22
[42]
The Appellant says they weren’t paid properly for working on a statutory holiday, so that’s why nobody wanted to work those days.
[43]
The Appellant asked the Human Resources manager not to say anything to the supervisor and to give him a couple of days to talk to the supervisor about the inventory. Instead, the manager told the supervisor what she and the Appellant discussed. The supervisor pulled the Appellant into his office and questioned him about going to Human Resources about him.Footnote 23
[44]
The Appellant was surprised by this because he asked the Human Resources manager not to say anything to the supervisor. The Appellant was disappointed because he believed that Human Resources should be neutral, at least, and not necessarily on management’s side. He felt sabotaged, betrayed, and like she would always side with the supervisor. The Appellant testified that he didn’t feel like he had a voice, because the Human Resources manager was always loyal to the Executive team, rather than her employees.Footnote 24
[45]
While I believe the incidents described by the Appellant occurred, I don’t find that they constitute harassment. Workers in Ontario by the Employment Standards Act and the Ontario Human Rights Code, which provide sick leave rights and job security. If the supervisor acted on his threat about future sick time, the Appellant would have been able to engage provincial authorities.
[46]
Being micromanaged by a supervisor about the number of times banking should be done is annoying but isn’t harassment.
[47]
Being written up for breaching the banking policy when the policy wasn’t breached isn’t harassment. It may be a sign of other management issues in the workplace, but I don’t find it fits the definition of harassment. I will consider this under another heading however.
[48]
The sudden change in the routine around taking inventory was unprofessional and inconsiderate but I don’t find it to be harassment either. I don’t see how it would cause offence, embarrass or humiliate the Appellant.
[49]
Furthermore, the incidents described didn’t render the workplace intolerable. I know this because the Appellant didn’t say he quit for this reason, and he also didn’t quit immediately following any of the incidents. He didn’t properly bring the allegation of bullying to Human Resources either, even though the Human Resources manager invited him to a meeting to discuss it. It was incumbent on the Appellant to provide the details of the alleged bullying and give the employer a chance to resolve it. It was wrong for the Appellant to assume the Human Resources manager wouldn’t take the matter seriously or act on the complaint.
[50]
This circumstance didn’t exist at the time the Appellant quit.

DiscriminationFootnote 25

[51]
Discrimination within the meaning of the Canadian Human Rights Act constitutes just cause for voluntarily leaving your employment.
[52]
The prohibited grounds of discrimination are race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, age, sex (including pregnancy), sexual orientation, marital status, family status, physical or mental disability (including dependence on alcohol or drugs), and pardoned conviction.
[53]
The Appellant is Jewish and says that his supervisor made a negative comment about Jewish hotel guests, calling them “F’ing Jews” during a lunch with several co-workers. The Appellant says the comment wasn’t directed at him or about him, but was made in his presence.
[54]
The Appellant was shocked to hear this. The Appellant thought it was especially bad for the general manager to act this way with staff present because it sets a bad example.
[55]
The Appellant didn’t report it to Human Resources right away because he felt that the Human Resources manager would protect the supervisor. The Appellant says there were 4 people on the Executive team, and they protected each other. He didn’t think anything would come of it.
[56]
While this conduct by a supervisor is offensive, I don’t find that a single remark, that wasn’t directed to or about the Appellant, reaches the level of discrimination. While it made the Appellant uncomfortable, there’s no evidence that this is what caused the Appellant to leave his job.
[57]
This isn’t a circumstance that existed at the time the Appellant quit.

Working conditions a danger to health or safetyFootnote 26

[58]
The law says a person who experiences working conditions that are a danger to their health or safety has just cause for leaving, provided they had no other reasonable alternatives.
[59]
When a person says they quit their job because their working conditions were dangerous to their health or safety, they need to: (a) give medical evidence; (b) attempt to resolve the problem with the employer; and (c) attempt to find other work before leaving. Before leaving for health or safety reasons, the person should tell their employer about the health problems responsible for their decision to leave.
[60]
A claimant must show that the work or the workplace caused the negative health effects and that they existed at the time they quit.
[61]
The Appellant quit his job and applied for sickness benefits. It appears that he received sickness benefits until his claim was converted to regular benefits in December 2024.Footnote 27
[62]
Although it appears the Appellant was able to satisfy the Commission that he qualified for EI sickness benefits, there’s no evidence that he told his employer that he had health problems until his exit interview.Footnote 28
[63]
The Appellant testified that the climate that he worked in was poor. His work location was in the basement, there was no fresh air and there was an exhaust fan blowing on him all day, which couldn’t be moved. He says this environment wasn’t healthy for him.
[64]
He didn’t say that he raised this concern with his employer or what their response was. I’m not aware that he asked to be moved to a different location or if there was some other remedy that might have been available.
[65]
The Appellant says he quit for mental and medical reasons but there’s no documentation supporting the need to quit before he quit. He says he suffered mentally over the loss of his son who was killed overseas in February 2022. A week before he quit, his niece attempted suicide. He says this “broke” him and he felt that his health was threatened. Footnote 29
[66]
He says he couldn’t see his doctor before he quit because the doctor was out of the country on vacation.
[67]
However, the Appellant testified that even if he had been able to see his doctor, she would have recommended that he take a leave of absence, not quit. He admits he didn’t discuss his decision to quit with his doctor beforehand because she was overseas at that time. He says he saw her after he had already resigned.Footnote 30
[68]
The employer says if the Appellant had submitted medical documentation to them, they would have granted a medical leave of absence for up to 6 months.Footnote 31
[69]
It appears to me that the Appellant told his employer that he was leaving to focus on health matters and that he was moving to Lucerne or to the Caribbean.Footnote 32 He stopped showing up for work and later sent an email to Human Resources saying that he was resigning.Footnote 33
[70]
The Appellant hasn’t met their burden with respect to this circumstance. He didn’t provide medical documentation to support the need to quit, and he didn’t try to address his health concerns with the employer (beyond stating that he was being bullied). The Appellant testified that he started looking for a new job in January 2024 and that he was always keeping an eye out for better opportunities. He said that the period from January to March isn’t a good time to look for work.Footnote 34
[71]
The Appellant hasn’t demonstrated that his workplace caused the negative health effects.
[72]
Since the Appellant hasn’t satisfied all the requirements to prove that his working conditions were a danger to his health, I find that this circumstance doesn’t apply.

Antagonism with supervisorFootnote 35

[73]
The law says a claimant has just cause for voluntarily leaving if the claimant had no reasonable alternative, having regard to antagonism with a supervisor, if the Appellant isn’t primarily responsible for the antagonism.
[74]
“Antagonism” isn’t defined in the Act, but the dictionary definitions suggest that antagonism includes an element of hostility. Merriam-Webster online dictionary defines “antagonism” as “actively expressed opposition or hostility.” The Collins online dictionary defines antagonism between people as “hatred or dislike between them.”
[75]
During the hearing, the Appellant described an incident that occurred in 2022. He says his supervisor was verbally abusive and physically threatening. The supervisor screamed at him, slammed doors, got red in the face and put his face within two inches of the Appellant’s face. The supervisor is twice the Appellant’s size and is a karate expert. The Appellant says the situation was very intimidating. The incident was witnessed by the Appellant’s 3 direct reports, who were so terrified by the incident that they left the room.
[76]
The Appellant says the supervisor overreacted to one of the Appellant’s direct reports going to him about an issue they should have gone to the Appellant about. The supervisor apologized later, and the supervisor was never aggressive like that again.Footnote 36
[77]
The Appellant submitted an email from a former co-worker who stated that she was denied a day off when she asked for one. After her condition worsened, she was hospitalized for a few days. She says the Executive team members protected each other, and it was difficult to get her concerns acknowledged or addressed properly in the workplace.Footnote 37
[78]
I accept the statements provided by the former co-worker but give them little weight since the statements weren’t made under an affirmation and it wasn’t possible for me to ask the former co-worker questions about her statements. Furthermore, her statements are about her own experience, and not anything related to the Appellant.
[79]
I acknowledge that the incident with the supervisor in 2022 was not caused by the Appellant. It was a very intimidating and unpleasant situation for sure. However, I don’t find that it caused the Appellant to leave his employment as the situation happened two years earlier and the Appellant said that was a one-off incident that didn’t happen again. The Appellant didn’t say he quit for this reason. He said he quit for health and medical reasons. So, I don’t find this circumstance existed.

Undue pressure by employer to leaveFootnote 38

[80]
The law says just cause for voluntarily leaving employment exists if a claimant had no reasonable alternative to leaving having regard to undue pressure by an employer to leave the employment.
[81]
“Undue” means excessive,Footnote 39 or to a level that is more than necessary, acceptable or reasonable.Footnote 40
[82]
In a previous case, a Board of Referees decided that an employer put undue pressure on an employee when the employer was making the job so stressful that it was likely the employee would leave.Footnote 41
[83]
The Appellant testified that, in the four months leading up to the time he quit, he thought the employer wanted him to leave.Footnote 42 Some of the situations that made the Appellant feel this way have already been covered earlier in my decision, including:

unscheduled, impromptu meetings with Human Resources/supervisor being written up about situations that didn’t warrant being written up a last-minute schedule change regarding inventory taking not being paid properly for working on a statutory holiday pressure to take time of as vacation even though Appellant had just had a vacation being told he couldn’t take anymore sick time micromanaging the Appellant regarding banking practices lack of support and trust with the Human Resources manager history of the Executive team always covering for each other

[84]
In addition to these examples, there are two others. The first one was the way that bonuses were paid. The employer paid substantial bonuses to several people over the course of two years and recorded the bonuses in a way that avoided payroll deductions.Footnote 43 The Appellant says there are accounting principles about this that weren’t followed. The bonuses should have flowed through payroll, but instead they were documented as expense reimbursements. When the Appellant brought this up to his supervisor, he was told not to say anything to anyone. The Appellant thought this practice was dishonest and unethical. The Appellant testified that his hands were tied and if he had said anything, his job would have been in jeopardy.Footnote 44
[85]
Secondly, the Appellant says his supervisor would lie to the staff during staff meetings about the amount of tips that had been collected by the employer and put into a shared gratuity pool and the amount of hold-back the employer retained with respect to the tips. The Appellant found the employer’s conduct in this regard unethical, and he had trouble with the staff being lied to, but it was his supervisor’s decision.Footnote 45
[86]
I agree that, taken together as a whole, all these relatively minor things could add up to create a situation where the Appellant felt so stressed at work that he might leave.
[87]
I find that this circumstance existed when the Appellant left his job.

Circumstance that existed

[88]
In conclusion, the only circumstance that existed when the Appellant quit was that there was undue pressure by the employer on the Appellant to quit.

The Appellant had reasonable alternatives

[89]
I must now look at whether the Appellant had no reasonable alternative to leaving his job when he did.
[90]
The Commission says that the Appellant had several reasonable alternatives to quitting, including requesting a leave of absence while he addressed his health issues or until his doctor returned from vacation, obtaining a medical note advising that he needed to quit, or addressing his concerns with his employer first.Footnote 46
[91]
The employer told the Commission that if the Appellant had requested a medical leave of absence, they could have accommodated him for up to 6 months. The Appellant never asked for a leave of absence.Footnote 47
[92]
The Appellant says that there was no written policy in the workplace about leaves of absence. He never knew of any department head taking a leave of absence. The Appellant says that there wasn’t anyone, besides his supervisor, who could have temporarily assumed his job. The Appellant says that he and his supervisor were already struggling with a heavy workload. The supervisor wouldn’t have been able to do it all by himself; they would have had to hire someone else. The Appellant didn’t feel like a leave of absence was an option.Footnote 48
[93]
The Appellant testified that his doctor would have recommended that he take a leave of absence rather than quit.
[94]
While I understand that the Appellant didn’t know how the employer could operationalize his leave of absence, he should have asked for one. The Appellant said that he had shared some of his health problems with the Human Resources manager before. He could have explained what was going on and asked for a leave. It’s up to the employer to figure out how to cover the employee’s role while they are off.
[95]
I find the Appellant also could have waited until his doctor returned from vacation to get a note that supported his need to quit for medical reasons. He didn’t do that. His doctor was absent for 3 weeks. He could have requested the earliest appointment upon his doctor’s return and stressed that it was urgent.
[96]
Alternatively, depending on the nature of the health issues he was facing, perhaps he could have gone to a walk-in clinic or an emergency department to obtain a medical note. If his health issues were as serious as the Appellant claims, he should have been able to get the necessary medical notes in place before quitting.
[97]
The Commission says the Appellant didn’t properly bring his concerns to his employer before quitting. I agree. The Appellant said that he was being bullied at work but then didn’t follow up to supply the details of that, even though he was asked to do so by the Human Resources manager.
[98]
He says he quit because of health issues, but we don’t know what the health issues were. He didn’t tell his employer that he had serious health issues and needed to quit because of them. I find that it was reasonable for the Appellant to discuss the concerns he had before resigning.
[99]
I find that the Appellant could have requested a leave of absence, obtained medical documentation that supported the need to quit before quitting or discussed his concerns with his employer before quitting. All of these were reasonable alternatives to quitting.
[100]
Even considering the circumstance that existed when the Appellant quit (undue pressure to quit), the Appellant had reasonable alternatives to leaving when he did, for the reasons set out above.
[101]
This means the Appellant didn’t have just cause for leaving his job.

Conclusion

[102]
I find that the Appellant is disqualified from receiving benefits.
[103]
This means that the appeal is dismissed.
