INTRODUCTION [1] XXXXX XXXXX and XXXXX XXXXX, the appellants, who are citizens of Kazakhstan, are appealing against a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD), alleging that it rendered a decision based on errors in its analysis of the internal flight alternative (IFA). [2] The Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness (the Minister) intervened in this appeal, alleging that the appellants did not show that the RPD's decision regarding their IFA was unreasonable. II. DETERMINATION OF THE APPEAL [3] Under subsection 111(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (the Act), the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) dismisses the appeal and confirms the RPD's determination, namely, that XXXXX XXXXX and XXXXX XXXXX are not "Convention refugees" under section 96 of the Act or "persons in need of protection" within the meaning of section 97 of the Act. III. BACKGROUND A. Basis of the refugee protection claims [4] In his Basis of Claim Form (BOC Form), which he signed on May 27, 2013, the male appellant stated that he is a citizen of Kazakhstan and is of Russian Orthodox religion, that he speaks Russian, that he was born on XXXXX, 19XX, and that his wife and stepdaughter lived in Montréal, and his mother and sister lived in XXXXX at that time.1 As for the other information included in his BOC Form, the male appellant referred to the information contained in his wife's BOC Form. [5] In her BOC Form, which she signed on May 27, 2013, the female appellant stated that she is a citizen of Kazakhstan and is of Catholic religion, that she speaks Russian, that she was born on XXXXX, 19XX, and that her husband and daughter lived in Montréal, and her brother and sister lived in Russia at that time.2 [6] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that she and her husband started having problems in early 2012, when young Kazakh men began settling in their area, because her husband is Belarusian and she is Polish. She also stated that, in XXXXX 2012, she and her husband were stopped in the street by a group of young Kazakh men, who hurled insults at them and told them that Northern Kazakhstan belonged to them and that she and her husband should leave.3 [7] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that, the following day, she spoke with XXXXX XXXXX, the police inspector, who told her that there was nothing to be done and that the government was relocating Kazakhs to the North.4 [8] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that, in late XXXXX 2012, her husband was threatened by new Kazakh workers, who yelled at him that Kazakhstan was not for Belarusian pigs. She also stated that, even though she was receiving her pension, she continued to work as a XXXXX and that, at work, she started having problems. The administration told her that she should make room for Kazakh women who needed work.5 [9] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that, in XXXXX 2012, someone telephoned her residence and threatened to attack her if she did not quit her job. She also stated that she had been scared and that she went to see the police inspector, but she found out that he had been replaced by a new inspector of Kazakh origin. He told her that he had other, more important, things to deal with than crazy old Polish ladies.6 [10] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that, in XXXXX 2012, someone broke the windows of their Polish church and that the police came but stated that the damage had been done by children, and no investigation was conducted. She also stated that, in the new year, her husband was dismissed from his job and a young Kazakh was hired. Her husband wanted to protest, but he was told that he should keep quiet or things would get worse.7 [11] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that, later in XXXXX 2013, she was stopped by a Kazakh man close to home, who told her that not only did they have to leave their jobs for Kazakhs from the South, but they also had to leave their apartment. She also stated that she went to the police for help, but that nothing was done.8 [12] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that she decided to quit her job on XXXXX, 2013, to avoid provoking reactions from the nationalists, but that, on XXXXX, 2013, after church, she was attacked by two Kazakh boys, who yelled at her, [translation] "Get out of our territory you Slavic pig, or we will kill you." She was struck on the shoulder, and she fell and injured her head. Consequently, she had to be hospitalized in order to receive care.9 [13] In her BOC Form, the female appellant stated that she then decided that she and her husband should flee, and she contacted her daughter and her husband, who helped them obtain visas to come to Canada. She also stated that, after she got out of the hospital, she and her husband moved in with friends, where they lived in hiding, and that, on XXXXX, 2013, they left Kazakhstan on a train headed to Moscow, where they purchased their tickets to come to Canada.10 B. RPD decision [14] The hearing before the RPD was held on August 1, 2013. In its decision dated August 16, 2013, the RPD rejected the appellants' claim for refugee protection.11 In its reasons for decision, the RPD indicated that, as a whole, it found the testimony of the female appellant, the principal claimant at the time, to be credible with respect to the threats and the attack that occurred on XXXXX, 2013. That being said, the RPD was of the opinion that the claimants had not established, on a balance of probabilities, that this behaviour by nationalist extremists is characteristic of the Kazakh population toward ethnic minorities or that it has become widespread throughout the country. Therefore, the determinative issue was the IFA.12 After reviewing the prongs of the IFA test and after taking into consideration the appellants' explanations in this regard, the RPD concluded that an IFA was available to them in Almaty, the former Kazakh capital and the country's economic centre, which is mainly Russian-speaking and has 1.5 million inhabitants, and it set out its reasons for this.13 C. Ground of appeal and remedy sought [15] In their memorandum,14 the appellants essentially identified one ground of appeal, that is, that the RPD, in its analysis of the IFA, failed to take into account all the evidence-in other words, that the RPD's analysis is incomplete. [16] The appellants request that the RAD allow their appeal, set aside the determination of the RPD and allow their claims for protection, or refer the matter to a differently constituted RPD panel.15 [17] In his memorandum,16 the Minister submitted that a decision that does not mention all the aspects of the evidence may be confirmed so long as it is falls within a range of possible, reasonable outcomes and that the appellants did not establish that, even taking into account certain specific pieces of evidence, the conclusion and decision arrived at by the RPD were unreasonable. [18] For these reasons, the Minister requests that the RAD dismiss the appeal.17 IV. EVIDENCE AND HEARING [19] The appellants did not indicate that they intended to present new evidence on appeal or request that a hearing be held before the RAD. V. STANDARD OF REVIEW A. Particular context of the RPD and the RAD [20] The standard of review analysis need not be conducted in every instance. Where the standard of review applicable to a particular question is well-settled by past jurisprudence, it may be adopted. Using jurisprudence as a guide, I am of the opinion that only where this search proves fruitless is it necessary to undertake such an analysis.18 [21] In this case, it should be noted at the outset that the RAD is not a court of law and that it does not review RPD decisions, but rather determines appeals in an administrative and non-judicial context. In fact, the RPD and the RAD are two separate divisions of the Immigration and Refugee Board (IRB).19 Sections 162 to 169 of the Act identify the provisions that apply to them both. Each division "has, in respect of proceedings brought before it under this Act, sole and exclusive jurisdiction to hear and determine all questions of law and fact, including questions of jurisdiction."20 Members have the powers and authority of a commissioner and may do any other thing they consider necessary to provide a full and proper hearing.21 Hearings are held in the absence of the public, although each division may conduct a proceeding in public, or take any other measure that it considers necessary to ensure the appropriate access to the proceedings.22 Sections 169.1 to 170.2 of the Act identify the provisions specific to the RPD, while sections 171 and 171.1 identify the provisions specific to the RAD. [22] The RPD and the RAD, in their respective roles, have to deal with whether to grant refugee status and protection to claimants. These divisions are therefore part of the Canadian refugee protection system under the Act that governs them both, the purpose of which is, among other things, to establish fair and efficient procedures that will maintain the integrity of this system, while upholding respect for the human rights and fundamental freedoms of all human beings.23 [23] The RPD's primary role is to hold hearings to determine whether refugee protection claimants are Convention refugees or persons in need of protection. Because it disposes of claims for refugee protection by means of hearings, the RPD has the opportunity to see and question refugee protection claimants, giving it a significant advantage with regard to findings of fact and the assessment of the refugee protection claimants' credibility. [24] Pursuant to subsection 110(1) of the Act, a person or the Minister may appeal on a question of law, of fact or of mixed law and fact to the RAD against a decision of the RPD to allow or reject the person's claim for refugee protection. Furthermore, under subsection 110(3) of the Act, the RAD generally proceeds without a hearing, on the basis of the record of the RPD proceedings. B. Right of appeal and deference extended to RPD decisions [25] When analyzing the respective roles of two administrative tribunals and deciding which standard of review the Law Enforcement Review Board should apply in determining an appeal against a decision made by the officer tasked with hearing a complaint about a police officer's behaviour, the Alberta Court of Appeal noted that the mere presence of a right of appeal-including appeals within an administrative structure-in no way means that no deference to the first-level decision-maker is called for.24 [26] In its decision, the Court of Appeal referred to the example of the relationship established between a trial judge and an appeal judge: The presumption underlying the structure of our court system is that a trial judge is competent to decide the case before him or her, and that a just and fair outcome will result from the trial process. Frequent and unlimited appeals would undermine this presumption and weaken public confidence in the trial process. An appeal is the exception rather than the rule.25 [27] In thus citing the Supreme Court of Canada, the Court of Appeal emphasized the importance of promoting the autonomy of the proceeding and its integrity, adding that the same principle applies within administrative structures: The same principle applies to the hearings before the presiding officers. If the Board was to continue to routinely rehear all matters on a de novo basis, and to extend no deference whatsoever to the decisions of the presiding officers, that would only undermine the apparent integrity of those hearings. As previously stated, that is inconsistent with the hybrid scheme of the Act. As the appellant noted, that approach undermines those hearings to the point that they become almost academic, and call into question the need of the interested parties to even participate in them. The hearing would be reduced to a type of preliminary inquiry.26 [28] I am of the opinion that the expertise and advantageous position of the decision-maker of first instance should also be recognized, as did the Supreme Court regarding the trial judge: The trial judge is better situated to make factual findings owing to his or her extensive exposure to the evidence, the advantage of hearing testimony viva voce, and the judge's familiarity with the case as a whole. Because the primary role of the trial judge is to weigh and assess voluminous quantities of evidence, the expertise and insight of the trial judge in this area should be respected.27 [29] After reviewing the respective roles of the decision-makers, their particular expertise and the general economy of the proceedings, the Alberta Court of Appeal identified which standards of review the Law Enforcement Review Board should apply to the decision of the decision-maker of first instance: [82] In conclusion, the decision of the Board to conduct a de novo hearing, and to assume that it owed no deference to the findings of the presiding officer was in error. The role of the Board is primarily to sit on appeal from the presiding officer. The Board is not a tribunal of first instance, and cannot simply ignore the proceedings before the presiding officer, and the conclusions reached by him. The focus of the appeal to the Board should be on its dual mandate of civilian oversight, and the correction of unreasonable results. [83] There is no general power to hold a de novo hearing in every case, and no requirement that a de novo hearing be held unless the parties consent to proceeding otherwise. Where a sufficient reason is shown or the issues on appeal warrant it, the Board has the power to admit fresh evidence. When sufficient cause is shown the Board can even rehear key evidence presented to the presiding officer. [84] The Board has a legitimate role to play in providing civilian oversight to the system of police discipline where oversight issues arise. The Board is not bound by the inferences and conclusions of the presiding officer, but it should be able to offer some articulable reason based in law, fact or policy when it interferes with a decision under appeal. The Board should proceed primarily from the record created by the hearing before the presiding officer. It should extend deference to the decision of the presiding officer on questions of fact, credibility, and technical policing issues. If the decision of the presiding officer was reasonable, the Board should not substitute its own view just because it might have come to a different conclusion. Where the appeal raises issues of acceptability of particular police conduct, or the integrity of the discipline process, the Board's mandate is more robust.28 [30] Relying on the reasoning of the Alberta Court of Appeal and the factors identified in its analysis in Newton, and making the necessary adjustments to the particular context of the RPD and the RAD, I am of the opinion that, except for the strict issues of law or natural justice, it is appropriate for us, as members of the RAD, to extend the same deference to RPD decisions. Indeed, this deference is the same as that which courts of law are required to extend to decision-makers of first instance when the issue is a question of fact or a question of mixed law and fact. C. The standard of review to be applied in this case [31] In their memorandum,29 the appellants essentially identified one ground of appeal, that is, that the RPD, in its analysis of the IFA, failed to take into account all the evidence. In other words, its analysis is incomplete. That said, they did not, however, identify which standard of review should be applied. [32] In his memorandum, the Minister submitted that the ground of appeal concerns the RPD's assessment of the documentary evidence, which is a question of fact that must be reviewed on the standard of reasonableness.30 [33] In my view, the question of whether the RPD took into account all the evidence in its analysis of the IFA is a question of mixed fact and law. Based on the Federal Court case law, I am of the opinion that the standard of review that applies to this question is that of reasonableness.31 [34] When a decision is reviewed on the standard of reasonableness, the analysis must relate to the justification, transparency and intelligibility within the decision-making process, as well as to whether the decision falls within a range of possible, acceptable outcomes that are defensible in respect of the facts and law.32 VI. ANALYSIS OF THE MERITS OF THE APPEAL Did the RPD make one or more unreasonable errors in its IFA analysis? [35] I have concluded that, taken as a whole, the RPD's decision concerning the appellants' IFA indeed falls within a range of possible, acceptable outcomes that are defensible in respect of the facts and law. Here is why. [36] In their memorandum, the appellants criticized the RPD for the following: [translation] - having conducted an incomplete analysis of the documentary evidence in concluding that members of the Slavic minority are not persecuted or mistreated in Kazakhstan;33 - in that regard, having relied on a portion of a passage from Tab 13.2 of the national documentation package, while failing to underscore the beginning of that same passage, which shows that the emigration of Russians is on the rise,34 and while failing to apply other passages indicating that the Russians feel they are being pushed out of the country;35 - not having understood the evidence before it, in particular, Exhibit P-6, submitted at the hearing, but also Tab 2.3 of the national documentation package, when it concluded that the appellants belong to religious groups and churches that are well accepted in Kazakh society.36 [37] In his memorandum, the Minister submitted that the fact that a decision-maker does not mention each piece of evidence does not automatically make the decision unreasonable, and that a decision that does not mention all of the evidence submitted may nevertheless be confirmed so long as it falls within the range of possible, reasonable outcomes.37 He also submitted that the appellants did not show that the presumption that the RPD considered all the evidence does not apply in this case, or that the fact that it did not mention all the documentary evidence rebuts that presumption.38 The Minister also submitted that the appellants did not demonstrate that, taking into account all the evidence, the RPD's conclusion and decision were unreasonable, even in referring specifically to certain parts of the evidence on file.39 [38] In its reasons for decision, the RPD proceeded as follows in its IFA analysis: [translation] [9] The test for assessing whether there is an IFA has two prongs. The first consists of determining whether, in the proposed IFA, there is a serious possibility of persecution as per section 96 of the IRPA or whether, on a balance of probabilities, there is a risk that the claimants would be subjected to one of the harms listed in subsection 97(1) of the IRPA. The second prong consists of determining whether it would be objectively unreasonable for the claimants to seek refuge there, taking into account their particular circumstances. [10] After the incident of XXXXX, 2013, the claimants went to stay with XXXXX friends living in the same city, to whom they sold their apartment before leaving the country. According to the female claimant, the population of Tainsha is approximately 16,000. When asked by the panel whether they had had problems in this part of the city, where they stayed for two months, the female claimant stated that they had remained inside their friends' home. When asked whether there were extremists in that part of the city, the female claimant replied that she did not know. [11] The panel asked the claimants whether they could relocate to Almaty, which is located in the eastern part of the country and is the former Kazakh capital and the country's economic centre. It is primarily Russian-speaking and has a population of 1.5 million.40 The female claimant stated that the nationalists are everywhere and that the same problems would arise no matter where they lived in Kazakhstan. The male claimant replied that he fears extremist violence and thinks he would be killed if he were to return to Kazakhstan.41 [39] After taking into account the appellants' explanations and analyzing the documentary evidence, the RPD found that an IFA was available in Almaty.42 [40] With regard to Russian speakers and the Slavic minority, the RPD's reasoning is as follows: [translation] [16] It is true that native-born Kazakhs receive "preferential treatment," particularly within state organizations, and that there is discrimination against ethnic minorities; however, the sources consulted do not report that members of the Slavic minority are subjected to persecution or mistreatment in Kazakhstan. It is reported that Kazakhstan pursued a sound policy on nationalities and was able to maintain the inter-ethnic harmony between Russians and Kazakhs.43 Russian speakers and the Slavic minority are not denied the opportunity to study, work, learn the language of the state or access positions of importance. Karim Massimov, former prime minister and current chief of staff of the presidential office, is of Uighur origin, and the governor of the central bank is of Ukrainian origin.44 Although there was an exodus of members of the Russian community in the 1990s, following the country's independence, it appears that some of them have returned to Kazakhstan because of the country's healthy economy.45 46 [41] With regard to the appellants themselves, the RPD's reasoning is as follows: [translation] [17] The principal claimant lived in Kazakhstan all her life and, despite the policy of "kazakhization" in the northern part of the country, she was able to continue to work as a XXXXX, even though she had been receiving a pension since 2003. At the hearing, she stated that she had normal relationships with her Kazakh colleagues. The male claimant had been able to work as a XXXXX for the same company for the past 15 years. He stated that he had been dismissed in XXXXX 2013 because his boss wanted to offer his position to a young Kazakh. However, on his visa application, he had written that he was still at the same place of employment in XXXXX 2013. At the hearing, when the panel asked whether the information on his visa application was correct, he replied in the affirmative. When confronted with the fact that he had reported that he was dismissed in XXXXX 2013, whereas on his visa application he had indicated that he was still working for this company in XXXXX 2013, the male claimant stated that he had bribed his former boss with two bottles of cognac in order to obtain proof of employment. [18] The panel is of the view that the male claimant did not conclusively establish that he had been unfairly dismissed. In addition, even if this had been the case, he did not establish, on a balance of probabilities, that he could not find other employment in his field in XXXXX, a city where the job opportunities are greater.47 Although it is more difficult for people who do not speak the Kazakh language to access government positions, it is not reported that, in private companies, Slavic minorities are denied employment. It is important to remember that most Kazakh people speak Russian, as the claimants do. Furthermore, according to the sources consulted, the Russians and the Kazakhs live in harmony.48 49 [42] With regard to the religious groups to which the appellants belong, the RPD's reasoning is as follows: [translation] [19] On the other hand, with regard to religion, the documentary evidence consulted50 and that which was submitted51 indicates that some minority religious groups face discrimination in Kazakhstan, including Baptist and Evangelical Christians, Jehovah's Witnesses, followers of Hare Krishna and Scientologists, as well as Muslims who are not affiliated with the spiritual association of Muslims in Kazakhstan, such as Ahmadis, Sufis and Salafists. However, the four religious groups that the government considers to be "traditional," namely, Islam, Russian Orthodox, Roman Catholic and Jewish, are very well accepted and are well tolerated in society: There were no reports of societal abuses based on religious affiliation, belief, or practice; however, there were reports of increasing discrimination against religious minorities. Muslim, Russian Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and Jewish leaders reported high levels of acceptance in society. There were no reports of mistrust of minority religious groups led to violence.52 [20] The male claimant is of Russian Orthodox Christian faith, and the female claimant is a Catholic Christian. They belong to religious groups and churches that are well accepted in local society.53 [43] Based on my understanding of the arguments put forth in their memorandum, the appellants are essentially asking me to reweigh the evidence that was heard by the RPD. However, my role in this appeal is not to reweigh the evidence54 or to conduct a microscopic analysis of the RPD's decision, but rather to determine whether, when analyzed as a whole, the RPD's decision falls within a range of possible, acceptable outcomes that are defensible in respect of the facts and law.55 The RPD's findings regarding the assessment of the evidence are entitled to great deference.56 [44] When reviewing an IFA, the RPD must first ask itself whether there is another part of the claimant's country where the claimant would not be subjected to persecution or to a risk to their life.57 The claimant has the burden of establishing that they had no internal flight alternative in another part of the country.58 In the RPD's reasons for decision, it is clear that, after taking into account the appellants' explanations and after referring to the documentary evidence, it concluded that the appellants had not established that, in Almaty, they would be subjected to persecution or to a risk to their lives: [translation] [21] Therefore, in light of the above, the panel concludes that the claimants could relocate without fear to the city of Almaty, where the population speaks Russian like they do and where they can freely practise their religion and live in harmony with the Kazakh people, as demonstrated in the above evidence. The female claimant is a pensioner. Her husband, as stated, can reasonably find employment in his field in the former Kazakh capital. It is therefore not objectively unreasonable for them to go live in Almaty. Other than the alleged fears, which were not established in this case, the claimants did not provide further reasons that would prevent them from relocating to this city.59 [45] The appellants stated that the RPD did not take into account all the passages included in the documentary evidence with regard to their fear of persecution and that the RPD did not take into account Exhibit P-6 when it concluded that they belonged to religious groups that are well accepted by Kazakh society. In my view, their arguments cannot be accepted. First, in its reasons for decision, the RPD explicitly referred to Exhibit P-6.60 Furthermore, it is presumed that the RPD read all the documentary evidence,61 and it did not have to refer explicitly to each of the documents included in the documentary evidence, as explained by the Supreme Court in Newfoundland and Labrador Nurses' Union v. Newfoundland and Labrador (Treasury Board), 2011 SCC 62, [2011] SCR 708: [16] Reasons may not include all the arguments, statutory provisions, jurisprudence or other details the reviewing judge would have preferred, but that does not impugn the validity of either the reasons or the result under a reasonableness analysis. A decision-maker is not required to make an explicit finding on each constituent element, however subordinate, leading to its final conclusion (Service Employees' International Union, Local No. 333 v. Nipawin District Staff Nurses Assn., [1975] 1 S.C.R. 382, at p. 391). In other words, if the reasons allow the reviewing court to understand why the tribunal made its decision and permit it to determine whether the conclusion is within the range of acceptable outcomes, the Dunsmuir criteria are met.62 [46] After concluding that there is another part of the country where the claimants would not be subjected to persecution or to a risk to their lives, the RPD must then answer a second question, namely, whether it would be objectively unreasonable or unduly harsh to expect them to relocate to another part of their country of origin, before claiming refugee protection in another country.63 In that regard, the bar must be set very high, and the claimant must present actual and concrete evidence of the existence of conditions which would jeopardize their life and safety in travelling or temporarily relocating to a safe area.64 After determining that it is not objectively unreasonable or unduly harsh to expect the claimants to relocate to this other part of their country of origin, the RPD can then conclude that there is an IFA available to them in their country. [47] In the RPD's reasons for decision, it is clear that it was of the view that it was not objectively unreasonable for the appellants to relocate to Altamy, the former Kazakh capital, where they could have freely practised their religion, worked or lived as pensioners while speaking their language, Russian. I see no error in this regard on the part of the RPD. [48] After having carefully read the appellants' memorandum, the Minister's memorandum and the RPD's reasons for decision, I conclude that, taken as a whole, the RPD's decision concerning the appellants' IFA falls within the range of possible, acceptable outcomes that are defensible in respect of the facts and law, and that the reasons for this decision are clear, intelligible and justified. VII. REMEDIES [49] For these reasons, I confirm the RPD's determination, namely, that XXXXX XXXXX and XXXXX XXXXX are not "Convention refugees" under section 96 of the Act or "persons in need of protection" within the meaning of section 97 of the Act. [50] The appeal is dismissed.
