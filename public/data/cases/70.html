ALLEGATIONS [3] The principal claimant is of Jewish ancestry. The claimants assert that anti-Semitism is present in Hungary and with the appearance of the Jobbik party the voice of anti-Semitism has become more intense. [4] On XXXX XXXX XXXX 2010 the principal claimant and his wife were coming from a demonstration against anti-Semitism when a man who lived on the same street asked him why he was wearing a kippa and made racist remarks to him. This man, XXXX XXXX and another neighbour, XXXX XXXX, began to put pressure on the couple to leave the country. [5] A letter was deposited into the family's mailbox on XXXX XXXX, 2010 saying that they had been warned to get out of the country. The family would receive hateful letters at least once a week and some of these contained threats of being killed. [6] On XXXX XXXX 2011, the couple was heading home when the principal claimant felt a blow to the back of his neck and his spouse was pushed to the ground. The attackers were dressed in black. The claimant went to the local police to make a report but the police officer in charge said that XXXX XXXX had already notified them that the claimant would be coming to make a report based on fabricated facts. The claimant went to another police station and a report was taken. [7] On XXXX XXXX XXXX2011, the female claimant was attacked by two men and warned to leave the country. On XXXX XXXX, 2011, on his way home from work, the principal claimant was physically attacked again. Reluctant to go to police because they had not helped him the past, the claimant and his family went to stay at his father's XXXX. He heard from his father's uncle who operated a XXXX XXXX in a town nearby that some men had come around looking for him. The claimants returned to Budapest. They received another anonymous threat in their mailbox when they returned. [8] The claimants came to Canada on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2012 and claimed refugee protection. DETERMINATION [9] The panel concludes that the claimants are not "Convention refugees" nor are they "persons in need of protection." ANALYSIS Identity [10] The panel is satisfied as to the identity of the claimants on the basis of their Hungarian passports on record. [11] In response to question 1(h) regarding their religion, the claimants indicated that they are all Roman Catholic. The wife has no Jewish ancestry. The husband stated that his father was of Jewish origin. The claimant was baptized as an infant in the Roman Catholic faith and did not practice the Jewish religion or follow any Jewish traditions. When asked about his last name, the claimant could not say whether or not it would be considered a Jewish name and when asked if he could be easily identified as Jewish by his physical appearance the claimant said he did not know. [12] The claimant did not belong to any Jewish organizations and had not been active in any such organization. He stated that in his adulthood he had not encountered any problems because of his father's Jewish ancestry. The claimant's problems with his neighbours began only after returning home from a demonstration against anti-Semitism. [13] When asked about this demonstration on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2010, the claimant said that he had heard it announced on the radio and had attended with it with his wife. He stated that there were some 200-300 people present that day and that some were of Jewish origin and others were not. The police was present at this demonstration but the claimant stated that there were no incidents of which he was aware which would have caused the police to intervene. [14] The claimant testified that the first anonymous letter that he received was on XXXX XXXX, 2010. He testified that some other neighbours had told him that some of the neighbours were scheming to find ways to make him leave the country. The claimant testified that he received many such threats and that he kept 6 or 7of them and brought these anonymous letters with him to Canada. These were included in Exhibit C-2. The claimants were asked if they had gone to the police when they continued to receive these threats on a regular basis. The wife testified that she thought they would be laughed at if they had gone to the police. Neither did the couple present these threatening letters to any Jewish organization or any human rights organization in Hungary. [15] When the principal claimant was asked if he had ever told the police about the letters he said that after the attack on him in XXXX 2011 he had mentioned the threats to the police but had not shown them the letters. He stated that the police did not attach any importance to this and concentrated only on the physical assault against him. [16] The claimant had been threatened verbally by his neighbour, XXXX XXXX. He had received a succession of threatening letters which he did not report to the police. The claimant testified that after the attack on him on XXXX XXXX 2011 he had gone to the district police station but was not well received because his neighbour XXXX XXXX had warned the police that the claimant would be making a fabricated case. The next day the claimant went to another station to file his report. The claimant did not bring the letters to show the officers at the other police station that he had been receiving such threats for some time. The claimant stated that they were not interested in this. Yet the claimant had been home and considering that he went the next day to the police to file a report it is surprising that he did not bring these letters with him, considering that he alleges that it was likely that XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX were behind his attack and that XXXX XXXX had somehow known about his attack on XXXX XXXX. Exhibit P- 21 is a copy of the complaint the claimant made to the police for the attack on him on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2011. The claimant signed this declaration. In this letter, he was asked by the police if he would recognize his aggressors and he responded that he could not name the persons who attacked him. Exhibit C-22 contains the decision of the police in this investigation and it is dated XXXX XXXX XXXX 2010 and states that the claimant made a complaint against unknown individuals who attacked him because of his Jewish heritage. However, since he could not identify his aggressors, the investigation was concluded. [17] While the claimant stated that he told the police about the threatening letters he stated that they did not appear to be interested in this. The fact remains however that the claimant had these letters in his possession and despite going to another police station on the day after the attack he did not bring these letters with him which would have corroborated his allegations that he was a victim of continuous attacks and that someone was behind a plot to make him leave the country. The panel finds that this important information would have helped the police in their investigation. While the claimant alleges he told them about these threats, nevertheless, nothing is written to this effect in the report made on XXXX XXXX, 2011 and no mention is made of the threats of the neighbours whom he believes to be behind the attack. The police in this case had little information to go on which would have helped them in their investigation. The claimant's complaint was received and recorded however. The panel notes that random assaults are difficult to investigate and protect against and even the most effective, well-resourced and highly motivated police forces will have difficulty providing effective protection.1 The panel finds the claimant did not provide the police with concrete information for them to possibly make any link between the attack on him and the actions of the neighbours. The claimant had other neighbours who had told them what XXXX XXXX had been plotting to do yet the claimant did not ask these neighbours to be witnesses for him at the police station. Neither did the claimant report the incident to the police when XXXX XXXX had issued racial remarks to him and had spat on him. The panel concludes that the claimants did not give the police all the information which would have helped them in their investigation and the police therefore cannot be faulted in this case. [18] Exhibit C-22 is a report made by the female claimant when she was attacked on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2011. While this report mentions the harassment in the form of letters that were received, the female claimant did not show these to the police either. In her oral testimony when asked to describe her aggressors the claimant stated that she was sure they were members of the Hungarian Guard. She testified that they were dressed in black and had black boots and had the Hungarian Guard insignia on their arms. The claimant's report to the police however does not mention that they were members of the Hungarian Guard. In the report, the description of the attacker given by the claimant is that her aggressor wore a sweater with a hood. The claimant was asked why the report would not have mentioned that the attackers had clothing with the insignia of the Hungarian Guard if this had been the case. The claimant responded by saying that she did not remember what was in the report. The panel does not accept her answer. Had the claimant been attacked by members of the Hungarian Guard with insignia of this organization on their clothing the panel would expect that this would have been mentioned in the report. The credibility of the claimant's allegations that her attackers were members of the Hungarian Guard was called into question by the absence of this information in the report. [19] While the male claimant alleges he was attacked again in XXXX 2011 he did not report this new incident to the police. He alleges that when he went to a town 120 km from Budapest to stay with his father that some men had come to his father's uncle shop asking about him in a forceful way. After returning to Budapest he received another threatening message but this was not reported to the police either. The claimant was asked how it was possible that some people would go to a town some 100 km outside of Budapest to search for him. The claimant said he did not know. The claimant testified that he fears the two neighbours, XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX, as well as the Hungarian Guard and its supporters. When asked if he could move to another area of Budapest to be away from his neighbours the claimant said that they would be able to find him anywhere but how they would do so he was not able to say. The claimant testified that now that they have left Budapest their house, which belonged to his father, is unoccupied. The panel did not find it credible that some people had gone searching for the claimant outside of Budapest for the simple reason that the claimants had moved from their neighbourhood which is what the neighbours allegedly wanted. Since their house is now empty, the panel would not expect that the two neighbours would continue to search for the claimants throughout Hungary today. [20] The determinative issue in this case however was state protection. In assessing the issue of state protection for the claimants, the panel is guided by a number of cases. There is a presumption that the state is capable of protecting its citizens, except in situations where the state is in complete breakdown.2 To rebut the presumption of state protection, the claimant must present clear and convincing evidence, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate. [21] Claimants from a democratic country have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status. The burden of proving an absence of state protection is directly proportional to the level of democracy that exists in the state.3 Hungary has functioning political and judicial systems in place, and an official apparatus sufficient to provide adequate protection to its citizens. Hungary is in control of its territory and has functioning security forces in place. There is no evidence of a total breakdown of state authority in the country. Therefore, the burden of attempting to show that one should not be required to exhaust all avenues of domestic recourse is a heavy one.4 [22] In order to determine whether the claimant has discharged his burden of proof, the panel considered whether the state of Hungary is capable of providing protection and willing to act. In Carrillo5 the Federal Court of Appeal stated that the refugee claimant, who claims that state protection is inadequate, bears the evidentiary burden of adducing evidence to that effect. As stated above, the panel finds that not all incidents were reported to the police. The written threats were not given to the police and the neighbours who were witnesses did not come forward to the police with the story about the schemes of the two anti-Semitic neighbours. [23] Hungary is a multiparty parliamentary democracy.6 The last parliamentary elections in April 2010 were assessed as free and fair. Fidesz's prime ministerial candidate, Viktor Orban, took office in May 2010. Security forces reported to civilian authorities. [24] According to the U.S. Country Reports on Human Rights Practices for 20117 the Jewish population is estimated to be between 80,000 and 100,000. While Jewish congregations reported a decline in anti-Semitic incidents, anti-Semitic remarks in public discourse became harsher than they were in 2010. The report mentions 145 reported instances of vandalism of Jewish and Christian properties, 14 in houses of worship, and 139 in cemeteries. Three teenagers confessed to vandalizing 75 gravestones in a Jewish cemetery to "show off their strength to one another." Prime Minister Orban condemned the vandalism, calling the act offensive to the Jewish community and all Hungarians. [25] This documentary evidence also states that the official weekly publication of the far-right Jobbik party continued to publish anti-Semitic articles during the year. There were numerous far-right Web sites available in the country, many of which were openly anti-Semitic and some of which were hosted on foreign Internet servers. NGOs reported that the government monitored the content of these sites to enforce the prohibition against public display of such symbols as the swastika, the hammer and sickle, the five-pointed red star, and the arrow cross. [26] During the year the prime minister, cabinet members, and opposition politicians routinely criticized extremist movements, condemned anti-Semitic incidents, and attended events commemorating the Holocaust. The government continued its effort to enhance dialogue and expand coordination with the domestic and international Jewish communities. The state secretary at the Ministry of Public Administration and Justice, Andras Levente Gal, held regular consultations with leaders of Jewish congregations and NGOs on various issues, including restitution for heirless Jewish properties and Holocaust education in public schools. Radical political groups, including the radical nationalist Jobbik party in Parliament, which grew in size and number, used anti-Semitic rhetoric, which observers stated worked to promulgate political friction and play on fears of economic uncertainty. [27] The constitution and other laws and policies protect religious freedom and, in practice, the government generally respected religious freedom. The U.S. embassy officers closely tracked anti- Semitic incidents and regularly consulted with leaders of religious groups to assess the threat and regularly discussed with the government the issue of anti-Semitic public incidents organized by extremist groups. [28] A UN report pointed to "rising anti-Semitism" in Hungary and called on the government to adopt measures to promote tolerance and diversity, train judges, prosecutors, and the police in detecting hate and racially motivated crimes, and implement hate speech legislation.8 In Hungary, "the incitement of hatred (as well as hate-inspired violence) is a crime, but simple outrageous hate speech is not." and "any law restricting freedom of speech requires that there be an imminent danger." But because of the courts' inability to adequately assess imminent danger, "hate speeches, such as incitement to hatred are not prosecuted at all." The US State Department also reports that NGOs criticized the courts for "failing to convict persons for inciting hatred unless the crime was accomplished by a physical assault." [29] In February 2010, the Hungarian Parliament amended the criminal code to criminalize denial of the Holocaust, making it punishable by imprisonment for up to three years. On 8 June 2010, the law was amended to remove reference to the Holocaust and "broaden[ed] ... to cover public denial, expression of doubt, or the minimization of genocide or other acts against mankind committed by either National Socialist or Communist regimes." [30] The U.S. International Religious Freedom Report July-December 2010 states that in Hungary there were reports of societal abuses or discrimination based on religious affiliation, belief, or practice, and prominent societal leaders took positive steps to promote religious freedom. 9 [31] On the basis of the documentary evidence the panel concludes that the government and politicians in Hungary continue their efforts to combat anti-Semitism by speaking out against extremism and social intolerance. The US Country reports do not mention that anti-Semitic incidents have increased over the past year. The incidents of violence reported appear to be against property and outright attacks on individuals appear to be rare. [32] The claimants identify themselves with the Roman Catholic religion and their children in Canada attend the Hungarian church. The principal claimant did not attend any Jewish public events or attend the synagogue while in Hungary. The one demonstration against anti-Semitism he attended was attended by Jews and non-Jews alike. This event was secured by the police and no violence was reported at that time. The panel finds that this is further indication of Hungary's commitment to defend all its citizens. [33] While the panel did not find the female claimant credible that her attackers bore the insignia of the Hungarian Guard, because it had not been mentioned in the police report the panel notes that the Hungarian government has taken steps to ban this organization. [34] While right wing extremist groups do exist in Hungary, an article in the National Documentation Package discusses state response to the attacks by fascist groups. 10 In 2011, the Hungarian government proposed changes to the Penal Code in part to deal with the spate of street marches by groups of people dressed in 'non-official uniforms'. In May 2011, the Hungarian Parliament adopted an amendment to the Criminal Code to protect citizens from "extreme right-wing groups." The amendment, which was a response to events involving right-wing groups dressed in uniforms, reportedly extends hate crimes legislation to include intimidation of ethnic, racial and religious or other groups. Furthermore, a 2011 Hungarian government report to the United Nations states that, in order to "prosecute the illegal activity of people not willing to acknowledge" the Hungarian Guard's court-ordered dissolution, there is legislation ordered to prosecute participation in the activities of the dissolved social organization as an infringement of the law. The law prosecutes even wearing clothes that resemble to the uniform, usual attire of the dissolved social organization and makes it possible to impose even custodial sentences. [35] In April 2010, charges were filed with the public prosecutor against the chief of the Hungarian Guard and two of his associates on "suspicion of illegally maintaining the uniformed organization". The AP similarly reported that on the same day, police filed charges against three leaders of the Hungarian Guard. Because the group was still operating in spite of its ban, the charges also included "incitement against authorities' decrees". Nine days later, MTI-Eco News reported that police filed charges against ten leaders of the banned Hungarian Guard for violating the laws of assembly. In July 2010, police arrested more than 70 members of the Hungarian National Guard at demonstrations in Budapest that were attended by around 600 members of the organization to commemorate the banning of the Hungarian Guard. Police also arrested another 20 Hungarian National Guard members attending the same rallies because they were in possession of minor firearms. [36] Having considered the totality of the evidence, the panel finds that the claimants, in the circumstances of this case, have failed to rebut the presumption of state protection with clear and convincing evidence. They did not report all incidents of violence to the police and especially did not show the police their anonymous threats that they received or come forward with witnesses who could have attested to the problems and threats from their neighbours which would have helped in the police investigations. [37] With respect to the minor claimants, the only incident of alleged discrimination concerned the younger boy's failure to be considered for an advanced class in pre-school. The panel does not find that there was sufficient credible evidence presented to show that the child's distant Jewish ancestry was the factor responsible for this school decision. [38] Because of the availability of state protection the panel concludes that the claimants would not face persecution or, on a balance of probabilities, face a risk to their life or to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or to a danger of torture, if they returned to Hungary. For all these reasons, the panel finds that the claimants are not Convention refugees or persons in need of protection.
