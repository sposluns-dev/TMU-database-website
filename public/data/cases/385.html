Motifs et décision Persons who are the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX XXXXXXXX XXXX XXXXXXXX XXXX XXXXXXXX XXXX XXXXXXXX XXXX XXXX XXXXXXXX XXXX Personnes en cause Appeal considered / heard at Ottawa, Ontario Appel instruit / entendu à Date of decision January 7, 2020 Date de la décision Panel Me Murielle Henri Tribunal Counsel for the persons who are the subject of the appeal Clémence Marie Camille Chevalier Conseil des personnes en cause Designated representative XXXX XXXX Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] The principal male appellant, XXXX XXXX, his spouse XXXX and their son XXXX are citizens of Lebanon and Israel. Their sons, XXXX, XXXX and XXXX XXXX are citizens of Israel. [2] The principal male appellant alleges that he was forced to join the Lebanese army after the invasion of Lebanon in 1982. He was considered to be a traitor and had to move to Israel with his family in 2000, where they were mistreated, intimidated, harassed and discriminated against by both Jewish Israelis and Muslim Arabs. They fear persecution and the sons fear they will be forced to join the Israeli army to fight Lebanon if they return to Israel. The Minister intervened and alleged that the appellants are not credible. [3] On May 10, 2019, the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) rejected the appellants' claim for refugee protection on the ground that the principal appellant was not credible. The appellants are appealing against the decision and allege that the RPD erred in finding that they are not at risk of persecution in Israel. DETERMINATION [4] The appellants have not demonstrated that they would be subject to persecution based on their race and religion, even if considered cumulatively, were they to return to Israel. They also did not demonstrate, on a balance of probabilities, the existence of a risk under subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). ADMISSIBILITY OF EVIDENCE SUBMITTED ON APPEAL [5] In support of their appeal, the appellants presented three articles as new evidence. These articles did not arise after the rejection of their claim; two of the articles are from 2014 and the other is from 2017. Furthermore, the appellants did not demonstrate that this evidence was not reasonably available or that they could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented it at the time of the rejection. Consequently, I do not accept these articles as new evidence. DECISION WITH RESPECT TO AN ORAL HEARING BEFORE THE RAD [6] The evidence presented is not admissible. The request for an oral hearing is dismissed. ANALYSIS [7] I analyzed the record, the RPD decision and the appellants' memorandum, considered the documentary evidence and listened to the recording of the RPD hearing. I also considered the appellants' additional submissions. [8] In this case, the determinative issue is whether the appellants are subject to discrimination that amounts to a risk under sections 96 or 97 of the IRPA. Therefore, I will not address the arguments pertaining to the RPD's analysis of the appellants' credibility. Breach of procedural fairness [9] In the memorandum, the appellants allege a breach of procedural fairness caused by translation errors during the hearing and in the Basis of Claim Form (BOC Form). [10] I am not of the same opinion. [11] During the hearing, the appellants were able to correct the translation errors and the RPD considered the appellants' corrected responses in making its decision. [12] Consequently, I am of the opinion that there was no breach of procedural fairness. Discrimination versus persecution [13] The RPD concluded that the appellants were not victims of persecution in Israel. In their memorandum, the appellants maintain that the RPD erred in concluding that they were not persecuted in Israel. [14] I am of the opinion that the conclusion of the RPD is correct. [15] It appears from the appellants' testimony that they are afraid to return to Israel because in that country they are considered to be inferior. People spit on them, throw rocks at them and set dogs on them. In his BOC Form, the principal appellant states that they remained in XXXX for four years, that is until 2004, where they were verbally abused and considered to be traitors. Their sons were unable to integrate into school, were struck by other children who told them [translation] "you are traitors", and their bicycles were stolen. In 2004, they moved to XXXX and lived there for 13 years, or until 2017. In his BOC Form, the principal male appellant states that they were looked down upon and their sons were bullied and discriminated against because of their religion. At school, their sons were denied the right to a proper education and food and were not allowed to practice their religion. At the hearing, the appellant stated that one of his sons was attacked by the neighbour's dog and the appellant filed a complaint with the police, who came to give the neighbour a warning. The appellant alleges that a fire was set in a storage area next to their house. [16] For the following reasons, I am of the opinion that, in Israel, the appellants were subject to discrimination that does not amount to persecution. In this case, I considered paragraphs 51 to 55 of the Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) handbook and the definition of the word "persecution" given by professor James C. Hataway and the courts to the effect that persecution is a "sustained or systematic violation of basic human rights demonstrative of a failure of state protection."1 Thus, for discrimination to amount to persecution, it must be serious and occur with repetition, and must have consequences of a prejudicial nature for the person, such as when an individual is denied fundamental human rights such as one's right to practice religion or to earn a livelihood.2 Education [17] The documentary evidence mentions that there are Jewish, Arab and mixed schools in Israel.3 According to the principal male appellant's testimony, his sons were able to attend school and he was allowed to choose the school. When they lived in XXXX on their own, the oldest son was of school age and he attended the Jewish school for one year before they moved because he was not accepted by the Arab schools. The principal appellant mentioned that his sons attended school in XXXX and confirmed that his son XXXX, completed grade 11, did not fail a grade, but was denied the right to be placed at a higher level, which prevented him from accessing higher-level instruction for those who do well at school.4 The female appellant stated that her sons XXXX and XXXX failed a grade and were placed in the class of weak students from then on even though they were both good students. Her son XXXX XXXX did not fail a grade. According to the female appellant's testimony, she complained to the school authorities and tried have her sons placed in more advanced classes, but to no avail. In the memorandum, we read that the children were not deprived of food at school and that it was actually the bullying and discrimination that caused them to lose their appetite.5 [18] In light of the foregoing, I am of the opinion that the associate appellants did have access to education while living in Israel. I am therefore of the opinion that the children's right to education was not infringed. Work [19] The principal appellant never worked in Israel. According to the female appellant, this was because he did not speak Hebrew and was stressed. When asked by the RPD if he had the right to work, the female appellant answered that he did have the right to work. As for the female appellant, she was a XXXX from 2009 to 2014, and then was unemployed until their departure in 2017. [20] The appellants did not demonstrate that they had any restrictions on holding employment in Israel. Housing [21] The appellants were able to find housing during the 17 years they lived in Israel. They lived for four years at the same address in XXXX and then they lived in XXXX for 13 years. The appellants did not mention that they had problems with housing. [22] Consequently, I am of the opinion that they did not demonstrate that they could not find housing. Religion [23] According to the memorandum, the appellants lived in the Jewish quarter and could not practice their Christian faith. If they did, they were stoned and threatened. Furthermore, the male appellant states that they could not barbeque on Saturdays. When walking around the city, people insulted Jesus and spat on them. In additional submissions, it states that when they went to church, officers asked them what they were doing there and pressured them not to practice their religion. There was also an incident one Saturday when they lit a fire in their yard and people yelled at them. When they wore religious symbols, people told them to take them off. Furthermore, on one occasion, a teacher at the school saw his son's cross, asked him to take it off and a student grabbed the cross from around his neck. In his memorandum, the male appellant states that it is easy to say that the law recognizes the different religions, but the reality is that people of different religions are treated differently in Israel. [24] It is well established that the analysis does not end just because a country purports to conduct itself in a democratic manner. Instead, it is important to determine, based on the evidence, whether and the degree to which its state institutions uphold democratic protections and ideals. [25] The National Documentation Package (NDP) provides little evidence on the conditions in the country showing that there are legal or other impediments preventing Christians from practicing their faith in Israel.6 On the contrary, the NDP states that there is legal protection for the practice of Christianity. On page three of Tab 12.1, we read that according to Supreme Court rulings the basic law on human dignity and liberty protects freedom to practice religious beliefs, including freedom of conscience, faith, religion and worship, regardless of an individual's religion. Furthermore, we read that the law recognizes Christianity and that it is prohibited to disturb any meeting of persons lawfully assembled for religious worship and the law criminalizes destroying, damaging or desecrating any object held sacred.7 The NDP also states that when a fundamental right is violated, Israeli citizens, including those of Arab descent or Christians, have administrative and legal recourse.8 [26] In light of the aforementioned, I am of the opinion that the events set out by the appellants do not represent persecution and that state protection exists for the practice of their religion. Recourse to the police and firefighters [27] In his BOC Form, the male appellant mentions that he contacted the police several times, but they did nothing. According to the appellants' testimony, they called the police on two occasions and in two different cities: once when their children were attacked and their bicycles stolen, and another time when a dog was set on them. In the case of the dog incident, the police came to the scene, asked the owner to put the dog in the house and gave the owner a warning. It appears therefore that the police went to the scene, took the complaint, prepared a report and asked the neighbour, in the dog incident, to remedy the problem. In my view, this shows that the police take action. Consequently, I am of the opinion that the appellants did not demonstrate that the state refuses or is unable to protect them. [28] At the hearing and in the memorandum,9 it was stated that some pieces of wood in a storage area behind the appellants' house was set on fire. The appellants put out the fire and did not call the police because they did not think that they would come. At the hearing, the principal appellant stated that he did not know who started the fire. Thus, he does not know who set the fire or why. Furthermore, it is impossible to know if the fire was set intentionally. [29] Consequently, this allegation does not demonstrate that it was an act of persecution against the appellants. Additionally, as they did not call the fire department, they did not demonstrate that the firefighters would not have come to help. [30] In summary, I am of the opinion that the discrimination alleged by the appellants does not amount to persecution. In fact, it was not demonstrated that their fundamental rights were denied. They did not demonstrate that their access to accommodation, employment and education was denied. There were no allegations that their access to social services was denied. Nor did they demonstrate that the discrimination they experienced was serious, occurred repeatedly and caused grave harm. Military service [31] The principal appellant alleges that they were persecuted by agents of the Israeli state so that his sons would perform military service. Additional submissions state that the agents told them that their lives would be at risk and that they would always be under surveillance if their sons did not perform military service. [32] The documentary evidence indicates that military service is not compulsory. Some people, including Christians, are exempt.10 In addition, it states that there is an alternative service program in which exempt individuals can participate and thereby receive benefits similar to those accorded military veterans.11 Therefore, the documentary evidence does not demonstrate that the state would persecute minorities as a way of compelling them to do military service. [33] Consequently, I am of the opinion that it was not demonstrated that the sons would have to perform military service in Israel. [34] Considering that they were able to find housing for 17 years, that the associate appellants were able to study, that the female appellant was able to work in Israel, that they were able to exercise their religion and that they were able to turn to the police, I conclude that the appellants did not establish a serious possibility of persecution. They did not establish, on a balance of probabilities, the existence of a risk under subsection 97(1) of IRPA if they were to return to Israel. State protection [35] The RPD's finding regarding state protection is not disputed by the appellants. [36] Having reviewed the entire record, I am of the opinion that the RPD was correct in finding that there is state protection in Israel. CONCLUSION [37] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, I confirm the determination that the appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. [38] The appeal is dismissed. (signed) Murielle Henri Murielle Henri January 7, 2020 IRB translation Original language: French 1 Ranjba v.
