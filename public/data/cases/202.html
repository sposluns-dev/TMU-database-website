Green Party of Canada, 2010 BCSC 804 Date: 20100609 Docket: S087690 Registry: Vancouver Between: John Shavluk Plaintiff And Green Party of Canada, Green Party of Canada Fund and Elizabeth May Defendants Corrected Judgment: A spelling error of a surname was corrected at paragraphs 61 and 95 where changes were made on June 21, 2011. June 9, 2010 Introduction [1] The plaintiff, John Shavluk, claims against the defendants, Green Party of Canada, Green Party of Canada Fund and Elizabeth May, for damages in defamation and conspiracy. Mr. Shavluk had been nominated to be a candidate for the Green Party of Canada in the riding of Newton-North Delta in the federal election held on October 14, 2008. On September 4, 2008, the Green Party issued a press release that announced, in part, that Mr. Shavluk would not be a Green Party candidate "following revelations that he made comments in 2006 on an online discussion forum that could be construed as anti-Semitic." [2] Mr. Shavluk alleges that the press release was false and defamatory and that the publication was motivated by malice. Mr. Shavluk alleges further that Ms. May defamed him again on September 16, 2008 in a conference call interview that was later posted on the internet. [3] In addition, Mr. Shavluk alleges that the defendants participated in an on-going conspiracy to injure him, particulars of which include the publication of a document dated April 7, 2009 entitled "Report and Recommendations: John Shavluk Complaint", as well as the passage by the Green Party National Council of Resolution G08-23, which precluded any member who had initiated legal action against the party or its officers from running for or holding elected office within the party. [4] The defendants take the position that the statements were not defamatory. In addition, the defendants have pleaded the defences of justification, fair comment, qualified privilege and responsible communication. Finally, the defendants deny the allegations of conspiracy. Facts [5] Mr. Shavluk is a passionate advocate for the legalization of marijuana. These efforts have included his involvement over the years with national political parties and his political efforts to change the laws pertaining to marijuana. His activities in support of this effort have included making numerous postings on various internet discussion forums. On November 4, 2006, Mr. Shavluk made a posting on the "Frost Cloud" forum that set in motion the events culminating in this litigation. The complete post is reproduced at Appendix A to these Reasons. The passage in the posting that was the source of the reaction of Ms. May and the Green Party was as follows: hey i heard some guy in Australia knows someone who says he had something to do with your governments complicate attack on your shoddily built Jewish world bank headquarters. you know "the 2 towers" (who has the ring i wonder)better invade there too eh,,oh no oil? He later decided to seek nomination as a candidate for the party in the next federal election. He submitted an application in March 2007. As part of this process Mr. Shavluk testified he went through a vetting process in which he provided a list of the forum sites on which he posted. He discussed his beliefs and his political activism. [7] It was his testimony that the link to the Frost Cloud forum would have come up in a search of his postings although this should be viewed in some context. Mr. Shavluk was clearly a frequent communicator on internet forums. For example, he had apparently contributed over 1,100 postings to the Frost Cloud forum alone by 2006. The Green Party issued a press release dated May 17, 2007 acknowledging his success in winning the nomination. Mr. Shavluk assured her that he would. Defamation [41] In an action for defamation, a plaintiff must establish: (a) that the words complained of were published; (b) that the words complained of refer to the plaintiff; and (c) that the words complained of, in their natural and ordinary meaning, or in some pleaded extended meaning, are defamatory of the plaintiff. See Hodgson v. Canadian Newspapers Co. (1998), 39 O.R. (3d) 235 at 248 (Gen. Div.), varied on other grounds (2000), 49 O.R. (3d) 161 (C.A.). [42] In the case at bar, there is no issue with respect to publication or that the words complained of referred to Mr. Shavluk. [43] Mr. Shavluk has alleged that the September 4, 2008 press release was false and defamatory. His pleading asserts "the said words meant and were understood to mean that the plaintiff held views which are considered odious by right-thinking people and that the plaintiff was unfitted to hold his said office or any office of trust." [44] Mr. Shavluk also alleges that the words in the press release were false, particulars of which are: a) The plaintiff never made any statements of an anti-Semitic nature in 2006, but had merely repeated comments of others which were already in the public domain. b) Ms. May did not communicate with the plaintiff as claimed in the press release. c) Ms. May had already signed the plaintiffs nomination papers, contrary to the claim made in the press release. [45] Mr. Shavluk has further alleged that certain statements made by Ms. May in the September 16, 2008 interview were defamatory. With respect to those statements, his pleading asserts that the "said words meant and were understood to mean that the Plaintiff held views which are considered odious by right-thinking people and that the Plaintiff was unfitted to hold his said office or any office of trust." [46] Mr. Shavluk also alleges that the statements made by Ms. May in the telephone conference were false. In particular, he alleges: a) The plaintiff made no statements in his blog which could reasonably be construed as unacceptable. The plaintiff had merely repeated comments of others which were already in the public domain. b) Ms. May had already signed the plaintiff's nomination papers. [47] The position of the defendants is that Mr. Shavluk is restricted to the defamatory sense alleged in the pleadings and that the defamatory sense alleged is not capable of sustaining a cause of action. The defendants note in particular that Mr. Shavluk has not alleged that the defamatory sense of the communications is that he is anti-Semitic or that he holds anti-Semitic views. [48] The court is to determine what meaning would be conveyed by the communication to the reader including the inferences and implications which the words might reasonably bear, see N R Developments Ltd. v. Thomas, 2002 BCSC 697. The next question is whether the meaning conveyed would tend to lower the plaintiff in the estimation of reasonable members of society. The test was stated by Madam Justice Abella, for the Court, in Color Your World Corp. v. Canadian Broadcasting Corp. (1998), 38 O.R. (3d) 97 at 106, 156 D.L.R. (4th) 27 (C.A.) as follows: I take as my starting point the following definition of defamation: A defamatory statement is one which has a tendency to injure the reputation of the person to whom it refers; which tends, that is to say, to lower him [or her] in the estimation of right-thinking members of society generally and in particular to cause him [or her] to be regarded with feelings of hatred, contempt, ridicule, fear, dislike, or disesteem. The statement is judged by the standard of an ordinary, right-thinking member of society. Hence the test is an objective one ... (R.F. Hueston & R.A. Buckley, Salmond on the Law of Torts, 21st ed. (London: Sweet & Maxwell, 1996) at p. 140 citing Sim v. Stretch 1936, 52 T.L.R. 669 at p. 671, and Vander Zalm v. Times Publishers (1980), 109 D.L.R. (3d) at 531 at pp. 535 and 543, 18 B.C.L.R. 210 (C.A.)). The standard of what constitutes a reasonable or ordinary member of the public is difficult to articulate. It should not be so low as to stifle free expression unduly, nor so high as to imperil the ability to protect the integrity of a person's reputation. The impressions about the content of any broadcast - or written statement - should be assessed from the perspective of someone reasonable, that is, a person who is reasonably thoughtful and informed, rather than someone with an overly fragile sensibility. A degree of common sense must be attributed to viewers. [49] With respect to the September 4, 2008 Press Release, reading the publication as a whole, I conclude that from the combination of the reference in the first paragraph to comments "that could be construed as anti-Semitic", the statement "We condemn anti-Semitism" in the second paragraph and the statement in the third paragraph that "he will not be a candidate because his views are not consistent with our philosophy" the ordinary reader would conclude that Mr. Shavluk holds views that are anti-Semitic. Further a reasonable person would understand from the press release that as a consequence of these views, Mr. Shavluk is unsuitable to hold an office of trust as a Member of Parliament. [50] With respect to the September 16, 2008 interview, in my view a reasonable person would conclude from these statements that Mr. Shavluk had made unacceptable, hate filled statements in his blog postings and that as a consequence he was not suitable to hold an office of trust as a Member of Parliament. [51] In my view, both statements convey a defamatory meaning that would tend to lower Mr. Shavluk's reputation in the estimation of reasonable members of society. [52] The defendants have submitted that Mr. Shavluk has not alleged that the defamatory sense of the communications is that he is anti-Semitic or that he holds anti-Semitic views and that he is restricted to the defamatory sense in his pleadings citing Slim v. Daily Telegraph Ltd., [1968] 1 All E.R. 497, [1968] 2 Q.B. 157 (C.A.). However, it is my view that while it is not open to the trier of fact to find a different and more injurious meaning than that alleged in the pleading, it is open to find a different meaning that is not more injurious. [53] Here there can be no suggestion that the defendants would be taken by surprise at the defamatory meaning of anti-Semitism as those were the very words used in the press release. Further, the meaning alleged in the Amended Statement of Claim is not in my view less serious. Hence, it is open to the court to find the meaning that I have held is the natural and ordinary meaning. [54] The defendants then submit that where comments are critical of a plaintiff's conduct in the public sphere, in this case his capacity to be a candidate for Parliament, those comments cannot be defamatory. The defendants submit that comments focusing on the personal philosophy of a public figure cannot support a cause of action based upon defamation, citing Lund v. Black Press Group Ltd., 2009 BCSC 937 [Lund]. [55] The issues in Lund arose from critical statements published concerning the plaintiff's conduct as the elected director for the Juan de Fuca electoral area in the context of a highly controversial issue. Mr. Justice Bracken noted that greater, but not unlimited, latitude is granted with respect to criticism of the performance of a public functionary. He introduces this idea at para. 110 as follows: ... An individual who accepts political office can expect a certain amount of attack and criticism on the ground that the public interest requires than an individual's public conduct should be open to searching criticism and that the holder of a public office should be prepared for critical appraisal of his conduct: Vander Zalm v. Times Publishers, A Division of F.P. Publications (Western) Ltd. (1980), 109 D.L.R. (3d) 531 (B.C.C.A.) at para. 5. [56] To further define the scope of the latitude, at para. 115 Bracken J. cites McLachlin v. Orr, Pollock and Co., 2 S.L.T. 275, [1894] 22 R. 38: It is only when private character is attacked, or when the criticism of public conduct is combined with the suggestion of base or indirect motives that redress can be claimed on the ground of injury to reputation. The claim in such cases may either take the form of an issue of damages founded on specific misrepresentation, as if a member of Parliament should be accused of bribery, or a member of a local board of corruptly influencing the disposal of public contracts, or where no specific charge has been named the pursuer may be entitled to an issue of holding up to public hatred, ridicule and contempt. But I venture to think that an issue of the last mentioned form ought not to be granted except where the libel imputes moral depravity of some kind, or is capable of being read as containing such an imputation, it being for the jury to say whether the purpose of the libel was to exhibit the pursuer as having laid himself open not merely to ridicule but to the odium of his associates and fellow citizens. [57] At para. 118 Bracken J. notes: The words were critical of the plaintiff and it is plain that they were intended to be critical of his performance as the elected director for the Juan de Fuca electoral area. But in my view, the comments did not go beyond legitimate criticism of a public official in the context of a highly controversial issue. While this criticism might not be recognized as justified in the case of a private person, it is acceptable as part of the public interest right to bring criticism to bear respecting the conduct of public officials: Langlands v. John Lang and Co. (1916), 1 S.L.T. 168, (1916) S.C. (H.L.) 102, where it was stated: The first thing I wish to say is that, in dealing with a newspaper written about a public functionary, considerable latitude is allowed by the law. Any gentleman who takes the position of architect under the School Board of Dundee is filling a capacity as a public official, and he must expect criticism, such criticism as the law might not recognize as justified in the case of a merely private person but readily admits when it is in the public interest that criticism should be brought to bear upon the conduct of public officials. When a person is in a public capacity, he may be criticized by the newspapers in the public interest; and that rebuts the presumption of malice in law which the court might otherwise make, and leaves malice in fact to be proved, and malice in fact to be found in either the special language of the article or in circumstances proved which point to some motive of enmity of the particular individual. [58] In my view, while Mr. Shavluk was seeking office, the comments at issue were not made in relation to his performance of a public function. Accordingly, the comments are not within the scope of what Bracken J. termed the "legitimate criticism of a public official acting in his official capacity" that any candidate for public office must accept. Rather, it was his private character or conduct that was the subject of the statement. Accordingly, I find that the statements are defamatory. Defences Justification [59] In R.E. Brown, The Law of Defamation in Canada, 2nd ed., (Toronto: Thomson Canada Ltd., 1999), the author notes at pp. 10-2 to 10-4: Justification or the truth of the matter asserted is a complete defence to an action for defamation. The published material must be false. "What is true cannot be defamatory." No one can be heard to complain if only the truth is published about him. A plaintiff has no right to have his or her character or reputation free of an imputation that is true. [60] The defence of justification was described by Saunders J.A., writing for the court, in P.G. Restaurant Ltd. (c.o.b. Mama Panda Restaurant) v. Northern Interior Regional Health Board, 2005 BCCA 210, 38 B.C.L.R. (4th) 77 at para. 33, Supplementary Reasons at 2005 BCCA 288, leave to appeal ref'd [2005] S.C.C.A. No. 270, as follows: ... The law on justification is simple to state, although more difficult to apply, and is described in Brown, The Law on Defamation in Canada, 2nd ed., vol. 1, at p. 513; quoting from Price v. Chicoutimi Pulp Co. (1915), 51 S.C.R. 179 at 199-200: The defence of justification will succeed if the defendant proves the truth of "every injurious imputation which the jury finds to be conveyed by the publication." Thus, if the defendants establish that the defamatory statements are true with respect to the injurious sting of the statements, it will be a complete defence. [61] Mr. Shavluk has argued that he never made any anti-Semitic statements but merely repeated comments of others. First, it is by no means clear to me that the portion of the posting at issue is a repetition of comments made by others or if so which portion. In particular it is not clear to me that the reference to "shoddily built Jewish world bank" in the positing is a repetition of a comment made by someone else. However in any event it is irrelevant whether Mr. Shavluk originated the comments or repeated them. By posting the comment he has made the statement. This is an application of the repetition rule which was described as follows by Chief Justice McLachlin in Grant v Torstar Corp, 2009 SCC 61, [2009] 3 S.C.R. 640 [Grant] at para. 119: The "repetition rule" holds that repeating a libel has the same legal consequences as originating it. This rule reflects the law's concern that one should not be able to freely publish a scurrilous libel simply by purporting to attribute the allegation to someone else. The law will not protect a defendant who is "willing to wound, and yet afraid to strike": "Truth" (N.Z.) Ltd. v. Holloway, [1960] 1 W.L.R. 997 (P.C.), at p. 1001, per Lord Denning. In sum, the repetition rule preserves the accountability of media and other reporting on matters of public interest. The "bald retailing of libels" is not in the public interest: Charman, per Sedley LJ., at para. 91. Maintaining the repetition rule is particularly important in the age of the internet, when defamatory material can spread from one website to another at great speed. Thus, it is the case for purpose of consideration of the justification defence that Mr. Shavluk made the statements at issue. [62] In my view, the 2006 posting is a statement that could be construed as anti- Semitic and accordingly, that particular defamatory sting has been justified. However, I have held that the press release, read as a whole, also carries the sting that Mr. Shavluk holds anti-Semitic views. Mr. Shavluk has denied that he holds such views and emphasized that the 2006 posting was a repetition of a statement made by others. The statement is equivocal in that respect. It is not clear from the statement which part, if any, of the passage Mr. Shavluk adopts. His evidence is that his purpose in making the posting was to make a sarcastic contribution to the ongoing discussion thread concerning the drug laws. [63] The defendants, citing Minors v. Toronto Sun Publishing Corp. (1999), 122 O.A.C. 43 [Minors] submit that in order to succeed in this defence, it is sufficient to establish, as they have done, that the statement could be construed as anti-Semitic. They do not need to prove the truth of the insinuation that Mr. Shavluk holds anti-Semitic views. In Minors, the trial judge found that the press had accurately quoted the plaintiff's statement that the Holocaust was not a racist act. The plaintiff argued that the defence of justification could not succeed because the readers would draw the insinuation from the statements that the plaintiff was anti-Semitic and the defendants had not proved that he was anti-Semitic. The trial judge found no merit to this argument concluding that the words quoted had a meaning that was plain and obvious. The Court of Appeal sustained this finding. [64] In the present case, unlike Minors, the defamatory sting does not reside with the words originally published by the plaintiff. These words are not reproduced in the press release. In addition, the press release stated that Mr. Shavluk had published statements that could be construed as anti-Semitic, which I have found to be justified. However, the press release went further and stated that, "he will not be a candidate because his views are not consistent with our philosophy". This adds a separate defamatory sting which must be justified. [65] The burden of proof is on the defendants. I find that the defendants have not discharged this burden with respect to the defamatory sting that Mr. Shavluk held anti-Semitic views. Fair Comment [66] The elements of the defence of fair comment were stated by Tysoe J.A., speaking for the Court, in Creative Salmon Co. v. Staniford, 2009 BCCA 61, 90 B.C.L.R. (4th) 328 [Creative Salmon], as follows at para. 25: the test for the defence of fair comment may now be stated as follows: (a) the comment must be on a matter of public interest; (b) the comment must be based on fact; (c) the comment, though it can include inferences of fact, must be recognizable as comment; (d) the comment must satisfy the following objective test: could any person honestly express the opinion on the proved facts?; and (e) the defence can be defeated if the plaintiff proves that the defendant was subjectively actuated by express malice. [67] The word "comment" was defined in Grant as follows at para. 31: In addition to privilege, statements of opinion, a category which includes any "deduction, inference, conclusion, criticism, judgment, remark or observation which is generally incapable of proof" (Ross v. New Brunswick Teachers' Assn., 2001 NBCA 62, 201 D.L.R. (4th) 75, at para. 56, cited in WIC Radio, at para. 26), may attract the defence of fair comment. [68] For the comment to be fair, the facts are to be fairly stated. With respect to the fundamental issue of the fairness of the comment, Tysoe J.A. stated in Creative Salmon at para. 61: I agree with the authors of The Law of Defamation in Canada and Gatley on Libel and Slander that the requirement to state the facts truly means in the present context that the commentator may not omit to state important or material facts that would falsify or alter the complexion of the facts stated in the commentary. It is not necessary to state all facts of a nature that may influence the opinion of the person hearing or reading the commentary. In order to defeat the defence, the omitted facts must be sufficiently fundamental that they undermine the accuracy of the facts expressed in the commentary to the extent the stated facts cannot be properly regarded as a true statement of the facts. [69] The facts must be sufficiently stated, or otherwise known so that the listener can arrive at his own conclusion with respect to the comment. As Justice Binnie, writing for the majority in WIC Radio Ltd. v. Simpson, 2008 SCC 40, [2008] 2 S.C.R. 420, stated at para. 31: It is true that "[t]he comment must explicitly or implicitly indicate, at least in general terms, what are the facts on which the comment is being made"; Brown, vol. 2, p. 15-36, and Gatley on Libel and Slander (10th ed. 2004), at par. 12.12. What is important is that the facts be sufficiently stated or otherwise be known to the listeners that listeners are able to make up their own minds on the merits of Mair's editorial comment. If the factual foundation is unstated or unknown, or turns out to be false, the fair comment defence is not available (Chicoutimi Pulp, at p. 194). [70] With respect to the first issue, I find that the matter is one of public interest. Mr. Shavluk had chosen to run for public office and as a consequence both his publications and his personal views were a matter of public interest. [71] As noted in Grant at paras. 137-139, some statements could legitimately be construed as either fact or opinion. In this case, with respect to the issue of distinguishing fact and comment, that a person has published a statement is a matter of fact. That such statements and views are anti-Semitic, or unacceptable, hate-filled can be viewed as a matter of opinion. [72] Further, in my view, those comments satisfy the objective test. That is, with respect to the press release, a person could honestly express the views that the 2006 post is anti-Semitic and that a person who would publish such a statement holds anti-Semitic views. With respect to the September 16 conference call, a person could honestly express the view that the 2006 post was unacceptable and hate-filled. [73] The difficulty here, however, is that the factual foundation in both the press release and the September 16th conference call were neither sufficiently stated nor known since neither set out the posting by Mr. Shavluk giving rise to the issue. Therefore the readers of press release and audience of the conference call could not make up their own minds about the merits of the comment. Accordingly, the defence of fair comment is not available to the defendants. Qualified Privilege [74] The purpose of the immunity provided by the defence of qualified privilege is stated in The Law of Defamation in Canada at pp. 13-15 to 13-19: The protection ... is justified on the basis of public policy and utility, and in furtherance of the "common convenience and welfare" or "general interest" and "advantage" of society. ... The purpose of the immunity is not so much to protect the parties involved as it is to promote the public welfare. ... As Bankes J. in Gerhold v. Baker [[1918] W.N. 368 at 368-369 (C.A.)] said: "It was in the public interest that the rules of our law relating to privileged occasions and privileged communications were introduced, because it is in the public interest that persons should be allowed to speak freely on occasions where it is their duty to speak, and to tell all they know or believe, or on occasions when it is necessary to speak in protection of some common interest." [Emphasis added.] [75] Qualified privilege was described in Hill v. Church of Scientology, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 1130 at para. 143 [Hill], by Cory J. as follows: Qualified privilege attaches to the occasion upon which the communication is made, and not to the communication itself. As Lord Atkinson explained in Adam v. Ward, [1917] A.C. 309 (H.L.), at p. 334: . . . a privileged occasion is . . . an occasion where the person who makes a communication has an interest or a duty, legal, social, or moral, to make it to the person to whom it is made, and the person to whom it is so made has a corresponding interest or duty to receive it. This reciprocity is essential. [76] The factors to be considered by the court in deciding if the occasion is one of qualified privilege were summarized by Williams J.A., speaking for the Court, in Moises v. Canadian Newspaper Co. (1996), 24 B.C.L.R. (3d) 211 at para. 19 (C.A.) as follows: There are a number of factors which a court must consider when deciding whether or not any given occasion is one of qualified privilege. In Sapiro v. Leader Publishing Co., [1926] 2 W.W.R. 268 at 271, 20 Sask. L.R. 449 (Sask. C.A.), Lamont J.A. said: In determining whether or not it is so privileged, the Judge will consider the alleged libel, who published it, why, and to whom, and under what circumstances. He will also consider the nature of the duty which the defendant claims to discharge, or the interest which he claims to safeguard, the urgency of the occasion, and whether or not he officiously volunteered the information, and determine whether or not what has been published was germane and reasonably appropriate to the occasion. [77] Justice Cory in Hill described at paras. 145-6 the two circumstances in which qualified privilege will be lost. In sum, they are: (1) Malice - which includes actual or express malice, publication from an indirect or ulterior motive that conflicts with the sense of duty or the mutual interest which the occasion created, and publication in which the defendant spoke dishonestly or in knowing or reckless disregard for the truth; or, (2) Where the limits of the duty or interest have been exceeded anything that is not relevant and pertinent to the discharge of the duty or the exercise of the right or the safeguarding of the interest which creates the privilege will not be protected. [78] With respect to the issue of the limits of the duty or interest, the test was stated in Ward v. Clark, 2001 BCCA 724, 95 B.C.L.R. (3d) 209 at para. 56 [Ward] as follows: The law was stated thus by Lord Atkinson in Adam v. Ward, [[1917] A.C. 309, [1916-17] All E.R. Rep. 157, 86 L.J.K.B. 849 (H.L.)] at p. 173: These authorities, in my view, clearly establish that a person making a communication on a privileged occasion is not restricted to the use of such language merely as is reasonably necessary to protect the interest or discharge the duty which is the foundation of his privilege; but that, on the contrary, he will be protected, even though his language should be violent or excessively strong, if, having regard to all the circumstances of the case, he might have honestly and on reasonable grounds believed that what he wrote or said was true and necessary for the purpose of his vindication, though in fact it was not so. [79] With respect to the issue of malice, in Smith v. Cross, 2009 BCCA 529, 99 B.C.L.R. (4th) 214, Kirkpatrick J.A., for the Court, at para. 36 cited the following discussion with approval: The question of motive in a defamation case was fully explored in Lord Diplock's speech in Horrocks v. Lowe, [1975] A.C. 135 (H.L.) at 149-50: So, the motive with which the defendant on a privileged occasion made a statement defamatory of the plaintiff becomes crucial. The protection might, however, be illusory if the onus lay on him to prove that he was actuated solely by a sense of the relevant duty or a desire to protect the relevant interest. So he is entitled to be protected by the privilege unless some other dominant and improper motive on his part is proved. 'Express malice' is the term of art descriptive of such a motive. Broadly speaking, it means malice in the popular sense of a desire to injure the person who is defamed and this is generally the motive which the plaintiff sets out to prove. But to destroy the privilege the desire to injure must be the dominant motive for the defamatory publication; knowledge that it will have that effect is not enough if the defendant is nevertheless acting in accordance with a sense of duty or in bona fide protection of his own legitimate interests. The motive with which a person published defamatory matter can only be inferred from what he did or said or knew. If it be proved that he did not believe that what he published was true this is generally conclusive evidence of express malice, for no sense of duty or desire to protect his own legitimate interests can justify a man in telling deliberate and injurious falsehoods about another, save in the exceptional case where a person may be under a duty to pass on, without endorsing, defamatory reports made by some other person. Apart from those exceptional cases, what is required on the part of the defamer to entitle him to the protection of the privilege is positive belief in the truth of what he published or, as it is generally though tautologously termed, 'honest belief'. If he publishes untrue defamatory matter recklessly, without considering or caring whether it be true or not, he is in this, as in other branches of the law, treated as if he knew it to be false. But indifference to the truth of what he publishes is not to be equated with carelessness, impulsiveness or irrationality in arriving at a positive belief that it is true. ... But despite the imperfection of the mental process by which the belief is arrived at it may still be honest', that is a positive belief that the conclusions they have reached are true. The law demands no more. [80] The statements at issue concern the fitness of a candidate for public office and were widely published. There are a series of decisions of the Supreme Court of Canada, which hold that publication of such statements in the press is not an occasion of qualified privilege. For example, the position is summarized by Cartwright J. in Douglas v. Tucker, [1952] 1 S.C.R. 275 as follows: The second basis on which qualified privilege is asserted is that the defendant as an elector, a candidate for election and the leader of his party had a duty to communicate to those having a legitimate interest in the result of such election facts which he honestly believed to be true, relevant to the fitness, or otherwise, for office of other candidates offering themselves for election. It has often been held that qualified privilege attaches to communications made by an elector to his fellow electors of matters regarding a candidate which he honestly believes to be true and which, if true, would be relevant to the question of such candidate's fitness for office. See, for example, Gatley on Libel and Slander, 3rd Edition, pages 250 and 251 and cases there cited. It is unnecessary on this appeal to decide whether such privilege is limited to publications made by an elector and to an elector or electors all of whom have a right to vote for the candidate about whom the communication is made and, if it is not so strictly limited, what is its extent. It is settled that whatever may be the extent of such a privilege it is lost if the publication is made in a newspaper. [81] However, a more recent line of cases can be seen to have expanded the scope of the defence in cases of wide dissemination of matters of true public interest, see for example Stopforth v. Goyer, 23 O.R. (2d) 696, 97 D.L.R. (3d) 369 (C.A.); Parlett v. Robinson (1986), 5 B.C.L.R. (2d) 26 (C.A.), leave to appeal ref'd [1986] S.C.C.A. No. 322.; Ward, supra; and Campbell v. Jones, 2002 NSCA 128, 220 D.L.R. (4th) 201. [82] In the present case, at the time of the publication of the press release giving rise to this litigation, a federal election was imminent. The Green Party had announced the candidacy of Mr. Shavluk with a press release. The decision to remove the endorsement of the party leader from the candidate was in my view a matter about which the public at large had an interest. Accordingly, I find that the occasion was one of qualified privilege. Moreover, I find that neither of the defamatory publications at issue exceeded the scope of the privilege. [83] The final issue then in relation to this defence is that of malice. Mr. Shavluk has alleged in his pleadings that malice can be inferred from the following: (a) the defendants knew of the 2006 post prior to accepting Mr. Shavluk's candidacy; (b) the defendants knew that Mr. Shavluk did not hold anti-Semitic views; (c) the defendants knew that Ms. May had not spoken with Mr. Shavluk at the time of the press release and further that she had already signed his nomination papers. [84] Mr. Shavluk alleges further that the true motivation for the actions of the defendants was to silence his cannabis activism and to retaliate for his support of the candidacy of Dr. Qais Ghanem. He points to a variety of conduct from which, he submits the court should infer that the defendants acted with malice. These included what Mr. Shavluk stated was the failure on the part of any representative of the defendants to contact him with respect to the issue, changes made to the text of the press release, cutting off Mr. Shavluk's access to the Green Party's internet site, the convention resolution, the cut off of the audio stream from the convention and what Mr. Shavluk alleges is Ms. May's manipulation of the OAC. [85] I find that there is no evidence that any of the defendants, or any representative of the defendants knew of, let alone approved of the 2006 post prior to the approval of Mr. Shavluk's candidacy. While it may be that Mr. Shavluk disclosed that he posted on the Frost Cloud forum, one cannot infer from that that anyone read, considered and approved the post in question, particularly given the number of posts Mr. Shavluk authored on the site. Further, there is no evidence of any discussion of the post in the sessions held by party representatives with Mr. Shavluk directed to dealings with press. Rather the attention was focused on his criminal record, and statements that he had been "framed" or "raped" by the justice system. In my view, if representatives of the Green Party had been aware of the posts prior to September 2008, they would have included reference to these as part of the posts he would be called upon by the press to justify. [86] I find further that there is no evidence that the defendants or members of the defendants knew that Mr. Shavluk did not hold anti-Semitic views. [87] I find that Ms. May attempted on two occasions to speak with Mr. Shavluk by telephone on September 4, that she left a message each time. The draft press release was changed to reflect the fact that she had not been able to speak with him directly but had communicated through her telephone message. While Ms. May had approved Mr. Shavluk's nomination, she had not provided the endorsement contemplated by the Canada Elections Act, S.C. 2000, c. 9. [88] With respect to the issue of cannabis activism, the defendants were aware of Mr. Shavluk's position on this issue when his candidacy was approved. Support for the legalization of marijuana was already party of the party platform and Ms. May raised the issue during the campaign. With respect to the notion that Mr. Shavluk was being singled out in retaliation for his support of Dr. Ghanem's candidacy, other candidates who also supported Dr. Ghanem were kept on. There is simply no evidence to support an inference that this was a reason for the actions. [89] It is simply not the case that no one from the defendants contacted Mr. Shavluk about the issue. Rob Hines contacted Mr. Shavluk on September 3 when the issue surfaced. Mr. Hines also spoke with Mr. Shavluk. He later spoke with Mr. Shavluk to inform him of the decision. Ms. May called twice and left messages. The press release was changed, however, the change was to soften the language, to substitute the words "could be construed" for the word "are". In my view, this does not support an inference of malice. [90] Mr. Shavluk was cut off from access to the members' area once he was no longer a candidate but running as an independent in order to protect the membership list and voter's list. He was later cut off from participation apparently due to a complaint. Mr. Shavluk testified that he had been banned from a number of internet forums. The evidence of access to internet forums being cut off does not support an inference of malice. [91] The convention resolution is not, in my view, evidence of malice or an oblique motive in removing his candidacy. As to the cutting of the audio portion of the convention feed, the evidence is that this was caused by a technical problem having nothing to do with Mr. Shavluk. [92] In the result, having considered the evidence as a whole I am satisfied that the decisions to withdraw support for Mr. Shavluk's candidacy, to issue the press release and to make the statements in the September 16 interview were not the result of malice. Rather, the purpose was to distance the party from statements by Mr. Shavluk that were viewed as anti-Semitic and considered to be unacceptable. [93] In the result, I am satisfied that the defendants have made out the defence of qualified privilege. Responsible Communication [94] The defence of responsible communication on a matter of public interest was articulated in the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in Grant. There are two elements to this defence. The publication must be on a matter of public interest and the defendant must show that the publication was responsible in that he was diligent in trying to verify the allegations. Unlike the defence of qualified privilege, the issue of whether the publication is in the public interest is concerned with the nature of the statement and not the nature of the occasion. [95] The first element of the defence is that the matter be of public interest. In that regard, public interest is defined by McLachlin C.J.C., writing for the majority, in Grant as follows at para. 105: To be of public interest, the subject matter "must be shown to be one inviting public attention, or about which the public has some substantial concern because it affects the welfare of citizens, or one to which considerable public notoriety or controversy has attached": Brown, vol. 2, at pp. 15-137 and 15-138. The case law on fair comment "is replete with successful fair comment defences on matters ranging from politics to restaurant and book reviews": Simpson v. Mair, 2004 BCSC 754, 31 B.C.L.R. (4th) 285, at para. 63, per Koenigsberg J. Public interest may be a function of the prominence of the person referred to in the communication, but mere curiosity or prurient interest is not enough. Some segment of the public must have a genuine stake in knowing about the matter published. [96] At para. 126 of Grant, the factors to be addressed in relation to the second element of the defence - whether the defendant acted responsibly - are defined: The publisher was diligent in trying to verify the allegation, having regard to: (a) the seriousness of the allegation; (b) the public importance of the matter; (c) the urgency of the matter; (d) the status and reliability of the source; (e) whether the plaintiff's side of the story was sought and accurately reported; (f) whether the inclusion of the defamatory statement was justifiable; (g) whether the defamatory statement's public interest lay in the fact that it was made rather than its truth ("reportage"); and (h) any other relevant circumstances. [97] On the first element, I am satisfied that the publications at issue were in relation to a matter of public interest. The suitability of a candidate for public office during a federal election is clearly such a matter. [98] With respect to the factors in relation to whether the communication was responsible, the allegation was serious hence requiring an elevated level of diligence. However, in assessing diligence, the circumstances in which this matter arose are important. The defendants learned of the post when approached by the press who intended to publish an article and were seeking a response. Mr. Shavluk was notified immediately, his views were sought. The matter was urgent and the timetable was not in the control of the defendants. In the circumstances, I am satisfied that there was urgency and that the defendants exercised reasonable diligence. [99] The status and reliability of the source are not at issue since the actual post giving rise to the issue was reviewed and Mr. Shavluk's response sought. [100] Neither the press release nor the September 16 interview included either the text at issue or Mr. Shavluk's explanation that he was repeating the views of others. However, this must be balanced against the fact that the purpose of each was not to report on the issue but to set out the position of the Party with respect to the issue. [101] With respect to the issue of the inclusion of the defamatory statement, Mr. Shavluk has submitted that it was not necessary for the Party to have given any reason for the decision, in other words, that it was unnecessary for the statement to be included. However, in my view, such a response would not be realistic in the circumstances. It was appropriate for the reasons to be given for the revocation of support for Mr. Shavluk's candidacy and it was also important to clarify that the Party distanced itself from anti-Semitic views. [102] In all the circumstances, I am satisfied that both statements were protected by the defence of responsible communication. Conspiracy [103] The elements of the tort of conspiracy were described by Justice Estey, for the court in Cement LaFarge v. B.C. Lightweight Aggregate, [1983] 1 S.C.R. 452 at 471-2 as follows: Although the law concerning the scope of the tort of conspiracy is far from clear, I am of the opinion that whereas the law of tort does not permit an action against an individual defendant who has caused injury to the plaintiff, the law of torts does recognize a claim against them in combination as the tort of conspiracy if: (1) whether the means used by the defendants are lawful or unlawful, the predominant purpose of the defendants' conduct is to cause injury to the plaintiff; or, (2) where the conduct of the defendants is unlawful, the conduct is directed towards the plaintiff (alone or together with others), and the defendants should know in the circumstances that injury to the plaintiff is likely to and does result. In situation (2) it is not necessary that the predominant purpose of the defendants' conduct be to cause injury to the plaintiff but, in the prevailing circumstances, it must be a constructive intent derived from the fact that the defendants should have known that injury to the plaintiff would ensue. In both situations, however, there must be actual damage suffered by the plaintiff. [104] Where the conspiracy alleged is a conspiracy to defame and where the alleged defamatory statements were actually published, the tort of defamation and conspiracy merge, see GlobalCom Solutions Inc. v. Nothstein, 2009 BCSC 1229, 77 C.C.E.L. (3d) 64. Accordingly, the allegations of conspiracy in relation to the publication of the press release (para. 5 and 6 of the Amended Statement of Claim) and the September 16 conference call interview are merged with the allegations of defamation. [105] Paragraph 14 of the Amended Statement of Claim pleads that the publication by the defendants of the document "Report and Recommendations: John Shavluk Complaint" was a further step in the conspiracy. This document is the publication of the OAC concerning its investigation of the complaint filed by Mr. Shavluk. There is no unlawful act alleged or established in relation to this document, accordingly it falls to be considered under the first branch of conspiracy. There is simply no evidence that these deliberations were undertaken with the predominant purpose of injuring Mr. Shavluk and nothing to support such an inference; indeed, the body concluded that at least one aspect of his complaint was well-founded and directed the matter of the ratification of the decision to remove the endorsement of Mr. Shavluk's candidacy be referred to Council. [106] Mr. Shavluk alleges in paragraph 20 of the Amended Statement of Claim that the passage of Resolution G08-23 by the National Council was a further act of the conspiracy. There is no evidence or allegation of unlawful conduct in relation to this resolution. Therefore, it is the first branch of conspiracy that is engaged. Mr. Shavluk submits that the fact that he was the only person caught by the application of the Resolution and the circumstances of the vote support an inference that its predominant purpose was to harm him. I can find no evidence of any irregularity or suspicious circumstances in relation to the vote. [107] Moreover, the fact that Mr. Shavluk was the only person caught by the resolution does not support the inference he seeks. The motion appears to have been prompted by the unusual circumstances created by Mr. Shavluk's litigation in conjunction with his campaign for elected office in the party. It is not an unreasonable response to that circumstance and was passed by the majority of those voting. There is no evidence that its predominant purpose was to harm Mr. Shavluk and the evidence does not support such an inference. [108] While not pleaded, it was suggested through the course of the trial that the decision to revoke the endorsement of Mr. Shavluk's candidacy was an act in furtherance of the conspiracy. The revocation was consistent with the provisions of the Canada Elections Act. Given the status of the campaign manager during a campaign, it is arguable whether there was a requirement for ratification by the Council since the campaign manager had assumed the functions of Council at the relevant time and had ratified. However, the OAC concluded otherwise and held that ratification was necessary. That ratification occurred. Accordingly, the revocation of the endorsement was lawful. I am satisfied that the decision to revoke the leader's endorsement of Mr. Shavluk's candidacy was not for the purpose of injuring Mr. Shavluk; rather, for the reasons given, that his positing reflected a view that was inconsistent with the fundamental principles of the Green Party. [109] In the result, I conclude that Mr. Shavluk has not established that there was a conspiracy under either branch of the tort. Summary [110] In the result, I have concluded that statements in both the press release and the September 16, 2008 conference call interview defamed Mr. Shavluk, were not justified on the evidence before me, and were not fair comment. However, I have concluded that the occasion of their publication was one of qualified privilege and that there was no evidence of malice. Further, I have held that the defendants have established the necessary elements of the defence of responsible communication. Neither branch of the tort of conspiracy has been made out. Accordingly, the action is dismissed with costs to the defendants. "Ross J."
