SUMMARY OF ALLEGED FACTS [2] The specifics of the claim are set out in the narrative of the principal claimant's Personal Information Form2 (PIF) and her testimony. The following is a summary: a) The claimants resided in the town of XXXXX in Russia and are citizens of Russia and Jewish by ethnicity. b) The principal claimant was subjected to verbal abuse and physical harm by her neighbors and by co-employees at work because of her Jewish nationality. Her son was also teased with insulting names at school and was forced to climb a tree, resulting in his fall and breaking his forearm. c) She reported the incidents of assault to the police but the police simply ignored her complaints because she was a Jew and was told to deal with her problems on her own. d) Fearing for their lives and the lack of police protection, the claimants left Russia, arrived in Canada on XXXXX, 2008, and filed their refugee protection claims on the same date. DETERMINATION [3] I find that the claimants are not Convention refugees pursuant to section 96 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act. I also find that, on a balance of probabilities, the claimants are not persons in need of protection under section 97 (1)(b) of the IRPA. ANALYSIS Identity [4] I find the claimants citizens of Russia. This has been established by prima facie evidence of their Russian passports and the principal claimant's testimony.3 Discrimination vs. Persecution [5] The principal claimant gave evidence as to the humiliation, harassment and physical assaults inflicted on her and her son as a result of their Jewish nationality. In 1999, a female neighbor, who was drunk and angry, approached her and called her racist names and pushed her, resulting in an injury to her XXXXX. In her Personal Information Form, the claimant had five (5) employers from XXXXX 2000 to XXXXX 2008. In 2005, she was subjected to anti-Semitic acts by her work supervisor. And in XXXXX 2008, she had an argument with a female co-worker and was set up and beaten by RNU nationalists. Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practice his religion or his access to normally available educational facilities.4 [6] Those incidents of mistreatment of the claimant may constitute discrimination or harassment, yet they are not serious enough to be within the context of the ascribed meaning of persecution. It is essential that the inflicting of harm occurs with repetition or persistence, or in a systematic way, and that the alleged persecutory acts threaten basic human rights in a fundamental way. In my view, the sole incident in 1999 of being insulted with anti-Semitic remarks and pushed by a drunken female neighbor, the space of time of the incidents and the different employers involved in those two confrontations in her work place may amount to discrimination or harassment, yet not serious enough to rise to the level of persecution. Perception of Jewish Nationality/Ethnicity [7] To prove her Jewish ethnicity, the claimant submitted a document5 from the Archive of the Department of Civil Status Registrations of XXXXX Region, showing the nationality of her maternal grandparents as Jewish. She also submitted a copy of her birth certificate6 showing the Russian nationality of her parents. Granting without admitting that the claimant is Jewish, the fact of the matter is that she is of Russian nationality by her birth certificate. Physical appearance is never a yardstick or an indication of someone's nationality or ethnicity. There was no evidence adduced in the hearing that the claimant was identified as a Jew by her attackers because of her physical appearance. Except for her statement in her narrative that she and her husband tried to socialize with Jewish families prior to the 1999 incident, there was no evidence alleged in her narrative or given in her oral testimony as to her attending Jewish synagogues or being actively involved in Jewish activities in Russia to be perceived as a Jew. There was no evidence submitted to establish her profile that she is a Jew or perceived to be a Jew. On the other hand, her birth certificate gives her Russian nationality, as her parents are stated to be of Russian nationality in her birth certificate. Her being perceived as a Jew is a key element of the claim. The RPD is not required to request relevant documents or information to establish the elements of the claim. This burden rests on the claimant7 and none was provided. Credibility [8] The principal claimant did not provide sufficient credible evidence in support of her claim. [9] The principal claimant testified that she fears the anti-Semitic groups, the Russian nationalists group and the Russian people in general because they do not like Jewish people. She provided evidence by way of the narrative to her PIF that in 1999, she was assaulted by a female neighbour while doing some gardening in her yard and resulted in her XXXXX being broken. She provided a medical certificate.8 The English translation of the certificate does not show any date, but the original (Russian) version shows a date with the year 1998. When asked to explain the inconsistent dates of occurrence of the incident, the claimant simply said that "I may have made a mistake on the dates. I had so much stress. I don't remember exactly." When asked if she had these medical certificates (including that for her son for his injury in XXXXX 2003) with her when she came to Canada, she testified that she did not have them as she was in a rush to leave Russia, she was so tired and she wanted to get away from the situation after that incident on XXXXX, 2008. When told that she did not leave Russia till XXXXX, 2008, she responded that that was not enough time to obtain those documents. The claimant testified that her mother mailed her these medical certificates but she could not remember when she received them. When asked if she had a medical certificate for the medical treatment she received at the polyclinic as a result of the assault on her by a group of RNU nationalists on XXXXX, 2008, she said that she asked for it at the clinic but "they did not have any." [10] The claimant completed the narrative to her PIF on January 17, 2009, with the assistance of counsel. Her mother in Russia sent her the medical certificate in question. Unfortunately, she could not remember when she received it, although it was translated into English very recently, in March 2012. Oddly enough, she remembered all the other details of her claim. The claimant did not rectify the alleged mistake in her narrative in placing the first incident of physical assault on her in 1999, after she received the medical certificate in or before March 2012. She did not set right the date of her assault when she was asked during the hearing of her claim if she reported that incident in 1999 to the police. The claimant was duly represented by counsel at the hearing. It was only when the inconsistency between her date of the incident as against the date on the medical certificate was pointed out during the hearing when she simply and conveniently stated that she could have made a mistake on the dates as she had so much stress. She had a month to settle down from the time she arrived in Canada on December 21, 2008, until she completed her PIF on January 17, 2009. Likewise, her testimony that she was not given a medical certificate for the medical treatment she received in XXXXX 2008 after she asked for it, is not quite believable. If it was not available on that day she requested for it, there was no valid reason not to get it a few days after. The claimant did not provide any explanation as to why no medical certificate was obtained by her mother for that XXXXX 2008 incident, given that her mother obtained for her medical certificates for those previous incidents in 1998 and 2003. The claim rests on the physical assaults inflicted on the claimant because of her Jewish ethnicity, resulting in her subjective fear. Evidence of the physical assaults in terms of police reports and medical certificates attesting to the medical treatment received for those physical assaults are key elements to her claim. Given all these circumstances, I draw a negative inference as to the truthfulness of the physical assaults on the principal claimant. I am not prepared to grant that presumption of truth to the claimant's allegations, as there are some reasons to doubt their truthfulness. While it may be an error to require documentary evidence to support the claimant's allegations, if there are valid reasons to question her credibility, and there are in this claim, the claimant's failure without a reasonable and justifiable explanation to provide any document to support the alleged physical attack on her in XXXXX 2008, which was the final straw for her to finally decide to leave Russia, is a significant factor in assessing the credibility of the claimant. When a refugee claimant's credibility is already called into question, it is justifiable for the RPD to draw a negative inference from failure to corroborate material elements of the story.9 The burden lay upon the claimant to produce documentary evidence in support of key elements of the claim.10 State Protection [11] The claimant did not provide clear and convincing evidence to rebut the presumption of state protection. [12] The claimant must also establish that she has a valid objective basis for her fear. This fear must be assessed in light of the situation in her home country. Although the Board's documentation and the claimant's documentary evidence report that Jewish sites, like synagogues and graves, were targets of hate vandalism, the availability of anti-Semitic literature, the rise of nationalist groups, that many hate crimes, including those motivated by anti-Semitism, are prosecuted only as 'hooliganism' and that law-enforcement bodies do not always properly investigate crimes committed against religious organizations, creating an atmosphere of 'impunity', there were also several instances in which the government successfully prosecuted individuals for anti-Semitic activities, statements or publications. In September 2009 skinheads in Khabarovsk threw Molotov cocktails into a synagogue and into the house of a policeman who had been investigating cases of extremism. Khabarovsk Anti-extremist Department police detained the group, and criminal proceedings were opened against two of the suspects. They faced up to five years' imprisonment for the synagogue attack and up to life imprisonment for the attack on the police officer. On March 12, a court in Izhevsk gave a one-year suspended sentence to neo-Nazi Russian National Unity member Andrey Mokrushin for painting swastikas and anti-Semitic threats on the walls of a local Jewish community center. On February 3, a court sentenced the editor of the anti-Semitic newspaper Orthodox Rus to three years in a prison colony for inciting ethnic and religious hatred for distributing an anti-Semitic film, Russia with a Knife in the Back. On June 30, the editor of the newspaper Russian Truth was fined 450,000 rubles ($14,720) for inciting ethnic hatred in a 2006 publication entitled Why don't people like the Jewish mafia.11 [13] The principal claimant gave evidence that she reported that incident of physical assault inflicted on her in 1999 to the police, and that her complaint was ignored by the police. They would not deal with her complaint as she was Jewish, that she went to them constantly with her previous complaints and that she was told she should deal with her complaints on her own. In 2005, her work supervisor placed a pair of scissors on her back and threatened to kill her if she did not leave her workplace. When asked if she reported that incident to the police, she testified that she did not because the police ignored her previous complaints. When asked if she approached or went to higher police authorities or other government agencies to follow up on her complaints, she testified that she did not, as she did not know where to complain. The fact that the claimant made no attempts to find out where and how to seek assistance from higher police authorities or other government agencies does not constitute an adequate search for state protection, just because certain police officers refused to intervene, especially in a democratic state.12 The evidence provided does not establish that making additional attempts to obtain state protection would have been objectively unreasonable. The burden of proving the ineffectiveness of state protection lies with the claimant. The claimant did not do so. [14] The Board's country documentation13 on the situation of Jews in Russia also confirms Estimates of the number of Jews living in Russia vary. The 2002 national census counted 233,439 Jewish people in Russia. The United States Department of State similarly estimates that 250,000 of Russia's 142 million people are Jews (US Sept. 2008, Sec. 1; ibid. 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 2). However, some media sources estimate that as many as 1,000,000 (Reuters 26 Feb. 2008; ibid. 6 May 2008) or 1,500,000 people in Russia are Jews (AP 9 Nov. 2007). Minority Rights Group International (MRGI) indicates that the Jewish population in Russia has decreased by half since 1989 due to emigration in the years leading up to and following the collapse of the Soviet Union. Sources report that most Jews live in urban areas, particularly in Moscow and St. Petersburg (US Sept. 2008, Sec. 1). Sources indicate that there has been a revival of the Jewish religion and culture in Russia (MRGI n.d.), including the construction of a number of new synagogues and cultural centres (AP 9 Nov. 2007; FJNCA 9 July 2009). According to Russian Jewish leaders, there are more than 200 Jewish communities throughout Russia, 5 Jewish universities, 94 Jewish Sunday schools (FJNCA 9 July 2009; US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 2), 50 synagogues and community centres, and 29 Jewish day schools (FJNCA 9 July 2009). There are also plans to establish a museum commemorating the Holocaust (ITAR-TASS 13 Aug. 2009; US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 2). According to the US Department of State's Country Reports on Human Rights Practices for 2008, the Moscow municipal government has donated land to house a number of Jewish community buildings, including a school, hospital and museum. Several sources also indicate that some Russian Jews who emigrated from Russia to Israel, the US, or other Western countries, are returning to Russia for job opportunities (Reuters 26 Feb. 2008; MRGI n.d.; US 25 Feb. 2009, Sec. 2). The President of the Russian Jewish Congress, as reported by Reuters, estimates that 80,000 to 120,000 Russian Jews have returned (6 May 2008). There are a number of national Jewish organizations active in Russia, including the Federal Jewish National and Cultural Autonomy, the Federation of Jewish Communities of Russia, and the Confederation of Jewish Organizations and Communities of Russia (FJNCA 9 July 2009). Russia's chief rabbi, Berel Lazar, is affiliated with the Federation of Jewish Communities of Russia (FJC n.d.a), which receives support from international foundations, non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and the Israeli government. [15] Sadly, anti-Semitism is experienced all over the world; more particularly in Russia, where there is a large concentration of Jews. While there have been violent acts against persons and property in Russia, and while any act of hate is abhorrent, the number of incidents cited in conjunction with the Jewish population of 250,000 to 1,500,000 in Russia, the reported revival of the Jewish religion and culture in Russia, the reported return of 80,000 to 120,000 Jews to Russia, the reported more than 200 Jewish communities throughout Russia, 5 Jewish universities, 94 Jewish Sunday schools, 50 synagogues and community centres, and 29 Jewish day schools and most importantly, the claimant's lack of profile to be singled out and targeted with anti-Semitic acts and the panel's findings of the lack of credibility of her allegations of physical assaults inflicted on her as a result of her Jewish nationality, do not result in a serious possibility that she would be persecuted or that she would be subjected, on a balance of probabilities, to a risk to life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a danger of torture, if she were to return to Russia. Internal Flight Alternative (IFA) [16] I find that there is a viable internal flight alternative for the claimants. [17] The leading case on IFA14 directs the Board to use a two-pronged test in considering whether a potential IFA presents itself. Firstly, the Board must be satisfied that there is no serious possibility of persecution in the part of the country in which the IFA is located. If the Board is satisfied of this, then, secondly, it must further consider whether or not it would be reasonable, in all of the circumstances, for the claimant to seek refuge there. [18] The claimant was asked if she could relocate to other cities in Russia, like St. Petersburg, and live there with relative safety. She testified that Jewish people are not liked all over Russia. The panel has determined that the claimant did not establish a profile of her Jewish ethnicity in Russia. Her birth certificate shows that she is of Russian nationality. It is highly unlikely that she would be identified or perceived to be of Jewish ethnicity if she were to relocate to other cities where nobody knows her. Hardships associated with dislocation and relocation are not, in themselves, sufficient for an IFA to be considered unreasonable.15 Relocating to St. Petersburg, or any other city in Russia, may be difficult but not unduly harsh for her. Coming to Canada was not a lot easier for the claimant, as she does not speak the language and has no relatives to rely on. I find that the claimant has a viable IFA. CONCLUSION [19] Based on the foregoing analysis and considering the totality of the evidence, I find that the principal claimant has not established the subjective basis for her fear of persecution and that there is not a reasonable chance or serious possibility of persecution, if the claimants were to return to Russia. I also find that, on a balance of probabilities, the claimants would not be personally subjected to a risk of torture, a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, if they were to return to Russia. Accordingly, their claims are rejected.
