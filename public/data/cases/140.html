RAD File No. / No de dossier de la SAR : MC0-08482 Private Proceeding / Huis clos Reasons and Decision - Motifs et décision Person who is the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX XXXX Personne en cause Appeal considered at Ottawa, Ontario Appel instruit à Date of decision August 24, 2021 Date de la décision Panel Me Philippe Rabot Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Me Molly Joeck Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] The appellant, XXXX XXXX XXXX, is a citizen of Cameroon. He is alleging a risk of persecution by reason of his ethnicity as a member of the Bamileke community. The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) concluded that there was no serious possibility of persecution stemming from the appellant's ethnic profile. He is arguing that this conclusion is erroneous because it does not take into account the cumulative effect of the discrimination against Bamilekes. He is also presenting new documentary evidence regarding recent incidents of violence and hate speech targeting the Bamileke community. DETERMINATION [2] The appeal is dismissed. Despite recent disturbing incidents suggesting that ethnic tensions have escalated between Bamilekes and the country's majority Francophone group, the Betis, the situation has not reached a point where it would be reasonable to conclude, on a balance of probabilities, that there is a serious possibility that the appellant would face persecution because he is a Bamileke. BACKGROUND [3] The appellant and his family lived in Douala, located in a French-speaking region of Cameroon. He is Francophone and belongs to the Bamileke ethnic group. His spouse is Anglophone. Three incidents in 2017 and 2018 reportedly prompted the appellant to leave the country. [4] First, in XXXX 2017, the appellant was assaulted when he and his spouse travelled by bus to an English-speaking region of the country. He asserted that he was attacked because he is Francophone. [5] Then, in XXXX 2017, the appellant was assaulted again, this time, according to him, because former co-workers were disciplined after he complained to his employer about their behaviour toward him. [6] Lastly, in XXXX 2018, the school where his daughter was studying in Douala was burned down. The appellant claimed that this was due to the fact that it was an Anglophone school. [7] The appellant then immediately arranged for his family to leave Cameroon, deeming that the situation was no longer safe for them. He came to Canada alone in XXXX 2018 and promptly filed a claim for refugee protection. He stated that one of his daughters is studying in the United States, another in British Columbia, and his other two children and his spouse are still in Cameroon. [8] The appellant argued that the security situation in Cameroon has greatly deteriorated in recent years, particularly due to a conflict between the Anglophone and Francophone communities. He asserted that Bamilekes, who are mainly Francophones but live in large numbers in the Anglophone regions, have been particularly affected by the conflict. This situation is allegedly [translation] "a time bomb." [9] According to the RPD, the documentary evidence in the National Documentation Package (NDP) on Cameroon contains no indication that Bamilekes are being persecuted.1 Furthermore, since the appellant lived in a Francophone region, there would be little chance that he would become a victim of the conflict now raging in the country's two Anglophone regions. [10] The appellant criticizes the RPD for underestimating the extent of discrimination against Bamilekes no matter where they are in Cameroon. He also claims that the security situation in Cameroon has worsened since the RPD's decision because of several different conflicts that are currently raging. In particular, the appellant reports increased tensions between Bamilekes and the main Francophone group that runs the country, the Betis. Regarding this matter, he is providing new documentary evidence reporting on violence and hate speech against Bamilekes by a minister in the government of Cameroon. ADMISSIBILITY OF NEW EVIDENCE [11] Under subsection 110(4) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), I can only admit evidence: i) that arose after the RPD rendered its decision; ii) that was not reasonably available at the time of the decision; iii) that the person could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented to the RPD before the decision was rendered; and iv) If it meets one or more of these requirements, I must decide whether the evidence is new, credible and relevant before admitting it.2 [12] I conclude that all nine documents submitted by the appellant are admissible. Seven of these were published after the RPD's decision and relate to recent events. Their relevance is clear, as they provide a better understanding of the seriousness of the current situation in Cameroon, including the treatment of the Bamileke community. The other two documents were published prior to the RPD's decision but provide a better understanding of the current context in light of the other documents provided in evidence. As such, I consider them relevant as well. All documents appear to be from credible sources. They are reports by renowned journalists. [13] Moreover, I reject the appellant's request for an oral hearing, as I do not consider it essential for assessing his credibility in light of the new evidence. [14] Here are the nine documents that I am admitting as new evidence: 1. [Tribune] Cameroun: "le 'vivre-ensemble' mis à rude épreuve ['Living together' put to the test]" December 3, 2020; 2. ActuCameroun, "Christian Ntimbane Bomo: 'Non au tribalisme anti Bamiléké à ciel ouvert dans la Vallée du Ntem' [Christian Ntimbane Bomo: 'No to open anti-Bamileke tribalism in the Ntem Valley']" August 16, 2020; 3. Freedom House, Freedom in the World 2020: Cameroon, March 4, 2020; 4. Human Rights Watch, "COVID-19 brings out government's ugly side in Cameroon," May 14, 2020; 5. Jeune Afrique, "Cameroun : un ministre accusé de propos antisémites après avoir visé les Bamilékés [Cameroon: A minister accused of anti-Semitic remarks after targeting Bamilekes]" February 4, 2019; 6. CamerounWeb, "Violences de Sangmélima: la vérité qu'on a caché au people [Sangmélima violence: The truth that was hidden from the people]" October 17, 2019; 7. Bertelsmann Stiftung Transformation Index, BTI 2020 Country Report: Cameroon; 8. Crisis Group, Easing Cameroon's ethno-political tensions, on and offline, December 3, 2020; and 9. Rights for Peace, Fanning the flames: Hate speech and elections in Cameroon, November 17, 2020. ANALYSIS [15] I have reviewed all of the information contained in the appellant's new evidence. This does not allow me to conclude that there is a political climate in Cameroon that has resulted in discrimination or even violence against the Bamileke community to a significant degree. There were some isolated incidents, but the reaction of the society in general was not to let itself be dragged into a movement of collective hatred that some people would have wanted to incite. [16] Articles published by Bertelsmann Stiflung and Freedom House point out that in the 2018 presidential election, a candidate from the Bamileke minority, Maurice Kamto, ran against outgoing President Paul Biya, who is from the Betis community. Kamto and 200 of his supporters were arrested for denouncing the fraud that took place during the counting of votes. Kamto himself was detained for nine months. Biya's re-election has plunged the country into turmoil, with several conflicts raging at the same time, namely the Boko Haram threat in the north, the Anglophone crisis in the southwest and northwest, and the tension between Bamileke and Beti people. [17] The International Crisis Group report indicates that political tensions between these two groups have increased hostility between them. In February 2019, a government minister compared Kamto and his Bamileke supporters to the Jews of Europe during the Holocaust, which was interpreted as a threat that they would be killed. In October 2019, several members of the Bamileke community were assaulted in Sangmélima. In December 2019, the Catholic bishops of Cameroon warned political elites that they were sowing the seeds of division through their rhetoric, which could lead to ethnic conflict. In August 2020, a major contract held by Bamileke businessmen to build a cocoa processing plant was suspended by the government of Cameroon. [18] The Rights for Peace report indicates that Bamilekes are one of the main groups targeted by hate speech. [19] The appellant rightly argues that he should not have to wait for the violence against the Bamileke community to reach Douala in order to demonstrate that he would face a serious possibility of persecution. However, there is still a need for evidence that the current situation in Cameroon goes beyond isolated incidents. [20] I acknowledge that this evidence establishes that tension is high between Bamileke and Beti people, the majority ethnic group in the country. Moreover, there have been outbursts in terms of speech and behaviour. Is this in itself sufficient to establish that the prospective risk of persecution to which the appellant would be subjected if he were to return to Cameroon is at the level of serious possibility? I consider that that is not what is demonstrated in the evidence presented. [21] I acknowledge that incidents of discrimination that may at first seem insignificant can, through their cumulative effect, amount to persecution. However, the evidence does not establish that the discrimination currently experienced by the Bamileke community has reached this level. [22] The appellant argues that the overall political and security situation in the country, including the Anglophone conflict and the threat of the Boko Haram group, should also be taken into account. In my opinion, it has not been demonstrated that these conflicts have a greater impact on Bamileke individuals than on the rest of Cameroonian society. [23] Furthermore, with respect to the three 2017-2018 incidents raised by the appellant, I see no indication of any prospective risk arising from either his ethnicity or his accusations against his former co-workers. The cause of the last two incidents is not at all clear from the evidence presented. With regard to the alleged assault on the appellant because he was a Bamileke travelling in an Anglophone region, this does not establish that he would be at risk elsewhere in the country, including Douala, his place of residence before coming to Canada. CONCLUSION [24] The evidence does not establish that there is a serious possibility that the appellant is being persecuted because he is a Bamileke. The evidence also does not establish, on a balance of probabilities, that he is personally targeted by a risk to his life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. [25] Therefore, the appeal is dismissed.
