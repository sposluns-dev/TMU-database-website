Singh Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Chloe Turner Bloom Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A ReprÚsentant(e) dÚsignÚ(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX (Appellant), a citizen of Hungary, appealed a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD), dated July 2, 2019, rejecting her claim for refugee protection. DECISION [2] The appeal is allowed. I substitute my own decision that the Appellant is a Convention refugee. [3] In reaching this decision, I have considered the Guidelines on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-Related Persecution (Gender Guidelines).1 BACKGROUND [4] The Appellant alleged before the RPD that she experienced discrimination and racism in Hungary in education, employment, healthcare, and in her daily life because of her Roma ethnicity and that she will continue to face the same or worse if she returns. [5] The Appellant's application for refugee protection was heard on May 16, 2019. By a decision of July 2, 2019, the RPD rejected her claim. The RPD found that, although the Appellant faced instances of discrimination, this did not rise to the level of persecution; and the Appellant's prospective treatment, cumulatively, would not rise to the level of persecution. The RPD also found that the Appellant's subjective fear was undermined due to a two-year delay in making her refugee claim. [6] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in its assessment of the Appellant's forward-looking risk, incorrectly determined that discrimination did not amount to persecution, failed to consider all country conditions evidence, and failed to assess state protection. ROLE OF THE RAD [7] My role is to look at all the evidence and decide if the RPD made the correct decision.2 ANALYSIS The Appellant has established a serious possibility of persecution The RPD's Findings [8] The RPD found the Appellant did not face discrimination in being able to access housing and that the Appellant would be able to find housing upon return to Hungary. The RPD also found that the Appellant experienced some discrimination in education, but that there was no risk of discrimination or persecution in education for her on a forward-looking basis, as there was no indication she would continue with further schooling. With regard to employment, the RPD did not find that the Appellant faced discrimination, and found that she would not face a serious possibility of persecution in her ability to work and support herself should she return to Hungary. The RPD also found that the Appellant faced incidences of discrimination in healthcare and in public by Hungarians, but that these did not amount to persecution. The RPD found that the Appellant's instances of discrimination did not cumulatively rise to the level of persecution, and that her prospective treatment may include discrimination, but cumulatively, it did not rise to the level of persecution. The RPD also found that the Appellant's subjective fear was undermined due to a two-year delay in making her refugee claim. The Appellant's Arguments [9] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred as follows: i) in its assessment of the Appellant's forward-looking risk by only considering her past experiences and not considering the country conditions evidence about the treatment of Roma individuals in Hungary. The Appellant points to several areas in the objective evidence to show the risks she faces in Hungary; ii) it was incorrect for the RPD to claim she did not establish a link to the country conditions evidence after finding she experienced discrimination; iii) the RPD ignored binding jurisprudence and the evidence of similarly situated individuals; iv) with regard to education and employment, the RPD erred in its assessment of whether the Appellant's past experiences contributed to her overall experience of persecution and forward-looking risk because the RPD's assessment was without regard for the Appellant's experiences of discrimination and the country conditions evidence, and a consideration that as a Roma woman, the Appellant was particularly vulnerable to discrimination; v) with regard to the Appellant's allegations of hate speech, violence, and difficulty with healthcare and housing, the RPD erred in failing to consider these allegations in light of the country conditions evidence; vi) with regard to delay, it is not determinative of subjective fear and cogent reasons were provided; and vii) in finding that her experiences did not cumulatively amount to persecution. viii) The Appellant also argues that adequate state protection would not be reasonably forthcoming and the Appellant points to several areas in the objective evidence to show that state protection is inadequate. The RAD's Assessment [10] I have reviewed the record and conducted an independent assessment of the evidence. I find the Appellant's arguments regarding the RPD's errors and the cumulative effect of the discrimination she experienced and would experience on a forward-looking basis in Hungary have merit. I find the RPD erred in assessing whether the Appellant's experiences of discrimination amounted to persecution cumulatively, both in the past and on a forward-looking basis. The RPD erred by finding that the Appellant had not established a link between the documentary evidence and her personal circumstances. There is, as the RPD notes, case law which states that not all Roma individuals are refugees based upon the country documents and that the Appellant must establish a link between their circumstances and the country documentation.3 However, I find the RPD's reasons as to why the Appellant has not established a link between the documentary evidence and her specific evidence are incorrect. [11] The documentary evidence, which the RPD accepts, demonstrates that Roma individuals in Hungary face discrimination in various areas, including employment, education, housing, medical care, and access to public spaces. The RPD accepted that the Appellant experienced discrimination in several of these areas. Given the evidence which was accepted as credible, I find such a link exists. Here, the Appellant is a young woman with limited education, who had difficulty obtaining and maintaining employment, received inadequate healthcare, and was the victim of physical and verbal assaults. Given these circumstances, I find the RPD erred by failing to consider the country evidence as a context in which to determine whether the Appellant has a forward-facing risk in Hungary. For these reasons, I find that the RPD erred in its assessment of cumulative discrimination. [12] On an independent assessment of the record, I find that the Appellant has established a serious possibility of cumulative discrimination amounting to persecution based on her Roma ethnicity. [13] In coming to this decision, I have considered the facts accepted by the RPD; namely, that the Appellant is of Roma ethnicity, she faced discrimination in education, healthcare, and in public by Hungarians, including physical and verbal assaults. [14] As explained in further detail below, the objective evidence supports that Roma individuals face discrimination in all fields of life and that violence against Roma individuals is an ongoing problem. Considering the Appellant's personal circumstances and experiences in Hungary, I find the Appellant would face a risk from the nationalist Hungarians and anti-Roma groups. [15] I accept that, when some of these facts are considered separately, they may be considered discrimination, but when I consider these facts cumulatively, together with the Appellant's personal circumstances, the risk she would face from the nationalist Hungarians and anti-Roma groups, and the context of the country conditions, I find that the Appellant faces a serious possibility of persecution. [16] With respect to the Appellant's personal circumstances, I find that they increase the risk of discrimination in the future. First, the Appellant is a XXXX XXXX XXXX-year-old female, she has some secondary education, but she was unable to obtain employment in Hungary. These personal circumstances increase the instability that the Appellant will face in Hungary. The documentary evidence shows that women generally, in Hungary, and Romani women in particular, experience higher levels of discrimination and violence.4 Additionally, given the discrimination the Appellant faced in the past and her difficulties in obtaining and maintaining employment in Hungary, she is more vulnerable to future unemployment or underemployment which will affect her ability to get housing and secure a stable life. [17] Similarly, when I consider the country conditions more broadly, I find this also supports my conclusion that the Appellant faces a serious possibility of persecution. As outlined above, the RPD found that the Appellant had not established a link between her circumstances and the country conditions, and I find this to be incorrect. The country documents, broadly, show that Hungarian Roma individuals face discrimination "in all fields of life, including education, housing, employment, healthcare ... and political participation."5 Given that the RPD accepted the Appellant's personal circumstances and she personally faced discrimination in education, healthcare, and in public by Hungarians, I find that this establishes a connection to the country documents. [18] I find the Appellant testified credibly about her experiences of discrimination and her fears should she return to Hungary. I have considered that testimony, as well as the information contained in the Appellant's Basis of Claim form, and I find that the Appellant experienced incidents of discrimination which ultimately led her to leave her home country. I am guided by the UNHCR handbook which states that: 54. Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practice his religion, or his access to normally available educational facilities. 55. Where measures of discrimination are, in themselves, not of a serious character, they may nevertheless give rise to a reasonable fear of persecution if they produce, in the mind of the person concerned, a feeling of apprehension and insecurity as regards his future existence. Whether or not such measures of discrimination in themselves amount to persecution must be determined in the light of all the circumstances. A claim to fear of persecution will of course be stronger where a person has been the victim of a number of discriminatory measures of this type and where there is thus a cumulative element involved.6 [19] In additional to the above, I have also considered the following documentary evidence regarding the situation for Roma individuals in Hungary. The United States Department of State report notes that human rights non-governmental organizations continued to report that Roma individuals suffer social and economic exclusion and discrimination in almost all fields of life.7 It is also noted in this document that Romani woman can suffer multiple forms of discrimination on the basis of gender, ethnicity, class and that they experienced barriers to equal access in education, healthcare, housing, employment, and justice. [20] There exists prejudice by the police against the Roma community and ethnic profiling. It is noted that police response to hate crimes is not adequate.8 There is evidence that right-wing paramilitary groups organize demonstrations against Roma individuals, Jews, and homosexuals.9 Racist violence against Roma individuals remains one of the most pressing issues in the country. Between January 2009 and September 2013 there were more than 100 cases of alleged hate-motivated violence targeting Roma individuals and/or their property. Roma individuals are often subjected to hate speech. These problems have been sustained by the continued hostility Roma individuals face from police forces and officials, reflected in persistent discriminatory practices including ethnic profiling and fines for even the most minor infractions. This also extends to a failure to protect Roma community members from attack.10 [21] Roma individuals in Hungary face discrimination in all fields of life including education, housing, employment, healthcare, and political participation. The Hungarian Ombudsman has reported that the disadvantageous social situation of Roma individuals is aggravated by discrimination in all of these fields. Direct and indirect discrimination in employment prevents many Roma individuals from breaking the cycle of poverty, and Roma individuals are in the most disadvantaged position in the labour market.11 A report from the Hungarian Central Statistics Office cites that the employment rate for Roma individuals was only 25.9 percent in 2014, as compared to the employment rate for non-Roma individuals, which was 60 percent. This report also states that 67.9 percent of Roma individuals were at risk of poverty. It is noted that 500,000 to 600,000 or 60 per cent of Roma individuals live in rural or disadvantaged regions of Hungary, mostly in segregated residential zones in poor conditions, some of which are classified as "impoverished and overcrowded Roma ghettos."12 [22] It is reported that there is an increasing rate of evictions against disadvantaged people and a decreasing amount of social housing. Roma individuals often face discrimination in accessing social housing and municipalities use a variety of techniques to prevent Roma individuals from living in more desirable urban neighbourhoods.13 [23] With regard to healthcare, there is information that Roma women often lack access to medical services due to direct discrimination and degrading treatment at the hospital. It is reported that Roma women are being segregated in certain medical facilities.14 In the Appellant's case, she was not thoroughly examined by the doctor when she complained about pain in her lungs, nor was she prescribed any medical treatment. [24] The documentary evidence references efforts to integrate Roma individuals into the general population. However, there is little information on how the government initiatives have benefited Roma individuals specifically.15 Moreover, the Hungarian state continues to have issues with nationalist groups with anti Roma rhetoric, including members of the government.16 [25] This is not to say that Hungary is not making any efforts to improve the situation of Roma; however, the country conditions show that, currently, the general climate in Hungary for Roma is still rife with discrimination, and it is one which the government is creating a situation of insecurity for Roma people. Moreover, given that the Appellant is a female, these conditions are more likely to have an adverse impact on her life. [26] Having considered the country conditions evidence concerning the situation for Roma individuals in Hungary, as well as the Appellant's personal circumstances and her past experiences of discrimination cumulatively, especially with respect to the physical and verbal assaults she experienced, I find that the Appellant faces an insecure future with regard to accessing adequate education, housing, employment, and medical care. Therefore, I find that the cumulative discrimination she has experienced gives rise to a serious possibility of persecution, should she return to Hungary, and that the Appellant would experience discrimination which would cumulatively lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature that would amount to persecution should she return to Hungary. As this persecution is based upon ethnicity, which is a Convention ground, I find the Appellant meets the test for Convention refugee status. [27] I further find that the documentary evidence indicates the police are not providing adequate state protection with regard to crimes against Roma individuals. It is reported that authorities are not effective in responding to complaints of Roma individuals,17 Roma individuals are not adequately protected against hate crimes,18 Roma individuals are disproportionately the targets of police sanctions, and ethnic profiling occurs.19 I therefore find that adequate state protection would not be available to the Appellant in Hungary. I further find that there is no Internal Flight Alternative available to the Appellant in Hungary, as the situation for Roma individuals is consistent throughout the country. [28] I further find that the issue of the Appellant's delay in making a refugee claim is not determinative in this appeal. CONCLUSION [29] The RAD finds that there is a serious possibility that the Appellant would suffer persecution because of her Roma ethnicity should she return to Hungary. For this reason, the RAD cannot uphold the decision of the RPD. [30] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to section 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes its own determination that the Appellant is a Convention Refugee.
