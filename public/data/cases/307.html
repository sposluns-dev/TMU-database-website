R. v. Cohen

2007 BCCA 276 R. v. Cohen COURT OF APPEAL FOR BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: R. v. Cohen, 2007 BCCA 276 Date: 20070503 Docket: CA034791 Between: Regina Respondent And Alan Robert Cohen Appellant Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Newbury The Honourable Mr. Justice Thackray The Honourable Madam Justice Kirkpatrick Oral Reasons for Judgment P.E. Briggs Counsel for the Appellant W.J.S. Bell Counsel for the Respondent Place and Date: Vancouver, British Columbia 3 May 2007 [1] NEWBURY, J.A.: On September 5, 2003, the appellant, then a 19‑year old high school student, drove his mother's Chevrolet in an exceedingly erratic and dangerous manner along Kingsway in Vancouver. He had a blood alcohol level three and one‑half times the legal limit. Over about three kilometres, he was involved in two collisions with motor vehicles, a near-collision with a bus, and a collision with a cyclist. The cyclist suffered serious head injuries when he was knocked from his bicycle. [2] The appellant did not stop after any of these collisions, but continued driving to an alley in the area of his mother's residence. There, he repeatedly banged into a concrete barrier and fence before backing into a wire barrier and then out of the alley into Glen Drive, where he parked facing northbound in the southbound lane. [3] The police officer who had been alerted as a result of two 9‑1‑1 calls by witnesses arrested him with the assistance of a police dog. Because the police dog bit him, Mr. Cohen required medical treatment at a hospital. There he refused to provide a police officer with a blood sample. However, the police later lawfully seized a blood sample taken for medical purposes about two hours after the incidents. The sample was duly analyzed and found to contain 294 milligrams of alcohol in 100 millilitres of blood. [4] On October 16, 2006, three years later, Mr. Justice Stewart convicted the appellant of six offences arising from these events. Two counts were stayed on the Kienapple principle. On October 27, 2006, Mr. Justice Stewart sentenced Mr. Cohen to two years on Count 1, which was impaired driving causing bodily harm; six months (consecutive) on Count 4 (failing to stop following a collision with a motor vehicle); 12 months on Count 5 (failing to stop following the collision with the bicyclist) concurrent to Count 4; and six months on Count 3, (failing to provide a blood sample) consecutive to Count 5. The global sentence was therefore three and one‑half years. [5] The trial judge was not persuaded that the appellant had accepted responsibility for deciding to drive a car after drinking to excess as he explained at paras. 9 and 11 of his reasons: [9] The offender in the case at bar is now 23 years of age. He was 19, I am told, on September 5, 2003, the date of the offences in question. He has no criminal record. However, what his counsel told me about his lifestyle before September 5, 2003, combines with his B.C. driving record, Exhibit 2, to convince me that for a significant period of time before September 5, 2003, the offender knew he was a drunk and knew that driving while intoxicated would bring him into contact with the law. As he was then an adult with no known mental infirmity, I proceed on the basis that he knew that for him to drink and drive was fraught with danger, danger to himself and to others. [See R. v. Prasad, 2006 BCCA 470, para. 11.] As of September 5, 2003, he was a person who chose to live a lifestyle that involved heavy drinking; and, for September 5, 2003, he was a person who, knowing all of the above, chose not to leave the car at home. [11] He knows he was a drunk on and before September 5, 2003, but blames others for his decision to drink copious amounts of alcohol on a regular basis. He says he has got rid of the bad influences upon him and is prepared to take whatever alcohol counselling courses are ordered. But in my respectful view, the firm awareness that, whether he has cut down or not for now, he is the drunk - he is the problem - just is not evident. I pause to make clear that I read the letters passed up, Exhibit 4, and am alive to the offender's completing a St. John's Ambulance first aid course as detailed in Exhibit 3. The offender told me he apologizes for his conduct on the night in question and is sorry about what happened to Bradley Davidson. I note that the offender's claim that prior to driving on the night in question he was mugged is irrelevant. Then, at paras. 16‑18, the trial judge explained his reasoning on sentence: [16] In my respectful opinion, in determining the applicable "range of sentence" as the Supreme Court of Canada puts in the R. v. Fice, [[2005] 1 S.C.R. 742], at stage 1 of the analysis, I must recognize the cumulate effect of the following: I take Code s. 718, 718.1, and 718.2 as read; the offender's culpability under Count 1 is great - see above about the circumstances in which he chose to drive that night; he chose to drive a long way while drunk that night; his committing hit and run, under Count 4 and Court 5, were entirely separate things from all else, and the global sentence must reflect that fact; committing hit and run under Count 5 in connection with an incident involving a human being, here Bradley Davidson, is an egregious example of blameworthy, cowardly conduct and that must be reflected in the length of the global sentence; the refusal to comply with a demand pursuant to s. 254 for a sample of his blood came at the end of the events that evening and is a separate and distinct crime that must be punished and not treated as of no significance in the sentencing process; as confirmed by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Proulx, [[2000] 1 S.C.R. 742]. at paragraphs 128, 131, and R. v. Homer, 2003 BCCA 15 at paragraph 9, the concept of denouncing crime and deterring like‑minded offenders makes perfect sense when one is dealing with offences of the kind committed by the offender in the case at car, in other words, offences that have their head and source in voluntarily consuming alcohol and then getting behind the wheel of a car: yes, the offender has no criminal record; yes, there is no law that says an order under s. 742.1 necessarily cannot suffice. [17] At this stage of the analysis, a judgment call is involved. [18] In my opinion, considering the cumulative effect of what this case throws up, as above, a global sentence of anything less than three‑and‑a‑half years imprisonment would not be fit. It would, in my respectful opinion, be woefully inadequate. I recognize that the Crown suggested a sentence "in the range of two years." So be it. Counsel make submissions, judges sentence. [6] The appellant makes two basic and related arguments, as I understand Mr. Briggs' submissions. First, he says that the trial judge failed to consider the fact that a period of three years had past between the incidents and the sentencing. During that time, Mr. Cohen apparently had no driving infractions or criminal convictions. He says this shows Mr. Cohen must have taken steps to change his lifestyle and his friends and that therefore the trial judge was wrong to find Mr. Cohen had not accepted responsibility and to recognize that "he is the problem" (para. 11). [7] With respect, I am not persuaded the trial judge erred in this regard. There was no evidence before him, and there was none before us tendered as fresh evidence, to the effect that Mr. Cohen has taken any concrete steps to overcome the "alcohol problem" his counsel acknowledged below. The trial judge had no pre‑sentence report or other background aside from Mr. Cohen's assertion he had stopped seeing his rowdy friends, whom he seemed to blame, at least in part, for his conduct. As far as we know, there has been no change in attitude and the fact that he has been prohibited from driving may well explain the fact that no further offences have occurred. [8] Having said this, I must say I agree with counsel for the appellant that the very cursory manner in which the trial judge dismissed the Crown's suggestion of a two‑year global sentence in this case and ("Counsel makes submissions, judges sentence.") left much to be desired from the viewpoint of the appellant and counsel and this Court. Such remarks can leave the impression of flippancy, which I am sure was not the case. [9] The appellant's second basic submission is that as a global sentence of three and a half years was not fit in this case and falls outside the range of sentences given in similar cases for similar offences. Mr. Briggs emphasizes Mr. Cohen's age - 19 at the time of the offences - and his lack of a criminal record. As well, he seeks to distinguish several of the Crown's cases on the ground that they involved either a death rather than bodily injury resulting from the impaired driving, or involved offenders with very serious criminal records. [10] Mr. Bell for the Crown responds that although several of those cases were "combined cases", i.e. dangerous or impaired driving causing both bodily harm and death, the moral culpability of driving while drunk is very high no matter what the consequence. At the same time, Crown counsel acknowledged that it would have been open for the trial judge to have made the sentence on Count 3 (refusing to provide a blood sample) concurrent with that on Count 5 rather than consecutive. This would result in a global sentence of three, rather than three and a half, years. [11] I am of the view that the global sentence was excessive, given that the Crown's suggested sentence of two years; and, again, the fact the trial judge did not explain his reasons for departing from that; the young age of Mr. Cohen at the time; and the sentences imposed on Counts 1, 4, and 5. These were all at the high end of the applicable range and the addition of another six months was not required to make the point that the appellant had done something very serious and indeed was lucky that the victim had not been killed. [12] I would therefore allow the appeal only to the extent of making the six months' sentence on Count 3 concurrent with that on Count 5. [13] THACKRAY, J.A.: I agree. [14] KIRKPATRICK, J.A.: I agree. [15] NEWBURY, J.A.: The appeal is allowed to the extent I have indicated. "The Honourable Madam Justice Newbury"
