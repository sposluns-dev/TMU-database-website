REASONS AND DECISION INTRODUCTION [1] The principal claimant, XXXX XXXX (TB1-09850), his father, XXXX XXXX (TB1-17422), and his sister, XXXX XXXX (TB1-17424), all citizens of Hungary, seek refugee protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act1 (the IRPA). [2] The claimants' personal identities, relating to their names, their country of nationality, and their ethnicity have been established through the claimants' testimonies and the documentation filed.2 ALLEGATIONS [3] The claimants, as Roma, fear discrimination and racial violence in Hungary. [4] Details regarding the claimants' allegations, going to the basis of their alleged fears, are included in the claimants' narratives.3 Additional details regarding the claimants' allegations were provided by the claimants at the hearing. [5] The principal claimant arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2011 and filed his claim for refugee protection upon arrival at the airport. The principal claimant's father and his sister arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2011; they too filed their claims upon arrival at the airport. DETERMINATION [6] The panel finds the claimants have not satisfied the burden of establishing a serious possibility of persecution for a Convention ground, or that they would personally be subjected, on a balance of probabilities, to a danger of torture, or to a risk to life, or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment upon return to Hungary. [7] Therefore, the panel determines that these claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the IRPA. DETERMINATIVE ISSUE [8] The determinative issue in this case is whether the claimants' fear of harm upon return to Hungary is objectively reasonable. In this regard, the panel considered whether or not there is adequate state protection for these claimants in Hungary, and whether the claimants have provided clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to adequately protect these claimants. ANALYSIS [9] In making the assessment in this case, the panel considered the claimants' testimonies, the claimants' Personal Information Forms (PIFs), including the narratives,4 country documents5 and case law submitted by their counsel,6 documents submitted post hearing,7 and the Board's country documentation.8 [10] Even if a piece of evidence is not referred to herein, the panel carefully considered it as part of the evidence.9 Background: Situation of the Roma in Hungary [11] The Romani community remained the largest ethnic minority in Hungary. According to the Central Statistics Office, in 2007 the Romani community accounted for 2 percent of the population, or approximately 200,000 persons. However, unofficial estimates varied widely and suggested the actual figure was much higher, ranging between 500,000 and 800,000 persons. Human rights non-government organizations (NGOs) reported that Roma were discriminated against in almost all fields of life, particularly in employment, education, housing, penal institutions, and access to public places, such as restaurants and bars. During the year, right-wing extremist groups continued to incite violence against Roma and held marches around the country aimed at intimidating local Romani communities.10 [12] In reviewing the totality of the evidence in this case, the panel acknowledges and considers that there is information in the documentation to indicate that there is widespread reporting of incidents of intolerance, discrimination, and physical abuse of Romani individuals in Hungary. The documentary evidence indicates that criminal acts against Roma are often promoted and carried out by right-wing extremist groups, such as members of the disbanded Hungarian Guard, whose members have continued their activities under different names and in newly formed different organizations.11 [13] Additionally, the right-wing Jobbik Party feeds on sentiments from its constituency, which contributes to the discrimination and physical abuse of the Roma. According to sources, Jobbik is an "extreme right-wing" political party with nationalistic roots and a strong anti-Roma and anti-Semitic agenda. Sources indicate that the Hungarian Guard's popularity, as well as the party's campaign against so-called 'Gypsy crime', significantly contributed to Jobbik's growth." On the other hand, while there seemed to have been some surprise at the Jobbik Party's moderate success in the last national election, in which they became the third-place party with nearly 17 percent of the vote, the documentary evidence also indicates that Jobbik support dropped in April 2011 to 13 percent among likely voters and in August 2011, it was 15 percent among decided voters. To gain back its support, the Jobbik Party has renewed its campaign against Roma with rallies in villages across the country.12 [14] The panel notes that the documentary evidence indicates that Roma are discriminated against in almost all fields of life, particularly in employment, education, housing, penal institutions, and access to public places, such as restaurants and bars, and are subject to violence from racially motivated radical groups. These are, however, complex social issues that must be viewed in the context of the state and its reaction and actions to alleviate such discrimination and criminal acts. [15] It is against this background, and considering the totality of the evidence adduced in this case including these claimants' particular situation and circumstances that the panel must determine whether or not adequate state protection exists for these particular claimants in Hungary. [16] The determinative issue in the case at bar is state protection. State Protection [17] There is a presumption, except in situations where the state is in a complete breakdown, that a state is capable of protecting its citizens, which underscores the principle that international protection comes into play only when a refugee claimant has no other recourse available. A claimant can rebut this presumption by providing clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect the claimant. [18] The burden for the claimant to prove an absence of state protection is directly proportional to the level of democracy in that state. The more democratic the state's institutions, the greater the onus on the claimant to show that he or she has exhausted all courses of action reasonably available to them.13 The documentary evidence14 shows that the claimants as Roma would have recourse to obtain adequate state protection against violence, to seek redress against denial of security services, and to seek remedies against discrimination upon return to Hungary. [19] The documentary evidence15 shows that Hungary is a multiparty parliamentary democracy and that there are free and fair elections; there is a relatively independent and impartial judiciary. The National Police Headquarters (NPH), which operates under the direction of the Ministry of Interior, is responsible for maintaining order nationwide. Nineteen county police departments and the Budapest police headquarters are directly subordinate to the NPH; city police are subordinate to the county police and have local jurisdiction. Two hierarchically equal units are directly subordinate to the minister of interior: the Counterterrorism Center, which is responsible for protecting the prime minister and the president and for preventing and investigating terrorist acts, including kidnapping and hijacking, and the National Defence Service (NDS). The Hungarian Defence Force is subordinate to the Ministry of Defence and is responsible for external security as well as aspects of domestic security and disaster response. The civilian authorities in Hungary maintained effective control over police, the NDS, and the armed forces, and the government has effective mechanisms to investigate and punish abuse and corruption. There is no evidence of a total breakdown of state authority in the country. [20] The burden for the claimant to prove an absence of state protection is directly proportional to the level of democracy in that state. The more democratic the state's institutions, the greater the onus on the claimant to show that he or she has exhausted all courses of action reasonably available to them.16 [21] The documentary evidence17 shows that the claimants as Roma had recourse to obtain adequate state protection against violence, to seek redress against denial of security services, and to seek remedies against discrimination. The claimants were questioned as to what efforts they made to seek state protection before fleeing Hungary. The principal claimant testified that he had not reported any of the incidents to the police. He testified that "Guardists and police have friendship" and that "They [the police] don't take us seriously, they laugh at us, say dirty gypsies you are here again". The principal claimant's father testified that he reported to the police once when the windows were broken. He testified that he called the police but they [the police] did not even come. He testified that "we went to the police station", "they told us to get out of the station" and "told us that they would jail us". He testified that they did not deal with the case. [22] The evidence adduced in this case shows that these claimants might have approached the police regarding some of their problems in Hungary; however, the evidence in this case does not establish that these claimants made a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection from the police in their own home country before seeking refuge abroad. [23] The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate.18 The panel finds the claimants did not provide, on a balance of probabilities, sufficient reliable and trustworthy evidence that the authorities in Hungary would not adequately protect them if they were to make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection from the police. State Response to Violence against Roma [24] The panel accepts that the attitude of some Hungarian people, including some in positions of authority, toward the Roma is certainly discriminatory and prejudicial. The panel also accepts that corruption is a problem in Hungary. Based on the evidence adduced in this case, however, the panel finds that there is legislation and infrastructure in place for Roma to file complaints to the police and obtain protection against racial violence and, moreover, to seek redress if they are denied security services or if their complaints are not taken seriously by the police. In a functioning democracy such as Hungary, the claimant must do more than merely show that he or she went to see members of the police force and that those efforts were unsuccessful.19 [25] Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, the panel finds the claimants did not provide sufficient reliable and trustworthy evidence to show that adequate state protection would not be reasonably forthcoming to these claimants in their particular situation and circumstances. The Board is not obliged to prove that Hungary can offer the claimant effective state protection; rather, the claimant bears the legal burden of rebutting the presumption that adequate state protection exists by adducing clear and convincing evidence that is reliable and probative, which satisfies the Board, on a balance of probabilities.20 [26] Even if these claimants were to have been denied state protection due to their Roma ethnicity and/or corruption in the police, the documentary evidence shows that the government has mechanisms in place to investigate and punish police abuse and corruption. Several government offices were responsible for combating corruption. For instance, the NDS recently commenced operations aimed at eliminating corruption within law enforcement agencies. Organized citizen groups, such as neighbourhood and town watches played an important role in helping police prevent crime. Far-right extremists have taken advantage of this law to form vigilante groups to intimidate Roma communities, but the government has responded by decreeing fines up to 100,000 forints ($414) for any failure of local neighbourhood watch members to cooperate with the police. [27] Based on the evidence adduced in this case, the panel finds these claimants would have recourse to seek redress if they were to be denied state protection and/or if their complaints were not to be taken seriously by the police due to their Roma ethnicity. [28] The documentary evidence21 shows that in the first nine months of the year, authorities found 3,022 police officers responsible for breaches of discipline, 766 guilty of petty offenses, 283 guilty of criminal offenses, and 10 unfit for duty. In the same period, courts sentenced four police officers to prison terms, gave suspended sentences to 39, fined 106, and dismissed 12. In the same period, courts convicted 37 officers of corruption. No information was available on the number placed on probation. [29] Victims of lesser police abuses may complain either to the alleged violator's unit or to the Independent Police Complaints Body (IPCB), which investigated violations and omissions by the police that affected fundamental rights. The five-member body, appointed by a two-thirds majority of parliament, functions independently of police authorities. At year's end the board had received 805 reports from the public. It reviewed 458 complaints (including some cases filed in 2010) and found serious legal violations in 67 and minor legal violations in 33. The board forwarded the 67 cases to the national police chief, who agreed with the findings in two cases, partially accepted the findings in three, and rejected the findings in three. The rest remained pending.22 The panel finds there is no significant reason why these claimants would not be able to complain to the IPCB or other similar state agency(ies) that oversee police inaction, misconduct and abuse. [30] Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, the panel finds that the authorities take action against police officers who abuse their power or engage in illegal practices. [31] The panel acknowledges that the government of Hungary received criticism for its handling of violence against Roma. In a 15 February 2012 statement prepared for a US Helsinki Commission Hearing on state response to violence against Roma, the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC) states that "racist or stigmatising anti-Roma rhetoric has been on the rise both in public and political discourse" in countries such as Hungary. In addition, the ERRC notes that "[e]xtremist groups, political parties and politicians have sharpened their anti-Romani rhetoric and actions, galvanizing segments of the public against Roma" (ERRC 15 Feb. 2012).23 [32] The panel finds that a fair reading of the documentary evidence indicates that criticism regarding the conditions of the Roma in Hungary may be warranted, and that it may be an understatement to say that state protection in Hungary is not perfect. The documentary evidence,24 however, also shows that the Government of Hungary does not condone and does not acquiesce to the racial and discriminatory behaviour of the police, the public or the radical racial groups. The law prohibits inciting hatred against persons, nations, communities, ethnic, linguistic or other minorities, majority groups, churches, or religious groups. The law also prohibits the public display of certain symbols. They include the swastika, the hammer and sickle, and the arrow cross, a symbol associated with the country's fascist World War II-era government. The law prohibiting the public display of the five-pointed red star remained in effect despite a 2008 ECHR ruling that declared it to be a violation of the right to freedom of expression. On November 3, the ECHR repeated this judgment in a subsequent case (see section 1.e., Regional Human Rights Court Decisions). Hungary is in control of its territory, and has a functioning security force to uphold the laws of the country; and there is infrastructure in place for victims, including Roma, to obtain state protection and to seek redress if they are denied security services or if their complaints are not taken seriously. A claimant must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.25 In a functioning democracy, a claimant will have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the reasonable recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status.26 [33] As mentioned earlier, these claimants might have approached the police regarding some of their problems in Hungary; the evidence in this case, however, does not establish that these claimants made a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection from the police in their own home country before seeking refuge abroad. [34] The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate.27 The onus is on the claimants to make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection in their own country of nationality before seeking protection internationally. Based on all of the foregoing, the panel finds that these claimants did not make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection in Hungary, in these claimants' particular situation and circumstances. [35] In canvassing the totality of the documentary evidence, the panel is of the view that, in spite of the undeniable challenges in changing deeply-rooted discriminatory viewpoints in Hungary, the government of Hungary continues to offer a vast number of programs and avenues to the Hungarian minorities, including Roma. It is clear that the government of Hungary is committed and is making serious efforts to address the issues of racism and associated discriminatory practices and violence in the country, and that police are able and willing to respond adequately to complaints filed by victims, and results are being achieved on the ground. [36] In view of all of the foregoing, the panel finds that these claimants have not provided the requisite clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, state protection in Hungary is inadequate for these claimants in their particular situation and circumstances. The panel finds that the claimants' evidence regarding the effectiveness of state protection was not persuasive, since the claimants' views were largely anecdotal and unsubstantiated and were not consistent with the documentary evidence. The panel prefers the current documentary evidence over the claimants' testimonies since they are drawn from a wide range of publicly accessible documents, from reliable non-government and government organizations. [37] Regarding state protection, the Court has indicated that it is not enough to say that steps are being taken that some day may result in adequate state protection. It is what state protection that is actually provided at the present time that is relevant.28 Regard must be given to what is actually happening and not what the state is endeavouring to put in place.29 Any efforts must have "actually translated into adequate state protection" at the operational level.30 The panel considered counsel's submissions and the case law. [38] The panel considered the case law provided and referred to in counsel's submissions. [39] In regard to the court's instruction that efforts concerning state protection must have actually translated into adequate state protection, the panel has reviewed a March 2011 report by the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC) 31 which provides information on the progress of 22 cases in Hungary in which Roma were victims of violent attacks between 2008 and 2009, with the following results being observed: * In six cases, the police investigation was suspended because no suspect was identified; * In one case, the police investigation was suspended for lack of crime; * An investigation against the alleged victims for false testimony was opened; * In 12 cases, prosecution was pending; * In one case, the perpetrator was convicted, resulting in 11 years' imprisonment. * In two cases, no information was available. [40] Based on this information from the ERRC, there is evidence to indicate that the police investigated the above-mentioned incidents and made specific findings resulting from those investigations, which is a demonstration of state protection at the operational level. [41] According to Amnesty International: [T]here were nine attacks against Romani communities in 2008 and 2009 which bore similar characteristics. The perpetrators used Molotov cocktails and firearms; there were usually two people shooting from very close range using shotguns. Although the victims of the attacks lived in various places across the country, their houses were mostly located on the peripheries of settlements close to motorways. The attacks caused fear among the Romani community throughout the country. The police reacted by taking several measures within a programme that was supposed to enhance community safety. Initially, the measures were taken in counties where the attacks were carried out. In April 2009 they were extended to "vulnerable settlements" where police believed similar attacks could be expected. These areas were patrolled at night and in early morning hours.32 [42] On August 21, 2009, police officers arrested four suspects and charged three of the individuals "on the grounds of multiple coordinated homicide, robbery and abuse of weapons as well as vandalism. Of the four, three were charged on the basis of DNA and weapons analysis; the fourth individual is being treated as an accomplice." The series of crimes carried out between January 2008 and August 2009 targeting Roma and their property has created of an atmosphere of fear in the Romani community.33 In this particular circumstance, the police responded adequately by providing greater protection to affected Roma communities and by arresting and charging four suspects. [43] The Board's documentary evidence34 demonstrates that it is recognized that the outlawed "right-wing extremist groups" continue to incite violence against Roma and have held marches around the country aimed at intimidating local Romani communities. It is also noted, however, that the criminal code has been amended to criminalize "unauthorized activities to maintain public order or public security, which induced fear in others", as well as "blatantly abusive behaviour against a community that might threaten members - real or perceived - of an ethnic, racial or other group. The Minister of the Interior has been quoted as saying that "these kinds of militia patrols would no longer be tolerated". To support this effort, "Article 174/B of the criminal code criminalizes violent acts committed against a person for belonging to a national, ethnic, racial or religious group." The victim, in reporting the incident, however, must state that the attack was due to the victim's ethnicity. It is recognized that the complaint, if it is supported by an NGO that applies pressure based on the "bias motivation", is more likely to be recognized. As per these claimants' testimonies, and based on the documentary evidence, the panel finds that there is a level of mistrust on both sides of the Roma issue in Hungary. Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, it is apparent, however, that both the government and numerous human rights NGOs are cognizant and watching closely to ensure that the rights of all ethnic, racial or religious minorities are being protected, and failure by officials to respond to human rights violations is being exposed as misconduct or abuse and being reported. [44] The European Commission Against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI) has strongly recommended that the Hungarian authorities keep the adequacy of the criminal law provisions against racist expression under review. It strongly recommended that they take into account international standards in this respect to combat racism and racial discrimination, according to which the law should penalise racist acts including public incitement to violence, hatred or discrimination as well as public insults, defamation or threats against a person or a grouping of persons on the grounds of their "race", colour, language, religion, nationality, or national or ethnic origin. ECRI notes with interest that since its report, some amendments have been introduced into Hungary's Criminal Code that may help to strengthen the fight against racism. Hungary's Parliament has amended the Criminal Code so that it prohibits assault against a person not only because he or she is (or the offender presumes he or she is) a member of a national, ethnic, racial or religious group, but also if he or she is (or the offender presumes he or she is) part of "certain groups of the population". Holocaust denial is also now a criminal offence in Hungary and a new offence of participating in the activity of a disbanded civil organisation has been introduced. In its full report, the ECRI states that, generally, Hungary has begun to implement its recommendations but that Hungary is still not on par with European Union standards.35 [45] The panel acknowledges that the documentary evidence is mixed. Although the claimants testified that they had consulted Romani organization regarding their problems in Hungary; however, in the circumstances particular to this case, the claimants have not demonstrated that state protection in Hungary is inadequate and that therefore they need not have taken all reasonable steps to seek state protection in their home country, such as seeking help from the government organizations higher in authority, or taking their complaints to government organizations with powers of oversight, such as the Minorities Ombudsman's Office, or the Independent Police Complaints Body (IPCB),36 or the Equal Treatment Authority (ETA) that operates under the Ministry of Justice and Public Administration before seeking international protection in Canada. The Board's documents indicate that these complaint mechanisms do, in fact, take complaints, make findings and then report those findings back to the appropriate authorities for their response (although final resolutions to these complaints appear to have a smaller number of resolutions in favour of the complainant, that is not a matter that is before the panel since it does not have the facts relating to these cases, only the reported numbers).37 [46] The panel acknowledges that the quality of existence for the Roma is not what it should be, but their existence is not threatened by the state. The progress in combating racism is not as rapid, and there have been set-backs and obstacles, but the panel finds the commitment of the state to continue the battle is not in question. The panel finds it unreasonable to expect that the state measures should have prevented or eliminated all racism or acts of violence related to race. Based on the totality of the documentary evidence, however, the panel finds that Hungary is not only making serious efforts to turn around the endemic racism that has historically been present in Hungarian society, including in the police force, rather it has enacted legislation, has infrastructure and a functioning police force in place, allocates funding, and there are numerous examples that, if complaints are made, authorities do take action. There are numerous examples in the documentary evidence, some of which had been cited in the foregoing, to show that victims have recourse and that, when complaints are made, the authorities take action. The documentary evidence demonstrates that it is reasonable to expect authorities to take action when complaints are made and that there are organizations in place to ensure that the police are held accountable. These claimants did not make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek protection in Hungary before seeking it internationally. [47] Based on the totality of the evidence adduced in this case, the panel finds that the claimants have not rebutted the presumption of state protection with clear and convincing evidence. State Response to Discrimination against Roma [48] The claimants testified that they suffered discrimination at the hands of racist Hungarians. The documentary evidence38 indicates that the Roma face "widespread discrimination" and "exclusion" in Hungary. [49] The documentary evidence, however, also shows that the government of Hungary is actively engaged in improving the situation of its minorities, including Roma. Pertaining to Hungary's anti-discrimination laws, in their March 2010 submission to the United Nations (UN) Human Rights Committee, the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC), the Chance for Children Foundation (CFCF), a Hungarian organization founded in 2003 to combat discrimination against Roma and socially deprived children in public education, and the Hungarian Helsinki Committee (HHC), a non-profit organization that monitors the enforcement of human rights, state that: * Hungary has one of the most advanced anti-discrimination laws and a system for minority protection in the Central and Eastern European region. A number of mechanisms have been developed to ensure that minority groups enjoy civil and political rights. It is also noted the central government's general failure to maintain strong and effective control mechanisms over rights violations takes its toll on Hungary's largest minority, the Roma .39 [50] The documentary evidence shows that many of the issues including unemployment, housing and health care are blamed on the post-communist economic crisis. In an effort to improve monitoring of Romany legal rights and treatment, a 27-member Roma Coordination Council was formed in 2011 bringing together the Minister for Public Administration and Justice, the Leader of the National Roma self-government, as well as representatives of local self-governments, NGOs and churches. The government established a new ministerial unit "with considerable resources for promoting the integration of disadvantaged (and among them, Roma) people. In December 2011, Hungary published its National Social Inclusion Strategy for 2011-2020 on extreme poverty, child poverty, and the Roma, as part of its commitment to the EU framework for National Roma Integration Strategies up to 2020, which was developed under the Hungarian EU presidency in 2011. The Ministry of Public Administration and Justice operates a legal service network providing free legal aid to Roma who have experienced ethnic discrimination. [51] There is also recourse to the Equal Treatment Authority. Since 2005, it has provided individuals with a direct avenue of redress for violations of the prohibition of discrimination in a variety of public and private law relationships. In the first year, there were nearly 500 complaints lodged and the number has risen steadily every since. There are also other remedies such as seeking compensation through the courts, or turning to one of the Parliamentary Commissioners where public authorities are concerned.40 [52] Regarding health care, the State Secretary for Health Care indicated that Hungary has "several legal institutions" to protect patients' rights, including the following: a. Patient Advocate who assists patients in accessing their medical records, is kept informed about investigations into patient complaints, and provides information to health care workers on patients' rights; b. Mediation Council, which provides an avenue for the resolution of legal disputes out of court; and c. Hospital supervisory boards and ethics committees, which "represent the interests" of patients and enforce their rights (Hungary 28 Oct. 2011).41 [53] Notwithstanding, it would be remiss of the panel not to consider that the latest Board documents indicate that conditions for the Roma have deteriorated.42 In a report on his May 2011 mission to Hungary, the UN Special Rapporteur on contemporary forms of racism, racial discrimination, xenophobia and related intolerance wrote that the situation of Roma individuals had not improved in recent years, but rather worsened. According to the Society for Threatened Peoples (STP), an independent human rights organization based in Germany, Roma in Hungary are "consciously despised by the majority population and pushed to the edge of society," while previously "hidden anti-Roma attitudes are becoming more open".43 There are criticisms directed against Hungary pertaining to its treatment of the Roma, in that Hungary's state protection mechanisms related to the Roma, consistently fall short of the EU's expectations.44 [54] In reviewing the totality of the evidence in this case, the panel is of the view that, even if criticism of Hungary's measures to combat racism is warranted, particularly against the Romani population, the panel finds that this in itself does not show that these claimants will not receive adequate state protection in Hungary, in their particular situation and circumstances, if they were to make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek it upon return. These claimants have recourse to obtain state protection, to seek redress if they were to be denied security services or if their complaints were not being taken seriously by the authorities, and to seek remedies against discrimination. [55] The panel finds that there is considerable activism in Hungary by the country's ethnic minorities and by NGOs for the protection of minorities' rights. The panel accepts that Roma Minority Government and other similar organizations as well as NGOs do not provide state protection, and that it is the police whose mandate it is to provide state protection. In reviewing the documentary evidence,45 however, the panel finds that many such organizations including the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Rights of National and Ethnic Minorities, the Equal Treatment Authority, the Parliamentary Commissioner for Civil Rights, and several NGOs like the Roma Civil Rights Foundation, the Legal Defence Bureau for National and Ethnic Minorities and the Legal Counselling Office of the Roma Parliament, which, among other things, are there to inform victims of discrimination and violence about their rights as citizens and guide/direct them to the appropriate government organizations and thus enable citizens, including Roma, to seek remedies if they are discriminated against and to seek redress if they are denied security services or if their complaints are not taken seriously. Many such organizations are supported by the government to assist victims, such as in these claimants' particular situation and circumstances to obtain state protection against discrimination and criminality. [56] There is no significant reason why the above and/or other similar organizations would not assist these claimants with their problems if these claimants were to approach them upon return in Hungary. [57] Based on the evidence adduced in this case, the panel finds that the state of Hungary would provide these claimants adequate state protection, should they make a reasonable and diligent effort to seek it upon return. The panel finds that these claimants have failed to rebut the presumption of state protection with clear and convincing evidence. [58] Therefore, the panel finds these claims fail under section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [59] Having given due consideration to all of the evidence, the relevant provisions of the IRPA, and to the jurisprudence on which this panel relies, the panel determines that these claimants have not satisfied the burden of establishing a serious possibility of persecution on Convention ground(s), nor that, on a balance of probabilities, they would be subjected personally to a risk to life, or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or to a danger of torture upon return to Hungary today. [60] Therefore, the panel determines that the claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. [61] Accordingly, the Refugee Protection Division rejects the claims of XXXX XXXX (TB1-09850), XXXX XXXX (TB1-17422), and XXXX XXXX (TB1-17424). (signed) "S.S. Kular" S.S.
