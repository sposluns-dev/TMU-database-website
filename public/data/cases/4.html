By the Court:

[1]
Trinity Western University (TWU) is a faith based educational institution

located in Langley, British Columbia. Brayden Volkenant is TWU’s Coordinator

of Business and Strategic Affairs. He describes himself as an evangelical

Christian. The mission statement of TWU is as follows:

The mission of Trinity Western University, as an arm of the Church, is to develop godly Christian leaders: positive, goal-oriented university graduates with thoroughly Christian minds; growing disciples of Christ who glorify God through fulfilling the Great Commission, serving God and people in the various marketplaces of life.

Presently TWU is expanding its educational operations to include a school of law which is scheduled to open in September, 2016. This law school was approved by the Federation of Law Societies of Canada in 2013. The British Columbia Ministry of Advanced Education approved the law school curriculum at the same time.

[2]
Promotional materials for this law school indicate that “students are annually required to read, understand and pledge to the terms of the community covenant.” This covenant directs students to adopt and practice a lifestyle that is consistent with biblical priorities. One such priority is that “sexual intimacy is reserved for marriage between one man and one woman.” Adherence to this covenant brings into focus a conflict between freedom of religion and the right not to be discriminated against on the basis of sexual orientation or otherwise.
[3]
Law societies across Canada have grappled with how they should respond to admission applications from TWU law school graduates. A number of societies have indicated they will accept these graduates if they meet all other criteria. Other societies take the view that admitting such graduates condones discrimination by TWU. The Nova Scotia Barristers’ Society (NSBS) falls into the latter.
[4]
The NSBS council is responsible for governance and regulation of the legal

profession in the public interest under the Legal Profession Act. On January

24, 2014 council assigned to the executive committee the tasks of receiving

submissions and identifying options respecting TWU law school graduates. On

April 25, 2014 council approved the following resolution:

Council accepts the Report of the Federation Approval Committee that, subject to the concerns and comments noted, the TWU program will meet the national requirement; Council resolves that the Community Covenant is discriminatory and therefore Council does not approve the proposed law school at Trinity Western unless TWU either:

i. exempts law students from signing the Community Covenant; or

ii. amends the Community Covenant for law students in a way that ceases to discriminate.

Council directs the Executive Director to consider any regulatory amendments that may be required to give effect to this resolution and to bring them to Council for consideration at a future meeting.

Council remains seized of this matter to consider any information TWU wishes to present regarding compliance with the condition.

This resolution was made pursuant to s. 4 of the Legal Profession Act.

[5]
On July 23, 2014 the NSBS amended Regulation 3.1 to implement the

resolution passed on April 25, 2014. That amendment provides:

(a) “Committee” means the Credentials Committee;

(b) “law degree” means

i) a Bachelor of Laws degree or a Juris Doctor degree from a faculty of common law at a Canadian university approved by the Federation of Law Societies of Canada for the granting of such degree, unless Council, acting in the public interest, determines that the university granting the degree unlawfully discriminates in its law student admissions or enrolment policies or requirements on grounds prohibited by either or both the Charter of Rights and Freedoms and the Nova Scotia Human Rights Act;

ii) a degree in civil law, if the holder of the degree has passed a comprehensive examination in common law or has successfully completed a common law conversion course approved by the Credentials Committee, or

iii) a Certificate of Qualification issued by the National Committee on Accreditation of the Federation of Law Societies of Canada;

[6]
On May 29, 2014 TWU and Mr. Volkenant filed a Notice for Judicial

Review respecting the NSBS resolution of April 25, 2014. The grounds for review

reference jurisdiction, human rights and the Charter of Rights and Freedoms. The

Applicants requested the following relief:

The applicants request an order quashing the decision of the Respondent and seek an order in the nature of mandamus requiring the Respondent to accredit graduates of the TWU law school on the same basis as graduates of other Canadian common law schools. The applicants request their costs of this application.

On June 23, 2014 the NSBS filed a Notice of Participation in which it supported

the resolution of April 25, 2014. The following positions are advanced at paras. 7

and 9:

7) The decision made on April 25, 2014 was a policy resolution made by Council (“Council’s Resolution”) pursuant to s. 4 of the Legal Profession Act. Council’s Resolution directs the Executive Director to consider any regulatory amendments required to give effect to the Resolution and to bring them to Council for consideration at a further meeting.

….

9) In exercising its statutory discretion, Council has properly considered and balanced the relevant Charter values, all relevant statutes and applicable legal principles in accordance with the statutory objectives in the Legal Profession Act. Council’s Resolution was reasonable. In the alternative, Council’s Resolution was correct.

This judicial review is scheduled for a hearing on December 16 – 19, 2014.

[7]
On July 28, 2014 a consent order issued out of this Court granting intervenor standing to the Justice Centre for Constitutional Freedoms.
[8]
The following organizations have applied for intervenor standing:

• Canadian Council of Christian Charities;

• The Attorney General of Canada;

• The Evangelical Fellowship of Canada and Christian Higher Education Canada;

• Association for Reformed Political Action;

• The Nova Scotia Human Rights Commission;

• The Christian Legal Foundation;

• Catholic Civil Rights League and the Faith and Freedom Alliance.

A consent order will issue granting all but the Nova Scotia Human Rights Commission intervenor standing. All intervenors will be permitted to file a 20 page brief and to make a 20 minute oral presentation.

[9]
The Nova Scotia Human Rights Commission supports the NSBS on this

judicial review. The Applicants oppose the motion for reasons succinctly set forth

in a letter to the Court dated August 14, 2014:

There is no question the HRC has expertise in the area of human rights. Accordingly, in the absence of some other factor, the applicants agree that the HRC should be granted intervener status in the present proceedings.

The difficulty in this case is that if the applicants lose the judicial review, then it is likely that Brayden Volkenant or another graduate of TWU law school would seek to make a complaint to the Human Rights Commission when denied admission to articles.

It is difficult for HRC to independently investigate and prosecute that complaint if they have already taken a public position against it. I raised this concern with the HRC on June 20 and again on August 7. I have not received a substantive reply.

What I have proposed as a solution is that HRC offer an undertaking to have any complaint that arises from a graduate of TWU independently investigated and the recommendation whether it should proceed to a hearing undertaken independently (by personnel not under the control and direction of the Human Rights Commission). On receipt of such an undertaking, we would withdraw our objection to HRC’s intervention.

In the absence of such an undertaking, the applicants object to the intervention of HRC.

The NSBS takes no position on this motion.

[10]
There are 2 additional motions before the Court. The Applicants seek the following relief:

Trinity Western University and Brayden Volkenant, the applicants in this proceeding, seek leave to file affidavit evidence as more particularly set out in the attached schedule, drafts of which are filed with this Notice.

The Respondent NSBS moves for an order:

1) granting leave to the Respondent to amend its Notice of Participation, in the form of the draft Amended Notice of Participation attached as Exhibit “D” to the Affidavit of Darrel Pink filed concurrently with this Notice; and

2) for directions of the Court supplementing and amending the directions given orally on July 3, 2014 and confirmed in a written Order issued on July 28, 2014, with such supplementary and amended directions to be generally in the form of the Order attached hereto as Schedule “A”.

Nova Scotia Human Rights Commission:

[11]
The Commission is an organization specifically empowered by legislation to protect the public interest with respect to human rights matters in Nova Scotia. Objection to the granting of this motion is not based on anything substantive. In fact the NSBS acknowledges the Commission’s suitability to be an intervenor in this judicial review.
[12]
Counsel for the Commission argues that potential complaints by future TWA law graduates would not be problematic. She does not see a conflict arising between the Commission’s position on this judicial review and maintaining impartiality on individual complaints. With respect I disagree. The impression of impartiality is as important as impartiality. Should the NSBS refuse to admit a TWU graduate, the Commission would be the logical next step. Any complaint would be advanced knowing the Commission had already taken a firm position on the issue.
[13]
The Applicants have advanced a proposal that would allow the Commission to be added as an intervenor and yet maintain the impression of impartiality in any future complaints by TWU law graduates. I support that proposal. I will grant intervenor standing to the Commission subject to an undertaking to have any complaint that arises from a TWU law graduate independently investigated. Further, the decision on whether to order a hearing should be assessed independently. Independent personnel are those not under the control and direction of the Commission.

Amended Notice of Participation:

[14]
The Notice for Judicial Review seeks a review of the NSBS resolution of

April 25, 2014. It does not mention the regulatory amendment of July 23, 2014 as

that was not in place when the notice was filed. The Notice of Participation filed

by the NSBS is also silent on the regulatory amendment for the same reason. It

should be noted that the April 25, 2014 resolution contained the following clause:

Council directs the Executive Director to consider any regulatory amendments that may be required to give effect to this resolution and to bring them to Council for consideration at a future meeting.

[15]
The NSBS seeks the following amendments to their Notice of Participation:

10) The Respondent says the court should not disturb any decision, action or other matter under review, should dismiss the Application and provide a declaration that Regulation 3.1 of the Nova Scotia Barristers’ Society, as amended, is not ultra vires the Legal Profession Act or contrary to the Applicant’s Charter rights and freedoms, or alternatively, that it is a reasonable limit prescribed by law under section 1 of the Charter.

11) The NSBS says that the Application for Judicial Review of the policy resolution passed on April 25, 2014 was premature and/or incomplete in that the Application alleges the resolution is contrary to NSBS regulations in circumstances when Council’s resolution expressly contemplated regulatory amendments to implement it.

12) The Applicants have been invited but have declined to amend their pleadings to acknowledge the regulatory amendments outlined in paragraph (8) and to identify whether or not they assert the amended regulation to be ultra vires the Legal Profession Act or contrary to the Charter and therefore of no force or effect pursuant to section 52 of the Constitution Act, 1867.

13) The NSBS requests that the Application be dismissed for prematurity and/or incompleteness or alternatively requests that the Court deal with all of the ultra vires and Charter issues raised by the resolution challenged by the Applicants, dismiss the Application and provide a declaration that Regulation 3.1, as amended, is not ultra vires the Act or contrary to the Applicants’ Charter rights and freedoms, or alternatively, that it is a reasonable limit prescribed by law under section 1 of the Charter.

The proposed Notice of Participation clearly brings the July 23, 2014 regulatory amendment into play, as well as the issues of vires and the Charter of Rights and Freedoms.

[16]
Civil Procedure Rule 83.03 provides for amendment of pleadings as follows:

A party to a proceeding other than an action may amend the notice by which the proceeding is started, or a notice of contest, participation or contention, with the agreement of the parties affected by the amendment or with permission of a judge.

Justice Bourgeois in Nova Scotia (Community Services) v. Hopkins, 2011 NSSC 382 determined that a motion to amend a Notice of Participation should be granted absent evidence of bad faith or prejudice that cannot be compensated by costs.

[17]
Civil Procedure Rule 7.08(3)(c) provides for an expansion of the record. It

states:

A notice of participation must contain the standard heading, be entitled “Notice of Participation”, be dated and signed, and include all the following:

[…]

(c) if the person contends that the decision under review is justified by grounds different than those expressed in the decision or should be reviewed on grounds different than the applicant’s grounds, a concise statement of the alternate grounds.

The proposed Notice of Participation contains a concise statement of the alternate grounds. There is no bad faith and the Applicants consent to the amendment in principle.

[18]
The April 25, 2014 resolution contemplated that a regulatory amendment may be required to implement that resolution. The July 23, 2014 amendment is the culmination of that direction. Both address a common issue. The NSBS argues it would be desirable to have all issues related to the April resolution, including the validity of the regulatory amendment, determined in a single proceeding. It is this objective that drives their application. The Applicants submit they should be entitled to choose what they are suing for, and not be obliged to challenge the regulation simply because they challenge the resolution.
[19]
I have reviewed the Applicant’s arguments but do not find them persuasive. Issues on this judicial review should include the merits, vires, and the Charter of Rights and Freedoms. There is great utility in resolving all issues at the same time. The parties are committed to calling extrinsic evidence to establish a factual record. Such issues are generally not the stuff of judicial review. Consequently, I am prepared to deem this proceeding to be both an Application for Judicial Review and an Application in Court. Applications in Court are the usual and appropriate proceedings to challenge the validity of legislation and permits the introduction of evidence beyond the record in the judicial review proceeding.
[20]
I will permit the NSBS to amend their Notice of Participation in the form attached to the Affidavit of Darrel Pink at Tab “D”. I will not attach any conditions to this decision as the trial judge should not be hampered by any ruling of mine.
[21]
The NSBS has provided the following draft order respecting directions. It is proposed to replace the existing order dated July 28, 2014.

1. The Respondent shall have leave to amend its Notice of Participation, in the form submitted as Exhibit “D” to the Affidavit of Darrel Pink dated August 20, 2014;

2. The Applicants shall advise the Respondents within 10 days hereof whether they challenge the validity of the July 23, 2014 amendment to Regulation 3.1 under the Legal Profession Act as being ultra vires that Act or contrary to the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (“the Charter”), and if so, on what basis, and further if so, shall forthwith give any notices required by the Constitutional Questions Act;

3. Unless Applicants take no issue with the vires or constitutionality of the amended Regulation 3.1, the issues in the proceeding shall include not only whether to grant the relief requested by the Applicants, but also whether the July 23, 2014 amendment to Regulation 3.1 under the Legal Profession Act is ultra vires that Act or contrary to the Applicants’ Charter rights and if it is contrary to the Charter, whether it is a reasonable limit prescribed by law under section 1 of the Charter.

4. The within proceeding shall be deemed to be a combined Application for Judicial Review and Application in Court, and the Applicants and Respondent may file affidavit evidence on the Charter issues to supplement the Record, notwithstanding that the proceeding was commenced as a judicial review.

5. The schedule for filing of documents as set orally on July 3, 2014 and as confirmed in the Schedule attached to the written Order for Directions issued on July 28, 2014 is replaced by the Revised Schedule attached to this Order.

6. In the event additional motions are necessary before the hearing on the merits, in order to challenge the admissibility of affidavit evidence or to determine whether the Application should be dismissed for prematurity or incompleteness, or for any other reason, counsel will notify the Court and a date or dates shall be set by correspondence or conference call, if possible.

[22]
The Applicants take no issue with paragraphs 1, 4, 5, and 6 but object to paragraphs 2 and 3. I will issue an order that includes paragraphs 1, 4, 5 and 6. I make no order respecting paragraphs 2 and 3 as those issues are best heard by the trial judge. Given the seniority of counsel I do not see this order holding up the hearing of this matter.
[23]
