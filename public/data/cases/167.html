They are all citizens of Tunisia. They are claiming refugee protection pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). [2] At the beginning of the hearing the father was confirmed as the designated representative for the minor claimant. ALLEGATIONS [3] The claimants fear persecution in Tunisia because they are Christians. The principal claimant converted from Islam in 1999 and suffered the rejection of his family and friends because he was considered to have betrayed his faith. The claimant's wife and daughter also converted to Christianity and suffered as a result. They did not seek the help of the authorities because it is the Tunisian state authorities which are the persecutors. [4] The daughter suffered in school because of her new faith and for her psychological well-being she was sent to Canada in May 2011. The adult claimants came to Canada on August 12, 2011. The family claimed refugee protection on August 19, 2011 in Moncton, New Brunswick. DETERMINATION [5] The panel finds that the claimants are not "Convention refugees" nor are they "persons in need of protection". Identity [6] The identities of the claimants were established to the satisfaction of the panel on the basis of their Tunisian passports on record. ANALYSIS [7] The claimants contend that they are Convention refugees pursuant to section 96 of the IRPA by reason of a well-founded fear of persecution on the basis of their religion, that of Muslims who have converted to become evangelical Christians. They believe that they would be subjected personally to a danger of torture and cruel and unusual treatment and punishment under section 97(1) of the Act. [8] The adult claimants presented baptism certificates from the XXXX XXXXh Centre stating that the principal claimant was baptized on XXXX XXXX, 1999 (Exhibit C-14) and that the wife was baptized on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2001 (Exhibit C-28). Both these certificates were signed by the Senior Pastor, XXXX XXXX, who the claimants explained was an Egyptian who had set up the XXXX XXXX Centre in Tunisia. Exhibit A-3 is a document which states that the XXXX XXXX Centre first began working in Tunisia in 2011. The panel questioned how the claimants could have been baptized prior to the establishment of the XXXX XXXX Centre if the organization had only began operations in Tunisia in 2011. The principal claimant explained that the XXXX XXXX Centre is an association of individuals who are Christians and from his testimony it would not appear that this organization is a church. The claimant testified that he had been baptized by the individual who had introduced him to Christianity and that according to the evangelical viewpoint one does not have to be baptised by a clergyman. The panel accepted the claimant's explanation. [9] The principal claimant testified that he had two jobs one of which was working in a bookshop selling books XXXX XXXX XXXX. In his principal other job in publicity he had no real problems because of his religious beliefs. He testified that at times undercover policemen would come to the bookshop where he worked and pretend to ask questions about religion. He would then be taken to the police station and asked questions about his beliefs. He was however never charged with any crime. He testified that the family attended religious services at private homes for the most part but that they had also visited the established Reform Church on special religious holidays. [10] The principal claimant stated that at first there were just a few couples who would attend the private house services. While the wife changed the schools where she taught she was never fired from her place of employment for her religious beliefs and continued to work as a teacher until her departure to Canada. While she testified that she had problems with parents of the children who complained about her speaking to her students about her beliefs, in Canadian secular society such criticism by parents would also not be unheard of. The daughter suffered at school and also felt isolated from the fact that she was the only Christian in her age group among the small group of Tunisian Christians who attended the private home church. She also had a problem with dyslexia which she attributed to being forced at school to write with her right hand. She changed schools many times because of the problems she encountered. [11] The panel accepts that the claimants are Christians and accepts the principal claimant's comments that as an evangelical Christian he would want to reach out to others with his new found faith. However it does not appear from his testimony that the claimant was active in proselytizing to others during the more than 10 years that he was a Christian in Tunisia. From his testimony it would appear that he would engage in gentle discussions when approached. Furthermore the claimant mentioned that at the beginning their religious meetings would take place only in the presence of a few other Christian couples. Exhibit A-3 is a document on the XXXX XXXX Centre which issued the baptismal certificates for the claimants. This document states that missionaries from their work in Egypt, with connections in Tunisia, planted a house church in 2011. Starting from zero, with just faith and a passion for the people of Tunisia, this embryonic plant has now grown to three house churches.1 At the time the claimants left for Canada the number of house churches still remained small. Furthermore it cannot be said that proselytizing is an obligation of the claimants' religion since the claimants did not belong to any organized church in Tunisia. According to the principal claimant's testimony the XXXX XXXX Centre which issued the baptism certificates for the couple is not a church as such but simply an association of Christians. Considering the changes that have taken place in Tunisia it would not appear that Christians who practice their religion in the privacy of their communities are being persecuted today. While the claimants have experienced incidents of discrimination in the past, the panel finds that with the changes that have taken place in Tunisia recently, the situation for religious minorities in the country has improved. [12] The claimants were asked what the situation was like for Christians in Tunisia today after the Revolution that took place in 2011. Both adult claimants testified that they felt that the situation in Tunisia is worse today than it was when they left the country. The panel finds that the documentary evidence does not support this point of view. The panel favours the documentary evidence over the claimant's statements since the expert evidence comes from an independent, objective source with no interest in the outcome of this claim. [13] The Convention refugee definition is forward looking. While the claimant may have suffered incidents of discrimination in the past, the panel must look to the future. The claimants left the country in August 2011 before the elections in October 2011. The government of Tunisia has changed and the situation in the country is no longer the same as when the claimants were living in Tunisia. Given the new country conditions the panel does not find that the claimants' fears of being persecuted for their religion are well-founded. [14] In December 2010, Tunisian citizens took to the streets protesting rampant corruption and lack of economic opportunities and these protests soon spread and evolved into larger demands for political liberties, basic freedoms, and dignity. Demands for the departure of President Zine el-Abidine Ben Ali were met on January 14, 2011, when Ben Ali fled to Saudi Arabia and a transitional government was formed. The government was reshuffled several times during the year in the face of continuing protests. The claimants left their country in August 2011. Elections held in October 2011 for a Constituent Assembly were deemed to be free and fair, and the new body produced a governing coalition of Islamist and secular parties. Hamadi Jebali, an Islamist, was chosen to serve as prime minister, and secular politician Moncef Marzouki was selected as president. [15] Tunisia has made significant progress toward democratization since Ben Ali's departure, and the post-revolutionary political climate in Tunisia is a stark departure from the authoritarian conditions under Ben Ali. [16] There is a new political openness in Tunisia. Moreover, restrictions on association have been greatly eased since the revolution. According to civil society leaders, between 7,000 and 10,000 new associations, unions, and professional organizations were registered within 10 months of the revolution. Few of these organizations were permitted to operate independently under Ben Ali. Many minority groups have begun to form associations to defend or promote their identity. The heavy restrictions on civil liberties under the Ben Ali regime have largely been relaxed since his departure and the purge of ministers and high-ranking civil servants linked to his Constitutional Democratic Rally party. [17] Tunisian Jews have funded the Tunisian Jewish Cultural Association to promote the country's Jewish heritage. Christians practice their faith without restriction. The government has taken concrete steps to promote interfaith tolerance, thereby demonstrating a trend toward improvement in respect for and protection of the right to religious freedom. Citizens now have the right to sue the government for violations of religious freedom. [18] The constitution and other laws and policies largely provide for religious freedom so long as its practice "does not disturb public order". 2 Even if the claimants were to speak about their Christian beliefs to others in conversation, this would not come under the definition of disturbing the public order. As discussed above the panel found that the claimants were not engaged in active proselytising but rather would engage in gentle discussions with others about their Christian beliefs. This type of activity would hardly be considered to be disturbing the public order. In practice, the new government enforced some restrictions on this new religious freedom, including pressing charges against citizens for disseminating material deemed "offensive" to religious sensibilities. The 1959 constitution remains in force and stipulates that Islam is the official religion and the state seeks to "remain faithful to the teachings of Islam." The National Constituent Assembly (NCA), elected in October 2011, continued its work to draft a new constitution. Religious minorities complained that the government took inadequate steps to protect their communities from harassment, vandalism, and intimidation. The government took steps to promote interfaith tolerance and ease restrictions on religious association. [19] The population of Tunisia is 99 percent Sunni Muslim. Christianity is the second largest religion and about 88 percent of Christians are Roman Catholic. Judaism is the country's third largest religion with approximately 1,500 members. Although religious conversion is legal, there was significant societal pressure against the conversion of Muslims to other religious groups. [20] The government allowed the Jewish community freedom of worship and paid the salary of the grand rabbi. It also provided security for all synagogues and partially subsidized restoration and maintenance costs for some. Government employees, the majority of whom are Muslims, maintained the Jewish cemetery in Tunis. Following arson at a Jewish site, government officials refuted the nature of the attacks and condemned actions against Tunisia's Jewish community. While instances of attacks on Christian churches and graves have occurred, in one case the police arrested a man for setting fire to the front door of a church. [21] The new draft law on associations eliminates the penal dispositions in the previous law as well as the prohibition of belonging to, or serving in an unrecognized or dissolved association, including religious associations. The registration procedure has been eased, and it is more difficult for government entities to hinder or delay the registration process. Most importantly, the Interior Ministry can no longer abolish an association without passing the case through the courts. [22] The government permitted Christian churches to operate freely and Catholic and Protestant religious groups have held services in private residences or other locations after receiving formal approval from the government. While Islamic religious education is mandatory in public schools, the religious curriculum for secondary school students also includes the history of Judaism and Christianity. [23] The constitution does not permit the establishment of political parties based on religious principles, though previously banned parties with "Islamic references" such as Nahda were granted political party registration during the year. However, the interim government denied political party registration to groups such as Hizb Al-Tahreer, a hard line Islamist political party, finding that its platform is based on religion.3 [24] The documentary evidence states that there were no reports of abuses of religious freedom by the government in the year.4 While the government neither prohibits conversion from Islam to another religion nor requires registration of conversion, officials occasionally harassed and discriminated against converts from Islam. [25] Previous restrictions on the freedom of speech and censorship of the press were lifted. Surveillance, restrictions, and harassment of Islamist activists that was previously practiced by the government also ceased during the year. The principal claimant had testified that he had been visited in his bookstore by undercover agents. It would appear that these practices have now stopped. [26] It remained illegal to proselytize among Muslims, as the government viewed such efforts as "disturbing the public order." Authorities under the former government deported non-Muslim foreigners suspected of proselytizing and did not permit them to return. However, there were no reported cases of official action against persons suspected of proselytizing during the year. [27] Tunisia is an electoral democracy. The balloting of October 2011 represented a dramatic improvement in electoral freedoms and practices than under the former regime of Zine el-Abidine Ben Ali. In the 2011 elections voters were able to choose from political parties representing a wide range of ideologies and political philosophies, including Islamist and secularist groups. [28] After the Ben Ali administration collapsed citizens have been able to exercise their new ability to engage in open political expression. Nongovernmental organizations (NGOs) were legally prohibited from pursuing political objectives and activities under the Ben Ali regime. However, new NGOs have begun to form and operate, such as the Tunisian Human Rights League (LTDH). [29] The Freedom in the World 2012 Report on Tunisia states that there are increased freedoms of speech, press, assembly and religious expression as well as greater freedom for academics and nongovernmental organizations, all of which followed the ouster of the long time president Zine el-Abidine Ben Ali in January 2011.5 [30] Based on the foregoing analysis, the panel finds that there is insufficient evidence to establish that the claimants would have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Tunisia today and there is no serious possibility that the claimants' return to Tunisia would subject them to persecution for their religious beliefs. The panel also finds that, on a balance of probabilities, the claimants would not personally be at risk to their lives or at risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or at a danger of torture should they return to Tunisia. CONCLUSION [31] The panel concludes that the claimants are not "Convention refugees" nor are they "persons in need of protection". Their claims are rejected.
