Conclusion [501] - [503] Summary: The accused were charged with four terrorism offences arising from a plot to place pressure cooker bombs outside the Parliament Buildings in Victoria on Canada Day in 2013. The plot was the culmination of a months-long undercover investigation by the RCMP. The trial judge directed an acquittal on the count of facilitating a terrorist activity on the grounds that the accused could not facilitate their own activity. She also directed the jury that if they convicted the accused of conspiring to commit murder for a terrorist group, then they were not to consider another conspiracy count. The jury returned guilty verdicts on the count of conspiring to commit murder for a terrorist group and on the count of making or possessing an explosive with intent to endanger life for a terrorist group. The trial judge entered a stay of proceedings on the other conspiracy count. The defence then applied for a stay of proceedings on the remaining counts on the basis that the accused were entrapped by the RCMP. The trial judge allowed the application and entered stays of proceedings on the final two counts. The trial judge found entrapment on three bases. First, the trial judge found that the RCMP provided the accused with an opportunity to commit the offences without reasonable suspicion that the accused would otherwise commit an offence. The trial judge found that the RCMP should have realized that the defendants were not capable of acting on their extremist beliefs when they committed to provide them with the explosive C-4. Second, the trial judge found that the average person in the accused's position would have committed the same crimes. She found that the RCMP had convinced the accused that they would be killed if they did not carry out the plot. Finally, she found that the police conduct exceeded the grounds of propriety and the RCMP effectively manufactured the crime. The Crown appeals the acquittal, the stay of proceedings on the conspiracy count, and the stays stemming from entrapment. Held: Appeal allowed in part. The acquittal is set aside and a stay of proceedings is substituted. The remaining appeals are dismissed. The trial judge erred in finding that the RCMP lacked reasonable suspicion that the accused would commit an offence. The trial judge also erred in finding that the average person would have committed the offences. The conduct of the RCMP could not have objectively been considered threatening. However, the trial judge did not err in finding that the conduct of the RCMP satisfied the residual category of entrapment. The RCMP knew early on that the accused did not have the ability to commit an act of terrorism. They pushed the accused to create a workable plan when they were unable to do so on their own. The overall conduct of the investigation was a travesty of justice and a stay of proceedings must issue. The trial judge erred in directing an acquittal on the facilitation count. The accused could have facilitated each other's terrorist activity. The acquittal must be set aside and replaced with a stay of proceedings. The trial judge also erred in staying the second conspiracy count. The result would be a stay of proceedings in any event, so this appeal must be dismissed. The terrorism legislation is unnecessarily complex and cumbersome and should be revisited by Parliament. Reasons for Judgment of the Honourable Madam Justice Bennett: A. Overview [1] On Canada Day, July 1, 2013, John Nuttall and Amanda Korody placed pressure cooker bombs outside the Parliament Buildings in Victoria, B.C. The bombs were timed to explode 15 minutes apart to ensure that maximum damage would be done to first responders who attended the scene to aid those harmed by the first explosion. Had the bombs been real, many people would have died or been injured. [2] But the bombs were not real and they did not explode. The minute sliver of explosive substance in the bombs had been provided by the police, who had participated in the entire scheme. The trial judge concluded that the police manufactured the crimes and stayed the charges on the basis of entrapment, despite the jury's verdict of guilty. [3] Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were recent converts to the Muslim faith. Mr. Nuttall had been attending mosques in the Lower Mainland, openly espousing violent jihadist views, and looking for like-minded people. One wonders what would have happened if the police had not intervened and Mr. Nuttall had actually met someone else with similar extremist views. Fortunately, and notably, he was consistently rebuffed and his views repudiated by Muslims who met him in those mosques and prayer rooms. [4] The couple were charged with four counts of terrorism-related offences arising from this plot. The trial judge removed one count in relation to facilitating a terrorist activity from the jury and directed an acquittal. She also instructed the jury not to consider a second count of conspiracy if they found the defendants guilty on the first conspiracy count. The jury convicted both accused of the two remaining counts, and the trial judge entered a conditional stay of proceedings on the second conspiracy count. The trial judge also stayed the remaining two counts on the basis of entrapment and abuse of process. At the same time she imposed an unconditional stay of proceedings on the second conspiracy count. The Crown appeals the acquittal and the stays of proceedings in relation to all of the counts. [5] The entrapment ruling is indexed as 2016 BCSC 1404 (the "Reasons"). In that ruling the judge also stayed the second conspiracy count. The reasons for the acquittal on the facilitation count are indexed as 2015 BCSC 943, and the reasons for the conditional stay of proceedings on the second conspiracy count are indexed as 2015 BCSC 962. [6] I would dismiss the appeals from the stays of proceedings entered on the basis of entrapment. While, in my view, the trial judge erred in her analysis on several bases, her ultimate conclusion was supported by the evidence. [7] I would dismiss the appeal from the stay of proceedings on the second conspiracy count and would allow the appeal from the acquittal to the extent of substituting a stay of proceedings. In my opinion, the trial judge erred in directing an acquittal on the basis that someone charged with "facilitat[ing] a terrorist activity" must be external to the terrorist organization. She also erred in her instruction to the jury on the conspiracy counts, which had the effect of removing one count from the jury. Nevertheless, the defence of entrapment would apply to both counts, as would the rule against multiple convictions. [8] Throughout these proceedings, in both this Court and the court below, the word "jihad" has been used synonymously with "Islamic terrorism". However, in Islam, jihad is a contested term with a range of possible meanings. In the Oxford Encyclopedia of Islam and Politics (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2014) entry for "jihād", Rudolph Peters and David Cook explain some of those meanings: [Jihād] may express a struggle against one's evil inclinations or an exertion for the sake of Islam and the ummah (Islamic community), for example, in trying to convert unbelievers or working for the moral betterment of Islamic society ("jihād of the tongue" and "jihād of the pen"). In books on Islamic law and commonly in the Qurʾān, the word means an armed struggle against the unbelievers. Sometimes the "jihād of the sword" is called "the lesser jihād," in opposition to the peaceful forms named "the greater jihād." Evidence Leading up to the Charges [14] The police began investigating Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody as a result of receiving information that Mr. Nuttall had been openly endorsing violent jihadist views. The investigation was based on 28 scenarios principally involving Corporal A., Mr. Nuttall, and Ms. Korody. [15] The investigation that led to the charges against Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody was conducted by the Royal Canadian Mounted Police ("RCMP") Integrated National Security Team ("INSET"). The impetus for the investigation came from information about Mr. Nuttall collected or obtained by INSET between July 2012 and February 2013 from a number of different sources: (a) A report dated July 5, 2012, from a Surrey RCMP patrol officer. The report described an incident in which a woman (who was intoxicated when she spoke with the police) overheard Mr. Nuttall on the street, speaking on a cellular phone in a loud voice about "the afterlife" and "blowing up" Islamic countries. (b) An initial report and a series of follow-up interviews with M.C. between October 12, 2012, and February 6, 2013. M.C. met Mr. Nuttall, whom M.C. knew to be a recent convert to Islam, at a local mosque. Among other things, M.C. told the police that Mr. Nuttall claimed to have shot a Jewish woman, and asked M.C. to help him travel to Afghanistan to commit violent jihad and become a holy warrior. M.C. said Mr. Nuttall had been asked to leave a prayer room, a Muslim school, and a number of mosques in the Lower Mainland due to his interest in extremist jihad. After the police approached Mr. Nuttall to discuss these matters, M.C. reported that Mr. Nuttall left him phone messages accusing him of being a "traitor to the cause". He also expressed concerns about Mr. Nuttall's mental health. (c) Two separate letters from the Canadian Security Intelligence Service ("CSIS"). On January 16, 2013, INSET received a letter from CSIS reporting that Mr. Nuttall was a potential threat to national security. This letter was nothing more than a "tip"; it was unsourced and did not contain any actionable details. On February 7, 2013, INSET received a more detailed letter from CSIS, reporting that Mr. Nuttall had recently made attempts to purchase potassium nitrate--a chemical precursor that can be used to manufacture explosives--from pharmacies in the Lower Mainland. At the time, the police were unable to confirm that Mr. Nuttall had in fact made attempts to acquire this material, as they did not want to lose the ability to use undercover officers in their investigation. Later in the investigation, they obtained surveillance video of Mr. Nuttall's attendance at a pharmacy on the relevant date, although Sergeant K. agreed at trial that the video did not advance the investigation and nothing in the footage corroborated "any effort" to purchase potassium nitrate. During an undercover scenario, Mr. Nuttall did tell Corporal A. that he had acquired potassium nitrate from a drug store in the past. (d) Police database queries indicating that Mr. Nuttall had a criminal record and had been the subject of numerous complaints and investigations. Mr. Nuttall's criminal record included: a conviction in 1995 for carrying a concealed weapon; convictions in 1996 for robbery, kidnapping, and aggravated assault; a conviction in 1997 for assault; a conviction in 2002 for assault; and a conviction in 2003 for robbery. Mr. Nuttall had also been the subject of multiple entries in the RCMP's PRIME database, including two different 2010 files in which witnesses had reported seeing Mr. Nuttall use physical violence against Ms. Korody, a 2003 file about Mr. Nuttall's involvement in mugging a person, a 2001 report that Mr. Nuttall had vandalized a pharmacy after the staff refused to fill his methadone prescription, and a 1995 report indicating that Mr. Nuttall was charged with attempted murder in relation to the collection of a drug debt. The databases indicated that Mr. Nuttall had medical and mental health issues as well as drug dependency. [16] Between February 2, 2013, and February 23, 2013, the RCMP conducted surveillance of Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody to determine their movements and travel patterns. The police determined that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody lived in a basement suite in Surrey with Mr. Nuttall's grandmother. They observed that the two stayed inside their basement suite for long periods of time. When they left, it was on foot to go to a gas station store or the grocery store. Directed Verdict on the Facilitation Count (2015 BCSC 943) [211] The defence sought a directed verdict at the close of the Crown's case, arguing that there was no evidence that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody facilitated a terrorist activity, the offence charged in the Facilitation Count. The defence submitted that the evidence suggested the accused were the ones who placed the explosive devices, and that the Crown led no evidence that they facilitated others within the meaning of s. 83.19 of the Code. [212] Section 83.19 of the Code reads: (1) Every one who knowingly facilitates a terrorist activity is guilty of an indictable offence and liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding fourteen years. (2) For the purposes of this Part, a terrorist activity is facilitated whether or not (a) the facilitator knows that a particular terrorist activity is facilitated; (b) any particular terrorist activity was foreseen or planned at the time it was facilitated; or (c) any terrorist activity was actually carried out. [213] "Terrorist activity" is defined expansively in s. 83.01 of the Code. [214] The primary defence argument was that the legislative intent of s. 83.19 of the Code demonstrates that the provision should only apply to persons who helped others to carry out terrorist activities, and that other sections of the Code apply to the principals of those activities. [215] The Crown submitted that the provision is broad enough to capture principals of the substantive offence of committing a terrorist activity, similar to a lesser included offence, which is implicitly part of a greater offence. The Crown also argued that the definition of "facilitate" is broad enough to include helping oneself to commit a later terrorist activity and that, on the facts, Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody's earlier purchase of pressure cookers was an act that facilitated their later "placing of an explosive device " as captured by count 2. The Crown's final argument was that the charge should be left with the jury, and if there was a question of overlapping convictions, the rule against multiple convictions as set out in R. v. Kienapple (1974), [1975] 1 S.C.R. 729, could have been applied to stay the Facilitation Count if the jury delivered guilty verdicts on both counts. [216] Because the Code does not define "facilitation", the trial judge turned to case law for a definition. She adopted the definition developed in R. v. Lindsay, 2009 ONCA 532, which interpreted the term "facilitation" in s. 467.1(1) of the Code. That provision defines a criminal organization as a group that has as one of its main purposes or activities "the facilitation or commission of one or more offences that, if committed, would likely result in a direct or indirect benefit to the group". Justice MacPherson defined "facilitation" in that context as "to make a matter easier", and as "the act of making it easier for another person to commit a crime": Lindsay at paras. 23 and 24. [217] The trial judge then looked to statements made by the federal government before the Special Senate Committee Regarding Bill C-36, which introduced s. 83.19 of the Code. She found that those statements demonstrated that the provision was intended to target those who could not be captured by existing party liability law in the Code because they did not know the specific offence that would be committed, but were nonetheless knowingly facilitating a terrorist activity. [218] The trial judge also engaged in statutory interpretation of s. 83.19 of the Code. She noted that knowledge is an element of the offence, such that the accused must know that the facilitation is for a terrorist activity. She reasoned that if Parliament intended s. 83.19 of the Code to apply to principals "who essentially aided themselves in the commission of the terrorist activity", the knowledge requirement would be superfluous, as would the caveat that the facilitator does not have to know whether the activity was planned at the time of the facilitation, or whether it was actually carried out. She held that it was unreasonable to interpret the act of facilitation as applying "to the acts of a terrorist group themselves or to the actual participants in the alleged terrorist activity". [219] In rejecting the Crown's argument, the trial judge held that its broad proposed interpretation of s. 83.19 of the Code "[built] in the spectre of double punishment", because a principal could be convicted of the substantive terrorist activity as well as being convicted of knowingly facilitating the very same activity. [220] Ultimately, the trial judge determined that the purpose of s. 83.19 of the Code is: to capture persons who were on the periphery of terrorist groups and activities, but nevertheless aided in carrying out the acts themselves where the Crown could not prove knowledge of the specific terrorist activity involved. It was not intended to create additional punishment for the principal offenders who actually commit the terrorist activity. [221] The trial judge determined that the conduct targeted by s. 83.19 of the Code was not encompassed by the evidence led by the Crown and was inconsistent with the theory of liability it maintained throughout its case. Accordingly, she directed a verdict of not guilty for both accused on the Facilitation Count. E. Ruling on the "Duplicity" of Counts 1 and 2 (2015 BCSC 962) [222] The defence also applied for a ruling that counts 1 and 2 of the indictment, which both deal with conspiracy, were duplicitous because they both related to the same agreement and merely represented two objects of the same conspiracy. [223] The operative part of count 1 reads that the couple: did conspire together, the one with the other, to murder persons unknown contrary to s.465(1)(a) of the Criminal Code, for the benefit of, at the direction of, or in association with a terrorist group [224] The Crown did not particularize this count. In its opening address to the jury, it limited the conspiracy to an agreement between Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody to murder persons unknown by placing the pressure cooker devices, which the Crown said were explosive, on the grounds of the Parliament Buildings in Victoria. [225] The operative part of count 2 reads that the couple: did conspire together, the one with the other, to commit an indictable offence, to wit: placing an explosive in or against a place of public use, or a government or public facility, with intent to cause death or serious bodily injury, contrary to s.431.2(2) of the Criminal Code, for the benefit of, at the direction or, or in association with a terrorist group [226] Similarly, the Crown limited its case on count 2 to a conspiracy to place the pressure cooker devices on the grounds of the Parliament Buildings in Victoria, with the intent to cause death, and the additional element of the intention to cause serious bodily harm, which is an element of s. 431.2(2) of the Code. [227] The trial judge noted that the sole difference between these two counts is the object of the conspiracy, in that the second count includes an intention to cause serious bodily injury as an alternative to an intention to cause death. [228] The trial judge observed that where there is only one agreement, but different illegal objects, there can only be one conspiracy conviction, because the "substance of the offence of conspiracy to commit an indictable offence is the agreement and not the commission of the offence". She noted that where a single conspiracy has multiple objects, the Crown need only prove one object to support a conviction. [229] The trial judge determined that the Crown did not allege two separate conspiracies in counts 1 and 2, but one conspiracy with two different unlawful objects. Accordingly, she rejected the Crown's submissions regarding Kienapple: In my view, this is not a potential Kienapple situation where the accused could be convicted of two crimes arising out of the same factual matrix. Instead, the accused would be convicted of the same crime twice for the same delict. As a consequence, this is not a situation where the court should leave the matter to the jury and then address multiple convictions based on Kienapple after conviction. Instead, the court must instruct the jury that there is only one conspiracy, and thus they cannot convict the accused on both counts. [230] Accordingly, the trial judge determined that if the jury were to convict on count 1, it should not go on to determine if the couple were guilty on count 2. She held that she would remedy the duplicity in the counts by giving an instruction to that effect to the jury. Because the jury ultimately convicted the accused on count 1, it did not return a verdict on count 2. The trial judge then entered a conditional stay of proceedings on count 2. She entered an "unconditional" stay of proceedings at the end of the case as part of the entrapment ruling. F. Ruling on Entrapment and Abuse of Process (2016 BCSC 1404) [231] After the jury returned its guilty verdicts, Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody sought stays of proceedings on the grounds of entrapment and abuse of process. The trial judge granted a stay of proceedings on the grounds that the police entrapped the couple. In a separate analysis, she found that police misconduct during the investigation rose to the level of an abuse of process warranting a stay. (1) Entrapment ruling [232] At the outset of her legal analysis, the trial judge recognized that R. v. Mack, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 903, was the leading authority on entrapment in Canadian law. She explained that entrapment is founded on the need to preserve the integrity of the justice system and is therefore a form of abuse of process. She found that she could enter a stay of proceedings if entrapment was made out. [233] The trial judge wrote that three strands of entrapment were recognized in Mack, all of which, she wrote, required an objective examination of the police conduct. The first is where the state provides a person with an opportunity to commit an offence either without acting on a reasonable suspicion that the person was already engaged in criminal activity or pursuant to a bona fide inquiry. The second is where the police go beyond providing an opportunity and induce the commission of the offence, such that an average person with the accused's "strengths and weaknesses" would have committed the crime. The third and final category the trial judge recognized is a residual category of cases in which other factors support a finding of entrapment. a. Launching the investigation [234] The trial judge found the police had "very little evidence" at the beginning of the operation to support a reasonable suspicion that Mr. Nuttall was already engaged in terrorism-related criminal activity. She noted that the RCMP had no knowledge of the reliability of the CSIS tip regarding Mr. Nuttall and could not assume it was accurate. The initial surveillance revealed no evidence that the couple were involved in criminal activity and, indeed, they did very little outside their home. She noted that, at the time, it was not an offence to voice support for extremist jihadist beliefs or to travel outside Canada to carry out violent jihad and that radical beliefs, without more, could not amount to reasonable suspicion that the couple were already engaged in criminal activities. However, she concluded that the decision to embark on the investigation could not be faulted, as infiltrating terrorist groups is an essential method of investigating terrorism-related offences. [235] Despite the lack of reasonable suspicion at the commencement of the operation, the trial judge was satisfied that after the first meeting with Corporal A., Mr. Nuttall's openly extremist statements, coupled with his conduct in the subsequent scenarios, "amply met the objective test for reasonable suspicion". [236] The trial judge acknowledged that distinguishing investigative steps from the provision of an opportunity to commit the offence required a complex inquiry, as there were many promises made to the couple that could be interpreted "as setting the stage for the offer to commit an offence". b. Opportunity to commit the offence [237] In May and early June, once it was clear that the train plot was nonsensical due to the lack of a passenger train in Victoria, the investigative team struggled with whether to end the operation. The trial judge noted that some officers articulated a concern that the operation was overly influential, and that the police might turn Mr. Nuttall into a terrorist when he lacked the capacity to be one on his own. [238] Of course, the operation continued. The trial judge found that the offer to provide the defendants C-4, given the context of that offer, constituted an opportunity to commit the offence. She found that the offer was "held out as a package deal", as Corporal A. told the couple that he would not finance any of Mr. Nuttall's other schemes because they were unfeasible. She found that "all of the steps taken by the police after the Kelowna scenarios were intended to bring this pressure cooker plan, using C-4 as the explosive, to fruition". [239] The trial judge found that Corporal A.'s offer of C-4 on June 16 was not merely an inquiry into whether the couple were willing to engage in a terrorist act or any other preliminary investigatory step. She found that it was "a firm and specific opportunity [to commit an offence]", whereby Corporal A. "committed unconditionally to provide [C-4] for the pressure cooker devices". c. Reasonable suspicion that the couple were already engaged in criminal activity [240] The trial judge found that Mr. Nuttall's constant rhetoric of jihadist violence may have been disturbing, but it was also tiresome because nothing ever came of those expressions of belief. She found that there was ample evidence by June 2013 that confirmed Mr. Nuttall's general ineptitude, scatterbrained character, inability to think logically, and his inability to remain focused, which would have been "essential to the articulation and execution of a terrorist plot". [241] The trial judge found that while Mr. Nuttall talked incessantly about violent jihad, by the time of the Kelowna trip, he had demonstrated the "imaginary nature of his ideas and a lack of capacity to plan and execute them". She found that even Corporal A.'s pointed and repetitive attempts to focus the couple to form a concrete plan were "singularly unsuccessful in motivating them beyond talking about their religious beliefs and their jihadist ideas". She also found that while there was no doubt that Mr. Nuttall expressed a desire to obtain a firearm, he did not do anything to further that desire beyond talking about it. [242] Apart from outings with Corporal A., the trial judge noted that surveillance revealed that the couple spent all of their time within a four-block radius of their suite and never associated with radical individuals in person or on social media. [243] In light of these findings, the trial judge concluded that at the time Corporal A. offered C-4 for the pressure cooker devices, there was "little objective evidence to support a reasonable suspicion" that the couple were already engaged in criminal activity related to terrorism. She concluded that the offer of C-4 amounted to random virtue-testing within the meaning in Mack, based on Sergeant K.'s belief that Mr. Nuttall was predisposed to violence and that given enough incentive, he would commit a terrorist act. [244] Ms. Korody did not have any meaningful contact with police before the May 5, 2013 Whistler trip. Before then, the trial judge noted that the only police knowledge of her desire to participate in a violent jihadist plot came from Mr. Nuttall, whom she found to be "a notoriously unreliable source of information", such that the police would not have had a reasonable suspicion that she was already involved in criminal activity based solely on Mr. Nuttall's statements about her intentions. [245] After the May 5 Whistler trip, the trial judge found that it was apparent that Ms. Korody was complicit in Mr. Nuttall's grandiose schemes, providing the police with a reasonable suspicion within the meaning of Mack from that point forward. However, as with Mr. Nuttall, the trial judge found that by mid-June 2013, Ms. Korody had shown no more capacity to carry out and plan a terrorist act than Mr. Nuttall had. Accordingly, she also found that the offer of C-4, as it related to Ms. Korody, amounted to the police's providing an opportunity to commit a terrorist offence without a reasonable suspicion that she was already engaged in criminal activity. [246] Based on these findings, the trial judge held that the police entrapped the couple. Accordingly, she entered a stay of proceedings on counts 1 and 4, and an unconditional stay on count 2. d. Inducement [247] The trial judge also considered the second branch of entrapment. The second branch applies where the police, although they have a reasonable suspicion that the defendants are involved in criminal activity, go beyond providing an opportunity and actually induce the commission of the offence: Mack at 959. [248] The trial judge found, in assessing the whole of the operation, that this was not a situation in which the police disrupted an ongoing criminal plan, but that the offences were instead "brought about by the police" in a "clear case of police-manufactured crime". She assessed the Mack inducement factors in groups, (summarized below) because much of the evidence informed multiple factors. In the aggregate, the trial judge concluded that "[v]irtually every one of those factors [was] in play in this case". [249] Use of deceit, fraud, and implied threats; no ongoing criminal venture: The trial judge found that the police effort to convince the couple that Corporal A. was part of a large and sophisticated terrorist organization engaging in international acts of violence informed these factors. While the couple were "naïve [and] gullible", she reasoned that an average person, unburdened by those "personal frailties", would probably have come to the same conclusion that the officers were part of a sophisticated organization that "would not hesitate to eliminate them if they failed to meet their expectations". She found it unnecessary for the police to have resorted to the "extraordinary investigative methods" that they did, considering Mr. Nuttall's extreme frankness about his extremist beliefs even upon his first meeting with Corporal A. Additionally, she reasoned that portraying Corporal A. as part of a terrorist organization "was not required to maintain public safety" in light of the lack of an independent threat she found that Mr. Nuttall posed and that, if anything, "the ruse increased Mr. Nuttall's excitement about doing jihad". [250] Exploitation of vulnerabilities and friendship: The trial judge found that the police conduct "involved blatant manipulation of the defendants to exploit their dependence" upon Corporal A. and his friendship, citing their reliance on social assistance, their lack of friends and family support, and their status as recovering heroin addicts. She found that the couple were highly dependant on Corporal A. "in every respect", that he wielded great influence over them, and that he used their attachment to manipulate their actions. She also found that Corporal A. ensured that Mr. Nuttall involved Ms. Korody in the operation, dictated the plan that Mr. Nuttall "had to choose for his jihadist statement and the date and timing of this mission", and tricked him into believing that whatever he did was Allah's will. Due to their "unsophistication and child-like nature", the couple came to trust and love Corporal A. completely, making it easy for him to manipulate them and to isolate them from outside influences like friends and family. [251] Police conduct undermining constitutional values: The trial judge found that Corporal A.'s conduct concerning the provision of spiritual advice and his repeated rejection of Mr. Nuttall's requests to connect with an imam undermined values enshrined in the s. 2(a) Charter right of freedom of conscience and religion. While she did not explore in her entrapment analysis whether Corporal A.'s actions actually violated s. 2(a), she concluded that "the gravamen of the police misconduct is the decision to use spiritual guidance to induce a crime that was motivated by religious beliefs". She found that the couple did not have a working knowledge of Islam, and found that Corporal A.'s actions undermined s. 2(a) Charter values by isolating the couple from outside religious guidance, providing them guidance solely based on introspection and Corporal A.'s own interpretation of the Quran, and encouraging Mr. Nuttall to only seek spiritual advice from Corporal A., given that Mr. Nuttall's dependence upon the corporal was evident. The trial judge accepted expert evidence adduced by the couple that by promoting an introspective approach to Islamic interpretation that advocated "looking into one's heart", combined with Corporal A.'s counsel to adopt an extremist view on the use of violence, the officer isolated Mr. Nuttall from a moderate viewpoint and "propelled him toward a more radical conception of jihad". She found that Corporal A.'s concept of predestination, which he often offered in response to Mr. Nuttall's repeated requests for spiritual advice, encouraged the couple to "put aside their qualms about killing people because Allah decides everyone's fate". [252] Persistence and proportionality of police conduct, including illegal acts: The trial judge found that the police efforts to "mould" Mr. Nuttall into someone who could execute the pressure cooker plot were overly persistent and disproportionate to the roles of the accused. Further, she found that certain police conduct that pushed the operation forward actually amounted to criminal offences. She found that there was no evidence that the couple crafted plans to carry out violent jihad while alone, and that it was only when officers were present that they talked about committing violent terrorist acts. According to the trial judge, Corporal A. was persistent in his conduct, which included repeated instructions on how to craft a plan, redirection away from the couple's "unfeasible and grandiose" plans, and pledges to set aside any obstacles, be they financial, technical, logistical, or religious. She found Mr. Nuttall's fear of the officers to be genuine and found that it would have been clear to them that he feared for his life when he broke down in tears after being confronted with the impossibility of the passenger train hijacking plan. [253] To the trial judge, the Victoria recce also amounted to police manipulation. She found that Corporal A. convinced Mr. Nuttall to go to Victoria before he had a specific plan, insisted that Ms. Korody attend, used the trip to expand his fictitious organization by introducing more officers, impressed the couple with his willingness to finance the trip, and allayed Mr. Nuttall's concerns that the plan to commit extremist jihad would be an affront to Allah. [254] Finally, the trial judge found that the police role in the mission violated the Code because almost all of their actions relating to obtaining and handling real C-4 were "unsanctioned", and beyond the scope of the s. 25.1 Code authorization that the RCMP secured at the outset of the operation. She dealt more fully with these criminal acts in her abuse of process discussion, but found that the police aided and abetted the couple in the commission of explosives- and terrorism-related offences under the Code. (2) Abuse of process ruling [255] The trial judge also ruled that the police conduct outlined above amounted to an abuse of process warranting a stay under the "residual category", satisfied where state conduct risks undermining the integrity of the judicial process: R. v. Babos, 2014 SCC 16 at para. 31. [256] For this ruling, the trial judge incorporated her entrapment findings--which related to the couple's vulnerability and lack of motivation and capacity to commit the offences on their own--with her findings on the police conduct. She found that the police conduct in this case was an abuse of process because it was a "multi-faceted manipulation" of the couple, whom the police exploited while they were "impoverished, socially isolated and searching for spiritual meaning in their lives". [257] After those general comments, the trial judge analysed what she identified as illegal acts committed by the police. Based on her analysis of the meaning of "facilitation" in s. 83.19 of the Code, outlined above, she found that the police aided and abetted the couple by providing them with C-4, tools, transportation, accommodation, a location for construction, technical and logistical advice, and food, which were all "critical to carrying out the terrorist activity". She noted that the police were aware of the couple's desire to commit terrorist activities, and thus met the mens rea requirement of "knowingly" facilitating those activities. She also ruled that the police aided and abetted the offence of making or having an explosive substance with intent, for the benefit of, in association with, or at the direction of a terrorist group under ss. 81(1)(d) and 83.2 of the Code. She noted that all of the elements of that offence were made out in respect of the principals (Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody) based on the jury verdict. The trial judge found that the police had knowledge of the couple's intent to make use of an explosive device to cause death and property destruction at the Parliament Buildings, and acted intentionally to assist them in carrying out that plan. She found that the police did not obtain the requisite authorization under s. 25.1 of the Code to use actual C-4 in the undercover operation, and that "[t]he law does not accord the police a general justification defence for engaging in criminal acts, even where they go undercover to investigate illegal activities". [258] She found that the police essentially treated the operation like a traditional Mr. Big investigation. However, the key difference she identified was that unlike post-offence assistance offered to a suspect to facilitate a Mr. Big investigation, "where the object of the investigation is to facilitate the offence, albeit in a controlled manner, the police cannot break the law with impunity unless they have authorization to do so under s. 25.1". [259] The trial judge concluded that the police conduct, although "egregious" for the purposes of the entrapment inquiry, did not interfere with the couple's freedom of conscience and religion in a non-trivial way. This was because although Corporal A. promoted certain religious beliefs and disparaged others, "[i]nducing a particular religious belief is not a state-imposed limitation on the defendants' freedom to choose their own religious beliefs". [260] The trial judge ruled that the police conduct amounted to an abuse of process warranting a stay of proceedings. While she did not make a finding that the police acted in bad faith, she found a need to curtail police action "in a prospective sense" to ensure that future investigations did not follow the same path, and found that to permit the convictions to stand in the face of this kind of misconduct would cause irreparable damage to the integrity of the justice system. G. Issues on the Appeals [261] There are two separate Crown appeals in relation to each respondent. The first relates to the trial judge's entrapment ruling, under which the Crown raises several issues. First, it says that there were many palpable and overriding errors in the trial judge's factual findings. Second, it says that she erred in law in finding that the police no longer had "reasonable suspicion" when they offered to buy C-4 explosives. Third, it says that the trial judge lost sight of the fact that one of the counts was a conspiracy charge, and that their ability to carry through with the act of building and placing the bombs was not dispositive of the offence. Fourth, and finally, it says that she was incorrect to conclude that the police conduct amounted to an abuse of process requiring that the two remaining counts be stayed. [262] The second appeal alleges two primary errors. They are procedurally and substantively distinct enough that I refer to them as separate appeals throughout these reasons. The first relates to the decision of the trial judge to direct a verdict of acquittal on the Facilitation Count, finding that members of terrorist organizations cannot facilitate their own terrorist acts. The second relates to the instruction to the jury on the two conspiracy counts, which essentially took count 2 away from the jury. H. Crown Right to Appeal [263] The Crown appeals the acquittal on the Facilitation Count, the stay of proceedings on count 2, and the stay of proceedings on counts 1 and 4. The Crown's right to appeal the acquittal is found in s. 676(1)(a) of the Code, and its right to appeal the stays of proceedings is provided by s. 676(1)(c): 676 (1) The Attorney General or counsel instructed by him for the purpose may appeal to the court of appeal (a) against a judgment or verdict of acquittal or a verdict of not criminally responsible on account of mental disorder of a trial court in proceedings by indictment on any ground of appeal that involves a question of law alone; (c) against an order of a trial court that stays proceedings on an indictment or quashes an indictment; [264] On an appeal from an acquittal under s. 676(1)(a), the Crown is limited to questions of law alone. By contrast, when the appeal is from a stay of proceedings under s. 676(1)(c), the Crown can appeal on the basis of law, fact, or mixed fact and law: R. v. Franc, 2016 SKCA 129 at para. 25; R. v. Nguyen, 2013 ONCA 169 at para. 46. I. Standards of Review (1) Questions of fact [265] Findings of fact cannot be reversed on appeal unless it can be demonstrated that the trial judge made a "palpable and overriding error": Housen v. Nikolaisen, 2002 SCC 33. The standard "employ[s] a high level of appellate deference to findings of fact" and will only be met when a finding is "clearly wrong": Housen at paras. 10 and 22. [266] At para. 14 of Housen, Justice Iacobucci and Justice Major quoted Robert D. Gibbens's article "Appellate Review of Findings of Fact" (1991-2) 13 Advocates' Q 445 at 446: The trial judge is said to have an expertise in assessing and weighing the facts developed at trial. Similarly, the trial judge has also been exposed to the entire case. The trial judge has sat through the entire case and his ultimate judgment reflects this total familiarity with the evidence. The insight gained by the trial judge who has lived with the case for several days, weeks or even months may be far deeper than that of the Court of Appeal whose view of the case is much more limited and narrow, often being shaped and distorted by the various orders or rulings being challenged. [267] The test of palpable and overriding error applies to both findings of fact and inferences of fact: Housen at paras. 10 and 19. [268] In Benhaim v. St-Germain, 2016 SCC 48, Wagner J. (as he then was) quoted Stratas J.A. and Morissette J.A. in his review of the meaning of palpable and overriding error: [38] It is equally useful to recall what is meant by "palpable and overriding error". Stratas J.A. described the deferential standard as follows in South Yukon Forest Corp. v. R., 2012 FCA 165, 4 B.L.R. (5th) 31, at para. 46: Palpable and overriding error is a highly deferential standard of review . . . . "Palpable" means an error that is obvious. "Overriding" means an error that goes to the very core of the outcome of the case. When arguing palpable and overriding error, it is not enough to pull at leaves and branches and leave the tree standing. The entire tree must fall. [39] Or, as Morissette J.A. put it in J.G. v. Nadeau, 2016 QCCA 167, at para. 77 (CanLII), [translation] "a palpable and overriding error is in the nature not of a needle in a haystack, but of a beam in the eye. And it is impossible to confuse these last two notions." [Emphasis added.] [269] To state it simply, a palpable and overriding error is an obvious error that erodes the result. [270] In H.L. v. Canada, 2005 SCC 25, Justice Fish discussed alternative formulations of the "palpable and overriding error" standard. He explained that such an error is "clearly wrong" in that the appellate court can plainly identify the imputed error and show how it affected the result: at para. 55. He wrote that the test is also met when the findings of fact are "unreasonable or unsupported by the evidence": at para. 56. That language is more familiar to the criminal law, which permits an appeal on that basis under s. 686(1)(a)(i) of the Code. In criminal law, this provision does not apply to an appeal from an acquittal, as an "unreasonable acquittal" is incompatible with the presumption of innocence and the burden of proof on the Crown: R. v. Biniaris, 2000 SCC 15 at para. 33, citing Schuldt v. The Queen, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 592 at 610. [271] Justice Fish, in H.L. at para. 57, relied on Prof. Adrian A.S. Zuckerman's explanation of the standard of review in England in Civil Procedure (London: LexisNexis UK, 2003) at 765-68: As a general principle, an appeal court must not interfere with findings of fact made by the lower court for the simple reason that the judge who saw and heard the witnesses is better placed to assess their reliability and draw inferences from their testimony. An appeal court will interfere only if it concludes that no reasonable court could have reached such conclusions, or if the lower court failed to take crucial factors into consideration. . . . . . . It follows that, if the appeal court cannot conclude that the lower court's inference from the primary facts was wrong, in the sense that it fell outside the range of inferences that a reasonable court could make, the appeal court should allow the lower court's decision to stand. The nature of the appellate evaluation of the lower court's decision will vary in accordance with the type of judgment that the lower court was called upon to make. But whatever the nature of the issues and however wide or narrow is the room for disagreement, the test remains the same: was the lower court's decision wrong. . . . A decision will be wrong if it was founded on an incorrect interpretation of statute, or if it wrongly applied a legal principle, or if it was based on a plainly erroneous factual conclusion. . . . Put another way, as long as the lower court's conclusions represent a reasonable inference from the facts, the appeal court must not interfere with its decision. [272] I note that the weighing of evidence is not a question of law alone, but an absence of evidence to support a finding is a question of law alone: Schuldt at 610. (2) Stays of proceedings [273] The standard of review from a stay of proceedings is also deferential. Justice LeBel framed the standard in R. v. Regan, 2002 SCC 12: [117] The decision to grant a stay is a discretionary one, which should not be lightly interfered with: "an appellate court will be justified in intervening in a trial judge's exercise of his discretion only if the trial judge misdirects himself or if his decision is so clearly wrong as to amount to an injustice" (Tobiass, [[1997] 3 S.C.R. 391] at para. 87; Elsom v. Elsom, [1989] 1 S.C.R. 1367, at p. 1375). Furthermore, where a trial judge exercises her or his discretion, that decision cannot be replaced simply because the appellate court has a different assessment of the facts (Stein v. The Ship "Kathy K", [1976] 2 S.C.R. 802; see also R. v. Oickle, [2000] 2 S.C.R. 3, 2000 SCC 38; R. v. Van der Peet, [1996] 2 S.C.R. 507). [118] This does not mean, however, that the trial judge is completely insulated from review. It is settled law that where the "trial judge made some palpable and overriding error which affected his assessment of the facts", the decision based on these facts may be reversed (Kathy K, at p. 808) [274] In Regan, LeBel J. concluded that "the trial judge made palpable and overriding factual errors which set his assessment of the facts askew" (at para. 118). He also concluded that the trial judge erred in law by misdirecting himself regarding the law for granting a stay of proceedings (at para. 121). [275] Recently, in Babos, Justice Moldaver, for the majority, reiterated the standard of review from a stay of proceedings: [48] The standard of review for a remedy ordered under s. 24(1) of the Charter is well established. Appellate intervention is warranted only where a trial judge misdirects him or herself in law, commits a reviewable error of fact, or renders a decision that is "so clearly wrong as to amount to an injustice" (Bellusci, at para. 19; Regan, at para. 117; Tobiass, at para. 87; R. v. Bjelland, 2009 SCC 38, [2009] 2 S.C.R. 651, at paras. 15 and 51) (3) Questions of law [276] Finally, questions of law are reviewed on a correctness standard: Housen at para. 8. No deference is owed to the trial judge's findings on questions of law. J. Entrapment [277] As has been oft quoted, "the investigation of crime and the detection of criminals is not a game to be governed by the Marquess of Queensbury rules" (R. v. Rothman, [1981] 1 S.C.R. 640 at 697, per Lamer J.). However, while criminal investigations may not be models of politeness and fair play, they are not "no holds barred". There are still rules. Justice Lamer (as he then was), in Mack, outlined when those rules will be broken and when the "ends do not justify the means" (at 938). [278] Early steps towards establishing entrapment as a viable aspect of the abuse of process doctrine, now enshrined in s. 7 (R. v. O'Connor, [1995] 4 S.C.R. 411 at paras. 70-71), were taken in the concurring decision of Chief Justice Laskin in Kirzner v. The Queen, [1978] 2 S.C.R. 487 at 492-94. Kirzner was a drug trafficking case in which the defence of entrapment was taken from the jury at trial. The decision was upheld on appeal. The Ontario Court of Appeal had concluded that no such "defence" existed. In the Supreme Court, the majority concluded that the evidence did not support entrapment and dismissed the appeal without considering if the defence existed. The minority also dismissed the appeal on the same basis, but explored the possibility of such a defence, leaving the question open for another time. The minority acknowledged that so-called "consensual crimes"--crimes involving willing persons--including gambling and drug trafficking, do not necessarily lend themselves to "ordinary methods of detection" (at 493). Chief Justice Laskin isolated the judicial concern where the police have gone beyond "mere solicitation or mere decoy work and have actively organized a scheme of ensnarement, of entrapment, in order to prosecute the person so caught" (at 494). He discussed the disparate approaches in the United States, where the dichotomy was a "subjective approach" which relied on the predisposition of the offender, or an "objective approach" which focused on the conduct of the police (at 494). Although Laskin C.J.C. raised the issues, he left the answers to another day and another case (at 498). [279] As we know, in Mack, the Court came down squarely in favour of an objective approach. [280] The issue was next raised in the Supreme Court in Amato v. The Queen, [1982] 2 S.C.R. 418. In Amato, another drug trafficking case, the majority concluded that if the defence existed, it did not arise on the facts presented. Justice Estey's dissent, which ultimately formed the foundation of the defence in Mack, discussed the issue at length and concluded that the defence did exist. It concluded that the police conduct was "shocking and outrageous" and that the defence applied in the facts of that case. The dissent also pointed out that although entrapment is called a "defence", it is not a true defence, as it does not justify or excuse behaviour. [281] Justice Estey explained that for some offences, including those involving violence, entrapment would usually be inapplicable. He wrote that it would most frequently arise for the "consensual offences" that Laskin C.J.C. referenced in Kirzner, such as drug trafficking and illegal gambling. Justice Estey wrote that "[p]olicing must react differently to this kind of crime" and, significantly, that "[t]he move from passive to active policing brings the courts into the process" (at 457). When policing was purely "passive", the police and courts filled familiar roles and addressed familiar problems: the police gathered evidence and made arrests, and the courts determined the admissibility of that evidence and the guilt or innocence of those arrested and charged by applying legal principles. With "active" policing came new issues (at 458): The appointed spy may, pursuant to arrangements, infiltrate and report upon participants in a crime to which the agent may be a party or which the agent has provided to others the opportunity to commit. In some circumstances, indeed, the offence would in all likelihood not have otherwise been committed. The law enforcement agency in these circumstances moves from the position of merely affording an opportunity for the commission of crime to persons who may be so disposed to a position where the agency itself encourages the commission of the crime. Expressed another way, the police may have by any such measures instigated the offence, or at least its planning, and did so simply to catch the otherwise innocent members of the public in order to prosecute them. The ensnared or entrapped must in such a situation have a defence in law for the police and the state have gone beyond mere crime detection by decoy work or solicitation of evidence. In fact the crime has been designed and committed by the state itself. [282] I turn now to Mack, where the Court unanimously embraced the existence of the "defence" of entrapment. In Mack, the accused resisted approaches by a police informer over a number of months in regard to drug trafficking. He testified that he eventually capitulated to the requests because of the use of threats and the inducement of a large amount of money. [283] In Mack, Lamer J. identified the "ubiquitous nature of criminal activity in our society" (at 916) and acknowledged that many crimes are committed in secret and are of a consensual nature. He also identified where the dividing line is between acceptable and unacceptable police conduct. That distinction is between the police's providing an opportunity to a person to commit an offence while acting on "reasonable suspicion" or in the "course of a bona fide inquiry", and "the state actually creating a crime for the purpose of prosecution" (at 917). [284] Justice Lamer explained, following Estey J.'s dissent in Amato, that entrapment is an aspect of the abuse of process doctrine. It allows a court to enter a stay of proceedings "in circumstances where allowing the accused to stand trial would offend the courts' sense of justice" (at 919). [285] The rationale for entrapment is the maintenance of the integrity of our court system. As Lamer J. found, "It is a deeply ingrained value in our democratic system that the ends do not justify the means" (at 938). Significantly, this means that recognizing entrapment is not a matter of a court inappropriately disciplining the police (at 942). The court instead invokes its legitimate power to stay the proceedings because to condone the state's conduct would be to sacrifice the integrity of the administration of justice. [286] Justice Lamer recognized that there are competing interests at play in the doctrine of entrapment. We do not accept police conduct that amounts to entrapment because there must be inherent limits on the way that state power can be wielded to "attain the specific objective of obtaining convictions" (at 941). He wrote that the "intuitive reaction" against entrapment arises from a constellation of overlapping concerns, including that the techniques may result in the commission of crimes by people who otherwise would not have committed them, and that the police should not engage in criminal conduct for the purpose of entrapping others. He identified the latter concern as engaging the rule of law: creating crime is an inappropriate use of the substantial powers that we afford the police. [287] On the other hand, Lamer J. recognized that repressing criminal activity is an important social interest. Crime brings great costs to individuals and society at large. We depend on the police to protect us from those costs. What is required, Lamer J. explained, is "balance between the concepts of fairness and justice and the need for protection from crime" (at 942). [288] Reviewing, once again, the debate on whether the focus is on the subjective state of mind of the accused or the objective conduct of the police, Lamer J. concluded that the predisposition and subjective state of mind of the accused is not the issue. The focus is on the objective conduct of the police, and whether the police acted improperly (at 954-55). [289] Justice Lamer concluded that entrapment will exist when: (a) the police provide an individual with the opportunity to commit an offence without reasonable suspicion or while acting mala fides, or (b) although they have a reasonable suspicion or are acting in the course of a bona fide inquiry, they go beyond providing an opportunity and actually induce the commission of an offence (at 959). [290] Justice Lamer said that in assessing whether the conduct of the police gave rise to the latter category of entrapment (at 959-60): it is useful to consider whether the conduct of the police would have induced the average person in the position of the accused, i.e., a person with both strengths and weaknesses, into committing the crime. I believe such a test is useful not only as an analytical mechanism that is consistent with objective analysis, but also because it corresponds to one of the reasons why the defence is thought desirable. In other words, it may be inevitable that, when apprised of the factual context of an entrapment case, members of the community will put themselves in the position of the accused; if a common response would be that anyone could have been induced by such conduct, this is a valuable sign that the police have exceeded the bounds of propriety. The reasoning does not go so far as to imply that the accused is therefore less blameworthy; rather, it suggests that the state is involved in the manufacture as opposed to the detection of crime. [Emphasis in original.] [291] However, Lamer J. also found that the hypothetical or average person model is not the only relevant method of analysis in the latter category. In some cases, even if the hypothetical person would not likely have committed the offence, the conduct of the police can nevertheless support the conclusion that they went beyond the bounds of propriety and instigated the crime. In such cases, the police conduct would thereby amount to an abuse of process (at 961-62). [292] Justice Lamer summarized some of the factors to be considered in an entrapment analysis as follows (at 966): - the type of crime being investigated and the availability of other techniques for the police detection of its commission; - whether an average person, with both strengths and weaknesses, in the position of the accused would be induced into the commission of a crime; - the persistence and number of attempts made by the police before the accused agreed to committing the offence; - the type of inducement used by the police including: deceit, fraud, trickery or reward; - the timing of the police conduct, in particular whether the police have instigated the offence or became involved in ongoing criminal activity; - whether the police conduct involves an exploitation of human characteristics such as the emotions of compassion, sympathy and friendship; - whether the police appear to have exploited a particular vulnerability of a person such as a mental handicap or a substance addiction; - the proportionality between the police involvement, as compared to the accused, including an assessment of the degree of harm caused or risked by the police, as compared to the accused, and the commission of any illegal acts by the police themselves; - the existence of any threats, implied or express, made to the accused by the police or their agents; - whether the police conduct is directed at undermining other constitutional values. [293] In summary, Lamer J. identified the rationale for entrapment as the recognition that there must be inherent limits on state power to "manipulate people and events" to obtain convictions (at 917). However, this recognition must be balanced against the competing social interest in the state's ability to protect the public from "the immense social and personal cost of crime". [294] The guilt or innocence of the accused is not the issue in an entrapment claim. Entrapment is not a defence; in most cases, police conduct caught by the doctrine will not have the effect of negating either the actus reus or mens rea of the offence: Mack at 941 and 943. Rather, the central issue on an entrapment claim is the "avoidance of the improper invocation by the state of the judicial process and its powers" (Mack at 942, quoting Amato at 461, per Estey J., dissenting). In the context of entrapment, "the court's sense of justice is offended by the spectacle of an accused's being convicted of an offence which is the work of the state": Mack at 942, citing Amato at 447, per Estey J., dissenting. [295] The Crown must first discharge its burden to prove the essential elements of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt before an entrapment claim is heard, and the accused's guilt or innocence must be determined apart from evidence relevant only to the issue of entrapment: Mack at 972. [296] Once in the entrapment phase of the proceedings, the onus is on the accused to prove on a balance of probabilities "that the conduct of the state is an abuse of process because of entrapment": Mack at 975. As I discuss further below, if the accused is successful in meeting that burden, then the remedy is a stay of proceedings. [297] Entrapment is a question to be the decided by the trial judge. Justice Lamer identifies the question as "one of law, or mixed law and fact": Mack at 967. He does not address the standard of appellate review for entrapment claims. However, in applying the entrapment principles developed earlier in the reasons, Lamer J. identifies an error in the trial judge's reasoning process that could be characterized as an extricable legal error reviewable for correctness (at 977): In determining whether the doctrine of entrapment applies to the present appeal, this Court is restricted to the summary of evidence provided by Wetmore Co. Ct. J. in his reasons. I am of the view that a stay of proceedings should be entered in this case. While the trial judge had the advantage of hearing the testimony of the appellant, and normally findings on entrapment cases should not be disturbed because of this, I am concerned that in this case too much emphasis was placed on the appellant's state of mind. Earlier in my summary of the decisions below I cited a passage from the trial judge's reasons wherein he stated that the fundamental issue was the appellant's state of mind and his predisposition to crime. This, perhaps, explains why in his conclusion the trial judge stated the appellant was not entrapped because he acted out of a desire to profit from the transaction. If the trial judge had been permitted only to evaluate the conduct of the police objectively, I think he might well have, and in any event, ought to have come to the conclusion the police conduct amounted to entrapment. [Emphasis added.] [298] In concluding, the Court emphasized that the burden is on the offender to establish that theirs is one of the "clearest of cases" of entrapment, the standard that must be met to establish a stay of proceedings as a remedy for an abuse of process. Furthermore, "the state must be given substantial room to develop techniques which assist in its fight against crime in society" (at 976). [299] One of the main issues in this case concerns the "reasonable suspicion" branch of the defence. This Court, in R. v. Gingras, 2013 BCCA 293, concluded that a reasonable suspicion could arise from an accused's offers to be involved in criminal activity. This Court, at para. 26, applied the decision in R. v. Kang-Brown, 2008 SCC 18 at para. 75, and concluded that the police were not required to operate at a level of certainty. Reasonable suspicion is something more than a mere suspicion but less than reasonable and probable grounds. (1) Alleged palpable and overriding errors of fact [300] The law on entrapment requires careful attention to the facts. If facts material to the foundation of an entrapment finding are set aside, the conclusion may be in jeopardy. [301] The appellant Crown alleges that the trial judge committed a wide range of palpable and overriding errors in the extensive factual findings underpinning her entrapment analysis. Most of the trial judge's factual findings functionally mirror the factual summary she delivered in her jury charge, outlined above. Accordingly, here I will set out only those specific factual findings the Crown impugns on appeal. [302] The Crown categorizes the alleged factual errors into five main areas: the impetus for the pressure cooker bomb plot, the timing of the pressure cooker bomb plot, the placement of the bombs, the construction and functionality of the bombs, and the respondents' fear that they would be killed if they did not follow through with the plot. The positions of the parties are set out in each section, along with the discussion regarding palpable and overriding error. [303] For the reasons that follow, I find that the vast majority of the trial judge's factual findings are devoid of palpable and overriding error. The only exception is the final area: the trial judge's finding that the respondents feared that they would be killed was inconsistent with an earlier finding she made in the course of the trial. Additionally, she applied a subjective test to those findings. For those reasons, that finding cannot stand. a. Impetus for the pressure cooker bomb plot [304] The trial judge's ultimate conclusion on this point was that the police "instigated and skilfully engineered the very terrorist act committed by the defendants" (Reasons at para. 718). [305] The Crown's position is that Mr. Nuttall first proposed the plan on April 26, based on a schematic he obtained online from Inspire magazine. Mr. Nuttall repeatedly stated his desire to emulate the Boston bombers. Ms. Korody declared "this is our plan" and "we actually got the idea from, uh, from the brothers in Boston". [306] The defence position is that the fact that Mr. Nuttall was the first party to mention a pressure cooker plot does not lead, logically or causatively, to the conclusion that the RCMP did not later redirect him to the pressure cooker plot, and were not its impetus. The Crown cites Mr. Nuttall's brief mention on April 26 of pressure cookers used in the Boston bombing as proof that the impetus for the ultimate plan was his. However, while Mr. Nuttall mentioned pressure cookers, the defence submits that he did not commit to that plan on April 26. He also spoke about other plans, like hijacking a nuclear submarine, and said nothing about pressure cookers for many weeks afterward (Reasons at para. 170). [307] The trial judge made the following seven impugned factual findings driving her conclusion that the police, not Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody, came up with the pressure cooker bomb plot. The Crown submits that the conclusion that the police instigated and skillfully engineered the terrorist attack lies in the following unfounded findings. For the reasons below, I do not think any of the seven factual findings impugned by the Crown in this category were palpable and overriding errors. I would not interfere with the trial judge's findings on any of the alleged errors. (i) When the police realized they could not watch over Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody indefinitely, they had to "redirect Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody's jihadist plans towards something that was feasible and realistic and which could be accomplished quickly and cheaply; namely, the pressure cooker devices" (Reasons at para. 724). [308] The Crown's position is that Mr. Nuttall first raised the possibility of a pressure cooker bomb attack, and repeatedly characterized it as a quick, cheap, effective means of pursuing violent jihad. The Crown notes that Mr. Nuttall said he wanted to proceed as soon as possible because Ms. Korody was very sick. [309] The defence says the trial judge acknowledged the police did not first identify the pressure cooker plot; however, Mr. Nuttall regarded the device as a tester when he mentioned it, and "the police seized on this idea and it became their plan" (Reasons at para. 775). The defence notes that Mr. Nuttall was not referring to the pressure cooker plan when he said he wanted to proceed as soon as possible. [310] In my view, this finding by the trial judge was not an error, and was amply supported by the evidence. The pressure was on the police to wind up the undercover operation. Mr. Nuttall had been discussing far-fetched plans, such as launching Qassam rockets, charging the Esquimalt naval base, and taking possession of a nuclear submarine. The police concluded that they would steer, and in fact pressured, Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody toward the pressure cooker bomb plan. (ii) "Shortly after the Boston bombing in mid-April 2013, Mr. Nuttall commented that the pressure cooker devices used by the Boston bombers was a good, simple plan. However, he had made no further reference to pressure cookers until the meeting on June 6th" (Reasons at para. 734). [311] The Crown says this is an error because Mr. Nuttall also repeatedly referred to the Boston bombing as "cheaper", "easy to do", and "faster" than his proposed rocket plan. [312] The defence position is that even accepting the Crown's submissions, this disparity does not amount to a palpable and overriding error of fact. Additionally, this evidence was more thoroughly reviewed at paras. 170-73 of her reasons, where the trial judge found that on April 26, 2013, Corporal A. redirected Mr. Nuttall from a discussion of Ms. Korody's health by saying that Corporal A. "understood why [Mr. Nuttall was] looking for an operation that [could] be done easily, fast, and [wouldn't] need that much money" (Reasons at para. 172). The trial judge noted that "[n]one of these assertions came from Mr. Nuttall". The defence also says there was evidence that Mr. Nuttall never said the bomb plot was cheaper, easier to do, or faster. In cross-examination, it was put to Corporal A. that those words referred to a totally different plan concerning a train. It was open to the trial judge to conclude that "fast" and "cheap" were not used to describe the pressure cookers. [313] In my view, the conclusion by the trial judge is not an error. There was no specific mention of the pressure cooker bombs between when Mr. Nuttall first mentioned them on April 26 and when he showed Corporal A. the instructions he found in Inspire on June 6. I agree with the defence that it was open to the trial judge to conclude that "fast" and "cheap" were in reference to one of Mr. Nuttall's other plans. (iii) Prior to June 6, 2013, Mr. Nuttall refused to use the Internet at home to research his terrorist plans because he was afraid of detection, and Corporal A. instructed him about Tor anonymity software to solve the problem (Reasons at para. 734). [314] The Crown says this is an error because a search of Mr. Nuttall's computer revealed he first downloaded the bomb schematic from Inspire two months before meeting Corporal A. [315] The defence says that the trial judge was making a finding on the discrete matter of Mr. Nuttall using the Internet for research at his own home. The defence says there was no evidence that when Mr. Nuttall downloaded Inspire two months before meeting Corporal A., he did so at his home, so the trial judge did not err. [316] Again, the finding that Mr. Nuttall did not download information at home before he learned of Tor is supported by the evidence. On March 7, he told Corporal A. that he did not download anything from extremist jihadist websites while at home, but only did so using public Wi-Fi hotspots. Corporal A. told him about Tor during the May 15 scenario. (iv) "Mr. Nuttall presented the pressure cooker diagram to [Corporal A.] as a means of testing his ability to make an explosive device in preparation for building the rockets. They were for a tester and 'not the real thing'" (Reasons at para. 734). [317] The Crown submits that this is an error because Mr. Nuttall also said, in the same meeting on June 6, "we could do the rocket idea, which I think is brilliant, but would take a year of planning"; by contrast, the pressure cooker bomb would not be "as spectacular", but "the body count [would] be the same"; it would be "pure terrorism". When Corporal A. asked what Mr. Nuttall would be doing if they had never met, Mr. Nuttall said he would be making the kettle bomb "right now". [318] The defence argues that Mr. Nuttall described the pressure cookers as "just for testing, playing around with and you know, practice". The defence says it was open to the trial judge to conclude on that date he described the pressure cooker as a tester. In ensuing scenarios, Mr. Nuttall described a variety of plans without mentioning pressure cookers (Reasons at para. 320). [319] In my view, the trial judge's finding is not an error. While Mr. Nuttall did tell Corporal A. he would be making a bomb if they never met, the missing context is that immediately afterward, Mr. Nuttall rambled on about a number of different plans. The Crown also ignores the trial judge's analysis of the events of June 6 and her findings regarding the broader context of the meeting (Reasons at paras. 318-21, 626-27). (v) Mr. Nuttall, Ms. Korody, and undercover officers discussed the non-feasibility of Mr. Nuttall's plan to storm a naval base or launch homemade rockets. The trial judge described Mr. Nuttall's "extremely downcast demeanor and tone of voice" in a subsequent interaction between him and Mr. Korody, in which he told her that "their rocket plan had to be put aside in favour of [Corporal A.]'s plan that they build pressure cooker devices, 'Not [as] a test but the real thing'" (Reasons at para. 739). [320] The Crown submits that this finding is an error because after expressing a belief that they were taking resources away from the mujahideen, Mr. Nuttall's exact words were: No we're not doing that idea anymore. We're doing the Boston idea. We're continue, we're going to continue their work *inshallah* okay. That's what this is about okay. Forget the rockets. *lnshallah* one day afterwards but we've got to continue their work those two brothers. I see that now, seriously. Look at it like you're continuing those brothers work. They got caught they can't continue. [321] The defence says notwithstanding that the precise words "not as a test but the real thing" were not spoken by Mr. Nuttall, the trial judge accurately captures the thrust of the conversation the Crown quotes. The trial judge further held that "it was clear that they had gotten the message that their rocket plan be scrapped in favour of [Corporal A.]'s pressure cooker plan" (Reasons at para. 346). The defence says that the reference to "real thing" versus "tester" should be considered in light of the fact that those terms, in relation to pressure cookers, arose elsewhere in the judgment, reflecting that these two potential uses of pressure cookers were a repeated theme (Reasons at paras. 319, 627 and 734). [322] In my view, although the evidence, like Mr. Nuttall's "plan", was "all over the map", it was open to the trial judge to conclude that Mr. Nuttall's initial idea was to use the pressure cooker bomb as a "test" before he constructed his rockets. (vi) After acknowledging that "[t]he police did not first identify the idea of exploding pressure cookers," the trial judge added, "however, Mr. Nuttall regarded this device as a tester" (Reasons at para. 775). [323] The Crown submits that this is an error because after showing the bomb diagram to Corporal A., Mr. Nuttall described it as a "tester", but in the same June 6 conversation posited that it represented a quicker yet equally effective alternative to his rocket plan, as his rocket plan would take a year. In a later conversation, Mr. Nuttall again referred to the bomb as a "tester", but said he wanted to make at least four bombs. Mr. Nuttall purchased four pressure cookers, intending to use one as a tester and the others as real bombs. Mr. Nuttall later returned one, as it had plastic hinges and would not explode. When Mr. Nuttall recognized there was no time for a test, he settled on three pressure cooker bombs, one with its own timer, and the other two linked together with one timer. [324] The defence position is that the Crown cites evidence from the last few days of the operation. Simply because by the end of the operation Mr. Nuttall no longer viewed the pressure cooker bombs as "testers", it does not follow that he did not view them as testers much earlier on, before he was pressured to adopt the plan. [325] In my view, the trial judge did not make a palpable and overriding error. She was correct that Mr. Nuttall considered the pressure cooker bombs to be testers for much of the investigation. Further, Mr. Nuttall again mentioned a number of different plans in a short period of time during the June 6 conversation with Corporal A. It was open to the trial judge to make the finding that she did. (vii) "At no time did Mr. Nuttall refer to the pressure cookers as his plan for jihad" (Reasons at para. 734). [326] The Crown says that this finding is in error because Mr. Nuttall repeatedly stated that he got the idea from Inspire magazine and that he wanted to "continue" or "build on" the work of the Boston Marathon bombers. Mr. Nuttall elsewhere stated it was "kind of all our plans", and "we all kinda chipped in", but also said he was "inspired by Inspire" and the Boston bombers. Mr. Nuttall begged Sergeant S. to give him the explosives so he could complete the mission. Mr. Nuttall described the pressure cooker bombs as his "back up idea", conceding that his other ideas were no longer possible, or were less feasible. Ms. Korody said at various times, "We actually got the idea" from "the brothers in Boston", credited Mr. Nuttall for coming up with the idea, and stated, "This is our plan". [327] The defence argues that far from taking ownership of the pressure cooker plan, Mr. Nuttall identified Corporal A. as the impetus for the plan. It was only in response to Sergeant S. feigning an angry outburst that Mr. Nuttall "cast off his diffidence regarding the pressure cooker plot". The defence says the interview with Sergeant S. fully supports the trial judge's findings (Reasons at paras. 412-20, 734). [328] In my view, while Mr. Nuttall may not have said the precise words as found by the trial judge, he clearly, from time to time, took credit for the plan as his own. On the other hand, the evidence could also support a finding that the pressure cooker bombs were not Mr. Nuttall's plan until much later into the undercover operation, after he was pressured by the police to follow through with the pressure cooker bombs. Read charitably in context, the trial judge's finding is that Mr. Nuttall did not take ownership of the pressure cooker bomb idea until later in the investigation, after he had been pressured by the undercover officers to do so. Given that there is evidence that supports the trial judge's finding, I cannot interfere with it. b. Timing of the pressure cooker bomb plot [329] The trial judge ultimately concluded that the RCMP dictated the date and time of the pressure cooker plan (Reasons at para. 692). [330] The Crown submits that the trial judge made five palpable and overriding errors in her factual findings related to police direction on the timing of the plot. I do not agree that any of those findings were palpable and overriding errors. (i) "The Victoria recce evidenced the police manipulation of the defendants in several ways. First, [Corporal A.] manipulated Mr. Nuttall into going to Victoria before he had decided on a specific plan or selected a target" (Reasons at para. 729). [331] The Crown submits that this finding is an error. The Crown relies on evidence that it says shows that "Mr. Nuttall consistently and repeatedly affirmed his desire to conduct a terrorist attack on a target in the Victoria area". This included evidence that Mr. Nuttall described Victoria as "familiar" and "easier", said that the Parliament Buildings were "always" the target, and said that he "picked" Victoria because he and Ms. Korody knew the city well. [332] The Crown also refers to a number of other facts in support of its contention. Mr. Nuttall first proposed to scout Victoria on May 5, 2013. On May 10, Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall he needed to research his plan. Mr. Nuttall responded that he needed to conduct a recce of potential targets in Victoria and he wanted to go some time that week. On May 14, Mr. Nuttall, of his own initiative, bought a digital camera to use for the recce in Victoria, telling Corporal A. that using the camera to photograph potential terror targets would amount to "conspiring to commit high treason". On May 15, 2013, Corporal A.'s handlers told him to discuss Mr. Nuttall's wish to conduct a recce. The Crown submits that it was only then that Corporal A. and Mr. Nuttall began discussing the logistics of the trip. [333] The defence asserts that the Crown is advancing a straw man argument. The defence says that the Crown is not attacking the trial judge's actual finding--that the RCMP manipulated the timing of the recce--but what are actually findings the trial judge did not make regarding manipulation unrelated to timing. [334] The defence first summarizes evidence supporting the trial judge's finding that the RCMP manipulated the Victoria recce timing. On May 10, Mr. Nuttall spoke to Corporal A. about conducting a recce in Victoria "that week". Corporal A. replied that Mr. Nuttall needed to make a plan before that could happen. The planning the Crown cites was a condition precedent to the recce occurring. On May 15, Corporal A. asked Mr. Nuttall when he wanted to do his first recce, and Mr. Nuttall said, "When do you think I should [do] it? I need help". Corporal A. said he would "take care of it", "let me plan it, check what I can do", and later "let me plan it and check when we can go". At a May 15 RCMP briefing, investigators discussed whether the Victoria recce should occur before any plan had crystallized (Reasons at para. 240). [335] Mr. Nuttall lost enthusiasm as the recce approached and did not do any of the required planning. On May 22, Corporal A. pressed Mr. Nuttall to work on targets. Mr. Nuttall said they should defer the recce, expressing logistical concerns. Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall not to worry and that he would take care of it, which Mr. Nuttall agreed to (Reasons at para. 261). [336] Turning to the evidence the Crown cites, the defence says the extracts relate to matters unrelated to the factual finding regarding manipulation of timing. The defence says the Crown cites evidence that makes no reference to the Victoria recce, and cites numerous references to instances where Mr. Nuttall discussed targeting Vancouver Island, but says nothing about whether the RCMP manipulated the timing of the recce. The defence points to three Crown citations of Mr. Nuttall "repeatedly affirming" his desire to attack Victoria on June 30, well after the recce occurred. In any event, the defence submits, the RCMP were interested in a Victoria recce before Mr. Nuttall ever raised it, as it was raised at an internal meeting on April 9 (Reasons at para. 148). [337] In my view, this finding is not a palpable and overriding error. On May 22, Mr. Nuttall thought they were getting ahead of themselves. He thought they should buy the rockets first because the targets could change. In addition, I accept the defence's point that the trial judge's finding was not that the police manipulated the target, but that they manipulated the timing of the recce to Victoria, a finding supported by evidence. (ii) "[Corporal A.] pressured the defendants to come up with a date for the explosion. He cornered them into a date before Ramadan by saying that he was going away for this holiday and did not know if he would be coming back to Canada after Ramadan. [Corporal A.] raised the question of Canada Day " (Reasons at para. 374). [338] The Crown asserts that this finding is an error because Corporal A. did not propose Canada Day to Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody. In a private conversation intercepted by the police on or about June 22, 2013, Ms. Korody said to Mr. Nuttall that they should target a fireworks festival like Canada Day or B.C. Day. On June 25, 2013, the topic of Canada Day arose after Corporal A. explained he would be going away for Ramadan, and Mr. Nuttall said they should get started right away. [339] The defence says that the finding is justified because the RCMP were planning from early on that the mission would take place on Canada Day. On June 4, Sergeant K. anticipated Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody would settle in July 1. On June 5, the RCMP discussed an arrest date of July 1. On June 16, Ms. Korody said the mission should not occur until after Ramadan. Mr. Nuttall said it would take a year to plan. Corporal A. said, "The sooner the better, why take a year?" The investigative plan for the June 25 scenario was to manipulate Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody into selecting July 1 without explicitly mentioning it: "[Staff Sergeant K.] would like them to confirm that July 1st is the day, given it's Canada Day and before Ramadan. However, this has to come from the targets". After Corporal A. said he was leaving for Ramadan and did not know if he would be coming back, Mr. Nuttall replied, "We gotta do it before uh ..." Corporal A. said, "If you wanna do it before, when you gonna do it? Do you have a, a date, like a day that will be like s-, like significant in mind. Something, I don't know." Nuttall continued to demur: "Let's get this tested, built and see, see what this does first." Corporal A. returned to the matter of timing in the meeting, and said, "The only thing is, like if we project a date, then we know how fast we have to work," and reminded Mr. Nuttall that the "brother" was on his way. Mr. Nuttall told Corporal A. he thought it was a mistake to rush. Corporal A. asked what the best time to do it was. Mr. Nuttall responded that they could do it during one of the fireworks celebrations, going on to note, "Canada Day is coming up, didn't you say?" Mr. Nuttall identified that day as June 1. Ms. Korody clarified it was July 1 and did not know if 5 days was enough time to prepare. Mr. Nuttall asserted they had a month to prepare, Ms. Korody reminded him it was in five days, and Mr. Nuttall replied, "It doesn't have to be July 1st. It'd be nice if it could be July 1st." Corporal A. interjected, "No, we can make it . we can try." Ms. Korody said there was another fireworks display August 1. Corporal A. said he would be away, but said, "Let's work", as if they could "make it" for July 1. Corporal A. pressured them to come up with a mission date within the short time remaining before he left Canada, and to select a "significant" day. Corporal A. had previously told Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody that Ramadan began on July 10. The defence submits that the trial judge had ample evidence to find that Corporal A. manipulated them into choosing Canada Day without mentioning the date explicitly (Reasons at para. 372). [340] In my view, it is clear that the police were pushing for Canada Day. They wanted the operation to conclude, and when Canada Day was mentioned as one of several dates, the police landed on it, and put the pressure on them to complete the act on that date. I would not interfere with the trial judge's finding. (iii) On June 25, when Ms. Korody reminded Mr. Nuttall that Canada Day was July 1, he felt this was too rushed and wanted to wait until the following year (Reasons at paras. 374 and 476). [341] The Crown submits that during the June 25 discussion in which Canada Day was identified as the day of the attack, Mr. Nuttall did not state he wanted to wait until the following year. He said they all had to "work hard". The Crown submits that in the June 29 interview with Sergeant K., Mr. Nuttall told him it was his idea to carry out the plan on Canada Day. [342] The defence submits that the finding is not an error, as Sergeant K. testified that a wiretap intercept of Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody's basement suite showed they were discussing whether to do it Canada Day 2013 or 2014, and whether the timeline was too rushed. The defence submits that the Crown's reliance on the interview with Sergeant S. is misplaced, as things Mr. Nuttall said on June 29 could not inform his words or feelings on June 25. If what Mr. Nuttall said on June 29 is material, the defence submits the key point is that he thought the timing was rushed. [343] On that day, Corporal A. said he thought July 1 was Mr. Nuttall's idea, and Mr. Nuttall responded, "No, no, no, it was your idea, brother." Mr. Nuttall only changed his tune after Sergeant S. stormed out of the motel room and erupted in anger. Even then, Mr. Nuttall maintained that Corporal A. had pressured the timing: "That part is because he has an engagement coming up he has to meet." Sergeant S. responded, "Before July 9th." Mr. Nuttall replied, "Yes. So, we have to do it before then ... so, why not on Canada Day?" Mr. Nuttall agreed Canada Day was his idea, but it was Corporal A's idea to "speed it up because he's gotta go." [344] In my view, there was evidence to support the trial judge's finding, as the defence submits, and it therefore cannot be said that there was a palpable and overriding error. (iv) After the June 25 discussion about Canada Day, Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody felt rushed and wanted to wait until the following year for Canada Day 2014 (Reasons at paras. 374 and 746). [345] The Crown says that this finding is an error because Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody agreed they were rushed and would prefer more time if it was available, but in their private conversations after June 25, they confirmed they were working toward a deadline less than one week away. Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody expressed both privately and to Corporal A. that the mission could not wait, and that Canada Day was a good, symbolic day for the bombing. [346] The defence says that this alleged error is duplicative of the previous one, and, in any case, the couple continued to feel rushed after the June 25 meeting. The trial judge found that Staff Sergeant K. directed Corporal M. that July 1st was the preferred date. He wanted Corporal A. to put pressure on Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody to accept that date. Corporal M. testified that investigators believed July 1 was a significant day, and that if Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody had this as their "D Day", it would spur them on (Reasons at paras. 371-72). The next day, after Corporal A. discussed Canada Day with them, Mr. Nuttall asked, "Isn't Monday gonna be Canada Day?" and said, "I don't wanna rush this". On June 27, Mr. Nuttall told Ms. Korody he was still uncertain which day Canada Day was. The trial judge relied on these communications about timing (Reasons at para. 374). On June 30, Mr. Nuttall continued to talk about the plan being rushed and said that the plan was "forced on them". [347] Although I do not agree that this alleged error is duplicative of the previous one, I do not think it is a palpable and overriding error. The previous alleged error concerned the respondents' state of mind during the June 25 meeting, whereas this alleged error concerns the trial judge's findings on their state of mind after that meeting. In any case, it is not a palpable and overriding error. As the defence outlines, there was evidence that supported the trial judge's conclusion. (v) "It was also [Corporal A.] who determined when the detonators would be set to go off. He instructed the defendants when they were going to plant the devices; and that they would plant the devices and be off the Island before the explosion occurred" (Reasons at para. 757). [348] The Crown says the evidence shows that the respondents determined when the devices would be planted. On June 25, Mr. Nuttall first proposed to plant the bombs at 4:00 or 5:00 a.m., when it was dark, so they could "take their time" planting the bombs. Ms. Korody said they could set the timers to give themselves enough time to be off the Island by the time the bombs went off. [349] The defence submits that the Crown is relying on decontextualized pieces of conversations on June 25. The defence says that no decision regarding timing can be said to have made here, as the very next day, June 26, Mr. Nuttall told Corporal A. they should plant the bombs at 10:01 a.m. The defence submits that what Mr. Nuttall said was "the same random scattergun expression of views that characterized the entire undercover operation". By June 29, an objective of that day's RCMP scenario was to confirm that the bombs would be placed by 5:00 a.m. This would have been redundant, the defence says, if timing had already been conclusively determined by Mr. Nuttall. Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody " Because five o'clock everything has to be done . And that's my plan. Like that's what I was working on " On June 30, Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody that the devices would be planted at 5:00 a.m. because there would be few police on the streets. On the alleged error regarding when the bombs would go off, the defence says Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody on multiple occasions that the plan would allow them to escape and that it was not a suicide mission. [350] In my view, while there was evidence to support another finding, as the Crown submits, there was evidence supporting the finding of the trial judge. It cannot be said that she made a palpable and overriding error. c. Placement of the bombs [351] The ultimate finding that the trial judge made on this point was that the RCMP pressured the respondents into agreeing to a target of the RCMP's choosing. [352] The Crown submits that the trial judge made 11 palpable and overriding errors relating to her findings that the police directed the placement of the bombs. In my view, all 11 of those impugned findings were open on the evidence, and therefore none can be disturbed. (i) "Instead of letting the defendants direct the timing of any mission, the police used [Corporal A.]'s influence over them to pressure them into agreeing to a target, a target that the defendants realized right away was acceptable to [Corporal A.] because he had pointed it out several times and called it a perfect location for the devices" (Reasons at para. 463). [353] The Crown says that from the outset, Mr. Nuttall proposed the Parliament Buildings as a potential target. The Crown also points to other evidence that suggests that the couple took a leading role in choosing the location of the mission. On June 25, 2013, when Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody settled on Canada Day for the bombing, Mr. Nuttall said he wanted to reconnoitre proposed targets when they arrived in Victoria--Corporal A. asked, "Where are you gonna put 'em?" and Mr. Nuttall replied, "That's why we need the recce." When building the bombs, Mr. Nuttall told Ms. Korody to research and develop a list of targets to be checked out during the recce. When Corporal A. drove Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody to various sites during the recce, Mr. Nuttall selected possible locations and Corporal A. acted "as nothing more than a sounding board". [354] The defence points to evidence that Ms. Korody's written list of targets did not mention the Parliament Buildings. Ms. Korody's target list was given to Sergeant S. on June 30, 2013. The defence says this means when Corporal A. drove the couple around Victoria on June 30, the Parliament Buildings were not among their potential targets. The trial judge found that the first thing Mr. Nuttall asked Corporal A. on the recce was "what was going to happen now", and that Corporal A. "was the leader of the group who instructed his foot soldiers what to do and how to do it." She found that Corporal A. acted as "more than a sounding board". His cover directed him to be more aggressive in his suggestions for targets, and he drove to the Parliament Buildings and pointed to bushes he strongly suggested would be good locations. [355] In my view, although there was no doubt that the Parliament Buildings were in Mr. Nuttall's sights, he continually changed his mind about where and what should be the object of their attack, including not only the Esquimalt base, and a nuclear submarine, but also a "gay nightclub" and a "strip bar". Ms. Korody did the same. Ultimately, the finding that Corporal A. directed them to the Parliament Buildings was not a palpable and overriding error. (ii) "[Corporal A.] also continually criticized Mr. Nuttall's plans regarding the planting of the devices (for example, burying them underground or leaving them in flower pots), until he came back to [Corporal A.]'s suggestion that they place the devices in bags and leave them like they did in Boston" (Reasons at para. 393). [356] The Crown says that it was Mr. Nuttall who first suggested it would take less time to "throw one in the bushes" or "get a bag and leave it and do what the, the guys in Boston did". From June 27 to 29, 2013, it was common ground between Mr. Nuttall, Ms. Korody, and Corporal A. that the final bomb placement decision would not be made until they reconnoitred targets in downtown Victoria using the list Ms. Korody developed. [357] The defence does not specifically respond to this alleged error. In my view, the trial judge's finding that Corporal A. directed the accused to place the bombs in the bushes does not amount to palpable and overriding error. The trial judge did not mention all of the evidence on this issue, and instead focused on the police conduct. She was entitled to do so. That finding, like the others, has a foundation in the evidence. (iii) "It is important that on the night before the interview with [Sergeant S.], the defendants continued to be unsettled on what to do with the pressure cooker devices" (Reasons at para. 405). [358] The Crown says that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody continued to debate the merits of various targets Ms. Korody had on her research list. The Crown says this was consistent with the plan they had embarked upon days earlier. [359] The defence does not specifically respond to this alleged error. In my view, this is not a palpable and overriding error. The Crown's reformulation of the trial judge's finding appears to be essentially the same as her finding. It is certainly not so different that the finding is obviously wrong. (iv) "When asked about targets, Mr. Nuttall said he had many ideas but in reality he wanted to storm the [Esquimalt] naval base and destroy the US nuclear submarine at Nanoose Bay" (Reasons at para. 416). [360] The Crown says that this is not an accurate account of the interview. It refers to five alleged problems with this finding. First, Mr. Nuttall said he wanted to blow up a building, but said they had to do a recce first, and only digressed to talk about the nuclear submarine hijacking when Sergeant S. asked what was in Mr. Nuttall's heart. Second, when Sergeant S. suggested that there was no real "plan", Mr. Nuttall replied that they had a plan. Third, when Sergeant S. asked what Mr. Nuttall would do with the C-4, Mr. Nuttall said his plan was to use the pressure cookers to kill nonbelievers, and referred Sergeant S. to Ms. Korody's research list of targets. Fourth, when pressed for details, Mr. Nuttall said the plan was to place the bombs in garbage bags and stick them in bushes at one or more of Ms. Korody's targets. Fifth, and finally, Sergeant S. asked why the targets were civilian when Mr. Nuttall's original plan was to attack soldiers. Mr. Nuttall explained that these were softer, strategic targets because the victims would be taxpayers. [361] The defence's response to this error, as well as many errors below in this category, is that the Crown's submission amounts to a complaint that the trial judge did not include all conceivably relevant facts in her entrapment reasons. In the respondents' submission, much of the evidence "missing" from the trial judge's reasons would bolster her entrapment finding, as she noted: [777] This bare summary does not capture the true nature of the police conduct because it was the day-to-day dealings with the defendants, which were recorded and videotaped, that demonstrate the absurd character of the undercover investigation Manipulating, cajoling, instructing, instilling fear, offering friendship, offering reward, offering religious guidance, throughout the operation, the police led the defendants to this endgame. [362] In my view, the evidence is overwhelming that the naval base and the nuclear submarine were Mr. Nuttall's preferred targets. No error was committed in this finding. (v) When Ms. Korody met Sergeant S., she "had no concrete idea of what to do with the pressure cooker devices; she thought they might have to dig a big hole and put them into it" (Reasons at para. 436). [363] The Crown says that Ms. Korody articulated that the plan was to place bombs at one or more of the sites on her target list. She said that Mr. Nuttall asked her to write the list but did not force her to, and explained her methodology of picking sites. She said that she and Mr. Nuttall would place the bombs and then Corporal A. would drive them away. [364] The respondent again submits that the Crown's submission is essentially that the trial judge did not make every possible finding. [365] In my view, although the Crown points to evidence that would have allowed the trial judge to come to another conclusion, this does not amount to demonstrating palpable and overriding error. The Crown's submissions amount to arguing that another conclusion was available. (vi) "It was [Corporal A.] who said the plan was no longer to bury the devices; they were going to be planted in something" (Reasons at para. 452). [366] The Crown says that in earlier discussions, Mr. Nuttall suggested placing a bomb in the bushes rather than burying it. Mr. Nuttall told Sergeant S. that the plan was to place the bomb in the bushes. On the trip to Vancouver Island, Mr. Nuttall defended his prior proposal to bury the bombs, explaining that if he had more time, he would have dug holes 2-3 days in advance. [367] The defence says that the Crown ignores the extent to which it was necessary for Corporal A. to explain in detail why the bombs should not be buried. The defence submits, again, that the trial judge should not be faulted for failing to make every possible finding of fact. [368] In my view, there is no error in the trial judge's finding. Corporal A. and Mr. Nuttall both talked about planting the pressure cooker bombs instead of burying them, and it was open to the trial judge to find that Corporal A. was the driving force. (vii) "[Corporal A.] continued to lead the group after their arrival in Sidney. It was apparent that the defendants had not chosen targets or decided what they were going to do with the devices" (Reasons at para. 760). [369] The Crown says that the uncontroverted evidence was that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody planned all along to select specific targets after arriving on Vancouver Island by reconnoitring the locations on Ms. Korody's list. [370] The respondent's response is, again, that the trial judge did not have to make every possible finding of fact. [371] The conclusion that the Corporal A. continued to lead the group is supported by the evidence. It is true that when they arrived in Sidney, they had not decided on a specific target; indeed, the targets kept changing. The Crown's submission is essentially that the trial judge did not contextualize the fact that she found. (viii) "[Corporal A.] said he did not want to drive all over Victoria or go to places more than once. He told the defendants that he needed only two places for targets, not five or six" (Reasons at para. 760). [372] The Crown submits that Corporal A. told Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody that they would have to select two targets from the "four or five places" targeted on Ms. Korody's list. Ms. Korody confirmed that all targets on the list were in the same area downtown except one listed target which she excluded because it was in the middle of nowhere. (i) Discussion [410] The trial judge had previously been asked to leave the defence of self-defence with the jury, arguing that the new Code provisions, particularly, s. 34(1), expanded the scope of self-defence such that it was available on the facts of this case. The defence did not seek to have the defence of duress placed before the jury, recognizing, no doubt, its inapplicability. The trial judge, in reasons indexed as 2015 BCSC 963, rejected the defence argument, concluding that the new provisions had not been expanded or altered to the extent that the defence argued. However, she continued, and concluded that if she was wrong in her legal analysis, she would also reject the defence based on finding that it lacked an air of reality. She said: [40] In my view, there is no evidence that there is a reasonable underpinning for any subjective belief in Mr. Nuttall or Ms. Korody that they faced either the use of force or they faced a threat of force. While the persona adopted by the undercover police officers would have led any reasonable person in the accused's circumstances to believe they were working with very dangerous people, nothing that the officers did or said could have reasonably led the accused to believe that anyone was using force against them or was threatening force against them at the time of the events. At its highest, the evidence indicates that the accused believed they had gone too far with the undercover officers to back out. There is no evidence of a threat hanging over their heads, either expressly or implicitly. Ms. Korody in particular believed that she could go home at any time before leaving Victoria, and it was only Mr. Nuttall's statements to her that led her to believe that she could not turn back after that point. [41] The defence also lacks an evidentiary foundation because the accused had time and opportunity to extricate themselves from the situation. They talked about getting lost in Victoria because they knew the city so well. They talked about calling the police. They had full access to their cellular telephones, and while they may have suspected they were being watched, in fact Ms. Korody left the hotel room in Sydney on her own for a cigarette break, and the accused also left the motel in Delta on their own and demonstrated no fear of doing so. [42] Lastly, there is no proportionality between the accused's response and the nature of the threat. As I indicated earlier, there is no evidence of a physical threat, there is only evidence that the accused believed the undercover officers were dangerous and might kill them. Without an actual threat to measure its significance, and without any sense of imminence to an actual or perceived threat, it cannot be said that a decision to place explosive devices in a public place where they could potentially kill and injure numerous people is a proportionate response. It is certainly well beyond what could reasonably be regarded as a proportionate response in the circumstances. [43] In light of the many avenues of escape, as opposed to going ahead with the plan to place the explosive devices in a public place thereby endangering others' lives, as well as the uncertainty of any threat or perceived threat and the disproportionate nature of the response, I find a properly instructed jury, acting reasonably, could not acquit the accused on the basis of self‑defence. [Emphasis added.] [411] These reasons show that the police did and said nothing that would have led Mr. Nuttall or Ms. Korody to believe that they would be killed if they did not complete the terrorist act. [412] Even if at some point Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody subjectively feared that they would be killed, the evidence is clear that they did not place the bombs because they feared for their lives; they placed the bombs because they wanted to do violent jihad. On the day of the attack, Mr. Nuttall told Corporal A. that he wanted to "do [terrorist acts] forever". [413] The defence submits that there was a much greater record before the judge on the entrapment hearing, which is true. However, they point to no specific evidence that changes what was actually said by the police to Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody. Nor do they explain how the additional evidence they do cite can affect the analysis on this point. As noted above, all of that evidence is contained in audio and video recordings. [414] The judge's finding that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody felt that they would "surely be killed" cannot be upheld. It is inconsistent with the findings of fact she made on the self-defence ruling, and is clearly not supported by the evidence, which depicts the couple actively considering escape and deciding to go through with the plan for reasons other than fear. [415] In any event, as a matter of law, what is relevant in entrapment is not Mr. Nuttall's and Ms. Korody's subjective beliefs, but the conduct of the police, considered objectively. Recall that in Mack, the trial judge committed an extricable legal error that was reviewed by the Supreme Court of Canada on a correctness standard. In this case, the trial judge's focus on the accused's subjective beliefs was incorrect in law. As found by the trial judge and supported by the evidence, the police did and said nothing to lead either of the two respondents to reasonably (or otherwise) believe that they would be killed or harmed if they did not carry out the planned attack; indeed, the police provided the respondents with a total of 36 opportunities to withdraw between them. They did not take those opportunities. Objectively, the police could not have made it clearer that the respondents could withdraw from the plan. [416] In my view, the trial judge erred in concluding that there was any conduct by the police that contributed to a belief in certain death or harm, and thereby erred in including that factor in her analysis on entrapment. I will address the effect of this finding later in these reasons, in my discussion of whether the average person in Mr. Nuttall's and Ms. Korody's position would have committed the offences. (2) Finding that the police "lost" their basis for reasonable suspicion that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were involved in criminal activity [417] The trial judge found entrapment on three bases. The first, discussed at paras. 615-660 of her reasons, was that the police provided the respondents with the opportunity to commit an offence without reasonable suspicion that they were already involved in criminal activity when they offered to provide Mr. Nuttall with C-4 on June 16, 2013. [418] The Crown submits that the trial judge erred in finding that offering the C-4 presented the respondents with an "opportunity" to commit an offence, and that she also erred in finding that when the police presented that alleged opportunity, they no longer had a reasonable suspicion that the two were already engaged in criminal activity. [419] I agree with the Crown that the police had reasonable suspicion that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were involved in criminal activity at the time the C-4 was offered. In order for this branch of entrapment to be made out, the defence must show that the police both provided an opportunity to commit an offence and lacked reasonable suspicion at the time they did so. Given my conclusion on reasonable suspicion, there is no need to consider whether offering to provide C-4 constituted the presentation of an opportunity. [420] The trial judge concluded that when the RCMP offered Mr. Nuttall C-4 on June 16, 2013, they did not have a reasonable suspicion that he and Ms. Korody were engaged in criminal activity: [648] at the time the offer of [C-4] was made for the pressure cooker devices, there was little objective evidence to support a reasonable suspicion that Mr. Nuttall was already engaged in criminal activity related to terrorism. As Lamer J. said in Mack, there must be a temporal connection to establish reasonable suspicion; events that are too remote cannot justify the objective test. Similarly, suspicions that have been allayed by more recent events cannot be ignored. It is the sum total of the objective facts that must be scrutinized, including evidence that supports a conclusion that the defendants were not otherwise engaged in the planning or execution of terrorist acts. [649] Realistically, the defendants had proven themselves to be marginalized, isolated people who espoused extremist jihadist views but were neither motivated to act on their beliefs nor capable of taking steps to accomplish acts of violence in support of their beliefs. Some of the officers involved in Project Souvenir appeared to hold this view of the defendants and advocated a different course of action than the one spearheaded by [Sergeant K.] [421] However, she also found that Sergeant K.'s decision to present the opportunity to the accused was based on his belief that Mr. Nuttall was predisposed to commit a terrorist act, and that given enough incentive, he would do so (at para. 650). She found that this was not a sufficient basis based on Mack and thereby concluded that the RCMP's conduct amounted to random-virtue testing. [422] In my view, the trial judge erred in reaching the conclusion that when the offer of the C-4 was made the police no longer had a reasonable suspicion that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were already engaged in criminal activity. In Gingras, this Court held that proposing to commit a criminal offence would "[p]resumptively" provide the police with reasonable suspicion (at para. 25). The trial judge failed to consider Mr. Nuttall's conduct in the days prior to the offer of the C-4. [423] On June 6, 2013, Corporal A. picked up Mr. Nuttall outside his residence. Mr. Nuttall had, on his own initiative, downloaded instructions onto his computer on how to build a homemade explosive device. Mr. Nuttall had also found information on how to build a pressure cooker bomb in Inspire and gave Corporal A. a drawing and math formulas. The instructions, according to Mr. Nuttall, were the same as the Tsarnaev brothers used. Mr. Nuttall said that if it worked for them, it could work for him. He also showed Corporal A. a one-page shopping list with the items he needed in order to build the pressure cooker bombs. Mr. Nuttall asked Corporal A. to buy gunpowder and suggested he could make C-4. Corporal A. said he was working on getting him C-4. Mr. Nuttall then spoke about building a model rocket that would explode. Mr. Nuttall talked about a big plan that would produce a body count like 9/11 to cause people to rethink sending troops overseas to kill Muslims. Corporal A. asked Mr. Nuttall what he would be doing if they had not met, and Mr. Nuttall said he would be making a kettle bomb. Mr. Nuttall wanted to shop for bomb-making parts right away, but Corporal A. stalled him. [424] In my view, this evidence alone provided sufficient basis for a finding of reasonable suspicion. However, there was more. On June 8, Mr. Nuttall called Corporal A. and said that he had found a natural substance that could be used as an explosive. On June 9, Mr. Nuttall told Corporal A. that he was talking about cow manure. Mr. Nuttall said that the recipe looked easy. [425] Mr. Nuttall was still actively planning a terrorist act. Ms. Korody was not as engaged as Mr. Nuttall; however, she was clear in her desire to participate in the terrorist acts discussed. [426] In my view, the trial judge erred in her conclusion that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were entrapped on the basis that the police lacked a reasonable suspicion that they were involved in criminal activity. The conduct of both offenders went beyond predisposition, but entered the realm of an objective suspicion that they may well participate in a terrorist act. [427] That conclusion is not the end of the matter, however, as the trial judge also found entrapment based on the second branch of the entrapment test, and found a distinct abuse of process, both of which, in her view, justified the stay of proceedings. (3) Finding that a reasonable or average person would have committed the offences [428] The trial judge concluded, at paras. 769-75 of her reasons, that the average person would have committed the crimes, and even if the average person would not have committed the crimes, the conduct of the police exceeded the grounds of propriety and the police therefore manufactured the crime. [429] The trial judge made the following findings: · The police knowingly exploited the demonstrated vulnerabilities of the defendants in order to induce them to commit the offences; · The police adopted a multi-faceted approach that included most of the factors in Mack that favour a finding of entrapment, including, o The use of trickery, fraud and reward, o The use of persistent direction to become more organized, focused and realistic in their extremist ideas, o The use of persistent veiled threats to adopt the pressure cooker plan as their own and to abandon the grandiose ideas that the police knew the defendants could never accomplish, o The exploitation of the defendants' social isolation and desperation for friendship with Corporal A., o The creation of an elaborate ruse that led the defendants to fear for their lives if they failed to satisfy Corporal A.'s sophisticated international terrorist organization, o The repeated angry encounters with undercover officers who played roles as terrorists, and o The decision to play the role of the defendants' spiritual advisor and exploit the influence Corporal A. had over them to direct their actions towards the use of violence to accomplish religious and political objectives; · The police involvement in the offence was overwhelming compared to the insignificant part played by the defendants; · The constant direction and prodding the defendants needed to accomplish their assigned tasks demonstrated that it was the police who were the leaders of this plot; · The police took over the leadership and committed illegal acts to enable the defendants to play their small part in the plan; · Mr. Nuttall repeatedly demonstrated that he lacked the focus required to make the arrangements that were necessary before the pressure cooker plan could be carried out, even if they had secured the financial resources required for the mission; · The police were exasperated with Mr. Nuttall's failure to follow through with any of the extremist objectives he talked about; · The police aggressively engineered a plan and made the defendants think it was their own; · The police acts were egregious; · There were no exigent circumstances that the police needed to meet with such extraordinary measures; · There was no imminent risk of harm to the public; · There was no need to disrupt ongoing criminal activity; · The police were acutely aware that without the constant supervision and direction of Corporal A., the defendants would be incapable of completing their part in the plan; · The role of the police in the execution of the plan greatly exceeded the role of the defendants; · The police removed all of the defendants' self-imposed obstacles, as well as the obstacles that the police concluded the defendants could never overcome because of their vulnerabilities and lack of resources; and · This was not a case where the police learned that there was a pre-existing plan to carry out a terrorist plot, that steps had been taken to formulate a terrorist plot, that the defendants were communicating with known terrorists or terrorist organizations, or that the defendants possessed any expertise that would have been of value to a terrorist organization. [430] The trial judge concluded that the average person, with strengths and weaknesses, with or without the vulnerabilities of the defendants, would likely have planted the pressure cooker devices to save their own lives despite the risk to others. She based her finding on the pressures placed on the defendants by the police during the undercover operation, as well as the multi-faceted control the police exerted over their actions and the beliefs. [431] As explained above, in my view, the trial judge erred in her conclusion that, objectively, the conduct of the police caused the defendants to plant the bombs in order to save their lives. If she was correct in that regard, her conclusion would clearly stand. However, in my respectful view, she erred, and her error drove her to a conclusion that was wrong in law, just as the trial judge committed an extricable legal error in Mack. The foundation for her conclusion that the average person would have planted bombs that would kill many people, with timers set to ensure the deaths or injury of first responders, falls along with her conclusion that they did so for fear of their lives. (4) Finding that the conduct of the police amounted to a manufactured crime [432] However, once again, that is not the end of the issue, as the trial judge continued with her analysis at paras. 774-780 of her reasons and found that regardless of the average person test, the conduct of the police in this case was so egregious that the only conclusion that could be drawn was that they manufactured the crime and thereby entrapped the defendants, referring back to the Mack analysis where Lamer J. found that even if the hypothetical person would not likely have committed the offence, the conduct of the police can nevertheless support the conclusion that they went beyond the bounds of propriety and instigated the crime, and thereby still amounted to an abuse of process (Mack at 961-62). [433] She continued with her analysis and found that: · This was a case of truly manufactured crime by the RCMP, and not a case where the police simply instigated, originated or brought about the offence; · The police focused their investigation on two people who held terrorist beliefs but no apparent capacity or means to plan, act, and carry through with their religiously motivated objectives; · The police counselled, directed, urged, instructed and moulded them into people who, with significant and continuous supervision and direction by the police, ultimately played a small role in the terrorist offence; · While Mr. Nuttall first identified the pressure cooker as an explosive device, he wanted to use it as a "tester", and the closest he got to an explosive device on his own was to talk about using cow manure; · The police seized on the pressure cooker idea and it became their plan; · The police had to provide the explosive substance; · The police had to take the defendants shopping and give continuous instructions and direction until they bought what they needed to make the bombs; · The police constructed the device and left the gluing of the nails and construction of the timers to the defendants; · The police had to arrange for a location for the defendants to work on the devices and provide constant supervision and direction; · The police had to ensure the defendants received their daily methadone prescription; · The police chose the date for the explosion; · The police made all of the arrangements for the accommodation and travel to Victoria; · The police chose where the devices would be planted; · The day-to-day dealings with the defendants demonstrated the "absurd character" of the undercover investigation; · Mr. Nuttall broke down after the failed train plan and said that he was not a general and could not be expected to create a workable plan; · Corporal A. responded by assuring him that he would be taken through to the end "one baby step at a time"; · The police in fact had to take the defendants "one baby step at a time" to bring the undercover operation to an end with an arrest; · The police led the defendants to the end through manipulation, cajoling, instructing, instillation of fear, offers of friendship, offers of reward, and offers of religious guidance throughout the operation; and · The police could not continue with an indefinite undercover operation, but rather than turn the operation back to CSIS, given there was no imminent risk of criminal activity, they manufactured a crime that amounted to entrapment. [434] From the above discussion, it is clear that the trial judge made some palpable and overriding errors of fact, as well as an error in the application of the legal principles of entrapment. In my view, the first two bases upon which she found entrapment, a lack of reasonable suspicion and the average person test, cannot stand. However, in my view, the trial judge's third conclusion is unassailable. This is one of the rare cases that falls outside of the average person test, but in which the conduct of the police amounts to an abuse of process in any event. [435] In R. v. Lising, 2010 BCCA 390, this Court examined the conduct of a police agent in the context of abuse of process. The Court identified the two operating factors that require careful balancing: the concepts of fairness and justice and the need to protect the public from serious crime. [436] Terrorism offences are some of the most serious crimes in our law. Unfortunately, Canada is no stranger to acts of terrorism. The 19th-century assassination of Thomas D'Arcy McGee, the bombings carried out by the so-called Squamish Five in 1982, and the bombing of Air India Flight 182 in 1985 are only a few of the violent terrorist acts that have been perpetrated throughout this country's history. Terrorists maim and kill scores of innocent people and threaten the confidence of the community in their collective security as they go about their day-to-day activities. There are few crimes so vile, regardless of where in the world they are committed. [437] Not all terrorist organizations are as entrenched in their methods of security and their ability to fend off law enforcement as the Hells Angels were in Lising (see paras. 16-20). But no doubt some are, and infiltration of those groups or the gathering of evidence against them could present almost insurmountable problems and require the police to engage in innovative and sometimes illegal acts in order to secure the type of evidence necessary for a prosecution. [438] On the other hand, the police do not have a free hand to do whatever they wish in order to investigate crime, even serious crime. The concepts of fairness and justice are still highly relevant and police conduct undertaken in the investigation of crime must be balanced against them. [439] The issue is not whether Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were predisposed to commit terrorist acts--clearly they were. It was also flawed reasoning on the part of the trial judge to suggest that the police were required to assess their mental health and determine whether they were likely to commit a terrorist act, because the police could not have been expected to make that assessment during the throes of this investigation. [440] However, in this case, the police eventually knew Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody had little to no ability to commit an act of terrorism. Certainly, they were right to commence the investigation, and certainly they had reasonable suspicion that the two might commit a crime. However, as found by the trial judge, the police went far beyond investigating a crime. They pushed and pushed and pushed the two defendants to come up with a workable plan. The police did everything necessary to facilitate the plan. I can find no fault with the trial judge's conclusion that the police manufactured the crime that was committed and were the primary actors in its commission. Although in some cases the police may have to be the primary actor, when all of the circumstances are considered, assessed, and weighed, the misconduct of the police in this case far outweighed their violation of the concepts of fairness and justice. [441] I therefore agree with the trial judge that the overall conduct of this investigation was a travesty of justice. [442] This is not to suggest that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody are harmless. They may be very dangerous. However, that danger was not apparent in the investigation, which was confirmed by Sergeant K.'s continually telling his superiors that they presented no imminent danger or risk. There was no danger because Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody had neither the means nor the ability to carry out any of the plans without substantial aid from the police. [443] Thus, I would dismiss the appeal from the stays of proceedings based on entrapment. K. Abuse of Process [444] The trial judge also performed an independent analysis concluding that the police conduct also led to an abuse of process. I do not need to comment on the grounds of appeal as they relate to this conclusion, as I have already found that her entrapment ruling is not sufficiently tainted by error to be set aside. [445] I would add only one thing in relation to her criticism of Corporal A.'s refusal to find proper spiritual advice for the accused. The evidence overwhelmingly demonstrates that Mr. Nuttall frequently refused to seek out or adhere to the beliefs of a moderate imam. His requests for guidance related to whether killing innocent people, including women and children, as a result of his violent jihadist acts would be contrary to his religion. He would not take this inquiry to a moderate imam, and Corporal A. was quite right in his refusal to take Mr. Nuttall to see an imam who supported violent jihadist acts instead. The trial judge's criticism in this regard was unfounded. L. Multiple Convictions [446] The Crown submits that the same analysis of "overlapping counts" applies to both the Facilitation Count and count 2. I agree that, generally speaking, the approach set out by the Crown is the proper approach where multiple convictions overlap. However, every nexus of potentially overlapping offences must be considered individually. [447] In my view, the Crown is correct that the trial judge erred in her analysis of both the Facilitation Count and count 2. Instead, those counts should have been put to the jury and stayed by the trial judge in accordance with the procedure in Kienapple if overlapping convictions resulted. (1) General approach to overlapping convictions [448] Before addressing the specific grounds of appeal, the Crown lays out a framework for what it describes as the "proper procedure for adjudication of multiple overlapping counts", which it says also applies to the appeal in relation to the conspiracy counts. The Crown says that where an indictment contains multiple counts with overlapping elements, the trial judge must instruct the jury to deliver verdicts on all counts. If guilty verdicts are returned, it would then be for the trial judge to determine whether Kienapple applies such that conditional stays should be entered on the less serious overlapping counts. [449] The Crown says the trial judge failed to follow this procedure in her rulings on the Facilitation Count and count 2, which prevented the jury from delivering verdicts on all counts. The Crown submits that both rulings "conflated the jury's fact-finding function with the trial judge's responsibility for guarding against multiple convictions or double criminality". [450] The Crown says it was prejudiced by these rulings by being deprived of verdicts on the Facilitation Count and on count 2. It says that if the jury's verdict on count 1 was set aside on appeal, then the Crown would be precluded from arguing that convictions should be entered on these counts since the jury never returned verdicts on them. [451] The respondents provide a functional response to the Crown's prejudice submissions: they are not appealing their convictions, so the Crown's theory of prejudice could never materialize. They further submit that the Crown erroneously attempts to characterize the trial judge's rulings as misapplications of the Kienapple principle. In reality, the respondents say that the rulings are, respectively, applications of the substantive law of conspiracy and statutory interpretation of the elements of s. 83.19 of the Code, which neither attract the Kienapple principle nor contain other reversible errors. [452] I agree with the defence that the trial judge's decisions were procedurally prior to any consideration of the Kienapple principle. The Kienapple principle is applied by a trial judge when she would otherwise enter convictions for multiple offences arising from the same delict. It does not deprive the trial judge of the ability or the obligation to perform statutory interpretation to determine the elements of an offence in the Code and to take counts away from the jury if necessary. Nor does it prevent a judge from severing counts from an indictment where appropriate. [453] Thus, if the trial judge's rulings were correct, there would be no need to resort to the Kienapple principle. However, for the reasons that follow, I agree with the Crown that both of the trial judge's rulings were incorrect. Had she resolved those issues correctly, both counts that she took away from the jury would have attracted the Kienapple principle if the jury rendered guilty verdicts. (2) Directed verdict on the Facilitation Count [454] The trial judge directed a verdict of not guilty on count 3 of the indictment in relation to the charge of facilitating a terrorist offence. Her reasons are summarized above. [455] The Facilitation Count charged the accused with an offence under s. 83.19 of the Code: Between the 1st day of February, 2013 and the 2nd day of July, 2013, at or near the Cities of Surrey and Victoria, British Columbia and elsewhere in the Province of British Columbia did knowingly facilitate a terrorist activity, thereby committing an offence contrary to section 83.19 of the Criminal Code. [456] The Facilitation Count was particularized as follows: Ms. Korody and Mr. Nuttall facilitated a terrorist activity, to wit, the placing of an explosive device in or against the British Columbia Parliament building grounds, Victoria, British Columbia, a place of public use and a government or public facility, with intent to cause death or serious bodily injury. [457] Section 83.19 of the Code reads: 83.19 (1) Every one who knowingly facilitates a terrorist activity is guilty of an indictable offence and liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding fourteen years. (2) For the purposes of this Part, a terrorist activity is facilitated whether or not (a) the facilitator knows that a particular terrorist activity is facilitated; (b) any particular terrorist activity was foreseen or planned at the time it was facilitated; or (c) any terrorist activity was actually carried out. [458] The trial judge determined that the purpose of s. 83.19 of the Code was "to capture persons who were on the periphery of terrorist groups and activities, but nevertheless aided in carrying out the acts themselves". Thus, she concluded that the conduct targeted by s. 83.19 of the Code was not encompassed by the evidence led by the Crown and was inconsistent with the theory of liability it maintained throughout its case. Accordingly, she directed a verdict of not guilty for both accused on the Facilitation Count. a. Positions of the parties [459] The Crown submits that although its theory at trial was that the accused were principals, its theory should not have been the sole or determinative factor in the trial judge's directed verdict ruling. The Crown says the defence position was also relevant. The Crown says that since the defence position was that the Crown failed to prove any completed offences, the Facilitation Count should have been left with the jury for it to decide whether, even if the accused's conduct did not involve completed terrorist offences, it still amounted to facilitation of terrorist activity. According to the Crown, because the trier of fact is not bound by the parties' theories, the Facilitation Count should have been left to the jury to decide in light of the allegations in the indictment and the evidence adduced at trial. As laid out above, any concerns about double punishment in light of overlapping convictions, says the Crown, should have been addressed through the Kienapple principle after the jury delivered their verdicts on all counts. [460] The respondents' position is that the Crown's argument on appeal misses the central point of the trial judge's ruling. They say that her ruling was a straightforward no-evidence ruling along the following lines: she determined that a necessary element of s. 83.19 of the Code was the commission of an act that made it easier for another person to commit a terrorist act, and that the Crown led no evidence speaking to that element of the offence. The Facilitation Count therefore could not go to the jury. The respondents say the Crown's particulars made it clear that the alleged "facilitation" was the placing of the devices at the Parliament Buildings, and they submit that there was no evidence suggesting they aided anyone else to do so. [461] Additionally, the respondents say that any defence theories of liability should play no part in a directed verdict application, because the defence position on any count in the indictment "is only imperfectly known, if it is known at all, at the time a directed verdict application is heard". [462] In reply to the Crown argument that the jury could have acquitted the accused of the "completed terrorism offences" but still convicted on the Facilitation Count, the respondents say that it is inaccurate to refer to a conspiracy charge in such terms, as a conspiracy is inchoate and does not involve the "completion" of another offence. Additionally, the respondents refer back to the foundational premise of the trial judge's directed verdict--that the Crown led no evidence that the accused assisted any other person's terrorist activity, which she found to be essential to convict under s. 83.19 of the Code. [463] Finally, the respondents say that, in any event, the trial judge's entrapment ruling would necessarily also apply to the Facilitation Count even if the trial judge did err in directing a verdict of not guilty. b. Discussion [464] Before I commence the discussion of the issue at hand, I find it necessary to comment on the needless complexity of the legislation that comprises Part II.1 of the Code. It would be beneficial if Parliament revisited the legislation, revamped it, and made it comprehensible, particularly for those members of the public who sit as jury members on such cases. [465] As noted above, the trial judge gave comprehensive reasons for concluding that the Facilitation Count should be withdrawn from the jury. Her reasoning has been followed by Justice David in R. c. Jamali, 2017 QCCS 6078 at paras. 43-57. [466] In my view, the trial judge proceeded on an incorrect premise when she stated, at para. 95 of her reasons, that " the particulars describe the facilitation as the placing of explosive devices on the legislative grounds". That is not how the offence was particularized. For convenience, I repeat the particulars of the count: Ms. Korody and Mr. Nuttall facilitated a terrorist activity, to wit, the placing of an explosive device in or against the British Columbia Parliament building grounds, Victoria, British Columbia, a place of public use and a government or public facility, with intent to cause death or serious bodily injury. [Emphasis added.] [467] If the particulars said that the respondents facilitated a terrorist activity by placing the devices at the Parliament Buildings, I would agree that the facilitating offence was limited to the act of placing the explosive devices. However, the particulars say that the terrorist activity was placing the devices at the Parliament Buildings. It is that activity that was facilitated, which is made clear by the use of the descriptor "to wit" in the particulars. Thus, the acts of buying the pressure cookers and other materials, the acts of scouting out suitable targets, and all other acts in preparation could amount to facilitating that activity. [468] I agree with the trial judge that in this context, the definition found in Lindsay is the applicable definition: "to make a matter easier", and as "the act of making it easier for another person to commit a crime": Lindsay at paras. 23 and 24. [469] The meaning of the word "facilitate", in the context of s. 83.19, includes making it easier for another person to bring about a terrorist activity. The criminal organization legislation bears many similarities to the terrorism legislation and provides a useful comparator. Multiple parties can do far more harm acting in concert than they can separately. This is of particular importance in both the criminal organization and terrorism contexts. However, I am not convinced that in all cases the definition is limited to facilitating "another" person. [470] Child luring legislation also contains "facilitation". In R. v. Legare, 2009 SCC 56, a similar definition was used at para. 28 that "facilitate" means helping to bring about, making easier or more probable. Legare involved s. 172.1: using telecommunication to communicate with a young child for the purpose of facilitating a specified sexual offence. As noted in Legare at para. 25, facilitation is an inchoate offence done in preparation of the actual crime, even preceding an attempt to commit that crime. The same may hold true for facilitating a terrorist activity: R. v. Khawaja, 2012 SCC 69 at paras. 57-63. In Legare, an accused could be both the principal and the facilitator. However, in s. 172.1, the offence was communicating for the purpose of facilitation. Here, the offence is actual facilitation of a terrorist activity. It is clear from the subject matter of s. 172.1 that the provision contemplates communication with the eventual victim of the facilitated offence by a perpetrator who will often act alone. It is an example where "facilitate" applies to being able to facilitate oneself. [471] I agree with the trial judge that the purpose of the facilitation offence is to capture those on the periphery of terrorist groups and activities. Indeed, the section ensures that facilitators excluded by virtue of the intent requirements of the party liability provisions in s. 21 of the Code are nevertheless captured under s. 83.19. However, I do not conclude that the section is limited to those on the periphery. While I agree that a person cannot be convicted as a principal and a facilitator of the same terrorist activity, one could be charged as a principal and a facilitator, just as one could be charged, but not convicted, as both a principal and an aider, abettor, or accessory after the fact. [472] I agree with the defence that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody could not facilitate their own completed activity. I agree that the elements of the offence of facilitation, by its definition, generally include facilitating another person to commit a terrorist activity. However, in circumstances such as these, in which multiple individuals allegedly carried out a terrorist activity in concert, depending on the evidence, each could facilitate the act of the other. [473] I also do not wish to foreclose the possibility of an accused facilitating themselves. There may be a case where the facts fall short of a completed offence, or even an attempt to commit a terrorist act by one person, but that person could have taken sufficient steps towards committing the act to be found to have facilitated a terrorist activity. [474] Thus, in the context of this case, the trial judge should not have directed a verdict of not guilty on the Facilitation Count, and should have instead left it with the jury to decide whether Ms. Korody facilitated Mr. Nuttall or vice versa. If the jury returned a guilty verdict on all counts, the trial judge would then have applied the principles in Kienapple to address any issue in relation to double jeopardy or double criminality. The defence position at trial was that the offences were not completed. The nature of what Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were planning changed constantly. If the jury accepted that submission, and acquitted one or the other on the conspiracy counts, there could still be evidence to support the Facilitation Count. [475] The next question is what remedy should flow from this error. The jury convicted both accused on count 1: conspiracy to commit murder. Therefore, a guilty verdict on the Facilitation Count would have been conditionally stayed by the application of the Kienapple principle. The verdict would also be subject to a stay of proceedings based on the entrapment finding. [476] This Court derives its power to allow or dismiss a Crown appeal in a criminal case from s. 686(4) of the Code. Under ordinary circumstances, the Crown would be entitled to a new trial on the Facilitation Count. The Crown sought a new trial but requested that the order for a new trial be conditionally stayed, depending on the outcome of the entrapment appeal. [477] When exercising its powers under s. 686(4) of the Code, s. 686(8) allows a court of appeal to make any additional order that justice requires. This Court has the residual discretion under that section to stay a charge where further proceedings would constitute an abuse of process: R. v. Hinse, [1995] 4 S.C.R. 597 at para. 23. Given that the entrapment argument was upheld on appeal, any further proceedings would constitute an abuse of process. [478] The proper course of action is to allow the appeal under s. 686(4)(b)(i), but to substitute a stay of proceedings on the basis that the respondents were entrapped and any further proceedings would therefore be abusive. (3) Ruling on the duplicity of counts 1 and 2 [479] The defence also applied for a ruling that counts 1 and 2 of the indictment, which both deal with conspiracy, were duplicitous because they both related to the same agreement and merely represented two objects of the same conspiracy. [480] The operative part of count 1 reads that the accused: did conspire together, the one with the other, to murder persons unknown contrary to s.465(1)(a) of the Criminal Code, for the benefit of, at the direction of, or in association with a terrorist group [481] The operative part of count 2 reads that the accused: did conspire together, the one with the other, to commit an indictable offence, to wit: placing an explosive in or against a place of public use, or a government or public facility, with intent to cause death or serious bodily injury, contrary to s.431.2(2) of the Criminal Code, for the benefit of, at the direction or, or in association with a terrorist group [482] Although the Crown did not particularize either count, it limited its case on both counts to a conspiracy to place the pressure cooker devices on the grounds of the Parliament Buildings in Victoria. [483] The trial judge noted that the sole difference between these two counts is the object of the conspiracy, in that the second count includes an intention to cause serious bodily injury as an alternative to an intention to cause death. [484] The trial judge held that where there is only one agreement, but different illegal objects, there can only be one conspiracy conviction, because the "substance of the offence of conspiracy to commit an indictable offence is the agreement and not the commission of the offence". She noted that where a single conspiracy has multiple objects, the Crown need only prove one object to support a conviction. [485] The trial judge determined that if the jury were to convict on count 1, it should not go on to determine if the accused were guilty on count 2. She decided that she would remedy the duplicity in the counts by giving an instruction to that effect to the jury. Because the jury ultimately convicted the accused on count 1, it did not return a verdict on count 2. The trial judge entered a conditional stay of proceedings on that count, and later deleted the conditional aspect of the stay. a. Positions of the parties [486] The core of the Crown's argument on this ground of appeal is that the proper procedure for the trial judge to follow would have been to require the jury to deliver verdicts on every count in the indictment and subsequently apply the Kienapple principle to avoid double punishment. It says that if the verdict on count 1 were set aside on appeal, the trial judge's ruling would deprive the Crown of the opportunity to argue for a conviction on count 2. While there may be considerable overlap between the two counts, the Crown argues that their legal elements were not identical, and the accused could have the requisite mens rea to place bombs with intent to cause serious bodily harm without having an intent to cause death. [487] The Crown says that multiple counts arising out of the same incident are not duplicitous, "even where they may overlap or duplicate each other". Thus, the trial judge should have directed the jury to deliver verdicts on all counts and then decide whether to conditionally stay some of them by applying the Kienapple principle. [488] The respondent first notes that the trial Crown (who is not counsel on appeal) took a different position in the court below, where the Crown submitted that count 1 and count 2 were two legally distinct conspiracies, such that, even on an application of Kienapple, the jury could convict on both. [489] In response to the Crown's appeal submissions, the respondents say that conspiracy jurisprudence establishes that "where a criminal agreement has multiple unlawful objects, there is nonetheless just a single conspiracy", and that the Crown need only prove an agreement to carry out one unlawful object to convict. [490] The respondents submit that the Kienapple principle, which addresses a situation in which a single delict is the basis for charges under multiple Code provisions, is inapplicable to conspiracy charges. The respondents provide two examples to illustrate that the Crown suffered no prejudice from the trial judge's declining to address the ruling through the Kienapple framework. If, as the Crown suggests, the jury delivered verdicts on all counts, with guilty verdicts on both conspiracy counts, there could only be two possible outcomes: (i) If the trial judge entered a conditional stay on one count based on Kienapple, the Crown would be in the same position on appeal as it already is, demonstrating that no prejudice flowed from the trial judge's declining to address the ruling through the Kienapple framework; or (ii) If the trial judge decided that the Kienapple test was not met, the accused would be convicted twice for the same conspiracy, leading to a successful defence appeal. [491] In example (i), the respondents say that there was no route to a successful Crown appeal from the trial judge's conditional stay of one of the conspiracy counts, because their argument presupposes that this was a situation where the laying of two counts was legally sound, as is the case in the usual situation where Kienapple arises; no legal principles forbid an indictment charging counts with overlapping elements, and Kienapple merely ensures no unfairness in the result. They submit that legal principles do prohibit, however, an indictment charging the same conspiracy twice. [492] The respondents also submit that any alleged prejudice the Crown suffered is meaningless because they did not appeal any of their convictions. Finally, they say that the trial judge's entrapment ruling also applies to count 2, even if a jury had delivered a verdict on that count. b. Discussion [493] The defence submits that Kienapple has no application to conspiracy, citing Cox and Paton v. The Queen, [1963] S.C.R. 500. That case determined that if there is one agreement, there is only one conspiracy. This does not mean, however, that the same agreement cannot be charged with different objects if the Crown is concerned about whether it can prove one of those objects. [494] The problem arises in this case because of the wildly different positions taken by the respondents in terms of what they were agreeing to during the investigation. While in hindsight, it would have been far better had the Crown not cluttered the indictment by laying two counts and had only proceeded with count 2, it appears that the Crown at trial was of the view that each count represented a separate conspiracy. That position has been withdrawn on appeal. Given that the jury could have accepted all, part, or none of a witness's testimony, there was a route to an acquittal on count 1 and a conviction on count 2. In this case, had there been a successful appeal on count 1, count 2 would not be available as a substitute because the jury did not return a verdict. [495] Prior to R. v. Provo, [1989] 2 S.C.R. 3, judges who applied the Kienapple principle sometimes entered acquittals on the overlapping count or directed juries to deal with the overlapping count by either acquitting or not considering a count depending on its finding on another. That is the approach the trial judge took here. In Provo, the Court set out the appropriate process for considering overlapping offences and concluded that the best approach would be for the trial judge to enter a conditional stay of proceedings on the lesser offence if the jury convicted the accused of both, thus preserving the guilty verdict on the lesser offence in case of an appeal. Thus, the Crown would not be obliged to appeal the stay (as it was here). If the conviction on the main count was either not appealed or affirmed on appeal, the conditional nature of the stay would become permanent. [496] In this case, because there were different objects alleged, the trial judge should have left both offences with the jury and then, if guilty verdicts were rendered on each count, engaged in the application of the Kienapple principle and recorded a conviction for only the more serious offence, the conspiracy to commit murder. She would then have entered a conditional stay on count 2: see Ontario (Ministry of Labour) v. Black & McDonald Ltd., 2011 ONCA 440. [497] As the Crown conceded, this conclusion makes little difference, as the result would be the same: a stay of proceedings on count 2 entered by this Court, on the basis of either Kienapple or entrapment. [498] It is well established that on an appeal from an acquittal on a question of law, the Crown must demonstrate both that an error of law was made, and that there is a reasonable degree of certainty that but for the error, the result would not necessarily have been the same: Cullen v. The King, [1949] S.C.R. 658 at 665; R. v. Morin, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 345 at 374; R. v. Graveline, 2006 SCC 16 at paras. 14-16. [499] In my view, there is no reason to limit this principle to Crown appeals from acquittals on questions of law. The Crown was limited to appeals on this narrow basis until 1985, when Crown appeals were broadened to include appeals from stays of proceedings and quashed indictments: Criminal Law Amendment, 1985, R.S. 1985, c. 27 (1st Supp.), s. 139. But even before the 1985 amendment came into effect, the Crown could appeal from stays of proceedings as though they were acquittals: R. v. Jewitt, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 128. [500] When the Crown appeals from a stay of proceedings, it must therefore establish that the stay was incorrectly entered, and must also satisfy the court that there is some real likelihood that the result would have been different. If there is no real prospect of a different result, then the Crown has not discharged its burden and the accused cannot be retried. [501] Here, the result remains a stay of proceedings. Thus, I would dismiss this appeal. M. Conclusion [502] In summary, I conclude that the trial judge did not err in finding that Mr. Nuttall and Ms. Korody were entrapped and in entering a stay of proceedings. I would dismiss the appeal from the stays of proceedings entered on counts 1 and 4. [503] I conclude that the trial judge erred when she removed the Facilitation Count from the jury. I would allow the appeal on count 3 to the extent of setting aside the acquittal and substituting a stay of proceedings on the basis that a new trial would be an abuse of process. [504] Finally, I conclude that the trial judge also erred in instructing the jury not to consider count 2 if it convicted on count 1. I would, however, dismiss the appeal from the stay of proceedings she entered. If the jury had rendered a guilty verdict on count 2, a stay of proceedings would be the result.
