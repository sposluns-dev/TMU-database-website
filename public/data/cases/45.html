Each defendant applied under Rule 9-5 for an order striking that proposed amended notice of civil claim. Background [9] The plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton competes, teaches and boards horses. The plaintiff Petra Pinkerton is her daughter and also competes and shows horses. [10] Jennifer Pinkerton states that on June 10, 2018, in the parking lot of the Saanich Fairgrounds, in Saanichton, British Columbia, she was preparing to compete at a Horse Council of B.C. recognized horse show. While saddling her horse, he tried to run after his stablemate who had just left the parking lot. She used what is referred to as a "bag stick" which is a plastic shopping bag taped on the end of a dressage whip and is waved in front of the horse to prevent it from breaking free. [11] She alleges Ruth Lick, a fellow competitor, aggressively approached her conveying her disapproval of this technique. Ms. Lick left and returned with Sheryl Williams, the entry secretary and a member of the Board of Directors of the Society. Jennifer Pinkerton alleges Ms. Lick continued her critical behavior and accused her of abusing her horse while Ms. Williams remained silent. [12] She further alleges that Ms. Lick verbally assaulted her and threatened "battery". [13] She also claims Ms. Williams later distributed a complaint letter she received from Ms. Lick. [14] The plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton further claims that on July 16, 2018, at the Capital City Classic Dressage Show, the Society Vice president and Competition Manager, Christina Nash, "deliberately and maliciously or recklessly or negligently" designed the competition schedule to deprive her of a fair competition. This "forced" her to withdraw from two of four classes she was entered to compete in. [15] The plaintiff Petra Pinkerton alleges that, because of the scheduling "deficit", she was thrown from her horse suffering an injury to her hip. [16] On August 9, 2018, the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton states she received a letter from the Society, or Sheila Skene and Heather Cormie, directing her to leave the warm up or competition area if her horse was likely to disrupt others. The plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton alleges the letter was "without basis and was written maliciously, or recklessly or negligently" in a continued effort to prevent the plaintiffs from competing. She further alleges it was a defamatory letter of "reprimand". The letter was sent only to the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton. [17] Jennifer Pinkerton further alleges the defendant Society refused to rescind the letter or permit her to respond to it, leading to the plaintiffs being unable to compete or participate in Society events fearing further harassment. She also alleges that on August 29, 2018, the letter was sent by a Society director Sheila Skene to a non-Society director, and thereby was published to a third party. [18] She further alleges she was constructively removed as a director of the Society and that neither she nor her daughter were awarded the year end awards they were entitled to receive. [19] The plaintiffs also claim that on or about May 12, 2019, Ms. Williams "deliberately, recklessly or negligently scheduled and refused to reschedule or insert the scores", for both plaintiffs. They state this forced them to compete adjacent to the plaintiff Petra Pinkerton's estranged father and the former husband of Jennifer Pinkerton, causing them to withdraw from the competition. [20] The plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton also alleges that on or about October 6, 2019, the Society director Clair Vessey prevented her from participating in an October 2019 training clinic. Finally, she states that, in February 2020, she was diagnosed with shingles and, in May 2020, with maladaptive anxiety disorder. She alleges both were caused by the stress and anxiety she continues to suffer "as a result of this unresolved Action". Position of the Defendant Society [22] The defendant Society submits the plaintiffs' further amended notice of civil claim discloses no reasonable claim, is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious and/or prejudices, embarrasses or delays the hearing of the proceeding and should be struck. [23] They do not object to the misnamed reference to the Society as an Association. I order the style of cause be amended accordingly. Position of the Plaintiffs [24] The plaintiffs submit the further amended notice of civil claim complies with the rules regarding pleadings, has merit and establishes causes of action for the torts of conspiracy, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, defamation and negligence causing harm to them. [25] The plaintiffs submit the further amended notice of civil claim be allowed and that the application to strike be dismissed. Law [26] Rule 9-5(1) Striking Pleadings states: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [27] The purpose of pleadings is "to define the issues and give the opposing parties fair notice of the case they have to meet. However, equally important in today's court process is the fact that pleadings must be relied upon to provide the boundaries and the context effective pre-trial case management, the extent of disclosure required, as well as the parameters or necessity of expert opinions" (Keene v. British Columbia (Ministry of Children and Family Development), 2003 BCSC 1544 at para. 27). [28] In Sahyoun v. Ho, 2013 BCSC 1143, Voith J. (as he then was) elaborated on the purpose of pleadings: [15] Rule 3-1(2) provides, in part: (2) A notice of civil claim must do the following: (a) set out a concise statement of the material facts giving rise to the claim; (b) set out the relief sought by the plaintiff against each named defendant; (c) set out a concise summary of the legal basis for the relief sought; ... (g) otherwise comply with Rule 3-7. [16] The new Rules alter the structure in which pleadings are to be prepared. The core object of a notice of civil claim, however, remains the same. That object is concisely captured in Frederick M. Irvine, ed., McLachlin and Taylor, British Columbia Practice, 3rd ed., vol. 1 (Markham, Ont.: LexisNexis Canada Inc., 2006) at 3-4 - 3-4.1: If a statement of claim (or, under the current Rules, a notice of civil claim) is to serve the ultimate function of pleadings, namely, the clear definition of the issues of fact and law to be determined by the court, the material facts of each cause of action relied upon should be stated with certainty and precision, and in their natural order, so as to disclose the three elements essential to every cause of action, namely, the plaintiff's right or title; the defendant's wrongful act violating that right or title; and the consequent damage, whether nominal or substantial. The material facts should be stated succinctly and the particulars should follow and should be identified as such... [17] These requirements serve two foundational purposes: efficiency and fairness. These purposes align with Rule 1-3 which confirms that "the object of [the] Supreme Court Rules is to secure the just, speedy and inexpensive determination of every proceeding on its merits." [18] I emphasize efficiency because a proper notice of civil claim enables a defendant to identify the claim he or she must address and meet. The response filed by a defendant, together with the notice of civil claim and further particulars, if any, will confine the ambit of examinations for discovery and of the issues addressed at the trial itself. Proper pleadings limit the prospect of delay or adjournments. They allow parties to focus their resources on those matters that are of import and to ignore those that are not. They facilitate effective case management and the role of the trier of fact. [19] A proper notice of civil claim also advances the fairness of pre-trial processes and of the trial. Defendants should not be required to divine the claim(s) being made against them. They should not have to guess what it is they are alleged to have done. [29] Evidence is not to be included in pleadings (Sahyoun at para. 29; Rule 3-7(1)). Pleadings are not to include a detailed narrative of facts and events that may relate to the claim. Instead, they must in a summary fashion, set out "the fact or combination of facts which give rise to a remedy in law." (Doerksen v. First Open Heart Society of British Columbia, 2010 BCSC 1291 at para. 25.) [30] The test under Rule 9-5(1) (a) is whether it is "plain and obvious" that the plaintiffs' claim discloses no reasonable cause of action. In Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959, Wilson J. for the court stated: 33 Thus, the test in Canada governing the application of provisions like Rule 19(24)(a) of the British Columbia Rules of Court is the same as the one that governs an application under R.S.C.O. 18, r. 19: assuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it "plain and obvious" that the plaintiff's statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action? As in England, if there is a chance that the plaintiff might succeed, then the plaintiff should not be "driven from the judgment seat". Neither the length and complexity of the issues, the novelty of the cause of action, nor the potential for the defendant to present a strong defence should prevent the plaintiff from proceeding with his or her case. Only if the action is certain to fail because it contains a radical defect ranking with the others listed in Rule 19(24) of the British Columbia Rules of Court should the relevant portions of a plaintiff's statement of claim be struck out under Rule 19(24)(a). [31] While no evidence is admissible on an application to strike under Rule 9 - (1)(a) and the pleadings are assumed to be true, the court must recognize what is actually plead. If the allegations in the claim are based on assumptions or speculation, such need not be taken to be true (Drummond v. Moore, 2012 BCSC 496 at para. 18 and Edmond v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2013 BCSC 1102 at para. 52). [32] Pleadings will also be struck if they do not establish a cause of action, do not advance a claim known to law, or are without substance because they are groundless and fanciful (Dempsey v. Envision Credit Union, 2006 BCSC 750 at para. 17). [33] A pleading is unnecessary, vexatious, or embarrassing if it does not go to establishing a claim known to law. See Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 (BCSC) where Romilly J. stated: 47 Irrelevancy and embarrassment are both established when pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is being pleaded: Gittings v. Caneco Audio-Publishers Inc. (1987), 17 B.C.L.R. (2d) 38 (B.C.S.C.). An "embarrassing" and "scandalous" pleading is one that is so irrelevant that it will involve the parties in useless expense and will prejudice the trial of the action by involving them in a dispute apart from the issues: Keddie v. Dumas Hotels Ltd. (1985), 62 B.C.L.R. 145 at 147 (B.C.C.A.). An allegation which is scandalous will not be struck if it is relevant to the proceedings. It will only be struck if irrelevant as well as scandalous: College of Dental Surgeons of B.C. v. Cleland (1968), 66 W.W.R. 499 (B.C.C.A.). A pleading is "unnecessary" or "vexatious" if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or does not advance any claim known in law: Strauts v. Harrigan, [1992] B.C.J. No. 86 (Q.L.) (B.C.S.C.). A pleading that is superfluous will not be struck out if it is not necessarily unnecessary or otherwise objectionable: Lutz v. Canadian Puget Sound Lumber and Timber Co. (1920), 28 B.C.R 39 (C.A.). A pleading is "frivolous" if it is obviously unsustainable, not in the sense that it lacks an evidentiary basis, but because of the doctrine of estoppel: Chrisgian v. B.C. Rail Ltd. et al., [1992] B.C.J. No. 1567, (6 July 1992), Prince George Registry 20714 (B.C.S.C.). [34] In Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083, B. Fisher J. (as she then was) stated: [19] The rule that material facts in a notice of civil claim must be taken as true does not mean that allegations based on assumption and speculation must be taken as true. This was discussed in Operation Dismantle Inc. v The Queen, [1985] 1 SCR 441, where Dickson J. (as he then was) stated that "[n]o violence is done to the rule where allegations, incapable of proof, are not taken as proven". In Young v Borzoni, 2007 BCCA 16, the court stated (at paras. 30-31) that great caution must be taken in relying on Operation Dismantle as a general authority that allegations in pleadings should be weighed as to their truth, but it is not fundamentally wrong to look behind allegations in some cases, and it may be appropriate to subject the allegations in the pleadings to a sceptical analysis. It was considered appropriate in Young, where the plaintiff made sweeping allegations of things like intolerance, deceit, harassment, intimidation and falsifying documents against the defendants, which the court concluded could only be viewed as speculation. [20] Under Rule 9-5(1)(b), a pleading is unnecessary or vexatious if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action, if it does not advance any claim known in law, where it is obvious that an action cannot succeed, or where it would serve no useful purpose and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v Canadian Jewish Congress, [1999] BCJ No. 2160 (SC); Skender v Farley, 2007 BCCA 629. If a pleading is so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is pleaded, it may also be unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious. An application under this sub-rule may be supported by evidence. [21] Abuse of process under Rule 9-5(1)(d) or the court's inherent discretion is a flexible doctrine. It allows the court to prevent a claim from proceeding where to do so would violate principles of judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice. A claim may be struck where it is a collateral attack on an administrative decision that is subject to appeal or judicial review: Cimaco International Sales Inc. v British Columbia, 2010 BCCA 342; Stephen v HMTQ, 2008 BCSC 1656; Varzeliotis v British Columbia, 2007 BCSC 620; Gemex Developments Corp. v City of Coquitlam, 2002 BCSC 412; Berscheid v Ensign, [1999] BCJ No. 1172 (SC). A claim may also be struck as an abuse of process where it is an attempt to re-litigate an issue that has already been decided: Toronto (City) v Canadian Union of Public Employees (CUPE), Local 79, 2003 SCC 63. Discussion [35] As is apparent from the amended notice of civil claim, the plaintiffs reference the conduct of several individuals yet, no claims are advanced against them as they are not parties to the action. [36] As noted, the claim arises from the letter sent to Jennifer Pinkerton by the Society respecting the complaints made to the Society at two horse shows regarding the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton's use of the "bag stick method" to control a horse. [37] The plaintiffs' most recent further amended pleadings alleging the Society committed the torts of conspiracy, defamation, intentional affliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress and negligence must be considered in the context of their genesis in the letter in issue. [38] Before turning to each of the alleged torts in the above context, I will first turn to the nature of the plaintiffs' relationship with the defendant Society. Membership in the Defendant Society [39] The defendant notes that the plaintiffs' claims ignore the contractual nature of a member's relationship with voluntary associations like the defendant. In McGee v. Beaver Valley Ski Club, [2006] O.J. No. 1691, the plaintiff member of the ski club was disciplined and suspended for a year. The court addressed the nature of the relationship between a member and a private club: 39 In Conacher v. Rosedale Golf Assn. Ltd., [2002] O.J. No. 575, 2002 CarswellOnt 527 (S.C.J.), the court held that the relationship between a private club and a member is a contractual relationship. Only in certain circumstances, or when there are contractual rights created by the club's by-laws, will the member be able to enforce rights under the law. 40 I set out a few points made by the Court in Conacher: The by-laws of not-for-profit or "voluntary associations" such as social clubs, philanthropic, sports or professional bodies constitute contractual obligations as between the members and the corporation. Membership in a club may give members valuable proprietary and social rights, including the right that the club's affairs be conducted honestly and bona fide in accordance with its rules. While the internal rules may create enforceable legal obligations on the part of the association to the member, it is a question in each case whether the particular rule in issue was intended to give rise to an enforceable contractual legal obligation. Cameron v. Hogan (1934), 51 C.L.R. 358 (H.C.) at 370-1. 41 The following passage from the case of Turner v. Pickering, [1976] 1 N.Z.L.R. 129 (S.C.) at p. 141 (also an expulsion case), is apt: It seems now established that the plaintiff can have enforceable rights of a contractual nature brought about by his membership of a voluntary association, including the right that its affairs will be conducted honestly and bona fide in accordance with its rules. It is no longer necessary for him to be protecting a private right of a proprietary character before he can ask the court to intervene; but public policy still suggests some limitation to exclude interference with associations of a wholly social nature, or where it is clear that no legal relationships of any sort were intended between members. (emphasis added) Clearly, the courts may intervene in a dispute where the members' property or civil rights are at issue and natural justice has not been followed. The rationale appears to be that a member joins an organization voluntarily and the relationship is contractual in nature. Lakeside Colony v. Hofer, [1992] 3 S.C.R. 165. At the same time, as a matter of policy, the law refuses to recognize a duty of care regarding the social expectations of others or to interfere in purely voluntary social relationships. ... Generally speaking, people have a right to associate or not associate with whom they choose and the courts do not regulate such aspects of social intercourse. No provision exists in the club's documents permitting a member to commence legal proceedings due to a breach of procedure in relation to a third person. There is no indication that the members of the club ever contemplated the creation of legal relations in respect of the rules and by-laws which were adopted for their governance. This is not a case where a member was arbitrarily expelled from the club. If not pleased with a decision, members can use the internal political democratic process to voice their views, and effect change. ... Upon a careful review of the plaintiffs' pleading, the materials filed and the submissions of counsel, it is my view that the true nature of the plaintiffs' claim is not rooted in law. It is not a claim our courts ought to deal with. There is no basis on which any reasonable trier of fact could say that the plaintiffs have a bona fide right to be before the court. The case does not raise "important issues of law and public interest," as alleged. My best assessment of the plaintiffs' claim is that the action appears to reflect "much ado about nothing." [40] In Piresferreira v. Ayotte, 2010 ONCA 384, the trial judge awarded damages in tort arising from an employment relationship. The Court of Appeal addressed the effect of a contractual relationship where a tort claim is advanced: 47 Accepting that Bell Mobility's Code of Business Conduct was part of the employment contract, a breach of a contractual duty cannot be the basis for the recognition of a common law tort. For concurrent tort liability to be available there must be a common law duty of care that would exist even in the absence of the specific contractual term which created the corresponding contractual obligation. In Central & Eastern Trust Co. v. Rafuse, [1986] 2 S.C.R. 147, Le Dain J. differentiated between a duty that is created by the contract and an independent common law duty. He wrote at p. 205: What is undertaken by the contract will indicate the nature of the relationship that gives rise to the common law duty of care, but the nature and scope of the duty of care that is asserted as the foundation of the tortious liability must not depend on specific obligations or duties created by the express terms of the contract. It is in that sense that the common law duty of care must be independent of the contract. 48 The meaning of this passage was clarified by the majority of the Supreme Court of Canada in BG Checo International Ltd. v. British Columbia Hydro & Power Authority, [1993] 1 S.C.R. 12, at p. 36: In our view, Le Dain J.'s use of the words "created" and "depends" indicates the meaning of this passage is simply that for concurrent tort liability to be available there must be a duty of care in tort that would exist even in the absence of the specific contractual term which created the corresponding contractual obligation. 49 Here, the trial judge rested her finding that Piresferreira had established the tort of negligent infliction of mental suffering on Bell Mobility's failure to treat her "in accordance with Bell Mobility's Code of Business Conduct." After itemizing Ayotte's acts on which her finding was based, she wrote, "None conformed with Bell Mobility's expectations as set out in its Code of Business Conduct." The trial judge erred in basing the standard of care on a contractual obligation. [41] Given the contractual nature of the relationship between the plaintiffs and the defendant as discussed in these authorities, the plaintiffs must plead grounds that surmount that relationship. For the following reasons, I find they have failed to do so for each alleged tort. Conspiracy [42] The amended notice of civil claim alleges only the Society has committed a conspiracy. The elements of the tort of conspiracy are summarized in Jevco Insurance Company v. Pacific Assessment Centre Inc., 2015 ONSC 7751: [17] Further, Wilson J. referred to an excerpt from Professor Fridman's text on torts as "a useful summary of the current state of the law in Canada with respect to the tort of conspiracy", quoting it as follows: In modern Canada, therefore, conspiracy as a tort comprehends three distinct situations. In the first place there will be an actionable conspiracy if two or more persons agree and combine to act unlawfully with the predominating purpose of injuring the plaintiff. Second, there will be an actionable conspiracy if the defendants combine to act lawfully with the predominating purpose of injuring the plaintiff. Third, an actionable conspiracy will exist if defendants combine to act unlawfully, their conduct is directed towards the plaintiff (or the plaintiff and others), and the likelihood of injury to the plaintiff is known to the defendants or should have been known to them in the circumstances. [18] In the case before this Court, the motion judge summarized the elements of the tort of conspiracy as follows (at para. 50) [(2014), 120 O.R. (3d) 43, [2014] O.J. No. 1704 (S.C.J.)]: The constituent elements of the tort of conspiracy are: (1) two or more defendants make an agreement to injure the plaintiff; (2) the defendants (a) use some means (lawful or unlawful) for the predominate purpose of injuring the plaintiff, or (b) use unlawful means with knowledge that their acts were aimed at the plaintiff and knowing or constructively knowing that their acts would result in injury to the plaintiff; (3) the defendants act in furtherance of their agreement to injure; and, (4) the plaintiff suffers damages as a result of the defendants' conduct. [page525] [19] This is a succinct and correct summary of the law... [43] As the allegation of conspiracy is only made against the Society and the tort of conspiracy requires two or more defendants agreeing to injure the plaintiffs, this claim is bound to fail. The Society cannot conspire with itself. Defamation [44] Only the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton alleges she was defamed. There is no allegation that the plaintiff Petra Pinkerton was also defamed. [45] The elements of the tort of defamation were summarized by McLachlin C.J. in Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61: 28 A plaintiff in a defamation action is required to prove three things to obtain judgment and an award of damages: (1) that the impugned words were defamatory, in the sense that they would tend to lower the plaintiff's reputation in the eyes of a reasonable person; (2) that the words in fact referred to the plaintiff; and (3) that the words were published, meaning that they were communicated to at least one person other than the plaintiff. If these elements are established on a balance of probabilities, falsity and damage are presumed, though this rule has been subject to strong criticism: see, e.g., R. A. Smolla, "Balancing Freedom of Expression and Protection of Reputation Under Canada's Charter of Rights and Freedoms", in D. Schneiderman, ed., Freedom of Expression and the Charter (1991), 272, at p. 282. (The only exception is that slander requires proof of special damages, unless the impugned words were slanderous per se: R. E. Brown, The Law of Defamation in Canada (2nd ed. (loose-leaf)), vol. 3, at pp. 25-2 and 25-3.) The plaintiff is not required to show that the defendant intended to do harm, or even that the defendant was careless. The tort is thus one of strict liability. 29 If the plaintiff proves the required elements, the onus then shifts to the defendant to advance a defence in order to escape liability. [46] Although publication is not specifically plead, the only possible publication of the letter alleged is found at paragraph 15 of the further amended notice of civil claim: 15. The Plaintiffs further assert that on August 29, 2018 the aforementioned letter was sent by VSCADORA Director Sheila Skene to a non VSCADORA Director without privilege and maliciously or recklessly or negligent to how the unverified complaints would lessen the readers opinion of JENNIFER DAWN PINKERTON. [47] The alleged defamatory letter was sent by the Society to the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton only. The allegation of publication appears to reference an allegation of the plaintiff that she contacted Mr. Barnes of the national dressage organization asking that he give directions and help to resolve the issues she had with the Society. It is not clear when she first communicated with Mr. Barnes. However, the defendant Society did email a copy of the letter in question to Mr. Barnes on August 28, 2018 after he was in contact with them. [48] The evidence also discloses that the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton sent a letter to Equestrian Canada on August 28, 2018 confirming a phone conversation she had with them on August 27, 2018 in which she states: In our conversation I described a set of very troubling circumstances whereby I have been notified, and duly warned, in writing by Victoria Saanich Cadora Board of Directors. I have included the letter [49] The defendant Society submits that, if the release of the letter to Mr. Barnes is the publication relied on by the plaintiff Jennifer Pinkerton, such is not publication by the Society as it was only further distributed at her request. In the case of the release to the defendant Equestrian Canada, it was further distributed by her. [50] Generally, a defendant is not liable if his or her communication is made only to the plaintiff and it is the plaintiff who publishes it to a third person. In Hills v. O'Bryan, [1949] B.C.J. No. 81, an action for libel and slander, the plaintiff worked in the home of the defendant (having been sent by an employment service). Having discovered items were missing, the defendant sent a letter to the plaintiff implying she had stolen the missing items. The plaintiff published the letter to a friend, her sister, her son and some days later an employee at the employment service. Manson J. found there was no reason for the defendant to anticipate the plaintiff would publish the letters. Hence, its publication was not the reasonable and probable result of the defendant's act. As a result, the plaintiff herself was the cause of any damage suffered and her claim was dismissed. [51] Unlike in Hills, Jennifer Pinkerton did not herself publish the letter to a third party, Mr. Barnes. Instead, the defendant Society published the letter, although because of the plaintiff involving the third party. [52] I conclude the letter was published by the Society. However, as I will later address, there was consent by the plaintiff to the publication. [53] That is not the case with the release of the letter to Equestrian Canada. This was done by the plaintiff and hence her responsibility. [54] However, that is not the end of the matter. Where a plaintiff knew or reasonably believed that the defamatory words would be communicated to a third party, such a plaintiff may be deemed to have consented to communication of the defamatory words to another. See: Motoretta Inc. v. Twist & Go Powersports Inc., 2014 ONSC 2469 at para. 59. [55] In Hanly v. Pisces Productions Inc, [1980] BCJ No. 1803, Murray J. stated: 15 I turn now to the defence of consent. This defence arises out of the meeting that the plaintiff had with the Executive Board of Local 891 on June 15th, 1979 at which meeting I have held that the plaintiff reluctantly agreed to a request being made to the defendants to write the letter of June 16th. That consent can be a good defence to a defamation action appears clear but there is a dearth of authority reported in connection with the defence. In Jones v. Brooks (1974) 45 DLR (3d) 413 MacPherson, J. says at page 419: "Whereas our law seems to have atrophied, there has been considerable judicial debate in the United States. There the leading case is Teichner v. Bellan (1959), 181 N.Y.S. 2d 842, a decision of the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court of the State of New York. Justice Halpern for the Court of five said at pp. 845-6: There are decisions in some States that a communication of defamatory matter to an agent of the person defamed in response to an inquiry does not constitute a publication to a third person .... But the better view seems to us to be that taken in another line of cases, holding that the communication to the plaintiff's agent is a publication, even though the plaintiff's action may ultimately be defeated for other reasons. The agent is, in fact, a different entity from the principal; the communication to the agent is, in fact, a publication to a third person .... Consent is a bar to a recovery for defamation under the general principle of volenti non fit injuria or, as it is sometimes put, the plaintiff's consent to the publication of the defamation confers an absolute immunity or an absolute privilege upon the defendant .... However, a plaintiff who had authorized an agent to make an inquiry on his behalf is not to be charged with consent to a defamatory statement made in reply to the inquiry, unless he had reason to anticipate that the response might be a defamatory one .... Only in such a case can it be said that he had impliedly agreed to assume the risk of a defamatory communication to his agent .... I have omitted only the learned Judges' authorities. I think that this passage represents a fair statement of the law to be applied to the present case in so far as it concerns the defences of no publication and volenti. Qualified privilege and malice are other matters to be considered later. See also Annotation 172 A.L.R. 208 (1948). It follows from the Duke of Brunswick case and Rudd v. Cameron, both supra, and the passage from Teichner v. Bellan, which I have quoted, that what was said by the defendants to the detectives constituted publication notwithstanding that they were agents for the plaintiff." And at p. 420 he says: "The plaintiff therefore had good reason when he sent the detectives to anticipate that the response by the defendants to the inquiries might be defamatory. There is the very essence of volenti non fit injuria - the knowing consent of the plaintiff to the defendant's wrong which the plaintiff expected. This defence was not considered in the Duke of Brunswick case. In Chapman v. Lord Ellesmere et al. (1932) 2 K.B. 431 (C.A.), a defamatory action, Slesser, L.J. applied the doctrine of volenti and used the following words (at p. 463) from Salmond on Torts (now in the 15th ed. (1969), p. 664): 'No act is actionable as a tort at the suit of any person who has expressly or impliedly assented to it: Volenti no fit injuria. No man can enforce a right which he has voluntarily waived or abandoned.'" 16 I also refer with approval to the Law of Defamation in Canada (1976) by Jeremy S. Williams at pages 106 and 107 where the following passages occur: "Consent will be a good defence to a defamation action. To constitute the defence of consent an ordinary person in the position of the plaintiff should be able to appreciate that the publication of a comment is being consented to and the plaintiff must also do some overt act signifying consent. A person who writes false news about himself in a letter to a newspaper cannot complain when it is published. The defence of volenti non fit injuria has been recognized in defamation actions. It was applied in Jones v. Brooks. In that case the defence of volenti non fit injuria was held to exist where agents of the plaintiff had actively solicited the defamatory comments. Other courts have recognized this defence as assumption of the risk. The defences of volenti non fit injuria and assumption of the risk are more usual in negligence and fault liability torts. The proper appellation of the corresponding defence in defamation is consent. The defence of consent will be available where the plaintiff has actively encouraged, solicited or induced the publication of the defamatory statements. The consent may be express or implied, but it cannot be passive. The consent may precede or be contemporaneous with the publication. If the consent is given prior to the publication there may be some question as to its extent. For example, in Chapman v. Lord Ellesmere the plaintiff contended that though he had consented to the publication of a report of an inquiry by the Jockey Club, he had not consented to its publication in such a form as to include an innuendo of impropriety. The English Court of Appeal decided that the plaintiff's prior consent contained an agreement to the form of the publication. The extent of the consent is a matter of fact. A contemporaneous consent should also be extensive enough to cover the publication in question to suffice as a defence. It should also encourage or cause the publication of a statement that would not otherwise be made." [56] Regarding the release of the letter by the defendant Society to Mr. Barnes, I conclude the defence of consent applies. Although the Society "published" the letter to Mr. Barnes, the plaintiff had specifically requested Mr. Barnes' directions regarding the letter and whether she could share the letter with him. That latter inquiry supports a finding of active consent by her that he could read the contents of the letter. [57] I am also satisfied that the plaintiff knew or would reasonably know that the alleged defamatory letter would be communicated to Mr. Barnes as a result of her actions in requesting his directions on the matter, knowing that the alleged defamatory statements would thereby come to his attention. This is particularly so given the evidence of her proposed release of it to him and her actual release to Equestrian Canada. [58] I conclude the claims in defamation are bound to fail. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress [59] The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress were recently reviewed in Lu v. Shen, 2020 BCSC 490: [257] To make out a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must demonstrate conduct that is (1) flagrant and extreme, (2) plainly calculated to produce harm, and which (3) results in visible and provable illness. See Rahemtulla v. Vanfed Credit Union, 1984 CanLII 689 (BCSC), at paras. 52-56; and also, Merrifield v. Canada (Attorney General), 2019 ONCA 205, at para. 45. [60] The plaintiffs do not allege flagrant and extreme conduct, nor do they allege conduct supporting this intentional tort. They fail to plead allegations of an intention to cause the kind of harm necessary or knowledge of the defendant that such harm was almost certain to occur. More significantly, the plaintiffs plead no visible or provable illness due to the conduct of the defendants. Instead, they plead the alleged illness arises from the "unresolved action" not the conduct of the defendants. A plea of "emotional distress" is not sufficient. There is a line to be drawn between "mere insult, which is not actionable, and 'extreme and outrageous' conduct, which is" (Allen Linden & Bruce Feldthusen, Canadian Tort Law, 9th ed. (Toronto: LexisNexis Canada Inc., 2011) at 57). [61] An example of significantly more serious allegations insufficient to establish the tort is found in Merrifield where an RCMP officer was being investigated for potential financial wrongdoings. The Court of Appeal described the findings of the trial judge: 55 The trial judge found that Proulx's decision to order a Part IV investigation into Merrifield's travel credit card usage was both flagrant and outrageous because: a) he did not ask Merrifield for an explanation for his cash withdrawals, b) he did not reply to Merrifield's question about whether he had reason to believe the credit card was being used for non-RCMP purposes, c) he provided no particulars of the transactions that concerned him or a date range for which the investigation was to be conducted, and d) he alleged discreditable conduct contemporaneous to engaging in discreditable conduct himself, namely attempting to procure a prostitute. She found that Proulx had a reckless disregard for causing Merrifield emotional distress, and that emotional distress was a consequence that was known to be substantially certain to follow from the decision to order the investigation. Finally, the trial judge found that Merrifield suffered from depression and post-traumatic stress disorder, based largely on his evidence and that of his family doctor that he was off work sick. Thus, she concluded that the tort of IIMS was established solely on the facts of the Part IV credit card investigation. The Court of Appeal overturned the trial judge's decision and concluded the claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress failed all three elements of the test: 60 There was no evidence that Proulx's conduct was intended to cause harm or that he knew that harm was substantially certain to follow from his decision to order the Part IV investigation, and "emotional distress" was insufficient in any event. Finally, despite multiple findings of flagrant and outrageous conduct, the evidence did not establish the requisite causal connection. [62] The pleadings of the plaintiffs also fail for the reasons above. The allegations made do not rise to the level required to advance the tort. I conclude this claim is bound to fail. Negligence [63] The further amended notice of civil claim alleges the Society's Vice President and Competition Manager negligently designed the competition schedule on July 16, 2018. [64] In addition, there is no standalone duty of care by a not-for-profit organization to investigate complaints. This court previously addressed the issue of a standalone duty of care of a not-for-profit organization to investigate complaints in Bulwer v. Canadian Mental Health Assn., 2016 BCSC 1110. In Bulwer, the plaintiff advanced a claim in negligence against the defendant alleging a failure to adequately investigate complaints against him by other tenants in this building before they evicted him and cause him psychological damage. Baird J. stated: [22] Is there a triable issue that the defendants owed Mr. Bulwer a duty to take care in investigating complaints against him before issuing an eviction notice? Such a duty has not been recognised before and the threshold question is whether the law of negligence should be extended to include it: Cooper v. Hobart, 2001 SCC 79. If the answer to this question is conclusively "no", then the amended notice of civil claim has no prospect of success and should be struck out. [23] It may be worth emphasising that on this hearing, after some discussion, Mr. Bulwer conceded that damages for mental distress would not typically be available in circumstances such as those presented by this case. He agreed, in other words, that it was not reasonably foreseeable that a person of ordinary fortitude would suffer psychological injury of a compensable magnitude merely by receiving an eviction notice in a garden variety dispute with a landlord. [Emphasis added.] [65] Baird J. then noted: [28] If I am wrong, and there exists a prima facie duty of care based on the posited special relationship between the parties, then in my view the policy argument against the legal imposition of the duty is unanswerable. There can be no better way of guaranteeing that CMHA-PA and similar philanthropic organizations will abandon the vital work of providing affordable housing to vulnerable citizens than by burdening them with duties and liabilities not shared by other landlords: see generally Cooper at paragraphs 21-39. [66] The plaintiffs have not plead facts which support a new duty of care owed, nor facts of its breach nor of foreseeable harm resulting therefrom. [67] This claim also is bound to fail. Baker, [1988] 24 B.C.L.R. (2d) 17 [(C.A.)].
