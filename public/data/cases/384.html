He has submitted no new evidence in support of his appeal. The Appellant asks the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) to set aside the determination of the RPD and either replace it with a positive determination or refer the matter back to the RPD for redetermination. DETERMINATION [2] Pursuant to Section 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. This appeal is dismissed. Background [3] The Appellant alleged before the RPD that, though he was born into a Muslim family, he converted to Christianity when he entered into a relationship with XXXX, an Armenian Christian girl, in Iran. His family was very angry and planned to harm him as a result of his rejection of Islam, while XXXX refused their demand that she become Muslim. Fearing that he was about to be arrested by his brother, allegedly an intelligence officer for the Iranian regime, he fled to Thailand, where he stayed for some time and was allegedly baptised. He utilized the services of a smuggler to travel to XXXX, then to XXXX, and on to Canada, where he claimed refugee status. [4] The Appellant's application for refugee protection was heard on April 17, 2014. By a decision of May 23, 2014, the RPD rejected the claim. The panel cited numerous credibility concerns with the Appellant's testimony, and it found that he was completely lacking in credibility. [5] The Appellant argues on appeal that the RPD made numerous errors in finding that he was not credible. Role of the RAD What is the role of the RAD in considering an appeal against a decision of the RPD? [6] While the IRPA sets out grounds for appeal as well as possible remedies, it does not specify the standard by which the RAD is to review the decision of the lower tribunal. [7] Although the RAD is a relatively new body, it now has the benefit of some Federal Court jurisprudence which speaks to the appellate tribunal's role in relation to that of the RPD. [8] In Iyamuremye, the Court found itself: ...entirely in agreement with the RAD that the standard of review to be applied to findings of fact made by the RPD is reasonableness. It is well established that an appellate body must review the findings of a trial court by applying a correctness standard to findings that involve questions of law and by applying a reasonableness standard to those involving questions of mixed fact and law....1 [9] The Court went on to hold that the RAD must review the evidence that was before the RPD and conduct its own, independent assessment of that evidence to determine whether the lower tribunal had properly considered the evidence and "reasonably justified its conclusion".2 [10] In the subsequent case of Alvarez, the Court clarified the role of the RAD in relation to the RPD.3 It held that the RAD is not to undertake a process of judicial review, but rather must perform its own assessments of the facts in order to determine whether the RPD made an error of law or mis-assessed the facts to the point of making a "palpable and overriding error".4 In the Court's view, the lower tribunal's ability to hear evidence viva voce makes it better situated than the RAD to make findings of fact, and therefore the appeal tribunal should show deference to the RPD's findings of fact and mixed fact and law. In contrast to its ruling in Iyamuremye, here the Court found that the RAD erred by assessing the decision of the RPD against a standard of reasonableness-but then required the use of a higher standard. [11] In Malhotra,5 issued after Alvarez and Eng, the Federal Court considered another decision of the RAD, in which the tribunal applied a standard of reasonableness to the RPD's decision under appeal. Although the Court did not specifically address the RAD's selection of the standard of review, it observed that the RAD had applied a standard of reasonableness and concluded that the RAD's decision did not contain any error which justified the intervention of the Court. [12] The Appellant, relying on Alvarez and Eng, submits that the RAD must perform its own assessment of all the evidence in this appeal, in order to determine whether the RPD relied on a wrong principle of law or mis-assessed the facts to the point of making a palpable and overriding error. [13] Since those two cases were decided, however, the Court in Huruglica considered the appropriate relationship between the RAD and the RPD.6 It found that the RAD erred in simply reviewing the RPD's decision on a standard of reasonableness, rather than conducting its own independent assessment of the Appellants' refugee claims. The Court held that the RAD's application of a reasonableness standard curtailed the tribunal's appellate role, contrary to the purpose of the RAD and the provisions of IRPA. In the Court's view, the RAD should not even engage in a standard of review analysis; rather, it should consider the purpose of the appellate tribunal, the statutory provisions, comparable expertise, and comparative appellate regimes.7 [14] While the Court had endorsed the standard of reasonableness in Iyamuremye, and accepted it in Malhotra, it found in Huruglica that the application of such standard was in error. In contrast to Alvarez and Eng, the Court held that the RAD is not restricted to intervening only in the event of a palpable and overriding error. In Huruglica, the Court determined that the RAD is to conduct a "hybrid appeal". The RAD will therefore review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of the Appellant's refugee claim, deferring to the RPD only where the lower tribunal enjoys a particular advantage in reaching a conclusion. Where the RAD's assessment departs from that of the RPD, the RAD must substitute its own determination. The RPD's Findings [15] The Appellant argues that the RPD made numerous errors in rejecting his claim for protection. [16] Knowledge of major Christian holidays: The Appellant had some trouble with Christmas and Easter. When asked by the RPD if XXXX and her family had any significant religious celebrations at their home, he said there was no celebration, despite the fact that he dated her over a time period that included both Christmas and Easter. When asked about major Christian holidays occurring in Thailand while he was there, he was unable to identify Christmas, even though he was baptised there on XXXX XXXX. When asked to identify major Christian holidays, he listed "Pentecost, Faisa, Pesa, Celebration of Sabbath that begins at sunset on Friday to sunset on Saturday when they pray." The panel did not know whether these were Christian holidays8 and noted that, though the hearing took place only days before Good Friday and Easter, the Appellant did not identify these important days until asked specifically whether an important religious holiday would occur soon. These difficulties, along with other evidentiary concerns, led the panel to conclude that the Appellant was generally lacking in credibility. [17] The Appellant argues on appeal that he was indeed able to name religious holidays and made specific reference to Easter and Good Friday. His religious knowledge, he submits, was at the level that could be expected of a recent convert. He argues further that the RPD erred in stating that XXXX never mentioned major holidays, as this was not his evidence. [18] The RPD saw and questioned the Appellant, and its credibility findings here are to be respected and recognized by the RAD. However, the RAD has reviewed the evidence, and it reaches the same conclusions as did the RPD. [19] The Appellant testified that he was in love with XXXX, wanted to marry her, and intended to convert to Christianity. He also claimed that her family wanted him to convert. The RPD asked him whether she mentioned any important Christian holidays. He replied, "Not that she didn't tell me, but as I was not attending the church, the things that may have been interacted with me and her, I don't remember."9 The RPD did misstate the Appellant's testimony here-he did not testify that XXXX did not mention major holidays, but rather that he did not remember. However, in the RAD's view, this error is insignificant. Although the Appellant claimed to be in a close relationship with XXXX during a time period that included Christmas and Easter, he simply had no recollection of her or her family observing these holidays. While the RPD and the RAD must be cautious in expecting individuals in another culture to celebrate a religious holiday in a certain way, the RPD here cited objective evidence that "ethnic Christians" are permitted to celebrate major religious holidays in Iran. Given that Christmas and Easter are indeed major Christian holidays, the RAD too finds it odd that the Appellant does not remember his girlfriend making any reference to celebrating such holidays. [20] However, the Appellant's problems with Christmas and Easter were not restricted to the celebration of these holidays by Mahaya's family. Although he was in Thailand over Christmas, he again claimed he did not remember if important religious holidays were celebrated.10 He was asked whether he has heard of Christmas, and he replied, "When the year was turned; I was there,"11 apparently referring to New Year's. [21] After failing to identify Christmas, the Appellant was asked if he knew of any other significant Christmas holidays. At this point, he listed: "Pentecost.... Fesah or in English Pesah, this celebration of Sabbath that starts from sunset of Friday and going on til sunset of Saturday. People rest and pray."12 Here, the Appellant failed to mention two of the most significant Christian holidays, and he instead mentioned one lesser Christian holiday and perhaps the Jewish holiday of Passover (Pesah or Pesach), which he then described as Sabbath observance. Given that the Appellant had claimed to be a Christian convert, who was in love with a life-long Christian, and that he claimed to read his Bible daily and attend church five times a week, this answer certainly raised valid credibility concerns. It is notable that the Appellant's hearing took place the day before Good Friday,13 and yet he did not identify Easter as a Christian holiday until specifically asked if there was a major holiday coming up. [22] It was not wrong of the RPD to make a negative credibility finding with respect to the Appellant's weak knowledge of the two most significant Christian holidays. Even if the RAD did not defer to these findings, it would nevertheless reach the same conclusion based on its own review of the evidence. [23] Fabrication of conversion claim: The RPD found that the Appellant had fabricated his conversion to Christianity in order to establish a refugee claim. On appeal, the Appellant reminds the RAD that freedom of religion is a basic human right, and points to jurisprudence holding that the RPD must not mischaracterize or ignore evidence. [24] It is for the Appellant to make full and detailed submissions about any error allegedly made by the RPD, and to identify the location of that error in the RPD's reasons.14 If the RPD ignored or mischaracterized evidence, the Appellant must provide specifics. [25] Here, the RPD found the Appellant to be generally lacking in credibility. As the Appellant's claim was based on a conversion to Christianity, the essence of the global credibility finding was that the Appellant is not a convert as he claims. For reasons set out below, the RAD concludes that this finding was not wrong. [26] Failure to attend a Farsi church: The RPD made a negative credibility finding with respect to the Appellant's attendance at a local English-speaking church. He explained that he could not afford to take public transit to a more distant Farsi congregation. [27] The Appellant argues on appeal that he gave a reasonable explanation, and further points out that his church is hardly local, as he needs to walk at least forty minutes to get there. He also submits that the RPD was speculating that a Farsi-speaking church even exists in Toronto. [28] The Appellant testified that there is a Farsi-language church in Toronto, and it is not open to him now to argue that the RPD speculated by suggesting that there is such a place. [29] While the RPD took "a dim view" of the Appellant's testimony that he could not afford to go to the Farsi church, it did not explain why his explanation-that he could not afford to travel to the more distant congregation-was inadequate. Further, the RPD stated that the Appellant "opts to go to a local Church where he does not understand what is being said." This is not consistent with his evidence that he understands what is said at the church "to some extent. Approximately."15 [30] The RPD erred by making a negative credibility finding with respect to the Appellant's attendance at an English-speaking church. [31] Acceptance of converts by Armenians: The RPD found it unlikely that an Armenian Christian family in Iran would welcome the Appellant, a Muslim, and encourage him to convert. It cited objective evidence that Armenians will not accept a convert within their family.16 [32] The Appellant submits that the RPD relied on a "single unsubstantiated document", which focused on ethnic Kurds in Iran and which is based on questionable, unidentified sources. He also argues that the RPD ignored numerous other documents speaking to the prevalence of conversion to Christianity in Iran. [33] The "single unsubstantiated document" maligned by the Appellant is in fact a lengthy and thorough report prepared by the Danish Immigration Service and the joint Norwegian-Danish fact-finding mission to Iran. The RAD has no reason to doubt the credibility or reliability of this report. [34] To argue that the document is focused on ethnic Kurds in Iran is to misread its title and ignore its contents. It is entitled "On Conversion to Christianity, Issues concerning Kurds and Post-2009 Election Protestors as well as Legal Issues and Exit Procedures". The title incorporates a list of somewhat unrelated issues. The introduction and contents of the document make clear that the report covers a wide range of human rights issues, including but not limited to the situation of Kurds. The Appellant's argument that "the report's title, introduction, and methodology, all confirm that the research focused on Iranians of Kurdish origin" is completely without merit.17 [35] The RPD did not err in relying on this report to assess the Appellant's evidence about his girlfriend's family. [36] It was also not an error for the RPD to fail to refer to other documents about conversions in Iran. It was dealing specifically with the issue of whether Armenian Christians would allow a convert from Islam in their family, and general documents about Christian converts are not of much assistance. The Appellant has not directed the RAD to documents which specifically address the subject considered here by the RPD. [37] Certificate of baptism: The RPD made a negative credibility finding with respect to the Appellant's Thai baptism certificate. The certificate is in English, and the name of the pastor who performed the baptism is a different name than the one given by the Appellant. [38] The Appellant submits on appeal that the RPD did not take into account his circumstances at the time-he had just arrived in Thailand and did not speak the language. He also argues that the panel erred when it failed to make reference to photographs and a video of the baptism ceremony. In his submission, the RPD was also wrong to speculate about why the Appellant's baptism certificate is in English. [39] The RPD was entitled to make a negative finding with respect to the pastor's name. He gave specific testimony about the name of the pastor who baptised him, and provided that pastor's name. There is simply no resemblance between that name and the name listed on the certificate. The Appellant was unable to resolve this contradiction, and it cannot be explained by his recent arrival in Thailand. [40] The Appellant testified that the church services were in Thai, though there was "a lady that translated" into English.18 He could not explain why he was given a baptism certificate in English, other than to say "it was given to me there." The RPD did not speculate improperly here; it was not wrong to expect that a church in Thailand, holding services in Thai, would issue a baptism certificate in that language. It was indeed remarkable that the certificate was issued in English-though the Appellant did not speak the language at the time-which was the language his refugee claim would later be heard in. [41] The panel should have referred to the photos and video. The RPD is presumed to have considered all of the evidence before it, and there is no requirement for it to refer to each document in the record.19 However, where there is evidence which is contrary to its decision, the RPD must analyze that evidence and provide reasons why it does not consider it relevant or trustworthy, or why it chose to disregard it.20 The panel reviewed the photos and video21 but did not address them in its reasons. [42] While the Appellant submits that the RPD found he was not baptised in Thailand, this is not clear from the RPD's reasons. The RPD wrote that "the contradictions also strongly suggest that the claimant was not baptised in Thailand as he says he was."22 There was no clear finding here. [43] If the RPD did indeed find that the Appellant was not baptised in Thailand, without addressing evidence to the contrary, this was an error. However, such an error cannot be determinative of this appeal. The Appellant had a multitude of credibility problems. Even if he had established that he was baptised in Thailand, this does not in itself prove that he is a Christian convert any more than attending church in Canada establishes this. The baptism pictures and video simply cannot overcome his other credibility problems. [44] Travelling with Bible: The RPD found the Appellant to be lacking in credibility when he claimed that he did not travel from Thailand with his Farsi Bible because the smugger told him not to. [45] The RPD's finding here is one of plausibility and, as such, attracts no deference from the RAD. The Federal Court has cautioned that the RPD "...must be careful when rendering a decision based on a lack of plausibility because refugee claimants come from diverse cultures, and actions which appear implausible when judged from Canadian standards might be plausible when considered from within the claimant's milieu." Plausibility findings should only be made in the clearest of cases.23 [46] It is not entirely clear to the RAD why the RPD found it implausible that the Appellant would leave his Bible behind. He was travelling to a predominantly Muslim country, on a false passport, and he was advised by the smuggler not to take the Bible. Carrying such a book might indeed raise unnecessary suspicion or questions. This was not the "clearest of cases", and the RPD was wrong to make a negative credibility finding. [47] Practice of Christianity upon return: The RPD did not believe the Appellant's testimony that he would practice Christianity upon return to Iran, even though he submitted a letter from a Toronto church indicating that he attends there frequently. The Appellant argues on appeal that the RPD made an erroneous and speculative finding that he would not practice upon return. [48] In the RAD's view, it is the sincerity of the Appellant's beliefs which is the crucial issue here. If the Appellant is a sincere Christian, he could be expected to practice his religion upon return, and this might place him at risk. If he is not a sincere Christian, he is in no position to argue that he will put himself at risk in Iran by practicing a religion to which he is not truly an adherent. [49] The Federal Court has noted that it would be absurd to grant a sur place refugee claim each time a pastor produces a letter attesting to the claimant's membership in his church.24 In the RAD's view, while evidence of church participation in Canada might reasonably be expected from an individual who claims to be a convert, such evidence does not in itself establish the sincerity of his beliefs. To put it simply, there is nothing to stop a refugee claimant from regularly attending a church in support of a fraudulent conversion claim.25 [50] The Appellant provided the RPD with a letter attesting that he has been active in a Toronto church for some months and participates in Sunday morning worship and "various midweek meetings." The pastor who authored the letter attested to the Appellant's "Christian faith and commitment."26 However, it was for the RPD, not the pastor, to determine the genuineness of the Appellant's conviction. When it considered his alleged beliefs in the context of his other credibility concerns, it concluded that he was not a genuine convert. It was therefore not an error for the RPD to disbelieve that the Appellant would practice Christianity upon return. In fact, having reviewed the evidence, the RAD reaches the same conclusion. [51] Evidence of persecution of converts: In the Appellant's submission, the RPD was wrong to fail to consider objective evidence about the persecution of Christian converts in Iran. The RAD disagrees. The RPD simply did not accept that the Appellant had converted to Christianity, and therefore it did not believe that he was or would be seen as a Christian convert. In this context, it was not necessary for the panel to consider evidence about the treatment of converts. [52] Consideration of claim under Section 97: The Appellant argues that the RPD erred by failing to consider his claim under Section 97 of IRPA where, unlike under Section 96, the test for risk of harm is purely objective. In his submission, a claimant found not to be credible under Section 96 might still obtain status under Section 97 if his profile is sufficient to establish risk. [53] The difficulty with the Appellant's argument here is that the RPD found him to be entirely lacking in credibility. After that finding, there was simply no profile remaining to be assessed under Section 97. The Federal Court has consistently held that a separate Section 97 analysis is not required if there is no evidence that could go to establishing the person is in need of protection.27 While the Appellant submits that the RPD should have considered objective evidence about the danger faced by Christian converts, this argument has little merit, as the RPD found the Appellant had not established that he is a Christian convert. In these circumstances, the RPD was not required to assess the claim under Section 97. Disposition [54] The RAD has found that the RPD made several errors in rejecting the Appellant's refugee claim. This does not, however, require the RAD to allow this appeal. The RAD must conduct its own assessment of the evidence in order to conclude whether it agrees with the RPD's determination of the refugee claim. [55] The Appellant claimed he was in a deep and committed relationship with XXXX XXXX and yet he could provide little evidence of that relationship; in fact, he testified that he has had no contact with her since his Thailand trip, as she cannot be reached because her telephone is off, and he cannot send her an e-mail. He could not name major Christian holidays without prompting, or he could not explain the discrepancy in the name of the pastor who baptised him. He provided inconsistent evidence about when he left Iran, and he rather improbably claimed that an interpreter in Canada simply invented a date to write in his port-of-entry form. He also provided inconsistent evidence about his travel in Thailand. When the RAD considers the evidence as a whole, it finds that the Appellant is generally lacking in credibility, and therefore neither his refugee claim nor his appeal can succeed. CONCLUSION [56] Pursuant to Section 111(1)(a) of IRPA, the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection.
