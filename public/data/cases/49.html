Motifs et décision Person who is the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX Personne en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, ON Appel instruit / entendu à Date of decision March 22, 2021 Date de la décision Panel H. Shepherd Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Robert Gertler Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] I allow this appeal. The Appellant, XXXX XXXX, is a citizen of India. [2] The Appellant did not apply to file new evidence, but requested a hearing. This request is denied because there is no new evidence to consider, and a hearing concerning evidence which was before the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) would have to be held by that Division. A transcript and digital recording of the hearing are available. Summary of Basis of Claim (BOC) narrative [3] The Appellant fears persecution because he renounced his belief in the Hindu religion and insulted the Hindu gods. The Appellant was beaten up by some Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS) gangsters in XXXX 2017, after which he started receiving threatening phone calls, including an extortion call from the Shiv Sena party on XXXX XXXX, 2018. The Appellant left India for Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2018, with a Canadian temporary resident visa which had previously been issued to him. The police went to the Appellant's house in India in XXXX 2018, to arrest him on charges related to the slaughter of cows. Fearing for his safety, the Appellant claimed for refugee protection in Canada. Summary of the RPD reasons for decision [4] The determinative issue for the RPD was credibility. It rejected the claim for the following reasons: i) Testimony and supporting documents raise credibility issues with respect to the prescription form, the phone call to his wife, and the statement to police; ii) The RPD drew an adverse credibility inference from the fact that the Appellant failed to provide evidence in the form of a warrant or affidavits which corroborate the police visit and interest in him after he left for Canada; iii) There is no evidence to support the Appellant's alleged fear of the Shiv Sena party if he returns to India; and iv) The Appellant's delay in claiming refugee protection undermines his allegations of fear. Grounds of appeal [5] Counsel submits that the RPD erred for the following reasons: i) The RPD erred by making unreasonable and speculative findings; ii) The RPD erred in finding an inconsistency between being an atheist and belonging to the Hindu religion. iii) The Appellant provided reasonable explanations for his failure to provide evidence regarding the phone call to his wife, the lack of police report, and the police visit to his home; iv) The RPD erred by misconstruing evidence and by failing to consider the supporting documents in their totality; v) The RPD erred by relying on peripheral issues which are not central to the claim; vi) The RPD erred by ignoring a reasonable explanation for his delay in claiming refugee protection. Summary of Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) reasons for decision [6] The determinative issues for the appeal are credibility and the objective basis for the risk. The RPD's adverse credibility findings do not stand up to legal scrutiny. The presumption that the Appellant's sworn testimony is true has not been rebutted. The documentary evidence establishes that the agents of persecution are Hindu nationalists who are working with the police to punish the Appellant for having insulted Hinduism. The risk is present throughout the country and adequate state protection is not available. DECISION [7] The appeal is allowed. I substitute my own decision that the Appellant is a Convention refugee.1 ANALYSIS [8] My role is to look at all the evidence and decide if the RPD made the correct decision.2 Can a Hindu be an atheist? [9] The RPD erred in concluding that the Appellant cannot be both a Hindu and an atheist because the two are contradictory. The Appellant testified that he is a Hindu, but went on to indicate that he did not want to belong to or practice Hinduism. When asked how the two propositions can be reconciled, the Appellant stated that he is not attached to this religion. Although the question was asked three times, the Appellant gave reasons why he did not practice Hinduism, but without answering the question.3 When counsel returned to this issue during his questioning, the Appellant said that he still belongs to the Hindu religion, although he does not believe it. When asked for more details, the Appellant replied that he is a Hindu by birth and that this status stays with him. People in India will consider him to be Hindu.4 [10] This is a reasonable explanation. I will take judicial notice of some well-known facts to respond. It is important to distinguish between inclusive faith traditions for which community membership is not contingent on religious beliefs, and those which exclude people whose opinions are considered to be erroneous. It is not possible to be a member of a conservative evangelical Protestant church and be an atheist at the same time. In their view, being a Christian requires an explicit confession of faith. The RPD applied this model to Hinduism. However, other faith traditions use a community model which is not contingent on belief. The Roman Catholic Church is an example of this approach. Canon 912 of the Code of Canon Law permits all who have been baptized by a Roman Catholic priest to be admitted to Holy Communion unless they have been excommunicated, as specified in Canon 844.5 I have not disclosed this document to counsel because it is a well-known document which benefits the Appellant. Although excommunication denies access to the sacraments, it does not remove the person from the Church. In particular, the person does not have to be readmitted to the Church if this status is revoked. According to Roman Catholic Canon Law, baptism confers an indelible status which makes the person a member of the Church for life, notwithstanding becoming an atheist. A similar ecclesiology can be found in the Anglican Church.6 The same thing is also true of Judaism which uses a community membership model which does not require a profession of faith to be considered Jewish. For this reason, it is possible to be a Jew and an atheist at the same time. An example of this can be found in the United Jewish People's Order which is a non-theistic secular organization which developed out of socialism in the 1920s. Belief in God is not among their operative principles. The Appellant testified that he became a Hindu at birth and that this status remains with him for life, including in the eyes of the general public. Given that this response is plausible in light of other faith traditions, the onus was on the RPD to cite evidence which could rebut his testimony. The panel did not refer to documentary evidence in support of its view that theistic belief is required to be a Hindu in India. The RPD erred because it ignored a reasonable and plausible explanation. The prescription form [11] The RPD made adverse observations with respect to the prescription form.7 First, the document was issued by a medical centre which treats children. The RPD did not accept the explanation that the clinic accepts adults and that it was close to where the assault took place. Second, the document refers to bruises, but not to facial injuries or bleeding below the li. Third, there is no evidence with respect to the distance between the assault and the nearest hospital relative to the clinic. Fourth, the prescription contains unnecessary confidential information about the nature of the injuries. The RPD concluded that the document is not likely authentic. [12] When asked about the physician's qualification as a consultant child specialist, the Appellant responded that he also treats adults and that he used to be seen by this doctor. He went there because the nearest hospital was not close by, and he was in pain. He said that he had bruises around his XXXX and XXXX, and some bleeding near his XXXX. He was also treated for XXXX injuries.8 [13] The letterhead identifies the clinic as being the "XXXX XXXX XXXX."9 Although Dr. XXXX is a consultant child specialist and ex-senior medical officer, the letterhead does not indicate that the clinic is restricted to children. The RPD had no evidence which rebuts the Appellant's testimony that this medical centre treats adults. Given that it is plausible that a paediatrician in India may treat adults who appear at his clinic, the RPD was required to provide good reason to draw an adverse inference from this. The use of Canadian standards is inadequate to draw an implausibility finding with respect to medical treatment in India without supporting evidence. [14] The document, dated XXXX XXXX, 2017, indicates that the Appellant had signs of beating on his XXXX and XXXX, bruises on his XXXX, body ache, fever and depression. The Appellant testified that he had bruises his XXXX and XXXX, and some bleeding near his XXXX. He was also treated for XXXX injuries.10 The Appellant previously testified that he was beaten with a rod on his side and his XXXX for two to three minutes. He was also thrown to the ground and punched and kicked.11 This is consistent with the medical report which indicates that he had bruises and signs that he had been beaten on his XXXX and XXXX. The fact that the prescription does not mention bruises to the XXXX and some bleeding near the XXXX is insufficient to conclude that the document is not likely genuine. It is plausible that the physician omitted minor injuries. In addition, it was unfair for the RPD to have drawn an adverse inference from this observation without having asked the Appellant for an explanation. [15] The RPD unfairly drew an adverse inference on the basis that the Appellant did not provide evidence concerning the distance from the scene of the beating to the nearest hospital. The RPD should have asked the Appellant about this if this were an issue of concern. The Appellant should learn about this issue for the first time when reading the RPD reasons for decision. [16] The RPD speculated that a physician would not mention the diagnosis in a prescription. In so doing, the panel applied Canadian standards without asking the Appellant for an explanation. This constitutes a breach of natural justice, and it is an issue which should have been put to the Appellant so that he could have responded. There is insufficient evidence which could support an adverse inference on this basis. [17] In summary, the RPD did not provide adequate reasons to conclude that the medical prescription is likely inauthentic. Phone call to the Appellant's wife [18] When asked if the Appellant has a record of the phone call to his wife after he was beaten on XXXX XXXX, 2017, he responded that he gave his phone to his wife when he bought a new one before leaving the country.12 The RPD drew an adverse inference from the fact that the Appellant did not contact his wife and ask her to provide evidence of the call. [19] Rule 11 of the RPD Rules13 states that a claimant: must provide acceptable documents establishing their identity and other elements of the claim. A claimant who does not provide acceptable documents must explain why they did not provide the documents and what steps they took to obtain them. The Federal Court has specified that the RPD cannot base an adverse inference from the failure to disclose a document. This can only be done if a reasonable explanation has not been provided for its absence. In addition, a negative credibility inference can only be drawn if the RPD has reason to doubt the claimant's credibility for other reasons. In other words, this is not a determinative issue in itself.14 The RPD erred by making a negative credibility finding resulting from the Appellant's failure to provide evidence without considering, or even asking for, an explanation, contrary to principles established by the Federal Court. The statement to the police [20] The RPD drew an adverse inference from the fact that the Appellant did not disclose a copy of his complaint to the police which was taken by two officers who interviewed him at the medical centre. The panel did not accept the explanation that the Appellant went to the police station the day after he received a threatening call, but that he was told that his statement was useless because he was unable to name the assailants, that he was mistaken that they belonged to the RSS, and that this organization is too large a group to arrest. The RPD concluded that it is implausible that the police would have cancelled his statement. [21] The Appellant testified that the police advised him not to make a report because there would be no use doing so, and that he would make an enemy of the RSS if he blamed them for the attack. Although two officers interviewed him at the clinic and took hand-written notes, the Appellant did not know what they did with the notes.15 He indicated that nothing happened after he was sent away. [22] The RPD misunderstood the Appellant's testimony. He did not say that the police cancelled his statement, but rather that they advised him to cancel his complaint. As a result, the police did not receive one from him. Although the interpretation could have been clearer, the meaning of the testimony was that the Appellant went to the police station the next day to file a formal complaint, but was advised against it for two major reasons. First, it would be of no practical benefit because he was unable to identify the assailant; and second, blaming the RSS for the assault would put him at risk. The Appellant accepted this advice and left the police station without filing a complaint. The RPD confused the issue of the proposed complaint at the police station with the interview notes taken by officers at the clinic. [23] The relevant question concerns disclosure of the police interview notes. As previously stated, the RPD is not entitled to draw an adverse inference from the absence of a document, but it can do so if a reasonable explanation has not been provided for its absence. The RPD did not ask the Appellant why he did not obtain and disclose the interview notes. The panel inquired about why the complaint which was made at the police station was not filed. The answer is simple; such a document does not exist. Without knowing anything about policies and procedures with respect to the disclosure of officer's notes, and without putting this question to the Appellant, the RPD had no grounds to make an adverse credibility conclusion. Police activity after the Appellant's arrival in Canada [24] The Appellant testified that his wife called him at the end of XXXX 2018, to inform him that armed men with an arrest warrant came to his house looking for him. He was accused of helping Muslims to slaughter cows without a license. The RPD drew an adverse inference from the fact that neither a copy of the arrest warrant nor an affidavit from his wife or mother were disclosed. The panel did not accept the explanation that they did not know what to do. The RPD then cited general legal principles which are applicable to documentary disclosure in Canadian courts. [25] The Appellant testified that the police showed the warrant to his wife, but they did not leave it with her. When asked why no affidavit was filed from his mother or wife, he stated that they did not know what to do and they have no male family members.16 The RPD did not ask any follow-up questions before moving on to another issue. In particular, the panel did not ask whether he requested a statement, and if so, what happened to this request. Rule 11 requires that the RPD consider what steps were taken to obtain a document and whether they were reasonable under the circumstances. Although the Appellant indicated that there were gender-related impediments to obtaining affidavits, the RPD did not inquire into what they were. [26] The RPD is a specialized administrative tribunal which is subject to codified disclosure requirements in section 106 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA) and Rule 11 of the RPD Rules, and as interpreted by jurisprudence. If was not enough to consider general principles of disclosure applicable to Canadian courts without considering the particular statutory provisions and case law which apply to the RPD in the specialized context of refugee status determination. [27] Sworn testimony is presumed to be true unless the RPD has good grounds to conclude otherwise.17 In this case, the RPD failed to ask sufficient questions to be in a position to conclude that the Appellant did not provide an adequate explanation for his failure to obtain affidavits from his mother and wife. Given the sensitivity of the allegations against the Appellant, cultural issues may be involved. The Appellant was entitled to have an opportunity to respond to the RPD's concerns, but he was not given the opportunity. In addition, the RPD cannot reject a gender-based and culturally contextual explanation without giving reasons. Delay in claiming refugee protection in Canada [28] The Appellant explained that he waited before claiming refugee protection in Canada because he wanted to see if the situation in India would change. Given the nature and reputation of the Shiv Sena, the RPD concluded that this is not a reasonable explanation. [29] The RPD misapprehended the Appellant's testimony. He stated that he did not plan to make a refugee claim when he arrived in Canada because he hoped to sort things out while in Canada. However, he changed his mind and decided to do so after he became aware that this had become a police matter.18 This is a reasonable explanation for the delay. The Appellant arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2018 and was admitted as a temporary resident. His BOC narrative states that the police went to his home in the last week of XXXX 2018, with a warrant for his arrest.19 The Appellant's intake documents were signed with the assistance of counsel on April 30, 2018 and the BOC was signed on April 27, 2018. This is not an unreasonable delay. Although the threatening call from the Shiv Sena was sufficient to cause him to leave the country to consider his options, the issuance of the warrant for his arrest caused him to conclude that he could not go back to India. The RPD erred by ignoring a reasonable explanation for his delay in claiming asylum. Conclusion on credibility [30] Given the presumption that sworn testimony is true, the onus is on the RPD to provide adequate reasons to rebut this principle. As outlined in my previous assessment, the RPD had insufficient reasons to reject the Appellant's credibility or to give no weight to the medical report. As a result, the Appellant's narrative of events remains unrebutted and presumed to be true. Country conditions The RSS [31] A Human Rights Watch report states that the RSS was founded in 1925 in order to promote the creation of a nationalist Hindu state. Western civilization, including Christianity and Islam, are considered to be enemies of Hindu culture. Social life should be based on Hindu nationalism. The ruling Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) party has associated itself with the RSS and promotes its values, particularly with respect to the protection of cows in accordance with Hindu beliefs. There has been an increase in rhetoric against minorities since the BJP formed the government in 2014. This includes hateful speech by elected officials against religious minorities. Over 44 people have been killed in violence caused by the slaughter of cows, most of whom were Muslims.20 [32] A United States Commission on International Religious Freedom report refers to a "family of Hindu nationalist groups" which include the RSS, the Vishwa Hindu Parishad (VHP), the Bajrang Dal, and the BJP.21 An Amnesty International study reports that the student wing of the RSS has used threats and violence to prevent events from taking place on campuses which they disagree with.22 A Freedom in the World report states that the ruling BJP is believed to have grown out of the RSS. It also confirms that the RSS has engaged in violent activities on campuses, including attacks against students and teachers.23 [33] A Minority Rights Group International report writes that radical Hindu groups have been emboldened since the BJP came to power as a result of this party's association with right-wing extremist groups such as the RSS.24 For example, the government has been reluctant to condemn hate speech, threats and attacks against members of minority groups on the issue of the slaughter of cows. This report states that the RSS has become more prominent since the BJP came to power. One reason for this is that RSS members have been appointed to senior positions in government. The report also indicates that the government of Gujarat introduced legislation in March 2017, that would make the slaughter of a cow punishable by imprisonment for life. [34] The preceding statements are consistent with information found in a United Kingdom Home Office report:25 There were reports of hundreds of religiously motivated killings, assaults, riots, restrictions on the right to practice religion and proselytize, discrimination, and attacks against property. Groups most frequently targeted were Muslims and Christians.26 Violence is often carried out by groups dedicated to the protection of cows which believe that their slaughtering is an attack against Hindu deities which represent motherhood. As a result, they have engaged in killings, beatings and harassment of those who either slaughter cows or eat beef. A climate of impunity for the perpetrators has developed which is attributed to bias in the police and in the judiciary. This has been promoted by members of the ruling BJP who have ties to Hindu nationalist groups and who inflame tensions by use of religiously divisive language. Victims often do not complain to the police because they consider this to be a waste of time. Large-scale communal violence has also been reported against minorities. Police are said to have turned a blind eye to it. The Shiv Sena Party [35] The Home Office report refers to communal riots in Mumbai which were organized by a right-wing party, the Shiv Sena. The Mumbai police are accused of being biased against Muslims and some have participated in riots, targeted and killed individuals, failed to act to protect Muslims, and did not register cases of violence or looting made against Hindus. [36] The International Religious Freedom Report indicates that the Shiv Sena party is a Hindu nationalist party which has called for family planning to reduce the Muslim population.27 [37] A Digital Empowerment Foundation report states that the Shiv Sena party approved an attack on the house of a well-known artist because he was accused of offending religious sentiments by painting depictions of nude Hindu gods and goddesses.28 [38] A document published by the Office français de protection des réfugiés et apatrides reports that a king of Maratha, Shivaji (1630-1680), became a hero because he fought against Muslim invaders and their practice of slaughtering cows which are considered to be sacred in Hinduism. The Shiv Sena party (Army of Shivaji) was named after him. This party is powerful in Maharashtra state where the killing of a cow is a criminal offence punishable by imprisonment of up to five years and a fine.29 Assessment [39] The Appellant owned a XXXX XXXX in Delhi. Although raised in the Hindu faith, he became an atheist in XXXX 2017, after which he stopped attending temple services or participating in Hindu festivals. When representatives from the local Hindu temple came to his store on XXXX XXXX, 2017, to ask for a donation, he refused on the grounds that he no longer gave credence to Hindu worship or beliefs. He soon began receiving threats from RSS members who accused him of offending Hinduism. They beat him with a rod and threatened to kill him on XXXX XXXX, 2017, for having insulted their gods. He received a call from a representative of the Shiv Sena party on XXXX XXXX, 2018, who demanded money as reparation for having insulted a Hindu deity. The Appellant then came to Canada to see how things would develop and consider his options. He made a refugee claim after the police arrived at his house with a warrant for his arrest in XXXX 2018, on charges that he illegally assisted in the slaughter of a cow. He believes that this is a false accusation made by Hindu nationalists who want to punish him for insulting Hinduism. [40] The RPD had insufficient reasons to conclude that the Appellant does not have a subjective fear of persecution. The documentary evidence provides an objective basis for this fear. The RSS and the Shiv Sena form part of an association of Hindu nationalist groups, which include the ruling BJP. The use of violence and other form of intimidation against those who are perceived to be a threat to Hinduism are a matter of record. Perpetrators can act with impunity with the acquiescence or assistance of the police. Although risk from the RSS and Shiv Sena is likely local, this is not the case with the police. The documentary evidence indicates that pro-Hindu bias in the police and judiciary would likely deny the Appellant the due process of law. The collaboration of the RSS and Shiv Sena with the police extend the risk throughout the country. State protection is not available. The Appellant has a well-founded fear of persecution. CONCLUSION [41] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(b) of the IRPA, I substitute my own decision that the Appellant is a Convention refugee. (signed) H. Shepherd H.
