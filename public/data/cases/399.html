Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXX XXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of Hearing August 2, 2011 Date(s) de l'audience Place of Hearing Toronto, Ontario Lieu de l'audience Date of Decision August 23, 2011 Date de la décision Panel R. Bebbington Tribunal Counsel for the Claimant(s) Vania Campana Conseil(s) du / de la / des demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer N/A Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre [1] XXX XXXX, the claimant, is a citizen of the People's Republic of China (China). She claims refugee protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA).1 ALLEGATIONS [2] The claimant alleges her friend introduced her to Christianity, in XXXXX 2006, following a difficult time in her marriage. The claimant began weekly attendance at an underground Christian house church. She found her disposition improved and she was able to rebuild her marriage. On XXXXXXX, 2007, the claimant came to Canada to visit her mother. She renewed her visitors visa a number of times. While in Canada the claimant received a telephone call on XXXXX, 2009, from her husband telling her the Public Security Bureau officers (PSB) had come to her family home looking for her because of her association with an underground Christian house church. The claimant was afraid to return to China and filed a refugee claim on April 20, 2009. DETERMINATION [3] The claimant is neither a Convention refuge nor a person in need of protection. In arriving at this conclusion, the panel has considered the post hearing submissions2 from counsel submitted on August 15, 2011. ISSUES [4] The determinative issue is the credibility of the claimant's oral testimony and Personal Information Form (PIF)3 narrative, concerning her identity as a Christian and pursuit by the PSB. The panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant is not a credible witness in this regard. ANALYSIS Personal Identity [5] The claimant was able to establish her identity as a Citizen of China with documents she submitted in support of her claim4 including her Resident Identity Card (RIC), certified copies of her passport and a Household Register (Hukou). As a result, the panel finds that the claimant has established her personal identity and her identity as a national of the People's Republic China. Credibility and Christian Knowledge [6] The claimant indicates she has been attending Christian worship since XXXXXXX, 2007. She testified that since being in Canada she attends Christian church services once a week. She was baptized into the Christian faith on XXXXX. 2008. Throughout the hearing, the panel asked questions that were predominately intended to assess the claimant's level of Christian understanding and commitment to her faith, rather than as an assessment of her ability to recite chapter and verse. [7] In assessing credibility, the panel was mindful of the claimant's age, education, and background. She alleges that she has 13 years of formal education. In addition, the panel is cognizant of the many difficulties faced by a claimant in establishing a claim, including cultural factors, the milieu of the hearing room, and the stress inherent in responding to oral questions through an interpreter and nervousness. The panel has taken these considerations into account in arriving at its credibility findings. Status in Canada [8] The claimant came to Canada to visit her mother and sister on XXXXXXX, 2007, utilizing a Canadian Visitors Visa (CVV). The claimant subsequently extended the CVV on two occasions allowing her to remain in Canada until XXXXX, 2009. During this time the claimant enrolled in a XXXXXXXXXXXXXX course at XXXXXXXXXXXX College in Toronto, Ontario.5 The panel notes that the terms of the CVV are quite specific, indicating that one may not attend an educational institution while in Canada. The claimant was asked if she had contacted Citizenship and Immigration Canada (CIC) to amend her status to allow her to attend an educational institution. She replied in the negative. The panel inquired if the claimant had also applied for a Canadian 'work permit'. The claimant responded, "Yes". The panel notes the claimant applied for a work permit6 to be employed by her sister, to provide care for her mother. The panel questioned the claimant as to the outcome of the application. The claimant answered that the work permit was denied on XXXXX, 2009, because the employee - employer relationship was not genuine. The claimant was asked to confirm when she made her claim for refugee protection and she stated it was April 20, 2009. The panel finds the claimant has engaged in a series of dubious actions designed to allow her to remain in Canada. The actions described by the claimant in this area of oral testimony cause the panel to question her ability to act as a credible witness. Pursuit by the PSB [9] The claimant testified that the PSB attended her family home on at least 12 or 13 occasions looking for her. The claimant was asked what the PSB had said to her husband and she answered that they told him they had raided an underground church and arrested all the members. The claimant stated the PSB knew she was a member of the underground church. She further added the PSB asked her husband to convince the claimant to return to China and turn herself in. The panel inquired if the PSB said or did anything different on any of their visits beyond what they did on their first visit to her home. The claimant replied, "The PSB tried to put pressure on my husband to make me go back". The panel queried as to what methods the PSB used to 'pressure' the claimant's husband. She stated the PSB came again and again, but added no additional detail. The claimant subsequently noted that her son (age 19), became distressed with the PSB visits and his grades at school began to suffer. The panel asked the claimant if she had considered providing transcripts from her son's school marks to assist in verifying her statements. The claimant responded that she had not thought about this. The panel draws a negative inference to the credibility of the claimant through her action of not providing adequate testimony or documentation to support her allegations. Lack of a Summons or Warrant [10] The panel inquired if the PSB had left a summons or warrant with her husband indicating their interest in her. The claimant said "no". She was next asked if the PSB said anything else to her husband, removed access to any services such as health care, social benefits or 'freezing his hukou' or causing him to lose his job, in order to influence the claimant to return to China. The claimant again replied "no, but indicated her husband did not want her to worry and may not have told her everything. The panel inquired whether the claimant's husband was taken in for questioning by the PSB and she replied in the negative. The panel confirmed with the claimant, that as far as she was aware, her husband and son were well and living safely in China. The panel explained to the claimant that country condition documents7 indicate that a summons is generally left with or shown to family members when the police want someone to come to their headquarters. In addition, the summons is the documentary basis for the subsequent issuance of an arrest warrant if the person in whom they are interested does not respond to the summons. Although this policy is not always implemented, it is reasonable that one would have been issued in respect of the claimant given that the claimant testified that the PSB had gone to her home in search of her on 12 or 13 occasions. To have allegedly attended her family home that many times would indicate that the PSB had more than a casual interest in the claimant. The lack of a summons when one should reasonably have been issued damages the credibility of the claimant. It is also reasonable to believe that the PSB may be frustrated in having attended her family home numerous times and become more aggressive in their action to influence her husband to convince the claimant to surrender. This causes one to question the activities of the PSB. The actions (or lack thereof) indicated by the clamant run counter to conventional beliefs that the PSB are a ruthless and fearsome police force. The claimant's testimony runs counter to documentary evidence about the PSB's methods and is found not to be credible. Lack of Documentation to Support Allegations [11] The claimant was questioned about the alleged arrest of all of the members of her house church, as well as the organizer. The panel asked if she had any supporting documentation such as arrest or sentencing documents or newspaper clippings that would confirm the raid on the underground church. The claimant replied, "No". The panel asked if the claimant had suggested that her husband contact the families of the house church members and find out what had happened. She responded that she had given her husband some telephone numbers for fellow church members, but he was unable to contact anyone. The panel noted for the claimant that documentary evidence8 indicates that a number of organizations who specialize in reporting on the persecutory activities of the Chinese Government in limiting religious freedom, have offices in Shanghai. The US Department of State maintains a consular office in Shanghai and has consistently urged the government to expand the scope of religious freedom in keeping with the rights codified in the constitution and internationally recognized norms. The panel explained to the claimant that an event such as she alleges, would certainly be of paramount importance to groups who strive to monitor religious persecution and would be readily reported. [12] The panel finds the lack of any documentation to substantiate any aspect of the claimant's allegations in her claim for refugee protection causes the panel to have a degree of apprehension. The claimant has provided oral testimony about an alleged incident, which often relies on information that the claimant indicated as having passed through a number of individuals prior to relating her version of events. I am also aware that the testimony of the claimant under oath is presumed to be truthful unless there are reasons to doubt it. In this case, I have reviewed several reasons to doubt the truthfulness and thus the credibility of the claimant. [13] The Federal Court of Appeal held in Orelien9 that "[O]ne cannot be satisfied that the evidence is credible or trustworthy, unless satisfied that it is probably so, not just possibly so." Therefore, findings of fact, as well as the determination as to whether the claimant's evidence is credible, are made on a balance of probabilities. A witness' testimony does not have to be accepted simply because it was not contradicted at the hearing. The Refugee Protection Division is entitled to make reasonable findings based on implausibility, common sense and rationality, and may reject evidence if it is not consistent with the probabilities affecting the case as a whole.10 The British Columbia Court of Appeal stated in Faryna;11 "In short, the real test of the truth of the story of a witness in such a case must be its harmony with the preponderance of the probabilities which a practical and informed person would readily recognize as reasonable in that place and in those conditions." [14] In any event, the claimant has not provided any documentary evidence to support allegations that she is being pursued by the PSB or that members of her house church were arrested. The sole basis for her allegation is her testimony and the information contained in her PIF narrative. In this regard, the panel draws a negative inference. Furthermore, the evidence of the claimant is contrary to the documentary evidence of the situation in Shanghai. Based on the foregoing cumulative negative credibility findings and negative inferences drawn above, the panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant's testimony that she is being pursued by the PSB is not credible. And further, based on the foregoing findings and negative inferences and in light of the lack of persuasive evidence that the members of the claimant's house church were arrested by the PSB and were incarcerated, and in the context of the analysis of the Situation of House Church Christians in Shanghai to follow, the panel finds on a balance of probabilities that the claimant's testimony in this regard is not credible. [15] While it is difficult to make a judgment regarding the genuineness of a person's religious practice it is necessary in this case. In doing so, the panel considered the totality of the evidence available. Although it is true the claimant does possess some knowledge of Christianity; that knowledge does not necessarily mean she is a genuine practicing Christian. The claimant produced a letter from Rev. XX and a baptismal certificate12 which indicates that the claimant was baptized in Canada. The panel accepts that the claimant is a practicing Christian in Canada. The Situation of House Church Christians in Shanghai [16] If the claimant was to return to China, the panel has considered whether there is a serious possibility that she would be persecuted for practicing Christianity in an unregistered church. The claimant is from Shanghai and has lived and worked there all of her life. [17] The Executive Secretary of the Hong Kong Christian Counsel recently stated in June 14, 2010 correspondence, that the Chinese authorities have demonstrated a high degree of tolerance to Christian activities in general and particularly towards non-registered groups.13 A recent article in The Economist14 states: House churches are small congregations who meet privately. ... House churches have an unclear status, neither banned nor fully approved of. As long as they avoid neighbourly confrontation and keep their congregations below a certain size (usually about 25), the Protestant ones are mostly tolerated. The panel notes that the article in question was dated October 2, 2008 which is viewed as substantially contemporaneous with the time period for the claimant's allegations of house church activities, is datelined as "Beijing and Shanghai", and makes extensive and specific reference to the situation of house churches in Shanghai. In response to this article, the claimant's counsel references an article from the Washington Post.15 It speaks about religious repression in China during the Beijing Olympics and China's effort to suppress contact between members of underground religious organizations and reporters from the west during China's Olympic 'showcase'. There is a reference as counsel has indicated to small house churches, but it is taken out of context. It is not necessarily specific to Shanghai. The reference to Beijing and Shanghai in this article refers to two large house church congregations, Show Wang and Wanbang (600 - 700 and 1200 members respectively), which are not representative of the average underground house church, nor the situation of the claimant. This article does not fully discredit the 'Economist' article as counsel has indicated. [18] The panel also notes that though there were arrests and incidents of persecution of Christians documented in 2006, 2007, 2008, and 2009, none of these arrests or incidents of persecution were noted as involving house church members in Shanghai. In 2005, there were reports of house church crackdowns in the provinces of Shanxi, Henan, Hubei, Jiangxi, and Jilin. In May 2005, Chinese officials coordinated raids on 100 house churches in Jilin province [northeast China], arresting approximately 600 Christians. The U.S. 2009 and 2010 International Religious Freedom Report16 specifically reviews arrests and incidents of persecution of Christians in China in 2008, 2009 and early 2010 notes arrests of Christians in Beijing, Shanghai, the XUAR, Jilin province, Hebei province, Henan province, Zhegiang province, Guangdong province, Anhui province, Hubei province, Sichuan province, Heilongjiang province, Inner Mongolia, and Shandong province. No mention is made of arrests or incidents of persecution of small house church (under 25 members) Christians in Shanghai. The panel has reviewed the most recent items17 in the exhibits covering 2008 and 2009 and 2010 and have noted arrests and incidents of persecution of Christians in Beijing, Shanghai, Shanxi province, Jilin province, Xinjiang province, Guizhou province, Hubei province, Guongdong province, Hebei province, Inner Mongolia, the XUAR, Henan province, Sichuan province, Shandong province, Zhejiang province, Anhui province, Jiangsu province, and Heilongjiang province. The recurring situation reported in Shanghai, involves the large, 1,200-member Wanbang Church, which now meets outdoors. The Public Security Bureau and SARA officials have on more than one occasion, ordered the church to stop holding services, declaring that the church was engaging in "illegal religious activities."18 This church does not fit the profile of the church attended by the claimant. As well there was the arrest of 2 church leaders and a 'Christian attorney' noted in Shanghai in 2008.19 The claimant's counsel in her post hearing submissions20 references the information found at Exhibit R/A-1, item 12.9. The statement counsel quotes from the documentary evidence references the US Department of State International Religious Freedom Report found at R/A-1 item 12.1, is very general in nature. Closer examination of this report under 'Shanghai' has been described above. In additional searching of the documentary evidence, the panel found references to a Jewish synagogue and Tibetan Buddhist monks listed as incidents pertaining to 'Shanghai'. None of these incidents fit the profile described by the claimant. Counsel additionally references the fact that Shanghai shares a border with a number of other provinces, the geographical references are not necessarily correct. As well, it is assumed that reports from various organizations refer to these regions individually because of differences in administration and perhaps culture. Similarly, it would be a mistake to state that because the provinces of Ontario and Quebec share a geographic border the administration of laws and culture are the same. [19] What is clear, however, is no arrests were made of any Chinese members of underground house churches with a profile similar to that of the claimant's. The panel has reviewed all of the documentary evidence submitted and has found no persuasive, recent evidence of arrests or incidents of persecution of members of small Christian house churches in Shanghai where the claimant is from. [20] The panel has considered all of the documents submitted about the situation for Christians in China, specifically the articles and reports documenting arrests and persecution. The panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that if there were recent arrests or incidents of persecution of House Church Christian members in Shanghai, there would be some documentation of these arrests or incidents of persecution by reliable sources. [21] The panel has considered that the China Aid Association (CAA), and its president, Bob Fu, have stated in its report21 that they have not documented all cases of persecution and religious repression occurs in every province in China. However, the panel notes again that there has been no persuasive evidence of recent arrests or incidents of persecution of House Church Christians in Shanghai in any of the documentation regarding religious persecution in China. As noted previously in this document, the U.S. Department of State operates a consular office in Shanghai22, as well as, a number of other organizations that strive to inform the world of the occurrence of religious persecution have a presence in Shanghai. It is difficult to believe an egregious incident where a full congregation of underground Christians and their 'organizer' were arrested, would pass unnoticed by those organizations that work to keep us aware of these occurrences. [22] According to the U.S. Department of State International Freedom of Religion Report,23 the extent of religious freedom in China continues to vary widely within the country. That report notes that unregistered religious groups have continued to expand and that most Christian groups, the majority of which are not registered, no longer operate in strict secrecy24 and in fact carried out public activities including convening seminars, publishing materials, renting space for offices and events, and disseminating information on the Internet. Many unregistered religious groups also carried out social service work.25 In parts of the country, unregistered groups grew rapidly and authorities did not pressure them to register.26 In some areas officials did little to interfere with the worship or social service activities of unregistered churches. It is noted that at least 20 million Chinese citizens worship in official churches and that 50 to 70 million worship in unregistered churches.27 Some unregistered church groups were able to hold meetings that hundreds attended without interference. Some unregistered groups had significant membership, properties, financial resources and networks.28 The government has granted approval for an increasing number of religious books to be published and the number of Christian websites on the Internet increased.29 The panel finds that the situation in Shanghai does not reflect what is happening in many other provinces where there have been arrests and incidents of persecution of ordinary Christians. [23] The British Home Office Report notes that there are fifty to seventy million Protestant Christians in China who practice without state sanction and that only twenty million practice their religion in state approved churches.30 That report also states that prayer meetings and Bible study groups held among friends and families need not register with authorities.31 The article, "Church and State in China" by K. K. Yeo,32 states that only gatherings of believers with more than 40 people are required by law to register with the government. [24] The claimant has stated that her church group remained small (12 members) and during their gatherings, they would recite and discuss Bible passages and pray. [25] The panel has considered the claimant's description of her house church and considered its location in Shanghai, where there have been no persuasive reports of arrests or incidents of persecution of Christian members of underground house churches. The panel has carefully reviewed the documentary evidence and finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the house church the claimant attended was never raided by the authorities and consequently, the claimant is not wanted by the PSB for that reason. In assessing the documentary evidence The panel has been guided by the Federal Court decisions in Yu, Wen Qiang v. M.C.I 33 and Li, Li v. M.C.I. 34 [26] As has been discussed, the supporting documentary evidence for the claimant's home City of Shanghai indicates, the risk of persecution for practicing Christians is very low. [27] The panel recognizes that even if the evidence speaks of some harm that would qualify as serious, the Refugee Protection Division must consider whether there is a serious possibility that the harm will actually come to pass. A statute which outlaws the claimant's conduct or characteristic may be in existence, and it may provide for unconscionably severe punishment for that conduct or characteristic, but this does not necessarily mean there is a serious possibility that the punishment will be inflicted on the claimant. The Supreme Court has emphasized that, in a determination as to whether the claimant's fear is objectively well founded, the relevant factors include the laws in the claimant's homeland, together with the manner in which they are applied. In this connection, the Court cited paragraph 43 of the UNHCR Handbook.35 [28] Enforcement measures may vary from area to area within a country, and if this is the case, "the reasonableness of a fear of persecution depends, inter alia, on the practices of the relevant local authority".36 A pattern of non-enforcement might imply that there is less than a serious possibility.37 [29] The panel has considered the documentary evidence about conditions in Shanghai and the claimant's personal circumstances. The panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant would be able to practice her religion, worshiping in the Christian congregation of her choosing, if she were to return to her home in Shanghai in China and that there is not a serious possibility that she would be persecuted for doing so. [30] The panel has chosen to rely on the documentary evidence because it originates from a variety of reputable independent sources, which would reasonably be expected to be knowledgeable with respect to the situation of Christians in Shanghai. The documentary evidence is seen as reliable, probative, detailed information, so as to provide the panel with a thorough understanding of the situation of the Christian Church in Shanghai. [31] On the basis of the totality of the evidence and the cumulative findings and negative inferences noted above, the panel finds that the claimant has not satisfied her burden of establishing a serious possibility that she would be persecuted or that she would be personally subjected to a risk to her life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a danger of torture by any authority in the People's Republic of China. CONCLUSION [32] The claimant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. The Refugee Protection Division rejects her claim.
