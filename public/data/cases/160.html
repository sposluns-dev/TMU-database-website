Fung Barrister and Solicitor Conseil(s) (de la/des) personne(s) en cause Designated representative XXXX XXXX Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION INTRODUCTION [1] The claimants, XXXX XXXX (the principal Appellant), his wife, XXXX XXXX (the female Appellant), his parents, XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX, and his three minor children, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, XXXX XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX (the minor Appellants) are seeking protection, pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). [2] The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) found that the adult Appellants are excluded from refugee protection under Article 1E of the Convention.1 The panel therefore did not consider the claim by the adults against China. [3] The RPD found that the minor Appellants failed to establish that they would face a serious possibility of harm amounting to persecution for a Convention reason under section 96 of the IRPA or that, on a balance of probabilities, they would be personally subjected to any of the enumerated risks of harm under section 97 of the IRPA in their country of nationality (Venezuela). [4] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. This appeal is dismissed. ANALYSIS Determinative Issue [5] The determinative issues in this appeal are state protection and internal flight alternative (IFA) in Venezuela. Standard of Review [6] For questions of fact, law, and mixed fact and law, I have applied the standard of correctness, as per Huruglica.2 Admissibility of New Evidence [7] The Appellant has submitted new evidence for my consideration. The Appellant submits three news articles plucked from a website which alleges that the source for these articles are bonafide newspapers in Venezuela. [8] The Appellant argues that the new evidence was not available at the time of the filing of the Appellant's Record and that it is credible and material to the Appellant's appeal. The documents are relevant, as they speak to the country conditions related to the reasons behind this claim and appeal. [9] The article titled, "Chinese supermarkets are being looted while restocking, male Chinese did not pay soda fee and his motorcycle was taken" was dated March 18, 2018 on the web.3 [10] The article, "Anger! An Asian Chinese in Venezuela was strangled by a thief in his store!" bears a web date of March 20, 2018.4 [11] The article, "Chinese businesses have become targets again! The mayor 'extorted' 10% of the merchant's goods!" is web dated February 7, 2018.5 [12] Counsel argues that, while the articles speak to issues that Chinese people in Venezuela have been facing since before the date of their RPD hearing and decision, the new evidence reliably establishes an ongoing pattern of discrimination amounting to persecution aimed at Chinese people in Venezuela. [13] Upon review of the three articles, it is clear, as counsel has argued, that these articles are reprints of articles published in Venezuelan newspapers previous to the publication on the web. This raises an issue under subsection 110(4) about the newness of the articles. As there is no information adduced as to when the articles were originally produced in the newspapers and as counsel has written that the articles speak to issues that Chinese people in Venezuela have been facing since before the RPD hearing and decision, I am unable to find that the actual content of these articles is new. [14] Subsection 110(4) does not provide an opportunity for Appellants to complete a deficient record submitted to the RPD; rather, it allows for the correction of errors of fact, errors in law, or mixed errors of fact and law.6 [15] In a recent Federal Court decision,7 the Hon. Justice Annis wrote: I agree with the RAD that the Applicant must put her best foot forward before the RPD, and present all the evidence that is available at the time, whether aware of it or not, unless there is an aspect of injustice arising from unexpected new facts, or old facts that no reasonable amount of due diligence could have turned up. It is not intended to be a tune-up procedure for the RAD that upon learning in (of) deficiencies in the Applicant's case, additional evidence that could have been presented to the RPD may be presented as new evidence before the RAD. [16] In this particular case, I have no way of confirming the date of the original publications. The onus is squarely on the Appellant to show how his proposed new evidence meets the statutory requirements for admission. He has failed to do so. [17] This evidence is therefore rejected. Oral hearing [18] As there is no request for an oral hearing and I see no reason to hold one, there will be no oral hearing on this matter. Arguments [19] Counsel argues that the panel erred in its conclusion that the Appellants do not face more than a mere chance of persecution on the basis of their race. Counsel points out that the principal Appellant detailed several incidents specifically aimed toward him or the people around him in which the principal Appellant's Chinese ethnicity was an issue. Attackers allegedly referred to the principal Appellant and his family as "stinky Chinamen" or "Chinese Pig".8 [20] In Ward,9 the Supreme Court of Canada spoke on the meaning of the term "persecution" as having been ascribed the meaning of "sustained or systemic violation of basic human rights demonstrative of a failure of state protection";... [21] As counsel points out, the panel wrote the following: [33] The claimants themselves did not provide any concrete examples of instances where they have been refused public services or medical care, and the parents of the minors did not alleged [sic] that they would not have had opportunity to send their children to publicly-funded schools. Although the growing social hostility and verbal insults and the psychological pressure which the claimants have described experiencing in the past is unfortunate, especially for their children, the panel finds that such treatment in the future would not amount to persecution.10 [22] Counsel argues that the panel's statement in paragraph 34, which was that the Appellants, "also did not provide documentary evidence to support their contention that they had to pay bribes at a higher rate than non-Chinese business operators" is a reviewable error because it would be unreasonable to expect the Appellants to adduce evidence that they had to pay bribes, as it would be unreasonable to expect such illicit activity as demanding bribes to result in any kind of a paper trail which would adduce such evidence. [23] I concur with counsel that such a requirement is unreasonable; however, that being said, it does not, of its own accord, affect the outcome of this appeal. [24] Counsel further argues that the panel erred in refusing to believe that the Appellants were denied public services, which would be forthcoming to non-Chinese Venezuelans, simply because the Appellants were unable to provide examples of being denied medical care. Counsel submits that, as the Appellants have personally experienced differential treatment from the police, which is a public service, on the basis of their ethnicity, such treatment evinces that Chinese people do not have meaningful access to police services in Venezuela. [25] The issue referred to above, however, is not necessarily evidence of a lack of meaningful access to the police. It is evidence of a lack of proper response to the Appellants' complaints by a couple of racist individuals on the police department. Further, as per the principal Appellant's own testimony, the police did respond to his complaint of the damage done to his business on XXXX XXXX, 2017 by looters. He testified that the police did attend the crime scene and chased away the looters. The police took a proper police report11 from the principal Appellant, which the principal Appellant has submitted as evidence. This is evidence of state protection and therefore evidence of the accessibility of police services to the Appellants. [26] In regard to the other incident mentioned above, it is quite possible that the Appellants ran into a racist police officer who did not do his or her job due to the Appellants' ethnicity. That being the case, however, it is incumbent upon the Appellants that they take the matter up with a higher authority such as the commander of the station at which the principal Appellant was insulted. One or two racist policemen does not rebut state protection. [27] I must consider just what it is that the Appellant must do in order to access state protection. A claimant must show that he or she has taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.12 In other words, if the frontline police officer did nothing regarding the case brought to the police, or in some way disrespects the plaintiff, the onus rests with the plaintiff (or in this case the Appellants) to seek redress from a higher authority, such as the detachment commander or the Chief of Police, and so on. There is insufficient evidence to indicate that these Appellants sought out help from a higher authority. Consequently, the Appellants have not taken all reasonable steps to seek protection. [28] In the absence of a compelling explanation, where the state is a functioning democracy with willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim.13 This is so even where the protective services of the state have gaps or deficiencies.14 It is not sufficient to rely solely on documentary evidence listing certain flaws or weaknesses in the country's justice system.15 Further, local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection.16 The Appellants have not shown that there is a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection. In fact, evidence adduced by the Appellants at their hearing indicates that Chinese are being targeted but only as a part of a much broader and diverse community also being targeted. The article, in part, reads: Yesterday, December 16, is the darkest day for the Chinese community in Venezuela in 2016, according to the security reminder issued by the Chinese Embassy in Venezuela, local time on the 16th, stating that Venezuela Bolivar, Sucre, Zulia, Barinas and other places have suffered from riots and looting of local supermarkets, shops were robbed on a large scale, including a number of shops owned by overseas Chinese, as of now there are no casualties.17 [29] The Appellants' own evidence indicates that it is not only Chinese businesses being attacked but others as well. In fact, the article from which the Appellants' web-based article came was interesting, as it indicated the President of the local Chamber of Commerce, Austerio Gonzalez, estimated that about 80% of stores selling food were looted. This is a strong indicator that the looting and destruction of shops, such as the principal Appellant's, although may have included racial slurs, were not racially motivated. [30] In regard to counsel's argument that the panel erred in its finding above, I cannot concur. The Appellants went to the police. The police took a report and followed that up with a site visit. The looting took place during a riot with about 20 of those rioters entering the Appellant's shop and causing severe damage. The Appellants were unable to identify any of the perpetrators to the police. Further, two days after the looting, the Appellants bought tickets to the USA. One month later, the Appellants left the country, via XXXX. It is not reasonable for the Appellants to expect the police to solve the looting issue in a month, especially given the fact that there were no IDs made of the perpetrators. There is insufficient evidence to indicate that the police either refused to do the work needed or were giving the Appellants different or substandard service to that which was due the two Appellants. As the Appellants could give no examples other than the police service issue of them being denied public services, I find that the Appellants have not generally been denied public services and are therefore not being persecuted by the state. [31] Counsel argues that the panel mischaracterized the Appellants' evidence and downplayed the Appellants' fears of ethnic persecution. Counsel refers to the principal Appellant's narrative in which the principal Appellant stated that Chinese people are seen as outsiders and are treated worse than the rest of the Spanish-speaking Venezuelan population. [32] I have scanned the documents in the National Documentation Package (NDP) and can find no references to Chinese in Venezuela being treated, generally, different than others. I have found that indigenous people, Jewish people and Afro-Venezuelans are discriminated against according to the NDP.18 [33] I have also found within the NDP that Venezuela has one of the most violent populations in the world and that violence may be directed in many directions. The NDP tells of the general violence of Venezuela and indicates that the violence is fairly random. I quote: Violent crime is the greatest threat in Caracas, affecting local Venezuelans and foreigners alike. Venezuelan NGO Observatory of Violence (OVV) listed Venezuela as the second most murderous nation after El Salvador. The OVV has tracked violence through police sources and media reporting. In its annual report, OVV stated that Venezuela had over 26,616 homicides in 2017, a rate of 89 per 100,000 inhabitants. This number is down from OVV's reported tally of 28,479 homicides in 2016, and according to OVV one potential factor for this decline was the regime's widespread deployment of security forces to counter (and prevent) protest activity between April and August, with the assumption being that an increased security profile may have tangentially diminished criminal activity in certain areas.19 [34] Venezuela is a violent and crime-ridden country, but crime is generally not specific against Chinese people. There simply is insufficient evidence to support that allegation. It is not unusual for someone to believe that a crime perpetrated against him may be committed for reasons other than the greed of the criminal. There are certainly incidences in which a person has been attacked simply because of his race and that may have occurred to the Appellants, herein. However, being a victim of a racially motivated crime, once or even twice, does not translate to persecution. As I have previously written, in Ward,20 the Supreme Court of Canada spoke on the meaning of the term "persecution" as having been ascribed the meaning of "sustained or systemic violation of basic human rights demonstrative of a failure of state protection;" that sustained or systematic violation of basic human rights, in this case has not been shown to have occurred, nor has it been shown as being likely to occur in the future. I find that the panel committed no error on this issue. [35] Counsel argues that the panel erred by failing to give weight to a notice issued by the Chinese Embassy in Venezuela warning Chinese people there to be careful of their safety. [36] This notice is no different in context than one issues by many countries in the world including the U.S.A. For example, an excerpt for the U.S. Department of State report "Venezuela 2018 Crime & Safety Report" begins with the following: According to the current U.S. Department of State Travel Advisory at the date of this report's publication, Venezuela has been assessed as Level 3: reconsider travel. Overall Crime and Safety Situation U.S. Embassy Caracas does not assume responsibility for the professional ability or integrity of the persons or firms appearing in this report. The ACS Unit cannot recommend a particular individual or location and assumes no responsibility for the quality of service provided. The U.S. Department of State has assessed Caracas as being a CRITICAL-threat location for crime directed at or affecting official U.S. government interests.21 [37] As in the Chinese Embassy warning, the U.S. issues general safety warnings for the benefit of its citizens who may be living or visiting Venezuela. The report certainly does not mean that American citizens are treated any differently than Venezuelans. It simply is warning its citizens of the prevalence of crime in Venezuela and advising those citizens accordingly. It is for this reason that I give little weight at all to the Chinese Embassy warning in regard to proving persecution of Chinese people in Venezuela. [38] Counsel argues that the instances of discrimination suffered by the principal Appellant and his family constitute persecution. Counsel bases that opinion on recitation of case law in which the Federal Court of Appeal has held that discrimination suffered by an applicant can amount to persecution "where it is serious or systematic enough to be characterized as persecution".22 Counsel argues that the principal Appellant has described repeated examples of ethnically motivated harassment suffered by ethnic Chinese at the hands of Venezuelan authorities and that these events should be seen collectively. [39] I see only that the Appellants, according to their testimony, have suffered some discrimination in Venezuela. What I do not see is systemic and sustained violation of the Appellants' basic human rights, as per Ward. [40] It is my finding that the Appellants have failed to convince me that they have suffered or are likely to suffer persecution in Venezuela but are merely victims of crime and, in one case with a police officer, of discrimination. [41] Counsel argues that the panel erred in its finding that the adult Appellants all have status in Venezuela equal to or similar to that of citizens and therefore are excluded from protection under Article 1E. [42] The panel excluded the adult Appellants due to their status in Venezuela. After finding that the adult Appellants would be excluded, the panel went on to assess their claim against Venezuela but not China. [43] Counsel argues that the finding of exclusion is in error, as the principal Appellant has testified that getting his status in Venezuela renewed every five years is not guaranteed. In the past, it has been necessary for the Appellants to pay a bribe to the officials in order to receive the renewal. [44] Although the panel seemed to accept that bribery was necessary, the panel found that, based on the family income of the Appellants, paying a bribe was not a major issue. Counsel argues that the family's financial status has changed substantially due to the loss of their business. Without that income, it may not be possible for the principal Appellant to pay the necessary bribes. [45] I refer back to Zeng, as did the panel in regard to exclusion under 1F. The Court said: Considering all relevant factors to the date of the hearing, does the claimant have status, substantially similar to that of its nationals, in the third country? If the answer is yes, the claimant is excluded. If the answer is no, the next question is whether the claimant previously had such status and lost it, or had access to such status and failed to acquire it. If the answer is no, the claimant is not excluded under Article 1E. If the answer is yes, the RPD must consider and balance various factors. These include, but are not limited to, the reason for the loss of status (voluntary or involuntary), whether the claimant could return to the third country, the risk the claimant would face in the home country, Canada's international obligations, and any other relevant facts.23 [46] In this case, the answer to question one (above) is yes. Therefore, the adult Appellants are excluded. In spite of the principal Appellant's testimony that he must pay a bribe in order to renew his status every five years and that he will not now be able to afford said bribe because of the loss of his business, there appears nothing preventing the Appellants, upon their return to Venezuela, from getting jobs and earning what they need. As I have found that the adult Appellants are not facing persecution for any Convention reason, in Venezuela, the exclusion under Article 1E stands. [47] As I have found that the adult Appellants are excluded under Article 1E and that they do not face more than a mere possibility of Convention related risk in Venezuela, there is no need for me to consider the claim against China. [48] In regard to the minor children, as their claims and appeal were entirely dependent on the claims and appeal of the adult Appellants, then the minor Appellants claims must fail also. Summary [49] The Appellants have failed to rebut the presumption of state protection, have failed to convince me that they have suffered or will suffer persecution based on any Convention ground, and the adult Appellants have been excluded under Article 1E of the Convention. Disposition [50] The RAD has found that the RPD's decision will stand. CONCLUSION [51] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. This appeal is dismissed.
