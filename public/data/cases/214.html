These are the reasons I reject the refugee claim of XXXXX XXXXX, a citizen of Ukraine. ALLEGATIONS The claimant alleges that due to his Jewish ethnicity, he suffered persecution and discrimination in his home area of the Ukraine (XXXXX district, XXXXX territory). He outlines in his Personal Information Form (PIF)1 various incidents and how in his home area the police and other authorities provided little or inadequate help. After the collapse of the Soviet Union, the claimant alleges that the situation for Jews became worse. Specifically, the claimant fears nationalist groups such as UNA-UNSO. He also testified that he feared "average people". DETERMINATION I am satisfied that both requirements for a viable International Flight Alternative (IFA) are met in this case. I reject the claim. ANALYSIS In my questioning, counsel objected to questions that clearly were exploring the possibility of Kiev being an acceptable IFA. Counsel asked that I step down since he believed I had already decided the matter before hearing the evidence. In addition, counsel submitted that IFA had not been identified on the screening form nor had I identified IFA as an issued before asking my questions. I did not accept this implied application of bias. I gave reasons during the hearing. At the beginning of the hearing, I was clear that it was my intention to ask my questions first and at the end of my questions identify what issues that still needed to be resolved in order to accept the claim. This process I have used whenever I questioned first. Often issues that are identified on the screening form or issue with which I have concerns are resolved during my questioning. The issues I identify at the end of my questioning are the only issues counsel needs address. Counsel's position that the possibility of an IFA should not be explored without it being identified in advance I do not accept. The simple act of questioning as to the two components of a viable IFA clearly identifies IFA as a possible issue and in this case after my questioning. I further identified this as the determinative issue in this matter. Counsel raised a further point that since IFA and the city being considered was not raised before the hearing, he had not had the opportunity to do proper preparation. I agree in this case where IFA for this type of claim has not been a common approach of the Board. As a result, I allowed counsel 28 days to make additional submissions and further disclosure on this matter. I have entered counsel disclosure of June 6, 2006 consisting of four items as Exhibit C-4. Counsel also raised that since I did not identify Discrimination vs. Persecution as an issue that the possibility that the claimant could face simple discrimination in the possible IFA would be sufficient to have the panel reject that location as a viable IFA. I do not accept this submission. The burden that must be met for acceptance pursuant to section 96 is that there is a serious possibility the claimant will face persecution in the identified IFA. Persecution has been well defined in numerous decisions. It is not necessary in every hearing with experienced counsel to formally announce such a trite point. To rebit Kiev as a viable IFA, I believe the claimant needs to establish that there is a serious possibility that he will face persecution in Kiev. Persecution can be serious harm and/or discrimination that cumulatively will amount to a violation of a core human right or, in the alternative, establish that Kiev would not be a reasonable location for the claimant. Is Kiev a viable IFA? As to the safety of Kiev for Jewish citizens of Ukraine, I examined the documentary evidence of people similarly situated to the claimant. Item 1 of Exhibit C-4 reports an attack on a Jewish student on the metro system of Kiev. The report outlines arrests and an investigation. The charges are hooliganism as opposed to hate crime. Even so, it is clear from this report that the authorities did take action and did lay charges. Item 2 reports on an anti-Semitic leaflet being posted on a synagogue door. The article states that the marginal political party the author claims to represent denies any association with production of the leaflet. Hence, I am satisfied that these statements represent the views of the author and not the views or even a marginal party. Item 3 is a report of the beating of a newspaper editor. It appears that this is an anti-Semitic act. The report does state the police are investigating the incident. Again, there is no evidence that anti-Semitic acts occur with impunity. Item 4 reports the arrest of a person who attacked a rabbi. The report ends by stating there has been an increase in the number of violent anti-Semitic acts in the Ukraine over the last year. Exhibit C-3 includes three articles that, when considered together, support there is an anti-Semitic undertone in the Ukraine in general and Kiev specifically. However, these articles ask for investigations or consideration by the authorities concerning the actions of persons who are fueling anti-Semitism in the Ukraine. There is insufficient evidence when I read Exhibits C-3 and C-4 in their entirety to find the state of Ukraine condones acts of anti-Semitism or that persons openly conduct the distribution of hate materials. I simply note that the authors of at least two of the materials chose to remain anonymous. These are not the actions of persons who believe that there will not be repercussions if their actions were open and if they used their names. The documents in Exhibit R-1 further support this position. Exhibit R-1, Item 2.1, US DOS Report 2005 states "government's human rights performance significantly improved in important areas ..." but identifies anti-Semitic acts as one area where human rights problems were reported. Section 5 outlines: * the Minister of the Internal Affairs is responsible for the police; * the ongoing problems with traffic police; * evidence of judiciary action against law enforcement officers. Page 9, Section 2, part A reports the law provides for freedom of speech. Page 12, Part C, states the government respected the law providing for freedom of religion. Page 15, in the last three complete paragraphs, refers to the Jewish community specifically: * as of 2001 more than 100,000 citizens were identified as Jewish; * anti-Semitism acts are reported; * skinheads attacked 13 students of a day school. Police were reported investigating; * an arrest of three charged with hooliganism as a result of the attack of two students in Kiev; * the September attack on a rabbi and his son and the actions of the police in charging two with holliganism * problems with police investigations of attacks on synagogues and cemeteries due to lack of evidence; * an instance of a successful conviction for vandalizing gravestones is reported. Page 16 outlines issues involving anti-Semitic acts not dissimilar to those found in Counsel's disclosures.2 A politician was expelled from his political party as a result of an anti-Semitic speech. Other actions resulted in no official actions. President Yushchenko condemned an organization responsible for the majority of anti-Semitic publications. Human Rights Watch Ukraine,3 Item 2.4 reports that in some of the incidents reported in the US DOS the police refused to acknowledge these attacks were the result of anti-Semitism. Item 13, B5, reports the conviction and a four-year sentence as a result of the attack on a synagogue in 2002. Item 13B.6 reported mixed statements by Jewish observers in the Ukraine as regards to the level of anti-Semitism. Likewise, Item 13B.7 is mixed. A review of the documents, both those disclosed by the Board and those by counsel, satisfy me that acts of anti-Semitism do occur in the Ukraine and in the capital. However, I am not satisfied these acts are condoned by authorities. In fact, the preponderance of the evidence supports that when the police have sufficient evidence, arrests are made and charges are laid. It may be that usually the charges are for criminal hooliganism as opposed to the more serious charge of hate crime, but nonetheless charges are laid. Acts of Anti-Semitism are condemned by all major political parties, including the President. Even though the claimant alleges in his home area the authorities refused to take action to provide assistance in the past, I am satisfied that if the claimant was to relocate to Kiev that in the capital the authorities are making serious efforts to provide adequate, although not always perfect, protection for its Jewish citizens. On this basis, I am satisfied that one requirement for Kiev to be a viable IFA has been met. Concerning the second requirement that Kiev be reasonable for the claimant, I note the claimant's testimony on this point was the following: * that he would need a place to stay in Kiev and apartments are expensive; * he would need to find employment in Kiev; * acts of vandalism occur in Kiev and the police do not do anything; and * life is not good in Kiev. Counsel submitted that country conditions supporting Kiev was the same as the rest of the Ukraine. The documentary evidence states in numerous places that the old registration system has been replaced and citizens are free to move within the country.4 The claimant has not always been employed in the Ukraine;5 however, there is insufficient evidence to support that a Jewish citizen would have more problems obtaining employment in Kiev than in any other area of Ukraine. Counsel's submission concerning accommodation is not supported by any document in evidence. The evidence satisfies me there is a viable Jewish community in Kiev, and hence the claimant would be able to practise his religion if he wished. A review of the evidence satisfies me that Kiev is reasonable for the claimant in all the circumstances. Hence, Kiev, meets both requirements for a viable IFA. To be accepted as a protected person, the claimant must establish there is a serious possibility he will be persecuted or face other forms of serious harm in all parts of his country of origin. The evidence supports that at least in Kiev this is not the case and further in Kiev there is adequate, although not perfect, efforts being made by the state to provide protection for persons such as the claimant. As a result, the Refugee Protection Division rejects the claim pursuant to both sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act.
