LAW 1. Amendments [14] Generally, amendments will be granted liberally to ensure that the real issues between the parties are identified and tried: Kapila v. Merit Interior Designs (Duncan), 2023 BCSC 1076 at para 37. [15] As set out by Justice G.P. Weatherill in Peterson v. 446690 B.C. Ltd., 2014 BCSC 1531 at para 37: [37] Finally, the general principles arising on an application to amend pleading can be summarized as follows: (a) Amendment to pleadings ought to be allowed unless pleadings fail to disclose a cause of action or defence: McNaughton v. Baker, [1988] 24 B.C.L.R. (2nd) 17. (b) Amendments are usually permitted to determine the issues between the parties and ought to be allowed unless it would cause prejudice to party's ability to defend an action: Levi v. Petaquilla Minerals Ltd., 2012 BCSC 776. (c) The party resisting an amendment must prove prejudice to preclude an amendment, and mere, potential prejudice is insufficient to preclude an amendment: Jones v. Lululemon Athletica Inc. 2008 BCSC 719. (d) Costs are the general means of protecting against prejudice unless it would be a wholly inadequate remedy. (e) Courts should only disallow an amendment as a last resort: Jones, McNaughton, Innoventure S & K Holdings Ltd. et al. v. Innoventure (Tri-Cities) Holdings Ltd. et al., 2006 BCSC 1567. [16] In Jones v. Bank of Nova Scotia, 2018 BCCA 381 at para 35, the Court of Appeal sets out factors that a chambers judge should consider when determining whether to permit amendments of pleadings that are allegedly bound to fail: a) the degree to which the pleadings are deficient; b) the extent to which the deficiencies may be addressed by an obvious or straightforward amendment to the pleadings; c) the apparent merit of the claim; and d) prejudice by dismissal of the claim. [17] A court will refuse to grant an amendment if the proposed pleading does not disclose a reasonable cause of action: Argo Ventures Inc. v. Choi, 2019 BCSC 86 at para 7. Nor will a court permit useless amendments: One West Holdings Ltd. v. The Owners, Strata Plan LMS2995, 2019 BCSC 707 at para 76. 2. Striking pleadings [18] Rule 9‑5(1) sets out grounds to strike a pleading: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [19] The test for allowing amendments is the same as that for striking pleadings pursuant to Rule 9-5(1)(a): Health Sciences Association of British Columbia v. Hewitt Associates Corp., 2019 BCSC 208 at paras 67-68. [20] Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 SCR 959 at 980 states with respect to Rule 19(24)(a), the predecessor of Rule 9-5(1)(a): Thus, the test in Canada governing the application of provisions like Rule 19(24)(a) of the British Columbia Rules of Court is the same as the one that governs an application under R.S.C. O. 18, r. 19: assuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it "plain and obvious" that the plaintiff's statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action? As in England, if there is a chance that the plaintiff might succeed, then the plaintiff should not be "driven from the judgment seat". Neither the length and complexity of the issues, the novelty of the cause of action, nor the potential for the defendant to present a strong defence should prevent the plaintiff from proceeding with his or her case. Only if the action is certain to fail because it contains a radical defect ranking with the others listed in Rule 19(24) of the British Columbia Rules of Court should the relevant portions of a plaintiff's statement of claim be struck out under Rule 19(24)(a). [emphasis added] [21] Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, (1999) 36 CPC (4th) 266, 1999 CanLII 5860 (BCSC) ["CFAR"], sets out principles governing subsections (b) and (c): [45] To succeed on an application under Rule 19(24) (b) or (c) it must be established that it is "plain and obvious" that the pleading offends either or both provisions. [46] The authorities provide little guidance as to what constitutes pleadings that are "unnecessary", "scandalous", "frivolous" or "embarrassing". However some principles do emerge. [47] Irrelevancy and embarrassment are both established when pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is being pleaded . . . An "embarrassing" and "scandalous" pleading is one that is so irrelevant that it will involve the parties in useless expense and will prejudice the trial of the action by involving them in a dispute apart from the issues . . . An allegation which is scandalous will not be struck if it is relevant to the proceedings. It will only be struck if irrelevant as well as scandalous . . . A pleading is "unnecessary" or "vexatious" if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or does not advance any claim known in law . . . A pleading that is superfluous will not be struck out if it is not necessarily unnecessary or otherwise objectionable . . . A pleading is "frivolous" if it is obviously unsustainable, not in the sense that it lacks an evidentiary basis, but because of the doctrine of estoppel . . . [authorities omitted] [22] CFAR also sets out principles governing subsection (d): [52] The ambit of abuse of process is very broad. Abuse of process may be found where proceedings involve a deception of the court or constitute a mere sham; where process of the court is not being fairly or honestly used, or is employed for some ulterior or improper purpose; proceedings which are without foundation or serve no useful purpose: Babavic v. Babowech (3 September 1993) Vancouver Registry C931968 (B.C.S.C.). In the case at bar, I am unable to find that the plaintiff's pleadings in paragraphs 9 and 10 of the Statement of Claim are vexatious and without merit, brought with the sole motive and intent to harass the defendants and to interfere with their ability to defend the action. [23] In R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42, the Supreme Court of Canada explained the important housekeeping function provided by civil procedure strike provisions: [20] This promotes two goods -- efficiency in the conduct of the litigation and correct results. Striking out claims that have no reasonable prospect of success promotes litigation efficiency, reducing time and cost. The litigants can focus on serious claims, without devoting days and sometimes weeks of evidence and argument to claims that are in any event hopeless. The same applies to judges and juries, whose attention is focused where it should be -- on claims that have a reasonable chance of success. The efficiency gained by weeding out unmeritorious claims in turn contributes to better justice. The more the evidence and arguments are trained on the real issues, the more likely it is that the trial process will successfully come to grips with the parties' respective positions on those issues and the merits of the case. [emphasis added] 3. Pleadings [24] Rule 3-1(2) sets out the requirements of a notice of civil claim: Contents of notice of civil claim (2) A notice of civil claim must do the following: (a) set out a concise statement of the material facts giving rise to the claim; (b) set out the relief sought by the plaintiff against each named defendant; (c) set out a concise summary of the legal basis for the relief sought; (d) set out the proposed place of trial; (e) if the plaintiff sues or a defendant is sued in a representative capacity, show in what capacity the plaintiff sues or the defendant is sued; (f) provide the data collection information required in the appendix to the form; (g) otherwise comply with Rule 3-7. [25] Courts have emphasised the need to be clear and concise in drafting a notice of civil claim, in reference to Rule 3-1(2). In Sahyoun v. Ho, 2013 BCSC 1143 at para 23, Justice Voith, before his translation to the Court of Appeal, stated that "brevity and lucidity are important" in meeting the basic requirements of a notice of civil claim. [26] The leading case on pleadings requirements was also authored by Voith JA, by then on the Court of Appeal: Mercantile Office Systems Private Ltd. v. Worldwide Warranty Life Services Inc., 2021 BCCA 362. There, Voith JA reminds us that pleadings are not a free-for-all forum for larding on evidence and argument: [21] Pleadings are foundational. They guide the litigation process . . . . . . [23] For the court, pleadings serve the ultimate function of defining the issues of fact and law that will be determined by the court. In order for the court to fairly decide the issues before them, the pleadings must state the material facts succinctly. . . . They must be organized in such a way that the court can understand what issues the court will be called upon to decide. . . . . . . [44] Nevertheless, none of a notice of claim, a response to civil claim, and a counterclaim is a story. Each pleading contemplates and requires a reasonably disciplined exercise that is governed, in many instances in mandatory terms, by the Rules and the relevant authorities. Each requires the drafting party to "concisely" set out the "material facts" that give rise to the claim or that relate to the matters raised by the claim. None of these pleadings are permitted to contain evidence or argument. [45] What constitutes a material fact is well understood. Material facts are the elements essential to formulate a claim or a defence. In Danyluk v. Ainsworth Technologies Inc, 2001 SCC 44, Justice Binnie said: [54] A cause of action has traditionally been defined as comprising every fact which it would be necessary for the plaintiff to prove, if disputed, in order to support his or her right to the judgment of the court . Establishing each such fact (sometimes referred to as material facts) constitutes a precondition to success. . . . [49] The judge's assertion that a pleading can contain the evidence, as opposed to the material facts, that a party may be entitled to establish at trial is an error in principle. . . . . . . [52] Such evidence and arguments are unrelated to the material facts Warranty Life was required to plead and have no place in the Response. . . . [58] I am of the view that the Response and Counterclaim suffer from the numerous and pervasive difficulties that I have described. These difficulties cause the Response and Counterclaim to be prolix and both confusing and inconsistent in various respects. They offend various mandatory requirements of the Rules and they frustrate the important objects that are served by proper pleadings. [emphasis added; some authorities omitted] [27] Kindylides v. Does, 2020 BCCA 330, upheld this court's striking of a claim that failed to plead the material facts necessary to support its causes of action. The Court provides a useful definition of necessary material facts: [32] Much of what is contained in the NOCC does not constitute "material facts" as defined in Young v. Borzoni et al, 2007 BCCA 16 [Young], citing Delaney & Friends Cartoon Productions Ltd. v. Radical Entertainment Inc. et al, 2005 BCSC 371: [20] "[facts] that [are] essential in order to formulate a complete cause of action. If a material fact is omitted, a cause of action is not effectively pleaded." [33] Rather the NOCC contains statements which, as pled, are incapable of proof and/or are based on speculation or assumptions. Such statements are not material facts: Samuda v. Knight, 2019 BCSC 133 at paras. 22-24; Imperial Tobacco at para. 22; Young at paras. 30-32. [emphasis added] [28] Bald assertions or conclusions of law are not material facts: Kindylides at para 34, quoting Owimar v. Stewart, 2019 BCSC 1198. The pleadings must be looked at as a whole to ensure that the pleadings define the issues with sufficient precision to make the pretrial and trial proceedings both manageable and fair: [34] I agree with the observations of MacDonald J. (in Chambers) in Owimar v. Stewart, 2019 BCSC 1198 when she stated: [19] Care must be taken to avoid undermining the role of pleadings in identifying the issues by allowing a party to plead bald assertions or mere conclusory statements of law. A bald assertion of a conclusion is not a pleading of material fact: [18] There is no bright line between material facts and bald allegations, nor between pleadings of material facts and the prohibition on pleading of evidence. They are points on a continuum, and it is the responsibility of a motions judge, looking at the pleadings as a whole, to ensure that the pleadings define the issues with sufficient precision to make the pre-trial and trial proceedings both manageable and fair. [19] What constitutes a material fact is determined in light of the cause of action and the damages sought to be recovered. The plaintiff must plead, in summary form but with sufficient detail, the constituent elements of each cause of action or legal ground raised. The pleading must tell the defendant who, when, where, how and what gave rise to its liability. Mancuso v. Canada (Minister of National Health and Welfare), 2015 FCA 227 at paras. 18-19 & 27. [emphasis added] [29] Similarly, a claim may be struck if it does not "set out a concise statement of the material facts giving rise to the claim", as required by Rule 3-1(2)(a). D. DISCUSSION AND DECISION [30] The plaintiff argues that the Caldwell J order allowing the plaintiff to attempt to present a properly pleaded amended claim is a complete answer to the defendants' strike application. The plaintiff further argues that his purported compliance with that order is also a complete answer to the defendants' strike application, based on abuse of process and other grounds that were before Caldwell J. The defendants advanced largely the same strike arguments before Caldwell J. The fact that Caldwell J declined to strike the claim, and gave the plaintiff one last chance to amend implicitly indicated that those arguments were not persuasive then and should not be renewed now. It also implicitly indicated that if there was meticulous compliance with his direction, then the amendments could proceed and the claim could continue. [31] I reject this argument. Caldwell J appropriately followed the usual course of giving the offending litigant a chance to provide a proper pleading: the usual remedy for a pleading impugned on a strike application or a rejected application to amend. That decision obviated the need to dive deep into the other bases for the strike application. At para 14, Caldwell J expressly authorised the defendants to renew their strike application to be heard at the same time as the amendment application. This dovetails wholly with the "last chance" admonition: the plaintiff's pleadings and case were on death row. Further, this present consideration of the resultant last chance pleading adds yet another prolonging chapter in this proceeding, and provides further grounds in itself to strike the claim. [32] The plaintiff also argues that Caldwell J's "last chance" direction is obiter dicta, and not binding on this court, citing Bilin v. Sidhu, 2017 BCCA 429. I agree that I am not bound by that direction, just as no judge is wholly bound by a colleague's previous direction that an adjourned matter be heard peremptorily. I will consider these issues and applications unfettered by that prior direction of Caldwell J. That said, it would take an extraordinary change of circumstances for me to depart from the effects of a clear judicial direction, particularly one based on largely the exact same materials and arguments before me today. [33] The plaintiff points to the jurisprudence setting out the traditionally liberal approach to amendments, as well as the general approach of providing a party an opportunity to amend, rather than striking a pleading for good, without a chance to provide a properly pleaded amended claim or defence. He argues that these principles especially apply to the admittedly novel claims he advances, such as that based on what he pleads to be a relationship with the Northern Health Authority akin to employment. The plaintiff cites Sommer v. Coast Capital Savings Credit Union, 2013 BCSC 881, Cicada Ventures Ltd. v. KGHM Ajax Mining Inc., 2023 BCSC 1746, and McMillan v. McMillan, 2014 BCSC 546. [34] In the present circumstances, of course, the plaintiff has already been provided an opportunity to amend his claim in a properly pleaded form, in the face of a strike application. Justice Caldwell did not only give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend: again he did so with the unambiguous language that it was the plaintiff's "'last chance' to amend his claim to an acceptable form which properly identifies his claims and abides the Rules and law relating to pleadings": para 14. Justice Caldwell provided that final warning after reciting the various deficiencies of the plaintiff's existing and proposed amended claim. [35] If that judicial admonishment and warning was not sufficient to prompt the plaintiff to provide impeccable pleadings and particulars today, the applications and arguments of the defendants, before Caldwell J and before me, setting out in detail the manifold manifest deficiencies in the plaintiff's pleadings, themselves provide a comprehensive list of what the pleadings now before the court should and should not contain. Those arguments included case law setting out that certain advanced claims, such as those based in discrimination, bad faith, and harassment, are not torts. In striking the pleading in the Canadian Society for the Advancement of Science in Public Policy v. British Columbia, 2025 BCSC 2051 ["CSASPP"], the Court noted that the plaintiff persisted with defective pleadings even after being expressly forewarned of those deficiencies: see para 146. [36] I agree that in these remarkable circumstances, and in light of the express judicial "last chance", the proposed pleadings presented by the plaintiffs today ought to have been meticulous, in complete and unimpeachable adherence to the Rules of Court and the rules of pleadings. [37] The plaintiff argues that he has, in fact, "meticulously" complied with the Justice Caldwell order. He argues that he has adhered to the directive by: A. Pleading Material Facts: Clearly outlining the specific tortious acts, including the suppression of exculpatory evidence and the use of discriminatory bias by the Defendants' agents (Proposed Amended Claim, paras. 45, 57-60). B. Refining Legal Claims: Correctly framing the negligence claims, the statutory immunity exception for bad faith, and the intentional torts (Misfeasance in Public Office). [38] Unfortunately, the proposed second amended notice of civil claim falls miles short of this express requisite expectation of meticulous compliance with the Rules and the rules of pleadings. [39] Following the lead of Caldwell J, these reasons will not attempt to identify all of the deficiencies in the proposed pleading. [40] Roughly 85 percent of the proposed pleading is underlined, to indicate that it is brand new and not carried over from the previous pleading. At the same time, the proposed pleading contains no strike-through text or carets ("^") to indicate paragraphs and words that have been removed, in breach of Rule 6-1(3). It is accordingly impossible to discern what portions are truly new, in content or in concept. [41] As we will see later in the proceedings, not only is this lack of clarity profoundly prejudicial to the defendants, and for that matter the Court, it is ultimately prejudicial to the plaintiff himself. [42] Far from the Rule 3-1(2), Mercantile, and jurisprudential requirements of concision and brevity, the proposed pleadings are prolix and bloated. This bloat is partly attributable to the repetition of particulars and facts throughout the proposed pleadings, again out of sequence. What would appear to be a claim primarily based in negligence, breach of contract, and defamation, spans 151 paragraphs over 29 pages. It is larded with adjectives, adverbs, and superfluous prose. It is argumentative, and, indeed, resembles less a pleading than a trial argument, complete with extensive citation of case law. It is unnecessary and inappropriate to cite case law in a pleading, except where that case is shorthand for a legal principle (e.g. Rylands v Fletcher): Pyper v. The Law Society of British Columbia, 2017 BCCA 410 at para 49; British Columbia Teachers' Federation v. British Columbia, 2012 BCSC 1722, paras 14-15. [43] The narrative of events and material facts is not pleaded in sequence. The timeline jumps around, making it extremely difficult to follow. As an illustration of this pervasive fogginess, it is not even clear from the proposed pleading whether and when the plaintiff stopped working with the Health Authority. [44] The pleadings sets out various communications between the plaintiff and the Health Authority and the College. We suddenly find ourselves on Vancouver Island and then we find ourselves back in communications with the defendants. A reader of the pleadings would have grave difficulty in discerning the basic facts alleged, and the basic dates alleged, let alone how they relate to the purported claims advanced. [45] I will note a few other problems and ambiguities with respect to specific claims purportedly advanced in the proposed pleadings under various bolded headings. [46] With respect to negligence, the plaintiff advances various complaints about the defendants' conduct. The claim does not plead clearly the source of the various duties that the plaintiff claims the defendants have breached: whether they are grounded in statute, common law, contract, or otherwise. Nor does it set out the content of those duties or the content of the standard of care that the defendants apparently failed to meet. [47] Of course, it may well be that some or all of these possible foundations for the negligence claim are intended in the pleading. The pleading does not make that clear, as required, as a fundamental precept. [48] To add to the confusion, the entirety of paragraph 69, under the heading of "Negligence", reads more as a plea of an intentional tort, rather than of negligence: "[d]eliberately concealing a complaint"; "[f]ostering a toxic environment"; "[m]aliciously obstructing employment"; "[u]nlawfully threatening co-workers"; and "[p]roviding false information". These assertions would not appear to be claims in negligence, notwithstanding the heading under which they are found. Apart from defamation, no intentional tort is expressly pleaded anywhere in the proposed claim. [49] Finally, with respect to the negligence section, the plaintiff does not plead a factual basis for a finding that the Health Authority or the College may be vicariously liable for any acts or omissions caused by its members. This is not a notional failure. The pleading is provided in the face of specific well‑established jurisprudence that physicians are typically considered independent contractors of a health authority, rather than employees: Bahinipaty v. Vancouver Coastal Health Authority (Vancouver General Hospital), 2019 BCSC 2112 at paras 17-19, Cupo v. Brady, 2013 BCSC 1749 at para 6, and Shannahan v. Fraser Health Authority, 2010 BCSC 144 at paras 9-13. Where physicians are not direct employees of a hospital -- that is, employees of a health authority -- the hospital and health authority is not vicariously liable for their conduct. Accordingly, the College cannot be vicariously liable for those same physicians, even if material facts of liability had hypothetically been provided. [50] With respect to the pleadings of breach of contract, found in Part 1, paras 12-17, 46-48, and Part 3, paras 2-8, it is unclear whether the plaintiff advances a claim for breach of contract in relation to a private contract between the Health Authority and the plaintiff, or an employment contract which engages a different legal framework. The pleadings refer to several different contracts, as well as contracts "the terms of which are found in the Health Professions Act (the "HPA"), the Defendant College's Bylaws, and the common law (collectively, the 'CPSBC Contract')": Part 1, para 16. Further, at Part 1, para 12, the plaintiff asserts that the contract between himself and the Health Authority "was one of direct employment or, in the alternative, was analogous to employment." He provides no material facts to support this vague and odd pleading, which is more along the lines of an argument. [51] The plaintiff does not provide particulars of the material terms of each contract, as required, or tie those terms, or the contracts themselves, to the breaches he alleges, or the remedies he seeks. While he lists various "express and implied terms", he does not indicate the source or basis of any of those terms. In a simple contract dispute with a single written contract that provides a solid, clear, and unambiguous point of reference in the pleadings, the court may turn a blind eye to such laxity. With multiple amorphous and imprecise references to contracts involving two different defendants, it is wholly inexcusable and confusing. [52] I agree that the proposed pleading is prejudicially unclear to the defendants, who would be hard pressed to identify which contract is alleged to have been breached and how. [53] Part 1, paras 18-45 asserts a "coordinated campaign of bad faith and discrimination", while paras 78-92 asserts "bad faith, malice, and discrimination." Part 3, paras 9-14 asserts that the campaign of bad faith and discrimination breached the defendants' duty of good faith, citing Bhasin v. Hrynew, 2014 SCC 71. [54] It is well established that bad faith is not an independent cause of action. Breach of a duty of good faith does not stand alone without a breach of some other underlying duty owed to the plaintiff, be it in tort, statute, or contract: Castrillo v. Workplace Safety and Insurance Board, 2017 ONCA 121 at para 74, and Alberta v. Elder Advocates of Alberta Society, 2011 SCC 24 at para 78. [55] The pleadings of material facts in Part 1 regarding bad faith are rambling, lengthy, repetitive, and out of sequence. They are peppered with impermissible argument and evidence. In an odd and prolix manner, the particulars are repeated in two locations under the Part 1 factual basis of the pleading. [56] Those material facts are also confusing as they purport to be particulars of intentional acts of bad faith, but on several occasions refer to "negligence", or the "bad faith" actions of individuals and entities who are strangers to this action. I agree that the pleadings leave the defendants guessing at which particulars apply to the generalised pleadings of bad faith, especially given that those Part 1 assertions remain unanchored to any particular legal right. [106] I turn to some of the grounds put forward by the defendants for striking the claim and denying an amendment based on allegations that the proposed pleading is vexatious or an abuse of process. [107] Vexatious pleadings include those that do not advance any claim known to law, those brought for an improper purpose (including the harassment and oppression of other parties by multifarious and duplicative proceedings brought for purposes other than the pursuit of legitimate rights), claims that do not establish the plaintiff's pleaded causes of actions, claims where it is obvious that the action cannot succeed, and claims where the action would serve no useful purpose and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources: Owimar v. Warnett, 2018 BCSC 2310 at paras 23-24. [108] Saskatchewan (Environment) v. Métis Nation - Saskatchewan, 2025 SCC 4, a decision this year of the Supreme Court of Canada, describes the doctrine of abuse of process: [33] The doctrine of abuse of process is concerned with the administration of justice and fairness (Behn, at para. 41). The doctrine engages the inherent power of the court to prevent misuse of its proceedings in a way that would be manifestly unfair to a party or would in some way bring the administration of justice into disrepute (Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E., Local 79, 2003 SCC 63, [2003] 3 S.C.R. 77, at para. 37; Behn, at para. 39; Abrametz, at para. 33). [34] In Abrametz, this Court reiterated that abuse of process is a broad concept that applies in various contexts (para. 34, citing Toronto (City), at para. 36, and Behn, at para. 39). The Court noted that the doctrine of abuse of process is "characterized by its flexibility. It is not encumbered by specific requirements, unlike the concepts of res judicata and issue estoppel" (para. 35, citing Behn, at para. 40, and Toronto (City), at paras. 37-38). [35] One way in which an abuse of process can arise is by relitigation, that is, "where the litigation before the court is found to be in essence an attempt to relitigate a claim which the court has already determined" (Behn, at para. 40, quoting Canam Enterprises Inc. v. Coles (2000), 51 O.R. (3d) 481 (C.A.), at para. 56, rev'd 2002 SCC 63, [2002] 3 S.C.R. 307; see also Abrametz, at para. 34; and D. J. Lange, The Doctrine of Res Judicata in Canada (5th ed. 2021), at pp. 1-5). Relitigation will be an abuse of process if it violates "such principles as judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice" (Toronto (City), at para. 37; Behn, at para. 41). Where warranted, the doctrine of abuse of process can be relied on to strike pleadings so as to prevent relitigation of an issue (see Behn; Canam Enterprises Inc.). [109] In Métis Nation, the abuse of process argument was directed towards the multiplicity of proceedings filed: [38] A multiplicity of proceedings which engage the same issues can amount to an abuse of process. In the foundational case of McHenry v. Lewis (1882), 22 Ch. D. 397, Sir George Jessel observed that: ". . . it is prima facie vexatious to bring two actions where one will do" (p. 400). Examples of where a multiplicity of proceedings has amounted to an abuse of process include: where two parallel class actions involving the same parties were brought in two different jurisdictions (Englund v. Pfizer Canada Inc., 2007 SKCA 62, 284 D.L.R. (4th) 94, at paras. 38-40); where plaintiffs initiated multiple actions claiming Aboriginal and treaty rights over the same land and natural resources (Dixon v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 ABQB 565); and where the plaintiffs provided "no viable explanation" for bringing a second action that duplicated the issue of ownership of a trade name which encapsulated the original defendants (Cashin Mortgages Inc. v. 2511311 Ontario Ltd., 2024 ONCA 103, 170 O.R. (3d) 107, at para. 14). [39] However, the fact that there are two or more ongoing legal proceedings which involve the same, or similar, parties or legal issues, is in itself not sufficient for an abuse of process. As this Court recognized in Toronto (City), "[t]here may be instances where relitigation will enhance, rather than impeach, the integrity of the judicial system" (para. 52). Similarly, there may be instances where parties have a valid reason for bringing separate, but related, proceedings; in such cases, a multiplicity of proceedings can serve to enhance the administration of justice (see, e.g., Birdseye Security Inc. v. Milosevic, 2020 ONCA 355, at paras. 20-22). The inverse can also be true: pleadings do not need to be identical in order for a multiplicity of proceedings to amount to abuse of process (see, e.g., Dixon, at para. 85; Fillion v. Degen, 2005 MBCA 58, 195 Man. R. (2d) 2, at para. 23). [40] Thus, the abuse of process analysis does not end when multiple or similar proceedings exist. Rather, the analysis needs to focus on whether allowing the litigation to proceed would violate such principles as judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice, as discussed above. Where, for example, having duplicative proceedings would waste the resources of the parties, courts and witnesses, or risk inconsistent results and therefore undermine the credibility of the judicial process, this can amount to an abuse of process.
