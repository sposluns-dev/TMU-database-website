[2] The appellant was appointed as the designated representative of her minor daughter. [3] The appellant states that she fears returning to Algeria because she allegedly converted to Judaism after her husband convinced her to. [4] The six new documents attached to the appeal memorandum as new evidence are not admissible, because they do not meet the tests set out in subsection 110(4) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA) or the jurisprudential tests. [5] The appellant is asking the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) to set aside the determination of the RPD and allow her and her daughter's refugee protection claims or to refer the matter to the RPD for re-determination before another panel.1 [6] After considering the entirety of the evidence, which included listening to the recording of the hearing, I am of the opinion that the RPD rendered the correct decision in view of the evidence. [7] The appeal is dismissed. FACTUAL BACKGROUND [8] In her Basis of Claim Form (BOC Form), the appellant states that in 2017, her husband suggested that they apply for a visa to visit Canada. According to her testimony at the hearing, her husband went alone in XXXX 2017. [9] The appellant alleges that one month later, he asked her to join him with their daughter. The appellant and her daughter came to Canada near the end of XXXX 2017. After she arrived, her husband told her that he would not go back to Algeria because he wanted his children to grow up in Canada, a country governed by the rule of law. The appellant alleges that her husband began [translation] "following the Jewish religion" little by little and that after a few months, he converted into "a Jew." He convinced her to follow him in adopting that religion. [10] The appellant fears being imprisoned in Algeria because the state does not accept Jews. She also fears members of her extended family because their conversion was a dishonour to their families. [11] In XXXX 2018, the appellant returned to Algeria with her daughter. In XXXX 2018, they returned to Canada. One month later, when she had already made the decision to leave Algeria permanently,2 the appellant and her daughter returned to Algeria under the pretext that she had to close the business registration she had with her husband. [12] The appellant alleges that during that stay, rumours had circulated about her and her husband converting to Judaism. Due to the threats that she and her father received from some of her in-laws, former jihadists, she allegedly took refuge in a hotel a few days before leaving Algeria. [13] On September 24, 2018, her husband signed his BOC Form. On XXXX XXXX XXXX 2018, the appellant and her daughter arrived in Canada. The appellant signed her and her daughter's BOC Forms on February 9, 2019. [14] The appellant and her daughter's records were joined to that of her husband; however, consideration of his refugee protection claim was suspended on December 17, 2019, in accordance with subsection 103(1) of the IRPA. [15] While in Canada, the appellant found out that her brother-in-law XXXX had been threatened by members of his own family who thought that he had also converted to Judaism. Impugned decision [16] The RPD considered these refugee protection claims under section 96 of the IRPA because the allegations have a nexus to one of the five Convention grounds, namely, religion. According to the RPD, the appellant did not invoke section 97 of the IRPA, and her allegations and the evidence on the record do not support consideration under that section. [17] With regard to her religious conversion, the decision can be summarized as follows: [translation] "The panel did not find the principal claimant credible with regard to her adopting the Jewish religion." Its decision was based on the following elements: * The conversion process was not established. * The reasons that led her to adopt the Jewish religion in XXXX 2017 or XXXX 2018 lacked depth and substance. * The appellant's knowledge of Judaism was limited to mentioning some of the religion's rites and practices, and turned out to be very limited with regard to the beliefs that characterize the Jewish religion. A negative inference has been drawn from the appellant's incomplete, evasive statements about the Jewish religion. * The documents submitted include a purchase receipt for a Jewish candelabra (Menorah) and photographs of the appellant, her husband and her daughter sitting near a candelabra and a "Happy Hanukkah" balloon; however, they have little probative value and are insufficient to offset the credibility issues raised by the panel. * The appellant's behaviour as of XXXX 2018 is not that of someone who fears for her safety. Arguments on appeal [18] The arguments on appeal can be summarized as follows: * The panel assessed her knowledge of Judaism and her religious practice based on too high a standard. * The analysis of the evidence was overly demanding and the decision demonstrates that there is significant evidence directly related to her fears that was not taken into consideration for erroneous reasons. * Contrary to what is stated in the decision, the appellant did invoke subsection 97(1) of the IRPA, but her refugee protection claim was not considered under that provision.3 SCOPE OF THE APPEAL [19] In this case, I will determine whether the RPD's decision is correct. These documents are relevant because they can establish some of the Jewish religion's dogmas and concepts, and it was not foreseeable to submit them earlier.12 Test for admissibility [22] For new evidence to be accepted, it must meet the test set out in subsection 110(4) of the IRPA, which provides that: 1) the person who is the subject of the appeal may present only evidence that arose after the rejection of their claim, 2) that was not reasonably available or 3) that the person could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented, at the time of the rejection. [23] Once that test is met, the RAD must determine whether they meet the jurisprudential tests, known as the Raza13 criteria. The Federal Court of Appeal identified the factors to consider as follows: credibility, relevance, newness and materiality. With respect to the latter criterion, Justice De Montigny states in Singh that the RAD may need to make some adaptations. Documents 1 to 5 are not admissible as new evidence [24] I have reviewed these documents. They are general articles taken from websites and were published between 2012 and 2017. They explain some concepts of Judaism, such as what the Torah is, that its study is a spiritual exercise, that Jewish women are not required to attend the synagogue and that there is a degree of mysticism in the religion. [25] The appellant argues that it was not foreseeable to submit these documents, which are intended to respond to the RPD's concerns. It was submitted that their absence prompted the member to make adverse credibility findings and had an influence on the decision. [26] With all due respect, I disagree with these arguments. [27] These documents were published between 2012 and 2017 and existed at the time of the rejection of the refugee protection claims on February 28, 2020. In Singh, the Federal Court of Appeal states the following with regard to the legislative tests: "...one should not lose sight of the fact that this provision departs from the general principle according to which the RAD proceeds without a hearing, on the basis of the RPD's record (s. 110(3)) and must for that reason be narrowly interpreted." [Emphasis added] [28] These refugee protection claims are essentially based on the appellant's alleged conversion to Judaism. It was reasonable to think that, at the hearing, the appellant would be asked about the circumstances of her conversion and that the panel would attempt to ensure that it was genuine at the least. Therefore, in my opinion, it was foreseeable to submit objective documentation concerning the Jewish religion's precepts, as she is attempting to do on appeal. [29] Furthermore, as the appellant was represented by experienced counsel, I cannot understand why she did not ask for more time to produce these types of documents, which are widely available on the internet, following the panel's questions. An appeal before the RAD is not an opportunity to complete a record. [30] The appellant submitted these documents in order to establish that her testimony at the hearing was credible, as it is in line with the information contained therein, especially with regard to Jewish women not being required to attend the synagogue and the statement that [translation] "the rules and actions to be fulfilled in the Jewish religion are perceived with love and not as being constraints." [31] The fact that the new evidence corroborates information, contradicts findings of the Immigration and Refugee Board of Canada (IRB), and clarifies the evidence before the IRB does not make it new evidence.14 However, that is not all. [32] Contrary to the arguments on appeal, I do not consider that the absence of these documents before the RPD prompted the member to make adverse credibility findings or had an influence on the decision. [33] In this case, a series of factors cast doubt on the appellant's claims that she converted to Judaism or that she is in the process of converting. I am satisfied that the panel was aware that not going to a place of worship is not a reason to doubt whether a conversion is sincere and that documentary evidence is not necessary to corroborate the appellant's statements that Jewish women are not required to attend the synagogue. ANALYSIS Consideration under section 97 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act [43] The appellant submits that the RPD erred when it concluded the following in paragraph 20 of its decision: [translation] "The claimants did not invoke subsection 97(1) of the IRPA and the evidence on the record does not support its application." [44] Her argument is based on Bouaouni,17 in which the Federal Court states the following: [41] A claim under section 97 must be evaluated with respect to all the relevant considerations and with a view to the country's human rights record. ...There may well be instances where a refugee claimant, whose identity is not disputed, is found to be not credible with respect to his subjective fear of persecution, but the country conditions are such that the claimant's particular circumstances, make him/her a person in need of protection. It follows that a negative credibility determination, which may be determinative of a refugee claim under s. 96 of the Act, is not necessarily determinative of a claim under subsection 97(1) of the Act. [45] I agree with counsel that different elements are necessary to allow a claim under section 96 versus section 97, especially with regard to the subjective element, which is not part of a consideration under section 97 of the IRPA. [46] I also agree that the RPD erred in paragraph 20 of its decision when it states that the appellant did not invoke section 97 of the IRPA, because counsel's representations also covered section 97 of the IRPA. However, that error is not fatal in this case. [47] A separate analysis of the two sections is not always required.18 Whether the failure to conduct such an analysis constitutes an error depends on the specific circumstances of each case.19 My decision is based on the Federal Court decision in Emamgongo.20 In that proceeding, in reference to statements made by her colleague Justice de Montigny in Ayaichia v. Canada (MCI), 2007 FC 239, Justice Kelen states the following: ...If the evidentiary basis for both claims is the same and the applicant's story is not believed, there will be no need to proceed to a separate 97 analysis, as there will be no evidence to ground the applicant's claim that he or she is in need of protection... [48] In this case, there are no reasons other than those related to religion that would support a decision that the appellants are persons in need of protection. [49] The situation in a country is insufficient on its own to conclude that a refugee protection claimant has a reasonable fear of persecution or that his or her life is likely subject to one of the risks set out in section 97 of the IRPA. It seems to me that there must still be a connection between the refugee protection claimant and that evidence. In this case, the appellant must first establish that she converted to Judaism or that she was perceived as being of that religion. However, the RPD concluded that this was not established on a balance of probabilities. Fear of the authorities by reason of her alleged conversion [50] At the hearing, the appellant stated that she fears being imprisoned because she is of the Jewish faith. She testified that synagogues are forbidden in Algeria and that she would be unable to practise her religion.21 [51] The appellant criticizes the RPD for [translation] "questioning the possibility of imprisonment" and failing to consider the multiple reports on the issue.22 [52] I am surprised by the appellant's alleged fear that, among other things, she would not be able to attend the synagogue in Algeria as they are closed, since the evidence establishes that she did not attend any in Canada despite being free to do so. [53] I am satisfied that the RPD has considered the country reports. The documentary evidence concerning the situation of Jews in Algeria is limited in this case. [54] Being Jewish in Algeria appears to be a complex issue. An article published in OpenGlobalRights23 in February 2015 states that "intolerance towards Algerian Jews has been driven by geopolitics and history, not religion." [55] In Tab 12.1 of the National Documentation Package on Algeria, the following is stated: The law grants all individuals the right to practice their religion as long as they respect public order and regulations. Offending or insulting any religion is a criminal offense. Proselytizing of Muslims by non-Muslims is a crime. The law does not prohibit conversion from Islam but proselytizing of Muslims by non-Muslims is a criminal offense. [56] In Tab 12.3, the following is stated: According to the US Department of State International Religious Freedom Report for 2013, "[c]onversion and apostasy are not illegal" in Algeria.... According to the US Department of State, Ordinance 06-03 provides for the freedom of non-Muslims to practise religious rites, on condition that the exercise thereof is in keeping with the ordinance, the constitution, and other laws and regulations, and that public order, morality, and the rights and basic freedoms of others are respected. Open Doors USA's World Watch List states that converts from Islam to Christianity in Algeria face societal "pressure" from members of their family. According to Freedom House, small non-Muslim communities do not face harassment. However, non-Muslims may gather only at state-approved locations, proselytism by non-muslims is illegal, and the government in 2008 began enforcing an ordinance [Ordinance 06-03] that tightened restrictions on minority faiths. [57] In light of the evidence, I cannot conclude that the RPD erred. Credibility [58] Several factors led the RPD to doubt the appellant's claims that her life was in danger in Algeria for the alleged reasons. Incomplete, evasive statements about the Jewish religion [59] The RPD concluded that [translation] "the appellant's knowledge of Judaism was limited to mentioning some of the religion's rites and practices, and turned out to be very limited with regard to the beliefs that characterize the Jewish religion." [60] The appellant submits that the RPD assessed her knowledge of Judaism and considered her religious practice based on too high a standard. [61] Upon reading the decision, it is evident the RPD applied the appropriate standard of proof. With respect to this, I point out the following passages: 1) [translation] "The panel is of the opinion that faith is not measured by the level of knowledge of a religion;" and 2) "The panel does not expect the claimant to recite the entire content of the Torah or to know all Jewish prayers." [62] Although it is important not to exaggerate with regards to the knowledge a person may have of a religion's precepts, the RPD was correct in considering the specific context of the appellant, an educated woman who claims to have converted to Judaism as an adult. The RPD did not err when it stated that converting to Judaism is a significant change for a person from an Islamic culture and religion and that in this context, it is reasonable to expect her to be able to explain the reasons for this change, even in simple terms. I would add that that seems even more important to me considering the history of Jews in Algeria and the alleged risk. [63] The RPD was correct in noting that the appellant gave general testimony when asked about her knowledge of Judaism, particularly when she was asked to explain the meaning of a Jewish prayer and what she had read in the Torah. [64] The appellant stated that Jewish holidays include Hanukkah, Passover and the Day of Atonement. She provided a few details about Shabbat, the day of rest. However, like the RPD, her testimony remained at the level of general knowledge. In my opinion, an educated person who is interested in other cultures or who searched on Google-as she did according to her statements-can obtain this type of information. [65] I was surprised by her inability to explain the meaning of a prayer to the panel. Despite stating that she picks up the Torah and reads, the appellant was unable to explain the meaning of Kiddush, which she identified as a Jewish prayer. She merely stated that she picks up the Torah and reads but did not provide details as to where this prayer is located. [66] In my opinion, this manner of testifying undermined her credibility. Conversion to Judaism was not established [67] The RPD concluded that the appellant failed to establish that she converted to Judaism or that she is in the process of converting. [68] In paragraph 46 of the memorandum, she argues the following: [Translation] The panel does not believe in the conversion process because it imagined a more formal conversion ritual, whereas the appellant met with the rabbi. However, this inference is not based on anything other than the comparison to the Christian religion's rite of baptism, which was referred to during the hearing. The appellant explained that she considered herself a Jew from the moment she decided to and that she wanted to have that status recognized by a rabbi. She makes a distinction between converting and joining the Jewish community. In any event, the panel cannot question the process with regard to that specific point because there is no judicial knowledge that this ritual should be performed in a specific way and the documentation produced did not demonstrate that either. [69] With respect for the arguments presented, I understand that the evidence on the record does not make it possible to determine with certainty that a conversion ritual exists. However, I do not consider that the RPD erred while questioning the appellant when it drew a parallel between Christianity in order to allow her to explain whether there is a similar ritual in her new religion. [70] It is reasonable to think that converting to Judaism as an adult is a path that requires a commitment to learning religious precepts and that a rabbi would guide a person who wishes to convert. [71] That said, here is why I consider that there are serious reasons to doubt that a conversion to Judaism occurred in this case. Vague and contradictory testimony [72] The appellant claimed that she converted to Judaism in XXXX 2017 or XXXX 2018 after discussing it with her husband. According to her statements at the hearing, she allegedly told her parents that she was Jewish when she returned to Algeria in XXXX 2018.24 [73] While listening to the recording of the hearing, I noted that the appellant gave vague and hesitant testimony when asked what steps she and her husband took to convert. In addition, she contradicted the statements she made at the hearing. [74] She stated that she had met with a rabbi twice: once at a synagogue and once at her home. When asked for the date on which she had met with the rabbi at the synagogue, the appellant hesitated. At first, she stated that she did not remember.25 To help her narrow down the time frame, the panel asked her whether it had been a long time after her arrival in Canada. The appellant responded, [translation] "Yes, it was after XXXX. It was in 2018 anyways."26 She added that she had not returned to the synagogue because it was too far from her home. [75] Later, when asked whether there is a ritual for converting to Judaism or whether the rabbi had given her a document when she allegedly met with him in XXXX 2018, the appellant insisted that she is Jewish. When the panel asked her whether it was in XXXX 2018 that she had met with the rabbi at the synagogue where she converted, she adjusted her testimony and stated that she had met with the rabbi in XXXX 2019. [76] The RPD did not err in considering that the behaviour of the appellant, who delayed in consulting a rabbi (according to one version of events, it was in XXXX 2019), undermines the credibility of her claims. [77] I am of the opinion that her manner of testifying undermined her credibility. It seems to me that meeting the rabbi at the synagogue was a significant moment in the process of adopting this new religion that will have an impact on her life. Her hesitation and the contradiction in her testimony certainly raises doubt in a decision-maker's mind. However, that is not all. Does not know the full name of the rabbi or of the synagogue where she converted [78] The appellant was unable to provide the full name of the rabbi whom she claims to have met on two occasions. She was also unable to provide the name of the synagogue where she allegedly met with the rabbi who gave her the Torah. Although Jewish women are not required to attend the synagogue and though faith is a personal matter, I am surprised by this lack of information coming from a new convert or a person who is allegedly in the process of converting. [79] Furthermore, her testimony was equally hesitant when she was asked when her husband had converted to Judaism. He allegedly became Jewish before her but she does not remember the date and hesitantly added that it may have been in XXXX 2018. When asked how her husband's conversion had played out, the appellant stated that she had not been with him27 and is therefore unable to provide any details. Her and her husband's conversions to Judaism are central to these refugee protection claims. I am surprised that the appellant did not attempt to obtain information about her husband's conversion and that she is unable to specify whether there is a ritual for joining the Jewish religion or whether a celebration follows. [80] As the RPD stated, religion is a personal matter. However, I do not consider that converting as an adult is as simple as the appellant makes it out to be. She states that she is Jewish because she considers herself as such and [translation] "that she wanted to be recognized by a rabbi," yet she is unable to provide the date she was recognized as Jewish without contradicting herself. [81] If the appellant was recognized as Jewish by a rabbi, her statements that she did not want to ask the rabbi for a letter confirming her status or her alleged conversion28 are surprising, considering that she submitted two unsigned letters29 from her husband's friends to attest to the conversion process. Weight given to the testimonial letters under C-830 [82] The RPD gave little weight to the testimonial letters from her husband's friend and a person he knew to attest to her husband's conversion process. [83] The RPD did not err. The two letters dated XXXX XXXXand XXXX XXXX XXXX 2020, are unsigned. One of them is accompanied by a Quebec health insurance card, but as the panel pointed out at the hearing, the information on it is illegible. I agree that these letters cannot remedy the issues with the appellant's testimony. [84] When considered as a whole, these elements raise a serious doubt in my mind and lead me to conclude that the appellant failed to establish that she converted to Judaism or that she is in the process of converting. Threats in Algeria [85] The appellant returned to Algeria in XXXX 2018. She stated that in XXXX or XXXX 2018, rumours that they had converted to Judaism began circulating due to her husband's posts on social media, in particular, Facebook and Instagram, and threats allegedly followed. [86] The RPD did not find the appellant's testimony credible with regard to the alleged threats. In support of this conclusion, the RPD pointed out the following elements: - The appellant's behaviour as of XXXX 2018 is not that of someone who fears for her safety: 1) the appellant travelled to Turkey in XXXX 2018 and she returned to Algeria; and 2) she lived with her in-laws until ten days before leaving the country despite claiming to have been threatened by some of her in-laws. - The appellant exaggerated when she stated that her brother-in-law XXXX had allegedly been tortured because he was also suspected of having converted to Judaism since he had been in Canada with the appellant and her husband. [87] The following was submitted on appeal: 1) the RPD erred when it stated that some evidence concerning the husband had no probative value; 2) when it failed to consider that she has no direct knowledge of what happened to her brother-in-law; 3) when it stated that the appellant confirmed that her BOC Form was complete and correct at the beginning of the hearing although she was not asked that question; and 4) when it failed to note that the appellant no longer wore a veil either at the hearing or in her photographs. [88] Last, it was submitted that her husband's multiple Facebook posts became necessary due to the overly rigid credibility assessment. [89] Although I disagree with the RPD's analysis concerning the omission from the written account, namely, that her brother-in-law XXXX allegedly had problems, I do have serious doubts about the appellant's claims that she allegedly had problems in Algeria after XXXX 2018. [90] The appellant testified that her husband had shared public posts showing that he had converted to Judaism. In my opinion, if that is true, it is inconsistent with the claims that there is a risk to their lives by reason of adopting the Jewish religion. Testimonial letters from members of the husband's family31 [91] In a bundle under P-4, the appellant submitted testimonial letters from her husband's father, sister and brother in order to corroborate her statements that she and her husband are subject to a risk in Algeria. [92] The RPD gave little weight to corroborate the alleged threats. [93] The RPD did not err. First, the letters are unsigned. Second, the letters from the male appellant's father and sister are not dated. Furthermore, I cannot make out the names on the identity cards accompanying the letters. The sister's letter raises questions, as it suggests that her brother XXXX attends the mosque. The appellant's statements at the hearing revealed that the father's statement that his son XXXX was tortured and his son's statement to that effect are exaggerated. [94] The RPD did not give any probative value to her husband's telephone records32 of calls to Israel to establish the appellant's religious conversion. The RPD did not err. I carefully reviewed these records and I cannot determine who the account belongs to. In addition, calling Israel does not mean that the appellant converted. With regard to her husband's conversion, I note that his case has been suspended. [95] When considered cumulatively, these reasons lead me to conclude that the RPD rendered the correct decision. CONCLUSION [96] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the determination of the RPD, namely, that the appellants, XXXX XXXXand XXXX XXXX, are neither "Convention refugees" nor "persons in need of protection." [97] The appeal is dismissed.
