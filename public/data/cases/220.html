XXXX XXXX acted as the designated representative of the minor claimants. ALLEGATIONS [2] The complete story alleging the basis of the claimants' fears in Israel is captured in the Personal Information Forms2 (PIF). The claimants allege fear in returning to Israel is due to stones thrown at their home and cars by Orthodox Jews, as well as suffering discrimination by Orthodox Jews given the male claimant is not perceived as Jewish as he is Catholic and that he and the claimants celebrate Christmas. Due to the problems as alleged, the claimants realized that life in Israel became dangerous for them and as a consequence they departed Israel for Canada on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2011 and advanced refugee claims shortly thereafter. DECISION [3] The panel finds that the claimants are not Convention refugees as they do not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Israel. The panel also finds that the claimants are not persons in need of protection in that their removal to Israel would not subject them personally to a risk of their lives or to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. Furthermore, on substantial grounds, there is insufficient evidence to believe that their removal to Israel would subject them personally to a danger of torture. In determining these claims, the panel followed the Chairperson's Gender Guidelines. on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender Related Persecution.3 IDENTITY [4] On a balance of probabilities, the panel accepts the claimants are citizens of Israel based on copies of their passports as seen in Exhibit R/A-2.4 ANALYSIS [5] The determinative issue in these cases is state protection. The claimants provide a variety of evidence to suggest that the problems of discrimination they faced or would face cumulatively amounts to persecution. The panel wishes to note the United States Department of State Report which states the following: Israel is a multiparty parliamentary democracy. Although it has no constitution, Israel's parliament, the unicameral 120-member Knesset, has enacted a series of "Basic Laws" that enumerate fundamental rights. Certain fundamental laws, orders, and regulations legally depend on the existence of a "State of Emergency," which has been in effect since 1948. The Knesset has the power to dissolve the government and mandate elections. The 2009 nationwide Knesset elections, considered free and fair, resulted in a coalition government led by Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. Israeli security forces reported to civilian authorities. The most significant human rights issues during the year were terrorist attacks against civilians; institutional and societal discrimination against Arab citizens--in particular issues of access to housing and employment opportunities; and societal discrimination and domestic violence against women. The government generally protected religious freedom, although there was institutional and societal discrimination against non-Orthodox Jews and some minority religious groups.5 [6] In regards to state protection, the notion of it has been thoroughly addressed by the courts, and in particular, the Supreme Court in Ward.6 In order to succeed on the notion that the state is unable to offer protection, the claimants must show, on a balance of probabilities, that they are unable, or by reason of such fear of persecution, unwilling to avail themselves of the protection of their country of nationality. Furthermore, the protection offered by the state need not be perfect nor can a state protect its citizens all the time.7 The issue of state protection goes to the objective portion of the test of fear of persecution.8 There is a presumption that every state is able to protect its citizens unless the state is unable, due to a complete breakdown of the state apparatus. This is not the case relating to Israel. [7] In this particular case, the claimants have not rebutted the presumption of state protection and the panel does not support the notion that the claimants could not obtain adequate protection from the police and other authorities in Israel even after alleged attempts to seek and obtain adequate protection in 2007 and 2009 when little or no action was undertaken by the police. The claimants were not able to sufficiently corroborate evidence of their attempts to seek and exhaust state protection in Israel based on the problems as described. In coming to this opinion, the panel has factored in Exhibit C-6, that being a letter from a friend or associate of the claimants since 2001. [8] The panel has read the documentary evidence and in particular Exhibit C-5, which contains a variety of articles pertaining to discrimination, racism and the country conditions of Israel. The panel recognizes the problems as it relates to discrimination as well as the varied problems that the claimants state they have faced or will face if required to return to Israel today. However, claimants cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state or a brief attempt to seek state protection. Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection.9 The fact that state protection is not perfect10 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability or unwillingness to provide protection.11 The claimants have not satisfactorily shown that as a mixed Jewish and Catholic family that they could not adequately obtain the appropriate assistance from state authorities regarding the discrimination they faced which they argue is tantamount to persecution. [9] The adult claimants obtained citizenship to Israel under the Law of Return on XXXX2009, 1997 and XXXX XXXX, 1998 and were eligible for an Absorption Basket provided by the government of Israel. Item 1.4 of the index gives some relevant information as to what new arrivals to Israel are entitled to. [10] Given the problems as alleged which amounts to persecution in the eyes of the claimants, the panel asked the male claimant, who gave the majority of the evidence on behalf of the family, when he realized that his life and the lives of the other claimants were at risk. The male claimant indicated that it was around XXXX 2010 when his vehicle was damaged, as well as a day in XXXX 2011 when there were hundreds of Orthodox Jews yelling and screaming threats at the students of the minor claimants' school, parents and at the school administration. [11] The adult claimants allegedly sought the assistance of a lawyer who assists Russian speaking people. However, the adult claimants were told that it is useless if the situation involves orthodox Jews. The claimants did not go further and complain to the Ombudsman's office, for example, regarding the problems experienced as they could not afford to. The panel was not presented with credible and trustworthy evidence, in a clear and convincing fashion, that as a mixed Jewish and Catholic family in Israel, who were victims of discrimination that, in the opinion of the claimants is tantamount to persecution and as a consequence could not obtain adequate state protection. [12] In reviewing the evidence further, the panel recognizes that there are acts of discrimination in Israel that are committed by and against a variety of citizens in the country. Unfortunately, discrimination exists in all forms and in all countries. However, the alleged acts of discrimination or any other acts that were committed against the claimants because of being a mixed Russian Jewish and Catholic family, or for any other reason, do not come within the realm of persecution. The panel was not presented with clear and convincing evidence that Israeli officials condone such discriminatory action against the claimants as a mixed family that would be tantamount to persecution. [13] The panel wishes to note the following from item 12.112of the index: There was no change in the status of respect for religious freedom by the government during the reporting period. Although the government generally protected religious freedom, some laws and policies continued to promote certain Orthodox Jewish values over other religious beliefs. There were reports of societal abuses and discrimination based on religious affiliation, belief, or practice. Some individuals and groups were responsible for discriminatory practices against Israeli-Arab Muslims, Christians, and Messianic Jews at the same elevated level cited in the previous report. Relations among religious and ethnic groups -- between Jews and non-Jews, Muslims and Christians, Arabs and non-Arabs, secular and religious Jews, and among the different streams of Judaism -- were strained during the reporting period. [14] Furthermore, item 12.313 of the index states: Israeli law guarantees freedom of worship and, according to the International Religious Freedom Report 2007, "government policy continued to contribute to the generally free practice of religion," although non-Jews "were subject to various forms of discrimination" (US 14 Sept. 2007, Intr.). Some Christian denominations are recognized by Israeli law and as such have jurisdiction over civil status matters such as marriage or divorce (Jewish Virtual Library n.d.; US 14 Sept. 2007, Sec. II). The government states that as of 2007 there were no religious groups awaiting formal recognition (ibid.). Israelis who feel that their rights have been infringed upon by a government agency because of "any action [or omission or a delay in acting] taken contrary to law, without lawful authority, contrary to sound administration or that involves too inflexible an attitude or a flagrant injustice" may file an oral complaint with the Ombudsman's office at any of its five branches located throughout the country (Israel 26 Mar. 2002). Christian community According to Israel's Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS), there were 152,000 Christians living in the country as of December 2007, representing 2.1 percent of the national population (The Jerusalem Post 26 Dec. 2007; US 14 Sept. 2007, Sec. I). Some four-fifths are Arab, while the remaining fifth is mostly comprised of the Christian relatives of immigrants from the former Soviet Union (The Jerusalem Post 26 Dec. 2007). While three-quarters live in Israel's northern Galilee region, approximately 11 percent live in Jerusalem (ibid.). In March 2006, some local residents blamed an attack against a Nazareth Church, in which a Jewish man and his Christian wife threw firecrackers but did not cause any injuries, as anti-Christian extremism, although police stated that the attack was not due to anti-Christian motives (The Miami Herald 5 Mar. 2006; Haaretz 4 Mar. 2006). In response to the incident, President Moshe Katsav stated that "Israel pledges to unconditionally defend the holy sites of all religions" (ibid.) and in September 2006, the Nazareth District Court reportedly convicted the couple for their role in the attack (ibid. 13 Sept. 2006). [15] The panel does not remotely believe that the claimants cannot practice their faith freely as Jews or Christians in Israel. Tensions clearly do exist between different religious and ethnic groups as noted in the documents. However, it cannot be said that the state, local or otherwise, is hindered or will not assist the claimants given they are a mixed family and not Orthodox Jews. The claimants should be reasonably expected to exhaust state protection in the country prior to seeking international protection. The fact that these options were not properly pursued or considered in a democratic society such as Israel minimizes the notion that adequate state protection could not have been sought. [16] Item 2.114 of the index also provides some information about complaints that can be made regarding police lack of actions: Under the authority of the prime minister, the ISA combats terrorism and espionage in the country and the occupied territories (see annex). The National Police, including the Border Police and the Immigration Police, are under the authority of the Ministry of Internal Security. Civilian authorities maintained effective control over the ISA and police forces, and the government has effective mechanisms to investigate and punish abuse and corruption. There were no reports of impunity involving the security forces during the year. The military is responsible for external security and has no jurisdiction over citizens. [17] In reviewing the documents further and in regard to the issue of state protection, item 13.115 of the index states the following: A persistent tension prevails in Israel concerning the Jewish and the national dimensions of the concept of "Jewishness," and Jewish old-timers do not always welcome the secular Jewish identity of most of the immigrants. Sixty-eight percent of Jewish old-timers think that "the Jewishness of most FSU immigrants is questionable"; 69% of the respondents also object to a family member marrying a non-Jewish FSU immigrant. (2009, 97) The Ombudsman of Israel has no specialized role and/or complaints procedure directed toward immigrants from the former Soviet Union, but rather treats them as any other complainants, without regard to background, age, religion or ethnic group. That said, the Ombudsman's Office has gone out of its way in order to enhance the access of immigrants to our services. New bureaus of the Office were opened in peripheral areas in the north and the south of the country, where many immigrants reside. Another such bureau is set to open this year in a former development town in the centre of the country, which will service, among others, a large immigrant population. The Ombudsman's Office also hired new professional advocates from within the immigrant communities (both from the FSU and from Ethiopia) who speak their languages and have unique insight into their problems and needs. In this way, we are able to accept and deal with complaints and enquiries from such immigrants who would rather turn to us in their own language, instead of Hebrew, Arabic or English. The result has been the receipt of a plethora of complaints from the immigrant community, concerning all manner of issues: for example, complaints concerning the level of support received through the Ministry of Absorption, education issues, complaints regarding exemptions/discounts from local taxes, enquiries about welfare and veteran's benefits, etc. All complaints are dealt with in an equitable and timely manner. (Israel 24 Feb. 2010) NGO Response The Ruppin Academic Centre Professor indicated that there are many NGOs focused on helping FSU immigrants (9 Feb. 2010). In a 15 May 2007 article, SHATIL, an organization that is part of the NIF and provides support to non-profit organizations and projects (NIF n.d.b), describes a Russian-language hotline based at a SHATIL office which assists FSU immigrants with various social problems. The founder of the hotline describes immigrants from the FSU as "a bit cut off from Israeli society and especially from civil society organizations" (NIF 15 May 2007). In an undated article on its website, IRAC provides the following information regarding its Legal Aid Center: IRAC's Legal Aid Center for Olim provides pro-bono legal assistance to immigrants from the FSU. The majority of cases involve difficulties with the Ministry of the Interior, and include: proving Jewish status and conversions, as well as helping to support the immigrant's right to family. [18] It should be noted that the panel has reviewed the Chairperson's Guidelines on gender related persecution. However, given the concerns raised earlier in these reasons pertaining to the issue of state protection, the panel cannot come to the conclusion that the claimants, specifically the adult female claimant as well as the minor female claimants as children meet the elements as indicated in the Gender Guidelines as well as the Chairperson's Guidelines on child refugee claimants which may off-set any deficiencies referenced earlier in these reasons. [19] Simply, after reviewing all the evidence, as it pertains to a well-founded fear of persecution or that the claimants are in need of protection pursuant to section 97 of the IRPA, there has not been a successful argument made to suggest a positive determination in this regard. [20] In Li,16 the Federal Court stated "pursuant to subsection 97(1) there must be persuasive evidence that is on a balance of probabilities establishing the facts on which a claimant relies, to say that he or she faces a substantial danger of being tortured on his or her return. Second, the danger or risk must be such that it is more likely than not that he or she would be tortured or subjected to other cruel or other degrading treatments." This is not the case in these claims. [21] In Bouaouni,17 the Federal Court stated "a claim under section 97 must be evaluated with respect to all the relevant considerations and with a view to the country's human rights record." In evaluating these claims under section 97 and considering the human rights record of Israel, the panel has not been persuaded that the claimants would face a substantial danger of being tortured, or that the risk and/or danger is such that it would be more likely than not that the claimants would be tortured or subjected to other cruel or other degrading treatments, based on what has already been mentioned earlier in these reasons. [22] Therefore, in a forward looking analysis and having reviewed all the evidence, the claimants have failed to establish that they meet the higher degree of harm of risk to their lives or risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, on a balance of probabilities. There is insufficient evidence to support a finding that the claimants face a danger of torture. CONCLUSION [23] Based on all the evidence adduced at this hearing, the panel finds that the claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. The cumulative reasons referred to above as it relates to the issue of state protection does not establish that the claimants have a reasonable chance or serious possibility of persecution for a Convention ground if required to return to Israel. The panel also finds for the same reasons that the claimants are not in need of protection. The panel rejects these claims pursuant to both sections 96 and 97(1) of the IRPA. (signed) ' K.
