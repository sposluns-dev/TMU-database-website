The determinative issue in this claim is state protection. ALLEGATIONS [3] All of the claimants submitted and relied on the same BOC narrative as the principal claimant and relied on his testimony. The claimants allege that they are citizens of Ukraine and members of the XXXX XXXX XXXX Church. They allege that they are being persecuted in their hometown of Kiev in Ukraine due to their religion. [4] After converting from Orthodox Christianity and being baptized as XXXX XXXX Christians in 2008, the claimants started publicly proselytizing their religious beliefs in their day-today-lives to their neighbors, at work or on the street. Their proselytizing was met with hateful graffiti on their property, vandalism to their property, as well as outright violence towards them. They sought medical attention for their injuries. They also reported incidents to the police and their insurance company. The claimants allege that the police were unresponsive and claimed to be too busy to assist them. [5] The claimants allege that the situation became intolerable and dangerous to their lives during the recent upheaval in Ukraine. They allege that pro-Russians accused XXXX of being agents of the USA and that their aim was the destruction of Ukraine. They allege this resulted in mass violence against XXXX. Following further threats directed at the claimants in 2014, having learned on the Internet about a XXXX pastor being blown up in his car and several sisters of the XXXX Church being kidnapped, and given the presence of a large number of unregistered firearms within the population, they felt the situation had become dangerous in general and specifically for them as Baptists. Identity [6] The claimants' respective identities have been established through the certified copies of their passports, the birth certificate for the minor claimant, the marriage certificate for the adult claimants2 and their testimonies. Designated Representative [7] The principal claimant was appointed as the designated representative for his daughter, the minor claimant. State Protection [8] Whether the claim is assessed under s.96 or s.97(1) of the Act, the claimants must rebut the presumption of state protection in Ukraine. The Panel finds that the evidence demonstrates that the claimants have not rebutted the presumption of state protection. General Principles of State Protection [9] States are presumed to be capable of protecting their citizens, except in situations where the state is in a state of complete breakdown.3 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact that the state's efforts are not always successful will not rebut the presumption of state protection.4 [10] To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide "clear and convincing" evidence of the state's inability to protect its citizens, absent an admission by the state of its inability to protect that national.5 A claimant must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.6 The claimant must provide the police with sufficient evidence to mount a successful investigation,7 and give the authorities sufficient opportunity to respond to the request for assistance.8 [11] The adequacy of state protection cannot rest on the subjective fear of the claimant and is premised on an objective assessment that must be made independently of whether the claimant possesses a subjective fear.9 A claimant who alleges that the state protection is inadequate must persuade the Board that the evidence establishes that state protection is inadequate. The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.10 The protection need not be perfect as no state can guarantee perfect protection.11 [12] In this case, the claimants have the burden of providing clear and convincing evidence that they cannot obtain adequate state protection in Ukraine. The Objective Evidence [13] According to the US Department of State's (DOS) Country Reports on Human Rights Practices,12 Ukraine is a democratic nation with a presidential-parliamentary system of government. The US DOS' International Religious Freedom Report13 explains that Ukraine is a democracy that protects the freedom of religion. The constitution and other laws and policies protect religious freedom and the government generally respects religious freedom. According to the 1991 core law on religion, the objective of domestic religious policy is to "restore full-fledged dialogue between representatives of various social, ethnic, cultural, and religious groups to foster the creation of a tolerant society and provide for freedom of conscience and worship." A 2012 law provides for anti-discrimination screening of draft legislation and government regulations, including on the basis of religion. [14] The US DOS' International Religious Freedom Report goes on to explain that the Evangelical Baptist Union of Ukraine is the largest Protestant community. The report goes on to explain that there were reports of societal abuses and discrimination based on religious affiliation. These included cases of anti-Semitism and anti-Muslim discrimination as well as discrimination and attacks against Christian denominations such as Jehovah's Witnesses, Catholics and Ukrainian Orthodox Christian groups. There is no specific mention of Baptists being the target of societal abuses and discrimination. Is Ukraine in a state of complete breakdown following recent events? [15] According to the UNHCR's July 2014 report on the International Protection Considerations Related to the Developments in Ukraine - Update I, the situation in Eastern Ukraine appears to be deteriorating:14 The overall situation remains volatile and political differences have hardened, in particular in eastern parts of Ukraine where the situation has continued to escalate.6 Fighting between government operations and armed groups has increased.7 Residents in many eastern regions live in an insecure environment, coupled with increasing economic and social hardship.8 The presence of armed groups and weapons has increased in the Donetsk and Luhansk regions. Human rights abuses are reportedly no longer only targeted at journalists, elected representatives, local politicians, civil servants and civil society activists. At present, abductions, detentions, acts of ill-treatment and torture, and killings by armed groups are affecting the broader population of the two eastern regions, which are marked by an atmosphere of intimidation and consequent fear.9 Hate speech and threats are also reportedly contributing to a deepening of the division between communities.10 In Crimea, the introduction of Russian Federation legislation has been reported to create a "legislative limbo" for many people and concerns for their future.11 Persons in Crimea known to be "Pro-Ukrainian" and others, depending on their profile and their individual situation,12 are also reported to be facing personal intimidations and threats, often by non-state actors.13 [16] The government of Ukraine continued to be mindful and in control of its human rights obligations even against the backdrop of the ongoing conflict:15 The protection of civilians in the eastern regions has been of increasing concern. On 4 July, the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights expressed concern about the safety of people caught in the crossfire between Ukrainian forces and the armed groups, and reminded all those involved in the fighting of the need to scrupulously respect residents' fundamental rights, including the right to life. The Government appeared to take notice and, on 8 July, said it would give special attention to the prevention of civilian losses and would not bombard the cities of Donetsk and Luhansk, to which the armed groups had retreated. Instead it would blockade those cities in an effort to force the armed groups to surrender. [...]. [17] The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights' report from 15 July 2014 acknowledges that the situation in the rest of the country is becoming affected by the troubles in the eastern regions. The report explains that the impact on the rest of the Ukraine has been the influx into the other regions of internally displaced persons as well as an increase in hate speech directed at pro-Russian forces:16 A second impact of the worsening situation in the east, are the instances of use of hate speech. High level public condemnations of such troubling developments are required. There are reports of Russian-owned banks and some businesses having been targeted by activists who charged them with 'financing terrorism 'in the eastern regions. Steps have been taken to ban the Communist Party. Negativity about IDPs has begun to surface, in particular on social media, further dividing opinions between east and west. [18] The other impact, aside from hate speech, is the influx into other regions of internally displaced persons (IDPs). Regardless, and perhaps most telling with respect to the relative stability or control of the government and availability of protection in Kiev is that persons internally displaced by the conflict in the eastern regions are relocating to Kiev in order to escape from the conflict:17 Individuals and families report having left their places of origin for diverse reasons. [...] Most are moving to Kyiv and Lviv regions. [19] With respect to the curtailment of human rights abuses and related violations, the government is actively addressing these issues as well:18 As promised, the Government published on 2 July its proposed amendments to the Constitution allowing for greater regional autonomy and for the use of their own languages. Other important legislation was passed tackling corruption and there were some institutional reforms, including the firing of staff who were found to be inept, corrupt or had committed other violations. Most notable among these, in terms of the large numbers involved, were the firing of 17,000 law enforcement offices and of 1,500 employees of the Office of the General Prosecutor, although the latter appeared to be more an austerity measure. It is essential that in undertaking such action, the rights of the individuals be fully respected. [20] That said, the government's attention on the conflict in the eastern regions has diverted its attention from other issues:19 With so much of its attention focused on the escalating security operation in the east and responding to the increase of violence of the armed groups, other key aspects of reform are beginning to receive less attention from the Government. Reform of the judiciary and the system of justice remain to be addressed. Initial steps have been taken to reform law enforcement with a pilot project to start in Lviv, the results from which will form the basis of a reform package. Reform also needs to address the powers of the State Security Service (SBU). The results of the investigations into the Maidan and Odessa violence are still awaited. [21] Despite these shortcomings, and despite the need for improvement and further training on the part of police forces, the police forces are maintaining protection at the ground level:20 No violence on the scale which occurred with the Maidan protests or in Odessa on 2 May has occurred at peaceful assemblies held during the reporting period. [...] law enforcement must receive adequate training to be able to handle rallies and protests, in line with international human rights standards. Overall, law enforcement has managed to contain violent intent, although there have been incidents of serious damage to property and some injuries. There have also been more isolated scuffles and clashes that generally have been kept to a minimum by law enforcement. Police have been criticized for sometimes not doing enough to stop violent actions (as for example, when the trade unions meeting was violently disrupted in Kyiv on 26 June) and, conversely, for cracking down unnecessarily hard on demonstrators to prevent any kind of possible public disorder from the very beginning. This was the concern in Kharkiv on 22 June, when a large presence of law enforcement officers successfully kept opposing groups apart but was later criticized by one side for abuse of power17. There remains a need to adopt legislation and other measures to clarify the role and responsibilities of law enforcement to ensure the principles of necessity, proportionality, non-discrimination and accountability underpin the management of peaceful assemblies. [22] Therefore, taking all of the above, the Panel finds that the while the situation is less than ideal given that there is a war raging within the borders of the country, the actual conflict appears to be isolated to the eastern regions. To be clear, Kiev is located in the north central part of Ukraine and is actually a city to which IDPs are fleeing from the eastern regions in order to seek internal refuge and protection. While the government's attention has been directed at the situation in the east, the government is still mindful of its obligations to remainder of the country. On the whole, despite the negative aspects about the police referenced above, the police are managing the violence in the other areas of the country including in Kiev, where the claimants reside. The government is also making serious efforts to contain the conflict in the eastern regions. As a result, it can be said based on the objective assessment of the situation that Ukraine is in effective control of its territory (as far as the claimants' residence in Kiev is concerned and non-eastern regions are concerned), has military and civil society in place and is making efforts to protect its citizens. All in all, the Panel finds that at the very least, Kiev (where the claimants reside) and the non-eastern regions are not in a state of complete breakdown and that serious efforts are being made to respond to the situation in the eastern regions. Is Ukraine in a state of breakdown with respect to the treatment of religious minorities in particular? [23] The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights reports that religious minorities are being targeted in the eastern regions:21 The freedom of religion or belief has come under increasing pressure in the last weeks. A disturbing number of incidents have been reported in the east and Crimea. The armed groups have declared that the main religion in Donetsk region was Orthodox Christianity (of the Moscow Patriarchate) and that sects were prohibited. This approach explains to a large extent, the increasing number of attacks on Protestant, Mormon, and Roman Catholic churches in the areas controlled by the armed groups. Religious leaders have been harassed, threatened and abducted. [24] The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights goes on to report that religious groups are being targeted in other parts of the country as well, but the references are to Jewish followers and Russian affiliated followers of the Christian Orthodox Church. There is no reference to other religious groups being targeted at this time. It is also telling, given the claimants' allegations to the contrary, that it is actually persons viewed as supportive of Russia or separatists that are being targeted in the remainder of the Ukraine outside of the eastern regions:22 There have been reports of incidents in other parts of the country. For example, in Odessa, law enforcement pre-emptively surrounded a synagogue that was to be a target of an anti-Jewish demonstration thereby deterring the protestors and no gathering occurred. In Kyiv, on 22 June, a more violent protest targeted a religious group perceived as being affiliated with the Russian Federation. The demonstrators included men armed with baseball bats and hammers, some wearing bulletproof vests and clearly intending to do damage. However, once again a large law enforcement presence prevented any violence. The demonstrators claimed the event, being held at the Orthodox Church, was an attempt by 'separatists' to form a 'Kyiv People's Republic'. [25] Therefore, while religious minorities are being targeted in the other non-eastern regions of Ukraine, the documentary evidence paints a picture to the contrary of that alleged by the claimants. The claimants alleged that a large number of pro-Russian persons are targeting Baptists because they are viewed as pro-American. The documentary evidence referred to highlights that while this may be the case in the eastern regions, it is those that are being viewed as pro-Russian separatists that are being targeted in the other regions of the country. Also, there is evidence of Jewish minorities being targeted but the police were able to offer protection by deterring protestors. There is no mention of Evangelical Christian Baptists being targeted. Even then, the claimants reside in north-central Ukraine in Kiev and not in the eastern regions. Having reviewed the evidence, there is no objective basis for the claimants' allegation of a breakdown in state protection available to them in Kiev. The Claimants' Evidence [26] The Panel is not solely looking at this issue from the perspective of the objective evidence in the NDP. The Panel is mindful that it has to look at the evidence that the claimants have provided to rebut the presumption of state protection. The claimants did not provide any objective documentary evidence that speaks to the inadequacy of the state protection available to them in Kiev. Therefore, the Panel will rely on their personal evidence23 and assess whether it rebuts the presumption of state protection. [27] The claimants submitted multiple medical, insurance and police reports (via the Ministry of Internal Affairs of Ukraine), photographs of hateful graffiti, residential leases, an employment termination agreement, and several pictures of them within their place of worship.24 The Panel will assess only the police reports as they pertain most directly to state protection. Without getting into specifics, at face value the other evidence corroborates the timing of the alleged incidents, the injuries to the person (and medical treatment received) and property of the claimants, that they moved and leased apartments, that the principal claimant's employment ended and they attended a place of worship. The letter by their Senior Pastor in Toronto confirms that they attend church on a regular basis. Again, the police reports go directly to the question of state protection. The Police Reports [28] The claimants testified consistently with their BOCs that the police were basically unresponsive and didn't provide them with adequate protection. [29] A report dated 5 August 2014 by the Ministry of Internal Affairs of Ukraine, in response to the claimants having reported an incident where unknown persons posing as police officers attempted to extort money from the claimants over the phone, merely contains the statement of the principal claimant reporting the incident. The principal claimant alleges in his BOC that he wrote to the police but that nothing had been done and that the police indicated his case was not important because no money was transferred to the extortionists. [30] When asked about this incident and whether he disclosed to the police that he suspected the incident was motived by his religious affiliation, the principal claimant explained that he did not disclose this to the police. The claimant must provide the police with sufficient evidence to mount a successful investigation.25 The Panel does not find it reasonable for the claimants to expect that the police could provide them with adequate protection in response to the alleged persecutory incident if material information was withheld from the police. The claimants' alleged response by the police that there was no fraud because money had not been transferred is reflective of what they were told about the incident. They police weren't told by the claimants that they were specifically targeted because of their religious affiliation and thus, that the incident was motivated by hate. The police did take the principal claimant's statement and the police's alleged response appears to be adequate based on the information they were given. Therefore, this report coupled with the police's alleged response, does not rebut the presumption of state protection. [31] The principal claimant reports that in May of 2014, unknown persons broke into his office, broke the furniture, robbed the office and wrote hateful threats directed at him and his religion on a wall. He noticed that it was perhaps the same handwriting he had seen in the past on graffiti on his car and cottage sometime before. The incident certificate by the Ministry of Internal Affairs of Ukraine, dated 5 June 2014, refers to the breaking and entering and the secret abduction of property items but makes no mention of the hate speech or the claimant's allegation that the handwriting may have been connected to past incidents. The report mentions that a criminal proceeding has been initiated. [32] When asked by the Panel why the police report doesn't mention anything about hateful threats or the principal claimant's suspicions about have seen the same handwriting before, he explained that the police did ask him about the hateful threats and that he did indicate that this was not the first time he's seen the writing. He explained that the police indicated a willingness to open a criminal investigation if the claimant was ready to testify and give evidence. The principal claimant responded that he was too shocked and anxious because of the incident in order to pursue that route. The police followed-up with him but they had nothing to add. Again, with complete sympathy to the principal claimant over the shock he alleges to have suffered by this incident, it's not reasonable for him to assert that he wasn't accorded adequate protection because of his own subjective reluctance to enable the authorities to pursue the matter. The claimant doubts the adequacy of state protection but hasn't really tested it in this case because of his own reluctance to pursue the matter.26 The police expressed pursuing the matter if the claimant was willing to testify. Therefore, this does not rebut the presumption of state protection. [33] The principal claimant reports that in XXXX 2014 his car was vandalized with personally and religiously hateful graffiti and the police were yet again unhelpful. The XXXX XXXX 2014 report from the Ministry of Internal Affairs, which the claimant clarified was regarding the incident with his vehicle, refers to an incident of theft, a pre-trail investigation on the grounds of a criminal offence, and summons the principal claimant to visit the investigator for the execution of investigative procedures. This summons to visit the investigator contradicts the claimant's assertion in his BOC that the police did not take action and indicated they had a lot of other work to do. When asked about this, the principal claimant clarified that the police took his statement, informed him that they were busy, that they opened the case and that was the end of it, and that the XXXX XXXX 2014 report was received by the principal claimant after he followed-up with the police. Therefore, it is not reasonable for the claimant to advance that the police were unresponsive when he himself admits that his follow-up with the police resulted in this letter which actually requests to meet with him about the investigation. Accordingly, this does not rebut the presumption of state protection. [34] Consistent with the foregoing car incident is another incident which also reflects the adequacy of protection and the police's willingness to protect. The XXXX XXXX 2014 conclusion by the Ministry of Internal Affairs, with respect to the XXXX 2014 incident of the claimants' car being broken into, refers to unidentified persons damaging the lock, the principal claimant's account of the incident, the police's inquest into the matter and conclusion that it was impossible to identify witnesses or the perpetrator. Contrary to the claimant's assertion that the police were not helpful, they appear to have considered the matter and concluded accordingly as demonstrated by their indicated as such in the report. Accordingly, this does not rebut the presumption of state protection. [35] The XXXX 2014 incident in which the claimants' cottage was broken into and vandalized with hateful graffiti was also reported to the police. The XXXX XXXX 2014 report from the Ministry of Internal Affairs again refers to a pre-trial investigation, the statement of the incident, and provides information about the assigned investigator. There is no mention in the report of any graffiti on the walls. [36] When asked by the Panel why the report does not mention the hateful graffiti if it was reported, the principal claimant explained that the investigator said he wasn't going to mention that in the report in order to avoid "bigger problems." When asked by the Panel if he attempted to clarify what was meant by "bigger problems," the principal claimant offered that he didn't ask but that it could mean something. It is unreasonable that if the principal claimant wanted the police to take action and offer protection based on the claimants' religious affiliation, he wouldn't ask what the officer meant by "bigger problems." It is unreasonable for the claimant to expect a further investigation and protection in response to a hate incident if a material element such as the hate speech itself wasn't going to form part of the incident report to the police. The claimant cannot then complaint that the police were unresponsive. Therefore, this does not rebut the presumption of state protection. [37] With respect to the XXXX 2010 incident in which the principal claimant's vehicle was vandalized with offensive graffiti, he claims in his BOC that he went to the police station and filed a report which included his suspicions and the motives for this incident. The claimant did not provide a copy of the police report which he says included his suspicions and motives for the incident. Instead, the claimant provided the "Judgment on Refusal to initiate the Criminal Case," dated XXXX XXXX 2010, by the Deputy Head of the XXXX XXXX. This Judgment makes no mention of the offensive graffiti nor the alleged suspicions and motives. In fact, the Judgment states that the damages detected were a dent on the hood, scratches on the front bumper and two cracks on the windshield. The Judgment goes on to state that no other damages were detected by the patrol crew while inspecting the vehicle. The Judgment also specifies that the purpose of the application to the Internal Affairs Authority was to obtain the police judgment in respect of the car damages with the purpose of its submission to the insurance company. The Judgment goes on to explain that residents of the building were also questioned and did not have anything to add and explained they did not observe any suspects. After investigating, the officer concluded that the case did not contain formal elements of a crime under the Criminal Code of Ukraine. [38] Contrary to the principal claimant's allegation in his BOC, there is no mention of suspicions or motives for the incident in the Judgment and he didn't provide the initial report. When asked why the Judgment doesn't refer to the offensive graffiti or the suspicions and motives if they reported these, the principal claimant offered that because the damage to the vehicle was considerable and the car was purchase don credit, the claimants needed the insurance to pay. If the insurance company were to learn that the damage was because of their religious affiliation as Baptists, the insurance wouldn't pay. The police had asked what the judgment was need for and the claimants indicated to the police they needed it for insurance purposes and that's why the Judgment didn't mention the offensive graffiti or suspicions and motives. [39] In this case, the claimants offered their own reasonable explanation as to why the police accepted their claim but allegedly did nothing to find those who inflicted the damage. The claimants admitted they only went to the police to get a Judgment to then submit it to their insurance and that is what they got. Even then, the Judgment explains that following an investigation and the questioning of other residents at the location, there was nothing more to go on in terms of evidence to identity any suspects. It appears that despite the insurance motive, the police still investigated the incident. Again, the presumption of state protection is not rebutted as the claimants got exactly what they asked for and although they may not be satisfied with the ultimate outcome, the police actually outlined that they did investigate. [40] The claimants have not rebutted the presumption of state protection for the foregoing reasons. They alleged that state protection was inadequate because the police were unresponsive or unhelpful. However, the Panel's findings above demonstrate that time and again the police were indeed responsive based on the interactions between the claimants and the police, the information conveyed from the claimants to the police about the various incidents, and the supporting documentation in the form of police reports submitted to the Panel for consideration. [41] The evidence submitted by the claimants has not satisfied the Panel, on a balance of probabilities and for the reasons stated above, that state protection is inadequate. As a result, the claimants have not discharged their burden of providing clear and convincing evidence that they cannot obtain adequate state protection in Ukraine and particularly, in Kiev which is not in a state of breakdown. The Other Claimant and Minor Claimant [42] The other claimant (XXXX XXXX) and the minor claimant (XXXX XXXX) relied on the BOC narrative and testimony of the principal claimant. While the panel has referred to all the claimants collectively in many of its findings above, for greater clarity, the claims of the other claimant and minor claimant fail for the same reasons as the claim of the principal claimant. [43] Additionally, there were incidents at paragraphs 8 and 12 of their common BOC narrative.27 In both these incidents, the minor claimant was injured because of her proximity to her mother who was allegedly attacked because of her religious affiliation. The minor claimant was medically treated for both of these incidents but the claimants did not report the incidents to the police. When asked about this by the Panel, the principal claimant explained that he didn't report instances of bodily injuries to the police because Baptists are considered sectarians and that property damage is easier to prove and therefore only reported property damage to the police. [44] This is not a reasonable explanation for not reporting incidents of bodily harm stemming from alleged religious persecution. This reasoning on the part of the claimants expresses a subjective reluctance to engage the state. Again, the presumption of state protection is not rebutted and it was not even tested in these instances. CONCLUSION [45] For the foregoing reasons, the Panel determines that the claimants are neither Convention refugees under section 96 nor persons in need of protection within the meaning of section 97(1) of the Act and therefore rejects their claims.
