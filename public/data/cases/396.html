Statement of Alleged Facts [2] The principal claimant was born in Azerbaijan. Her father was of Azerbaijan nationality and her mother was Jewish. The principal claimant stated that in 1990, the extremist group, Popular Front, began to be abusive and went door to door of the Jewish people telling them to return to Israel. She claimed that she and her daughters were subjected to harassment on several occasions. In XXXXX 1993, she alleged that two men from the Popular Front came at her place and brutally raped and beat her. She claimed that she went to the police the next day to report the incident but was just laughed at and sent home. In XXXXX 1993, she moved to Israel with her daughters. However, before leaving Azerbaijan, she stated that she was instructed to return her Azerbaijan passport and was issued a temporary external passport for five years. She was told that she was allowed to leave Azerbaijan but she would lose her Azerbaijan citizenship after stamping her passport. In Israel, the principal claimant stated that she was called dirty names and harassed everywhere she went. Her daughters experienced similar difficulties and assaults in and outside school. In XXXXX 1999, the claimant received a Certificate from the Rabbinate of Haiffa confirming that she was Jewish. However, in XXXXX 2000, the principal claimant alleged that she was called to attend the office of the Ministry of Internal Affairs in XXXXX. She was allegedly advised that her citizenship papers were reviewed and concluded that her documents were not genuine on the basis that there were handwritten notes on her mother's birth certificate where her date of birth was written. The principal claimant stated that she tried to explain that those handwritten notes were made by her daughter when she was very young. She claimed that she was instructed to give up her Jewish nationality and status. In 2006, the claimant's daughter, Sabina applied to the Rabbinate for a marriage licence and was invited by the Rabbinical Court to prove her Jewish status. On XXXXX, 2006, XXXXX was advised by the Rabbinate Court (with three Judges sitting) in Haiffa that she was not Jewish and could not marry in Israel. On XXXXX, 2006, the principal claimant was advised by the XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX that the birth certificate of her mother was sent to the Forensic Science Lab in 1998 and was found to be a forgery. On XXXXX, 2006, the principal claimant received a ruling from the Rabbinical Court of Haiffa (with three Judges sitting) stating that her mother's birth certificate, which was the basis of her being determined as Jewish, was a forgery and, as a result, the Certificate of being Jewish issued to her on XXXXX, 1999 was revoked.1 The claimant stated that on XXXXX, 2006, she applied for external passports for herself and her children and received passports that were valid for only one year. On XXXXX, 2007, she received a call from the Ministry of Internal Affairs of Haiffa and wanted her to sign documents renouncing her Jewish nationality and Israeli citizenship. She claimed that she consulted a legal aid lawyer, who advised her that if she signed the documents, she would be deported with her daughters from Israel. She did not sign the documents. As they were unwanted in Israel and could not return to Azerbaijan, the claimants decided to move to Canada. They arrived on February 15, 2007 and filed their refugee protection claim on February 19, 2007. Identity [3] I find that the claimants are citizens of Israel. This has been established by prima facie evidence of their Israeli passports.2 Determination [4] I find that the claimants are not Convention refugees under section 96 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act. I also find that the claimants are not persons in need of protection under section 97(1) of IRPA. Status in Azerbaijan [5] Article 16 of the Law of Azerbaijan on Citizenship provides grounds for termination of citizenship of the Azerbaijan Republic. It states that citizenship of the Azerbaijan Republic shall be terminated in the following cases: ...as a result of renouncing citizenship of the Azerbaijan Republic...3 The principal claimant stated in the narrative of her Personal Information form (PIF) that she wrote a letter to the Azerbaijan passport office, returned their Azerbaijan passports and was told that they would lose their Azerbaijan citizenship after their passports were stamped. The principal claimant also testified that they lost their Azerbaijan citizenship after they left Azerbaijan and moved to Israel. Status in Israel The claimants' Jewish nationality was revoked by the Rabbinate Court in XXXXX. The claimants are still Israeli citizens as they did not renounce their Israeli citizenship. Their external passports expired in XXXXX 2007. Credibility [6] The principal claimant testified in a spontaneous and forthright manner. Although there was an inconsistency in her testimony in terms of how the alteration on her mother's birth certificate was done by her daughter, it is not significant to discredit the otherwise credible evidence and, ultimately, the outcome of her claim. State Protection [7] The principal claimant did not provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect its citizens. She stated in the narrative section of her Personal Information Form (PIF)4 [that although she reported to the police the assaults on her in XXXXX 2004 and on her daughter, XXXXX, in XXXXX 2006, the police did not investigate the attacks. When asked if she went to higher police authorities or other agencies to seek protection, the principal claimant testified that she did not. [9] There are avenues of recourse to file a complaint of police inaction (in response to reports of crimes, harassment or discrimination). "The Website of the Israel Police indicated that the Public Complaints Unit (PCU), located at the National Headquarters in Jerusalem, handles all complaints from members of the public against serving officers. In response to dissatisfaction with the complaints mechanism among members of the public and police officers, the police commissioner launched a pilot project in January 2004 that would involve mediation between police officers and complainants. According to the Israel Police, the project, which operates in the Jerusalem District and the Sharon Sub-district, was a success, and the police commissioner has decided to implement this strategy across the entire public complaints system and continue its expansion.5 [10] The Police Investigation Department (PID), within the Ministry of Justice, reviews complaints against police officers and may impose disciplinary measures or recommend indictments. [Ibid 2.1, DOS]. [11] The Ministry of Immigrant Absorption offers a number of services to settle newly arrived immigrants through 26 different departments. These departments include: ... Community Absorption Department, which specializes in socio-cultural and linguistic integration, 'while encouraging ethnic cultural expression and cultivation of community-based leadership'. The Ministry of Immigrant Absorption offers services in Russian.6 [12] The Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI) deals with complaints from Israelis who believe their human rights or civil liberties have been violated. In order to receive these complaints, ACRI operates a hotline available in Hebrew, Arabic and Russian (ACRI 2005, 18).7 [13] While there maybe incidents where the police failed to investigate complaints of assaults and the police have not interfered and made no arrests or have not shown interest in policing minority communities, local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection, unless they are part of a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection.8 No state can guarantee perfect protection.9 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police, and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact that the state's efforts are not always successful will not rebut the presumption of state protection.10 The claimant's (evidentiary) burden of proof is directly proportional to the level of democracy in the state in question: the more democratic the state's institutions, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to her.11 [14] The principal claimant also alleged persecution by the staff of the XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX. She was told that in a stern voice "You are not Jewish and you do not belong here". [15] While it may be true that obtaining legal services is beyond the claimants' financial ability and should not be burdened with such recourse, there is the Office of the Ombudsman. "In 1971, the Office of the Ombudsman was created in order to "examine complaints against government offices, state institutions, local governments, and certain other bodies referred to by the State Comptroller's Law", of which chapter seven provides a detailed description of the role and methodology of the Ombudsman's Office As a special unit within the State Comptroller's Office, the Office of the Ombudsman investigates some 7,000 complaints on an annual basis 'from every stratum of society,' particularly from more vulnerable groups such as new immigrants, the poor, minorities, and seniors . According to the Office of the Ombudsman, the Ombudsman is very accessible. Any person can file a complaint with the Ombudsman; a complaint may be written in any language, not necessarily in Hebrew. It is also possible to file a complaint via the internet or through one of the branch offices in Jerusalem, Tel Aviv and Haifa, and in the future also in Nazareth and Beer Sheva. The service provided by the Ombudsman is free of charge."12 [16] There is also the Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI) which deals with complaints from Israelis who believe their human rights or civil liberties have been violated.13 [17] Country conditions documentation also confirms that "Reports of discrimination against immigrants from the former Soviet Union (FSU) were scarce among the sources consulted by the Research Directorate. It is estimated that there are some one million immigrants from the former Soviet Union (FSU) living in Israel. Among these immigrants, there are approximately 300,000 people who, according to the Orthodox religious authorities, are not Jewish under Jewish law. According to Elazar Leshem, a professor of social work at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem who has studied Russian gentiles in Israel, Russian immigrants see Judaism as a nationality, not a religion. In Russia, they were seen as Jews, they were mistreated as Jews, and they suffered as Jews. It's only when they come to Israel that they discover that they're not Jews, they're Russians.14 [18] The country conditions documentation confirms that Israel is a multiparty parliamentary.15 Israeli law provides for an independent judiciary and the government generally respected this in practice.16 Israel is a democratic country with an independent judiciary where security forces are under civilian control.17 The presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens underscores the principle that international protection comes into play only when a refugee claimant has no other recourse available. I find that the claimant has not provided clear and convincing evidence to rebut the presumption of the state's ability to protect its citizens.18 Section 97(1)(b) [19] Given that the claimants have failed to rebut the presumption of state protection, I find that, on a balance of probabilities, the claimants are not persons in need of protection under section 97(1)(b), as there is no risk to their lives or a risk of unusual and cruel treatment or punishment, should they return to Israel. There was also no evidence adduced that would support a finding that the claimants face a danger of torture under section 97(1)(a). Return to Israel [20] The principal claimant testified that she personally attended the Israeli passport office in Toronto and submitted their applications for new external passports19 on XXXXX, 2009, which were allegedly returned to her the following day without being processed. The claimants' counsel sent a follow up letter on XXXXX, 2009 to the Consulate General of Israel inquiring as to the claimants' right to go back to Israel after filing refugee claims in Canada. To date, the panel has not been advised of any response from the Israeli passport office. [21] The country conditions documentation states that "citizens of Israel who seek to obtain refugee status in another country will not have their passports extended 'without prior approval of the Ministry of Interior or the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Israel'. Refugee claimants are not eligible to receive a new Israeli passport. During a 24 January 2006 telephone interview with the Research Directorate, a consular official from the Embassy of Israel in Ottawa stated that Israeli citizens whose refugee claims are rejected would be issued a one-way travel to Israel document. Israeli citizens who, for whatever reason, are not entitled to an Israeli passport, can obtain a 'one-time travel document,' valid for seven days 'only in places which have direct flights to Israel' in order to return to Israel. In order to obtain the one-way travel document, Israelis must present themselves to the nearest Israeli mission where they must submit: two photographs, a completed copy of the 'registration form for an Israeli national abroad' as well as the 'application form for a passport or travel document', and an administrative fee. Once in Israel, they must go to the Ministry of Interior to apply for the renewal of their passport. Although each case is dealt with separately, the Ministry of the Interior can choose not to issue a new passport in cases where one sought to obtain refugee status abroad; in this case, a 'travel document in lieu of a passport' may instead be issued, so that the holder may exit Israel if they wish."20 Conclusion [22] Based on the foregoing analysis and considering the totality of the evidence, I find that there is not a reasonable chance or serious possibility of persecution, if the claimants were to return to Israel. I also find that, on a balance of probabilities, the claimants will not be personally subjected to a risk of torture, a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, if they return to Israel. Accordingly, their claims are rejected.
