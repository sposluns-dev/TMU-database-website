Motifs et décision Person(s) who is(are) the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX Personne(s) en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, Ontario Appel instruit à Date of decision August 10, 2016 Date de la décision Panel Roslyn Ahara Tribunal Counsel for the person(s) who is(are)the subject of the appeal Evguenia Rokhline Barrister and Solicitor Conseil(s) du (de la/des) personne(s) en cause Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX (the Appellant), who is a citizen of Ukraine, is appealing the decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) denying his claim for refugee protection. New evidence with respect to the Board's NDP package has been submitted, which will be analyzed below. The Appellant is asking the RAD to allow the appeal. DETERMINATION [2] This appeal is dismissed. Pursuant to subsection 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD. Background [3] The Appellant alleges that he cannot return to Ukraine as he fears persecution based on his religious denomination, namely, Jehovah's Witnesses. ROLE OF RAD [4] The Appellant has made no submissions on the Role of the RAD. . However, the RAD finds that the recent Federal Court of Appeal decision, in Huruglica,1 provides us with guidance in relation to the appropriate standard that must be applied by a specialized tribunal such as the RAD to decisions of a first-level administrative tribunal. The justices stated at paragraph 103: [103] I conclude from my statutory analysis that with respect to findings of fact (and mixed fact and law) such as the one involved here, which raised no issue of credibility of oral evidence, the RAD is to review RPD decisions applying the correctness standard. Thus, after carefully considering the RPD decision, the RAD carries out its own analysis of the record to determine whether, as submitted by the appellant, the RPD erred. Having done this, the RAD is to provide a final determination, either by confirming the RPD decision or setting it aside and substituting its own determination of the merits of the refugee claim. It is only when the RAD is of the opinion that it cannot provide such a final determination without hearing the oral evidence presented to the RPD that the matter can be referred back to the RPD for redetermination. No other interpretation of the relevant statutory provisions is reasonable. SUBMISSIONS BY THE APPELLANT * Did the RPD fail to consider the most recent NDP report? * Did the RPD err when it found that state protection was adequate? * Did the RPD err when it required the Appellant to seek additional protection after the police failed to provide protection? * Did the RPD err in its assessment of the Appellant's credibility by ignoring evidence contradicting the RPD's finding? Most recent NDP report [5] The RPD cited the 2014 DOS report in its decision as this was the National Documentation Package (NDP) in front of it at both sittings of this claim held on January 13, 2016 and February 3, 2016. The RPD's decision was rendered on May 02, 2016. Meanwhile, a new NDP package came into effect on April 29, 2016. Counsel submits that the RPD ought to have considered this package as it came into effect prior to the decision being rendered. With due respect, the RAD finds that the RPD did not err in this regard, as it would have had to disclose this package to counsel prior to citing its contents. [6] The RAD finds that the Appellant has not made a proper application to submit new evidence; however, given the nature of this evidence, the RAD will allow it to be admitted into the record. [7] The RAD finds that it is possible that counsel was unaware of its availability prior to the rejection of the claim, and therefore, the RAD finds that it meets the statutory provisions as set out in ss. 110(4) of the IRPA. [8] Having admitted this document, however, the RAD finds that the Appellant has not pointed out wherein this document, the changes relate to the Appellant's claim of persecution, based on his religion. In fact, although the Appellant has not cited the page in the DOS report, the RAD has located the passage wherein it states "but rarely took action to investigate and punish abuses committed by security forces."2 Counsel simply cites pp. 211-286. The RAD finds that the reference to the foregoing quotation is in regards to impunity for abuses by law enforcement, but makes no mention of the religious component, which is the material aspect of this claim. The Appellant fears persecution due to his practice of Jehovah's Witnesses. The RAD, therefore, finds that this citation is not relevant to the outcome of the claim as it is not in reference to the religious ground of this claim. [9] The RAD has also considered the submissions by counsel that the RPD quotes the item 2.1 of the outdated NDP on eight [emphasis added] occasions. However, he fails to indicate what issues are in dispute between the 2014 and the 2015 report. The RAD finds the citations by the RPD are in relation to the following:3 * Ukraine is a republic with a presidential-parliamentary system of government. * The constitution provides for a directly elected president, a unicameral parliament, and a prime minister appointed by the president and confirmed by parliament. * The Minister of Internal Affairs is responsible for maintaining internal security and order and reports directly to the president and oversees police and other law enforcement personnel. * The parliamentary ombudsman for human rights also initiates investigations into reported problems and abuses by security forces. * The overall findings in the evidence is that the security forces generally prevented or responded to societal violence * Civilian authorities maintained control over law enforcement agencies and took action to investigate and punish abuses committed by security forces. * The government provided training to law enforcement personnel to curb abuses and NGOs provided human rights training. * There are reports of security forces committing human rights abuses and acting with impunity; however it appears that these were committed to further the political interest of the presidential administration during the Maidan protests. [10] The RAD notes that although the RPD cited the foregoing, it went on to state that there was scare objective evidence to support a finding that these abuses are being committed against Jehovah's Witnesses or that police are systemically refusing to investigate crimes committed against Jehovah's Witnesses in the area in which the Appellant resides. [11] The RAD concludes that the RPD considered the objective evidence with respect to the grounds of persecution, namely religion, and much of its evidence came from item 12.1 and the Appellant's testimony. Accordingly, the RAD finds that the newer NDP package has no effect on the material aspect of the claim. Objective evidence of religious-based violence against Jehovah's Witnesses [12] The RPD found that the documentary evidence indicated that the government generally respected religious freedom.4 Although the RPD acknowledged that there were societal abuses and/or discrimination, it found that this was neither widespread nor systemic and tended to be concentrated in Eastern Ukraine and Crimea, where the current conflict has led to an overall atmosphere of violence and instability.5 The RPD also noted that the reports indicate that the majority of abuses were committed against members of the Jewish and Muslim communities, as opposed to acts against Jehovah's Witnesses.6 It did, however, acknowledge the unequal treatment by authorities in their allocation of land for churches and the government's refusal to issue visas to foreign religious workers.7 However, the RPD concluded that these issues did not objectively satisfy the Appellant's allegations, as they failed to rise to the level of persecution and the government is attempting to rectify these concerns.8 [13] The RPD acknowledged reports of vandalism and arson to Jehovah's Witnesses Kingdom Halls; however, it further noted that the actual number of reports was infrequent. It cited statistics in this regard from 1991 to 2011, which was less than 0.01 percent. It did acknowledge an increase between 2012 and 2013, however, the RPD was not alarmed by this increase as it was concentrated in the war torn Donetsk Region.9 [14] The RPD noted reports of vandalism tended to be concentrated in Eastern regions affected by the war. However, the RPD considered the fact that 44 of these acts identified involved only the breaking of a window, and it was unclear if the acts of violence were motivated by religious intolerance, general civil unrest or accidental.10 [15] The RPD found that reports of physical assault and abuse against members of the Jehovah's Witnesses congregations were also infrequent, notably less than 0.01 per cent for most years. Based on this evidence, the RPD found that this did not establish a pattern of abuse or discrimination that would objectively satisfy, on a balance of probabilities, that Jehovah's Witnesses face persecution in Ukraine.11 State Protection [16] The RPD noted that the onus of establishing that protection would not be available rests with the Appellant. The RPD asserts that the Appellant cannot overcome the burden simply because he believes that the state will not offer him protection, but rather he must also establish this with objective evidence. The RPD stated that this evidence can be in the form of country documents, evidence of other similarly situated persons who did not receive protection or through the Appellant's own first hand experiences with authorities. However, the Appellant must provide clear and convincing evidence that the state will not provide adequate protection. [17] The RPD described Ukraine as a republic with a presidential-parliamentary system of government. It concluded that the overall finding was that the security forces generally prevented or responded to societal violence and that civilian authorities maintained control over law enforcement agencies and took action to investigate and punish abuses committed by security forces (government provided training to law enforcement personnel and NGOs provided human rights training). The RPD concluded that the security forces, including the police, provide adequate and effective protection in response to societal violence and that the government is making efforts to further improve law enforcement capabilities.12 [18] The RPD did acknowledge that there are reports of security forces committing human rights abuses and acting with impunity, however it noted that it appeared that these acts were committed to further the political interests of the presidential administration during the Maidan protests. More importantly, the RPD stated that there is scarce objective evidence to support a finding that these abuses are linked to Jehovah's Witnesses or that police are systemically refusing to investigate crimes committed against Jehovah's Witnesses. The RPD considered counsel's evidence from the European Association of Jehovah's Witnesses who allege that the police are not properly and effectively investigating criminal acts against Jehovah's Witnesses. However, it noted that the most recent reports of 2015 indicate that in each case, investigations were either ongoing or the case was before the courts.13 [19] The RPD also considered a report in the exhibit from counsel,14 wherein it indicates that the report concluded that justice was, in fact, served. [20] The RAD finds that most, if not all, of the above is contained in both NDP packages. It is particularly noteworthy that both packages contain scarce information with respect to crimes committed against Jehovah's Witnesses. Furthermore, the submission by the Appellant was addressed by the RPD when it acknowledged abuses on behalf of security forces. However, it found that these were likely committed to further the political interests of the presidential administration during the Maidan protests. Appellant's evidence with respect to the police [21] The RPD acknowledged that the Appellant alleged that the police did not respond to his concerns. However, it was further noted that the Appellant at no time brought his concerns further. It cited civilian authorities who maintain control over law enforcement agencies and do take action to investigate and punish abuses committed by security forces and noted that the Appellant never pursued these avenues. The Appellant is well-educated (university) and he was asked why he would not take his complaints to either a higher ranking office, the prosecutor's office or any other civilian agency. He provided two different responses: initially, that he believed that as a Jehovah's Witness his faith was being tested, and later, he explained that he did not report individual officers' actions to a higher oversight body because "the authorities are all the same in the Ukraine," indicating that they all discriminate against Jehovah's Witnesses. The RPD found that the latter explanation had not been established by the objective evidence. This led to the conclusion by the RPD that the Appellant had not provided clear and convincing evidence that Ukraine is unable to provide protection to Jehovah's witnesses or that Jehovah's witnesses face persecution in Ukraine. [22] Counsel submits that the RPD overlooked the fact that Ukraine is currently in the midst of a civil war that the country has lost control over a part of its territory, and therefore, the presumption of state protection in the case at bar is lower. It is further submitted that since the security forces are rarely investigated and punished by the civilian authorities, they act with impunity, and therefore, there is no adequate state protection at the operational level.15 It is submitted by the Appellant that he sought state protection on three separate occasions, to no avail. It is further argued that efforts on behalf of the state to curb abuses and to protect the state's citizens are insufficient to justify the availability of the protection. [23] It is argued that there is no requirement to seek additional protection after the police failed to provide protection. Moreover, there is no evidence that had the Appellant approached these authorities, the outcome would have been different. It is submitted that the Appellant believes that the level of corruption precludes any ability to obtain state protection. [24] Counsel further submits that the evidence from the Appellant's witness further corroborates the Appellant's allegations. On this issue, with due respect, this witness is from Crimea; the Appellant is not. Each claim must be decided on its own merits. Although counsel is correct that the RPD failed to comment on this testimony in its reasons, the RAD is required to conduct an independent assessment and in doing so, finds that the witness is not, in fact, a similarly situated person. [25] The Appellant submits that the RPD cited numbers of reports of vandalism and arson to Jehovah's Witnesses Kingdom Halls, but found the statistics showed less than 0.01 percent for most years. It is submitted that the RPD erred in this plausibility finding. [26] The Appellant further submits that although the RPD did not say so in its reasons, it is apparent that it preferred the evidence from the NDP package over the voluminous package submitted by the Appellant.16 [27] With due respect, upon review of the RPD's reasons, the RAD finds that the RPD considered both counsel's documentation and the NDP package. In fact, the RPD cited portions of counsel's corroborative evidence. For example, it did so, when citing statistics related to incidents which were committed against Jehovah's Witnesses. It found that attacks were concentrated in the war torn Donetsk Region. It also noted that there were reported acts of vandalism against Kingdom Halls, however after a careful analysis of these reports, many identified involved only the breaking of a window and it was unclear if the damage was motivated by religious intolerance, general civil unrest or accidental. [28] Upon review of the transcript of the proceedings, although the RPD accepted the credibility of the Appellant, the RAD finds that at least some of the testimony with respect to the incidents was speculative as to the cause, namely, the Appellant's religious affiliation. For example, when the Appellant attempted to approach five 18-year-olds who were drinking alcohol, the RAD concurs with the RPD that he was asking for trouble. The RAD finds that the Appellant's testimony with respect to the February 2015 incident raises concerns when the evidence reveals that the Appellant did not state in his Basis of Claim (BOC) that he had the licence number of the perpetrator and that the case is still not resolved. [29] More importantly, however, the RAD concurs with the RPD, that the Appellant is university educated. He would then presumably have the ability to research the authorities to which he could reach out to voice his complaints with respect to his perceived impunity on the part of the police. The RAD notes that the Appellant provided no explanation for not doing so, other than general statements that he had read in the newspaper and the internet, which led him to believe that state protection would not be forthcoming. [30] The RAD finds that it is important to reiterate some of the state protection principles as set out by the courts. To rebut the presumption of state protection, a refugee claimant must provide "clear and convincing" evidence of the state's inability to protect its citizens.17 A refugee claimant who alleges that state protection is inadequate must persuade the Board that the evidence establishes that the state protection is, in fact, inadequate. The evidence that state protection is inadequate must not only be reliable and probative, it must also satisfy the Board, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate.18 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact that the state's efforts are not always successful will not rebut the presumption of state protection.19 A refugee claimant cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state.20 Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection.21 [31] Refugee protection is meant to be a form of surrogate protection to be invoked only in those situations where the refugee claimant has unsuccessfully sought the protection of their home state. The onus is on the refugee claimant to approach the state for protection in situations where state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.22 In the absence of a compelling explanation, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim, at least where the state is a functioning democracy with a willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens.23 [32] The refugee claimant's burden of proof is directly proportional to the level of democracy in the state in question: the more democratic the state's institutions, the more the refugee claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them.24 In a functioning democracy, a refugee claimant will have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status.25 [33] A refugee claimant must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the refugee claimant's interactions with the authorities.26 Local failures by authorities to provide protection do not mean that the state as a whole fails to protect its citizens, unless the failures form part of a broader pattern of the state's inability or refusal to provide protection.27 No government is expected to guarantee perfect protection to all of its citizens at all times, and the fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens is not enough to justify a claim, especially where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authorities in place and is making serious efforts to protect its citizens.28 Less than perfect protection is not a basis to determine that a state is either unwilling or unable to offer reasonable protection.29 [34] Based on the foregoing principles as mandated by the Federal Court, the RAD finds that the Appellant by his own admittance never approached any higher authority to complain about the alleged inaction by the police. RAD'S CONCLUSIONS [35] After conducting an independent assessment of the entire record, the RAD finds the following: * Much of the Appellant's arguments are in relation to the civil unrest in Ukraine. * Since the claim was based on a religious ground, namely, that of Jehovah's Witnesses, the RPD carefully analyzed the religious reports and the RAD finds its conclusion absent of any error. * The RAD concurs with the RPD that there is widespread and systemic problems being concentrated in Eastern Ukraine and Crimea. * The RPD found that actual incidents of assaults on adherents to the Jehovah's Witnesses' faith was less than 0.01 percent for most years. The RAD concurs that this evidence was adduced by the Appellant. * The RPD found that there were mechanisms in place to address impunity by the police, which the Appellant had failed to address. The RAD concurs that the NDP provides evidence with respect to the higher authorities to which the Appellant could have turned. * The RPD considered the evidence from the European Association of Jehovah's Witnesses who alleges that the police are not properly and effectively investigating criminal acts against Jehovah's Witnesses. However, when analyzing these, the RAD concurs with the RPD that the most recent 2015 report indicated that in each case investigations were either ongoing or the case was before the courts. [36] The RAD concurs with the findings of the RPD. This claim is based on religious grounds, and the Appellant is not residing in the area where societal abuses tend to be concentrated. The RAD finds that the Appellant failed to discharge his burden of proof when he speculated in most of the incidents that he was targeted because of his religious affiliation. Moreover, he failed to follow up with any other authority notwithstanding his displeasure with what he perceived to be inaction by the police. The RAD further notes that, although not stated in the RPD's reasons, the Appellant failed to provide any court documents or police reports in support of his alleged personal circumstances. Upon review of the transcript, the RAD finds that he provided no explanation for this lack of corroborative evidence. [37] The RAD concludes that the submission by counsel for the Appellant of a similarly situated person by way of testimony from the witness has no merit. In the absence of the particulars of his refugee claim, coupled with the fact that he resided in Crimea, the RAD finds that little weight can be placed on this testimony. [38] The RAD finds that the RPD conducted a proper analysis of state protection and concluded, in the particular circumstances of this claim, that the Appellant had not provided clear and convincing evidence that Ukraine is unable to provide protection to Jehovah's Witnesses or that Jehovah's Witnesses face persecution in Ukraine. [39] For the foregoing reasons, the RAD finds the Appellant is not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. [40] This appeal is dismissed.
