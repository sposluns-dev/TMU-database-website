ANALYSIS Identity [33] The claimants' identity, including their citizenship, was established by the supporting documentation submitted, including a copy of their passports, the originals of which were seized by the Canada Border Services Agency (CBSA).1 Fears and credibility of the principal claimant (female claimant) [34] In light of the many gaps in the file, the panel is of the opinion that the female claimant did not discharge her burden of credibly establishing her allegations of encountering problems because of her Jewish- and Christian-sounding name and because of her father-in-law's political involvement. [35] At the hearing, the female claimant testified that she was ordered to appear at the police station on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and XXXX XXXX, 2012, which she did. She testified that she was arrested and detained twice in total, on those two dates, both times at the same police station. She testified that she was released the day after she was detained on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and the day after she was detained on XXXX XXXX, 2012. The female claimant testified clearly and repeatedly at the hearing that both times she was called into the police station, it was because her family name, XXXX, sounds Jewish or Christian. The female claimant stated at the hearing that it was the police who told her that people in her community filed a complaint against her because of her family name and felt that she should change it. She testified that even though she is Muslim and attends prayers on Friday, the neighbours think that she is a spy. It should be noted that in her written account annexed to the BOC Form, the female claimant clearly indicates that she was arrested by the police because of her name.2 [36] However, when claiming refugee protection at the port of entry, the female claimant stated only that it was [translation] "in connection with her husband" that she was detained by the police in Djibouti from XXXX XXXX to XXXX XXXX XXXX 2011, and from XXXX XXXX to XXXX XXXX, 2012.3 [37] When confronted with this significant contradiction, the female claimant stated that in fact it was the authorities who had caused her problems but that their real target was actually her husband; she was just part of the pretext. The panel does not accept her explanations. It is clear from her previous oral testimony and her written account that she was arrested because of her name. What is more, the female claimant clearly testified at the hearing (in the context of explaining contradictions addressed later in this decision) that both she was called in, she was the only one targeted, that it was her name alone that was on both notices to appear, but that her husband and son, XXXX, accompanied her to the station on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and her husband accompanied her to the station on XXXX XXXX, 2012. The hearing and the female claimant's written account indicate that the main reason she was called in those two times and arrested was because of complaints related to her family name. It was only at the port of entry that she stated it was because of her husband. The female claimant's credibility is undermined with respect to the notices to appear, arrests, and detentions on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and XXXX XXXX, 2012. [38] At the hearing, the female claimant stated and confirmed that she was the only one called in by the police on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and that the notice to appear was in her name only. However, in her written account, the female claimant indicates that on XXXX XXXX, 2011, she was called in to the station along with her husband and eldest son. When asked to explain that contradiction, the female claimant alleged that the notice to appear was in her name but that her husband and son accompanied her to the station. When confronted again with that contradiction, the female claimant stated that the notice to appear was in her name but that in order to go after her husband, the authorities came after her. The panel is not satisfied with those explanations. There is a clear difference between her written account and her oral testimony. Again, her credibility is undermined with respect to the notice to appear, arrest and detention on XXXX XXXX, 2011. [39] Furthermore, the objective documentary evidence does not support the female claimant's allegations that Jewish or Christian individuals have problems in Djibouti. The objective documentary evidence indicates that the constitution, laws and policies of the country protect freedom of religion and that, generally speaking, the government, in practice, respects the freedom of religion of its citizens and foreign residents. Although Islam is the state religion, there are no laws imposing sanctions on those who do not practise or respect Islamic beliefs or who practise other religions. There have been no reports of societal abuses or discrimination based on religious affiliation, belief or practice. The relationship among religious groups in society contributes to religious freedom. Only some representatives from Christian denominations have noted rare incidents of individual animosity toward non-Muslims.4 [40] It should be noted that this same objective documentary evidence indicates that social norms and customs discourage people from converting from Islam but that people have still converted. The female claimant's counsel submitted as objective documentary evidence exhibits C-13 to C-15, which are articles and reports on the Arabic and Christian minorities in Djibouti. However, it is clear that these documents are referring to citizens of Djibouti who have converted to Christianity from Islam, who have seemingly been rejected by their community, and must remain discreet. Immigrant or expatriate Christians are rarely limited in their activities.5 [41] However, the female claimant is neither Christian nor someone who has converted from Islam to Christianity. She stated that she is Muslim and that she attends prayer. Her allegations to the effect that she was arrested by the authorities in her country and that complaints were filed against her because of her family name that sounds Christian or Jewish, are not supported by the objective documentary evidence previously mentioned. When confronted with this in the room, the female claimant stated that she ran into problems in the area of XXXX where she lived, that these things had happened to her. However, as mentioned earlier, in light of significant contradictions, her credibility is undermined with respect to the alleged problems (notices to appear, arrests and detentions). [42] In light of all the above, the female claimant is not credible regarding her allegations of problems in her country because of her name that sounds Christian or Jewish. [43] In addition, as part of her refugee protection claim, the female claimant alleged that she is in danger of being persecuted, tortured, or killed by the authorities in her country because her father-in-law, XXXX XXXX XXXX, was involved in politics. She testified at the hearing that he was a member of the government under the first president, XXXX XXXX XXXX. She testified that he was the XXXX XXXX for four or five years and she believes that he began in 1985. He was XXXX XXXX XXXX in 1989 and could no longer fulfill his role. The female claimant testified that since the current president came to power in XXXX 1999, her father-in-law has criticized the regime; he did not vote in XXXX 1999-he supported the opposition. Because he did not vote, members of the government told him that he would have problems because of his previous political involvement. The female claimant testified that since XXXX 1999, her father-in-law had stopped participating in the political system and had kept his distance from politics. When asked whether he had ever gotten involved in politics again, the female claimant stated that he did not go back into politics but that, gradually, members from the previous government joined the opposition. However, her father-in-law never joined the opposition. When the panel confirmed that her father-in-law had never joined the opposition, the female claimant stated that after he had been pushed and threatened, he joined the opposition in 2011. She stated that it was the current government that had pushed and threatened her father-in-law. The panel then wanted to know why the current government had threatened him if he was not involved in politics, and the female claimant stated that it was because her father-in-law did not support the new president's government and he supported the opposition. She testified that her father-in-law joined the UAD, which she believes stands for Union alternance démocratique [union for democratic change]. She stated that she is not up to date on her father-in-law's political activities because she herself is not involved in politics and is not politically active. She stated that her father-in-law XXXX XXXX to the UAD; he was a consultant, but it was not an official position. He was active within the UAD until he died XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX 2013. [44] It should be noted that in the written account in her BOC Form, the female claimant states that she fears for her life in Djibouti because of her father-in-law's involvement in politics. Also, in paragraph 7 of her BOC Form, she states the following: [translation] In recent years, the authorities were not targeting my father-in-law directly. The threats against him focused on his family and my husband in particular. Six of his children are already refugees in Canada. During each arrest, the authorities referred to their father and insisted that he stop his political activities immediately, even though we knew he was not involved in the least. [45] At the hearing, the female claimant stated that the problems related to her father-in-law's political involvement began on XXXX XXXX, 2011, when she received a notice to appear from the police and that, the same day, she, her husband and their son, XXXX, were arrested and detained at the police station. However, according to the female claimant's subsequent testimony, it was actually only her name that appeared on the notice to appear issued by the police; she was called in and arrested because of her Jewish- or Christian-sounding name; and her husband and son, who decided to accompany her to the police station, were also arrested at the same time as the female claimant. Also according to the female claimant's oral testimony, the police told her husband to [translation] "stop what he and his father were doing." [46] According to the female claimant's written account, it was her husband who was targeted by the authorities because of his father's political activities, and her husband (as well as the female claimant and their son) was called in, arrested and detained by the police on XXXX XXXX, 2011. At the port of entry, the female claimant stated that she was detained on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and XXXX XXXX, 2012, [translation] "in connection with her husband." However, at the hearing, the female claimant put more emphasis on the fact that she alone was called in by the authorities on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and XXXX XXXX, 2012, because of her name, which sounds Jewish or Christian. She even testified that only her name appeared on the notices to appear, which she submitted as Exhibit C-6. [47] Also, as previously mentioned, there are significant contradictions regarding the supposed notices to appear, arrests and detentions on XXXX XXXX, 2011, and XXXX XXXX XXXX 2012, and they undermine the credibility of the female claimant's allegations to the effect that she or her husband encountered problems with the authorities because of her father-in-law's political activities. [48] The female claimant submitted no evidence establishing her father-in-law's alleged political activities, either as a member of the government of the first president of Djibouti or when he joined the UAD opposition in 2011. At the hearing, the female claimant explained to the panel that she did not find any documents on the Internet that mentioned her father-in-law, and she has no evidence of his political activities. When questioned as to whether she asked for such evidence-from the UAD, for example-she responded that she had not, stating that she did not think it was necessary, that she had not thought of it. The panel is not satisfied with her explanations and notes that the female claimant has a representative. Rule 11 of the Refugee Protection Division Rules requires that refugee protection claimants provide acceptable documents establishing their identity and other elements of the claim. The political activities of the female claimant's father-in-law are indeed central to her refugee protection claim. Therefore, it would have been relevant and important for the female claimant to make an effort to obtain documents establishing those political activities, which is not the case. [49] At the hearing, the female claimant's counsel submitted an affidavit sworn by XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, who is supposedly the female claimant's sister-in-law (her husband's sister). The female claimant's counsel indicated that the sister-in-law was supposed to testify, but since she could not, she swore an affidavit. In that affidavit, she states that she came to Canada with her sister because of her father's political problems, and they were recognized as Convention refugees. She states that the female claimant is her sister-in-law and that XXXX XXXX XXXX, the female claimant's husband, is her brother. [50] The female claimant testified at the hearing that her sister-in-law, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, came to Canada in 1998 and was granted refugee status. However, she did not submit any evidence, such as XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX birth certificate, to establish that she is her sister-in-law, stating that she did not know it was necessary. Although Ms. XXXX XXXX swore an affidavit, it is a very vague document that contains limited information and it has little probative value. Also, it should be noted that, at the hearing, the female claimant testified that her father-in-law has three children in total-her husband and her two sisters-in-law in Canada, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX However, according to her written account, six of her father-in-law's children are already refugees in Canada. In order to explain that contradiction, the female claimant stated that in her written account she included the children of her father-in-law's daughters-his grandchildren. The panel is not satisfied with this explanation, given that the female claimant attested that her BOC Form (including the written account) was complete, true and correct. [51] In any event, even if XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX is indeed the female claimant's sister-in-law and has been granted refugee status in Canada, this does not establish that the female claimant and her husband, many years later, encountered problems with the authorities in Djibouti because of her husband's father's political activities. [52] It should be noted that there are gaps in the evidence, including contradictions and a lack of corroborating evidence regarding the political activities of the female claimant's father-in-law, which is a central element in her claim. [53] In light of the preceding, the panel is of the opinion that the female claimant did not discharge her burden of credibly establishing the alleged facts, namely that her family is being targeted by the authorities in Djibouti because of her father-in-law's political activities and involvement. The female claimant did not credibly establish the fact that she or her husband were targeted by authorities in their country. [54] Given that the panel does not believe the female claimant's account regarding her alleged problems with the authorities in her country, it grants no probative value to the two notices to appear that she submitted as Exhibit C-6. Fear and credibility of the male claimant (female claimant's son) [55] The male claimant alleges that he was targeted by the authorities in Djibouti because of his political activities. However, because of numerous significant contradictions and omissions in his evidence, the panel finds that he is not credible and does not believe his account. The reasons are as follows. [56] At the hearing, the male claimant testified that he had been politically active since some young people at his school approached him in XXXX 2013. He stated that since XXXX 2013 he has been part of a group called MJO and stated that it stands for Mouvement des jeunes opposants. He testified that he was involved with the MJO until he left the country. He stated that MJO's goals include promoting democracy, human rights and freedom of expression. He testified that his role in the group was to distribute pamphlets and provide water to protestors. Also, the male claimant testified that, in order to meet its goals, the MJO organized protests so it would gain recognition and acceptance. [57] However, in the refugee protection claim he made at the port of entry, the male claimant did not declare that he was a member of a political organization. In fact, in his IMM 5669 form (Schedule A), in question 9, which asks what organizations he has supported, been a member of or been associated with, the male claimant states: [translation] "None."6 [58] When asked to explain this very significant omission, which is central to his refugee protection claim, the male claimant responded that when he had filled out the forms at the port of entry, he did not understand the questions and there was no interpreter at the port of entry. The panel does not accept the male claimant's explanation. When he filled out his BOC Form, he did not use an interpreter and he signed a declaration in his BOC Form to the effect that the information he was providing was complete, true and correct; that he is able to read French; and that he had fully read and fully understood the entire content of that form and all attached documents. What is more, at the beginning of the hearing, the male claimant confirmed to the panel that he could read and understand French when he filled out his BOC Form. The male claimant's credibility about his political involvement with the MJO is undermined. [59] In his written account and at the hearing, the male claimant stated that, because of his political activities, he was arrested and detained by the authorities in his country on three occasions (XXXX XXXX, 2013; XXXX XXXX, 2013; and XXXX XXXX, 2013) for 48 hours each time. However, when claiming refugee protection at the port of entry, he did not declare that he had been arrested or detained. In fact, in form IMM 008 (Schedule 12), in question 9, he was asked if he had ever been sought, arrested or detained by the police or military or any other authorities in any country. The male claimant indicates that he had not. In response to that same question, the male claimant states that his father had been involved with the police in Djibouti. It should also be noted that in form IMM 5669, in response to question 6(j), which asks if he has ever been detained, incarcerated or put in jail, the male claimant indicates that he had not. [60] When confronted with his major omission-that is, his failure to declare at the port of entry that he had been arrested or detained by the authorities in his country, as he later alleges in his BOC Form and then at the hearing-the male claimant alleged that he made those statements at the port of entry because he did not want to go back to his country. The male claimant also explained that he did not want to make a bad impression on the Canadian authorities. The panel does not accept his explanations. The male claimant came to Canada, with his mother, to claim refugee protection at the port of entry. Consequently, he should have declared from the outset to the Canadian authorities from whom he was requesting refugee status that he had been arrested and detained by the authorities in his country. It would have lent support to his refugee protection claim. His credibility with respect to the arrests and detentions at the hands of the authorities in Djibouti is seriously undermined. [61] It should be noted here that the female claimant intervened following her son's explanations regarding his failure to declare at the port of entry that he had been arrested and detained by the authorities in Djibouti. The female claimant stated that they filled out the forms at the port of entry on their own and that perhaps they had not fully understood. However, the panel notes that the female claimant's intervention and explanation are inconsistent with her son's explanation. He stated that he did not want to declare his arrests and detentions at the port of entry because he did not want to return to his country, and did not want to make a bad impression on Canadian authorities. [62] In his written account sent to the panel on June 3, 2014, the male claimant stated that he was arrested for a third time and taken to the station on XXXX XXXX, 2013. However, in the copy of his passport that the panel received from the CBSA, on page 7 specifically, there is an exit stamp from the Republic of Djibouti, XXXX checkpoint, dated XXXX XXXX, 2013, and an entry stamp, from the same checkpoint, dated XXXX XXXX XXXX 2013. This demonstrates that, on a balance of probabilities, the male claimant was outside of Djibouti between XXXX XXXX, 2013, and XXXX XXXX XXXX 2013, which contradicts his allegations in his BOC Form to the effect that he was detained in his country on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2013. [63] The day of the hearing, August 28, 2014, the claimants' counsel submitted, as Exhibit C-26, an addendum to the male claimant's written account, signed by the female claimant. In that addendum, the female claimant states that her son signed his written account in his BOC Form by himself and that she did not have the opportunity to re-read her son's BOC Form. As they were preparing, she noticed an important error in her son's written account: he failed to mention a series of events that took place during his arrest on XXXX XXXX XXXX 2013, which is described in paragraph 7 of his written account. In the addendum submitted at the hearing, the female claimant states that after her son was released from his second detention on XXXX XXXX, 2013, she sent him to Somalia to stay with extended family. He was supposed to stay for one month, but without informing the female claimant, he returned to Djibouti on XXXX XXXX, 2013. He was arrested on XXXX XXXX, 2013. After he was released, the male claimant went home and the female claimant learned what had happened. After conducting some research, they went to the border checkpoint on XXXX XXXX to pick up the male claimant's passport, which had been confiscated when he was arrested upon entry. [64] At the hearing, the male claimant stated that he was on vacation in Somalia on XXXX XXXX, 2013. On XXXX XXXX, 2013, when he entered Djibouti, there was a photograph of him at the border. He was arrested at the XXXX border. When confronted with the fact that his passport contained an exit stamp from Djibouti dated XXXX XXXX, 2013, and an entry stamp to the country on XXXX XXXX, 2013, but no entry stamp for XXXX XXXX, 2013, the male claimant testified that when the soldiers arrested him at the border, they confiscated his passport. After 48 hours, he was released and went to see his family. They went to various police stations looking for his passport. They were told that he had to pick up his passport where it had been confiscated. The male claimant stated that an entry stamp, dated XXXX XXXX, 2013, had been added to his passport. That is the day he went to the border to pick up his passport. [65] When confronted with the fact that the male claimant had made a major amendment to his written account at the very last minute, the day of the XXXX XXXX, 2014, hearing, he responded that he had forgotten and then found the error in his initial written account. The panel is not satisfied with his explanation. The male claimant and his mother had secured representation months before the hearing, at least since the day they submitted their BOC Forms on XXXX XXXX, 2014. If it were true that there had been such a significant error in the male claimant's BOC Form, it would have been reasonable to make that declaration long before the day of the hearing. His credibility with respect to his alleged detention on XXXX XXXX, 2013, is undermined. What is more, the panel concludes that it makes no sense that, as alleged by the male claimant at the hearing, the authorities would have decided to stamp his passport with an entry stamp dated XXXX XXXX, 2013, when he went to pick it up at the border when he actually entered the country on XXXX XXXX, 2013, was detained and released two days later. [66] Three times during the hearing, the male claimant stated that the first protest he participated in was on XXXX XXXX, 2013, the same day as the legislative election. However, in paragraph 2 of his written account, he indicates the following: [translation] "I participated in some protests on Fridays, after prayers, but I was never caught by the police. But, on XXXX XXXX, 2013, the day of the municipal election, I truly feared for my life." According to his written account, the protest on XXXX XXXX, 2013, was not the first protest the male claimant participated in, contrary to his statements at the hearing. When asked to explain this significant contradiction, the male claimant responded that the protests on Fridays took place after prayers and that he was behind the sheiks. The panel is not satisfied with that explanation. Once again, his credibility regarding his political activities is undermined. [67] It should also be noted that while the male claimant testified that XXXX XXXX, 2013, was the date of the legislative election, he states in his written account that it was the date of the municipal election. In order to explain that contradiction, the male claimant stated that he made a mistake in his BOC Form. However, he signed a declaration on his BOC Form to the effect that the information in the form was complete, true and correct; he also attested to that fact at the beginning of the hearing. Also, according to his allegations, he was politically active in his country, belonged to a political group, and was calling for change in his country. It is therefore reasonable to expect that he would have realized sooner that he had made a significant error in his written account with respect to a municipal versus legislative election. This does not help his credibility. [68] At the hearing, the male claimant stated that on XXXX XXXX, 2013, he was arrested by the police and detained at the downtown station for 48 hours. However, in his written account, he states that he was released that same night, on XXXX XXXX, 2013, which is far less than 48 hours. In order to explain that contradiction, the male claimant stated that he had made a mistake. The panel is not satisfied with his explanation given that the male claimant had previously attested to the fact that his written account was complete, true and correct. Once again, his credibility is undermined. [69] In light of the aforementioned gaps, including significant contradictions and omissions, the panel finds that the male claimant is not credible, overall. The panel does not believe his account. It does not believe that he was politically active in his country or that he was targeted by the authorities in Djibouti. [70] It should be noted that at the end of the hearing, during questioning from his counsel, the male claimant alleged that members of the MJO are angry at him because he left the group. However, the panel does not believe that he was part of the MJO or that he was politically active. [71] Finally, the male claimant alleged that all young men in Djibouti are persecuted. However, those allegations are not supported by the objective documentary evidence7 and the male claimant did not submit evidence to that effect. Fear concerning the female claimant's minor daughter: genital mutilation [72] In her BOC Form and at the hearing, the female claimant alleged that she fears that her daughter will be subjected to genital mutilation. She declared that both she and her husband are opposed to their daughter being subjected to genital mutilation; however, her husband's grandmother, the matriarch of the clan engaging in the practice, absolutely intends to circumcise her. The grandmother vowed that their daughter would continue the family tradition and that she would be kidnapped if necessary. The female claimant testified that the grandmother could also send other individuals to circumcise the girl. All of the women in her husband's family were circumcised at the age of 13. [73] With respect to her fear for her daughter, the female claimant's oral testimony and BOC Form contained no contradictions or omissions. Her testimony on this point was spontaneous and fluid. The female claimant was credible when she stated that her daughter's paternal family wants to have her circumcised. [74] The objective documentary evidence8 also indicates that female genital mutilation (FGM) is a nearly universal practice in Djibouti (approximately 93% of women between the ages of 14 and 45 have been mutilated). The most extreme forms of mutilation are the most common in Djibouti. Although the government launched campaigns against this practice, they were unsuccessful. Communities as a whole must decide to abandon the practice, not just a few individuals. [75] In societies where genital mutilation is practised, both men and women support it, typically without question. It is often acknowledged that men will only marry a woman who has been circumcised. Members of the extended family are usually involved in making the decision about genital mutilation. [76] The panel is of the opinion that it would be difficult to prevent the paternal family from getting involved in and managing the minor claimant's affairs because she comes from a traditional family in which all girls and women have been circumcised. It is a question of family honour. [77] The panel is of the opinion that there is no reasonable internal flight alternative for the minor claimant because Djibouti is a very small country and genital mutilation is a practice that is widely supported by the public, particularly the members of her paternal family. It is reasonable to think that the members of the extended family would be able to find her anywhere in Djibouti. [78] In addition, there is no adequate state protection, as the objective documentary evidence9 indicates that although genital mutilation has been prohibited by law since 1992, incidents are generally not reported, prosecuted, or punished. The government has not taken sufficient sustained and systematic action to modify or eliminate stereotypes, discriminatory cultural values, and harmful practices such as FGM. [79] For these reasons, the panel concludes that the minor claimant, XXXX XXXX XXXX, would face a serious possibility of persecution if she were to return to Djibouti. Genital mutilation is indeed a form of persecution. Finally, it should be pointed out that the record includes a medical report, submitted as Exhibit C-25, which attests to the fact that the minor claimant has not already been circumcised. [80] It should also be noted that at the end of the hearing, during his submissions, the claimants' counsel argued that the adult claimant (the female claimant) would face persecution within the meaning of section 96 of the IRPA or be personally subjected to a danger of torture, a risk to her life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment under subsection 97(1) of the IRPA because she is opposed to having her daughter circumcised. The female claimant's counsel argued that she would be at a higher risk of facing violence because she does not agree with her in-laws. In addition, the female claimant's counsel argued that the female claimant would be excluded from the family network and could experience other difficulties, such as economic hardship. [81] However, the adult claimant (the female claimant) did not indicate in her BOC Form or at the hearing that she fears for her safety because she is opposed to having her daughter circumcised. She spoke only of her fear that her daughter would be circumcised and the fact that she wants to protect her daughter from mutilation. The female claimant never raised the issue of persecution, threats, or other risks to her because of her in-laws or other individuals given that she opposes her daughter's genital mutilation. When the female claimant's counsel asked her what the consequences would be for her if she and her husband openly opposed her husband's grandmother, who wishes to circumcise their daughter, she stated that she cannot contradict the grandmother, that she could not speak openly and that she went through her husband. He has already spoken to his grandmother about their opposition to their daughter's circumcision, but the grandmother did not listen, saying that they needed to respect her age and wishes. [82] It should also be pointed out that the female claimant's husband is also opposed to the practice and that the female claimant has her husband's support in Djibouti. Also, when she was living there, she worked at a bank for nearly 30 years.10 She would not be excluded and would not be someone who would face difficulties, including economic hardship, that would constitute persecution under subsection 97(1) as her counsel argued. [83] In fact, the evidence on record does not lead the panel to conclude that the adult claimant would be subjected to a serious possibility of persecution if she were to return to her country based on the fact that she opposes her daughter's genital mutilation. In addition, the evidence on record does not lead to the conclusion that, on a balance of probabilities, she would be personally subjected to a danger of torture, a risk to her life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. CONCLUSION [84] The panel determines that the adult claimant and the male claimant (her son) did not establish that there is a serious possibility of persecution on Convention grounds or that, on a balance of probabilities, they would be personally subjected to a danger of torture, a risk to their lives or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if they were to return to their country. [85] However, the minor claimant would be subjected to a serious possibility of persecution if she were to return to her country.
