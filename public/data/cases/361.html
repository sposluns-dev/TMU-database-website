R. v. Ali

2022 BCCA 83 R. v. Ali COURT OF APPEAL FOR BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: R. v. Ali, 2022 BCCA 83 Date: 20220217 Docket: CA47959 Between: Regina Appellant And Hashim Ali Respondent Restriction on publication: A publication ban has been imposed under s. 486.5 of the Criminal Code restricting the publication, broadcasting or transmission in any way of evidence that could identify the victim in this case. Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Willcock The Honourable Madam Justice Fisher The Honourable Madam Justice DeWitt-Van Oosten On appeal from: An order of the Provincial Court of British Columbia, dated November 16, 2021 (sentence) (R. v. Ali, Richmond Docket 65099‑1). Oral Reasons for Judgment Counsel for the Appellant (via videoconference): M.B. Rankin Counsel for the Respondent (via videoconference): E. Warren Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia February 14, 2022 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, British Columbia February 17, 2022 Summary: This is a Crown appeal from a nine‑month conditional sentence for robbery using a weapon. The Crown says a conditional sentence was not available for the offence; the judge gave inadequate emphasis to the principles of deterrence and denunciation; and the sentence is demonstrably unfit. Held: application for leave to appeal granted; the appeal is allowed; the conditional sentence order is set aside; and the sentence is varied to an 18‑month term of conventional imprisonment, followed by two years' probation. Assuming, without deciding, that a conditional sentence was available at law, it is a demonstrably unfit sentence in the circumstances of this case. [1] DEWITT-VAN OOSTEN J.A.: Following a Provincial Court trial, Hashim Ali was found guilty of robbery contrary to s. 344(1)(b) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C‑46. He robbed a young woman who was using an automated teller machine ("ATM") and threatened to stab her with a screwdriver. [2] This offence carries a maximum punishment of life imprisonment. The generally applicable range of sentence for robbery involving overt violence, including the use of a weapon, is often stated as between two and nine years' imprisonment: R. v. Brogan, 1999 BCCA 278 at para. 10; R. v. Beguin, 2019 BCCA 50 at para. 19; R. v. Marks, 2016 BCCA 480 at para. 11; R. v. Weber, 2016 BCCA 279 at para. 16; R. v. Heatley, 2015 BCCA 453 at para. 24. [3] Of course, that range is not binding. A judge has the discretion to impose a sentence below or above the range, depending on the circumstances of the offence and the offender: Brogan at para. 10. Sentencing is a highly individualized inquiry and ranges function only as guidelines. For that reason, a decision to depart from a range does not by itself constitute legal error: R. v. Parranto, 2021 SCC 46 at para. 36. [4] In this case, the Crown identified the appropriate range specific to Mr. Ali, a first‑time offender, as somewhere between 12 to 18 months' imprisonment, but said the sentence should fall at the higher end. Initially, Mr. Ali sought a conditional sentence. He later determined that a conditional sentence is likely precluded by s. 742.1(e)(iii) of the Criminal Code. As a result, he sought a suspended sentence. In the further alternative, a conventional jail term of "time served" (the equivalent of 69 days). Finally, if that sentence was insufficient, Mr. Ali suggested a conventional jail term that did not exceed six months. [5] On November 16, 2021, the judge imposed a nine‑month conditional sentence, followed by 12 months' probation. Mr. Ali received credit for two months' pre‑sentence custody. For the first three months of the conditional sentence he was made subject to house arrest, with limited exceptions for leaving the residence. Mr. Ali was ordered to perform 100 hours of community work service. He is currently serving the conditional sentence. [6] The Crown appeals the sentence, contending that: (1) a conditional sentence is not available for a robbery involving a weapon; (2) the judge erred in principle by giving insufficient weight to denunciation and deterrence; and (3) the sentence is demonstrably unfit. The Crown asks that Mr. Ali's sentence be varied to 18 months' conventional imprisonment, followed by two years' probation. [7] For the reasons that follow, I would grant leave to appeal, allow the appeal and vary the sentence as requested by the Crown. Circumstances of the Offence [8] The robbery occurred the evening of November 3, 2020. Mr. Ali approached a young woman using an ATM on the University of British Columbia campus. It was approximately 8:45 p.m. She was alone. Mr. Ali came up from behind, pressed a screwdriver into her side and demanded that she give him money. He asked: "Do you want to get stabbed?" He sounded desperate and pushy. [9] The victim could see a black‑handled tool with a silver point sticking out from Mr. Ali's sleeve. He told her to input her personal identification number. He then took control of the victim's hand and manipulated it, forcing her to withdraw $400 from the ATM. The victim was crying hysterically. [10] Mr. Ali fled the scene with the money. The victim followed him and called out for help. A civilian witness joined the pursuit and maintained continuity of Mr. Ali, who was seen to collect a backpack from some bushes, change his clothing and discard a toque. The police had been called and Mr. Ali was arrested. The $400 was not recovered. However, Mr. Ali had a screwdriver. The toque was located and contained his identification. [11] Identity was the main issue at the trial. Mr. Ali testified. He denied committing the offence. He said he was on campus because he was sightseeing. He carried a change of clothes because of inclement weather. He had the screwdriver for work purposes. The judge rejected his testimony. [12] Understandably, the victim was significantly impacted by the robbery. In recognition of that fact, she testified behind a screen at the trial. The offence affected her mental and physical well‑being, resulting in fear, loss of motivation for school, reduced social interaction, sleep deprivation and increased anxiety when outside of her home. She lost the $400 and she missed work because of the trauma. [13] At the time of the offence, Mr. Ali was subject to a court order arising from another matter. That order, dated September 15, 2020, stipulated that he not "possess any weapons as defined in the Criminal Code". Mr. Ali does not dispute that a screwdriver used to threaten someone during a robbery is a weapon. Circumstances of the Offender [14] A pre‑sentence report was prepared for sentencing. Mr. Ali is 30 years old. He declined to provide collateral contacts to the author of the report. This meant his background could not be independently verified. The Crown did not allege a criminal record. [15] Mr. Ali came to Canada from Pakistan three years before the offence. He says he fled Pakistan because of conflict with his extended family over land and danger to his life. Mr. Ali has claimed refugee status in Canada. He describes emotional and financial hardship after arriving here. He resides in the Downtown Eastside of Vancouver and receives social assistance. He does not maintain contact with his family. [16] He has a grade 12 education. He took some college courses, but stopped participating due to lack of motivation. The pre‑sentence report stated that he has not been employed since arriving in Canada. (It later came to light that Mr. Ali worked periodically between April 2019 and February 2020. However, his work permit had expired and had not been renewed.) Mr. Ali told the author of the pre‑sentence report that he does not suffer from substance abuse or mental health issues. At the time of the offence, he was in a difficult relationship with an intimate partner who was struggling with substance abuse. [17] The judge asked whether the robbery conviction would result in deportation. She was told it would not. Instead, it might delay Mr. Ali's application for refugee status. (This is another matter about which there is clarifying information. Mr. Ali has since been told he is inadmissible to Canada because of his conviction.) The judge gave Mr. Ali an opportunity to provide character references or other collateral information for her consideration. He declined to do so. Reasons of the Sentencing Judge [18] After reviewing Mr. Ali's background and the impact of the offence, the judge set out the principles she was obliged to consider at sentencing. She identified the predominant principles in this case as denunciation, deterrence and the acknowledgement of responsibility. [19] The judge then turned her mind to aggravating and mitigating circumstances. Aggravating circumstances included: the robbery occurred at night; it was committed against a vulnerable young woman who was alone; Mr. Ali used a weapon and threatened to cause her bodily harm; and he stole money from the victim, which had not been returned. The offence also had a significant impact on the victim, sufficient to meet the test for a deemed aggravating factor under s. 718.2(a)(iii.1) of the Criminal Code. Finally, Mr. Ali was subject to a court order that prohibited him from possessing a weapon. [20] The judge described the robbery as very serious. She found that Mr. Ali had a high degree of moral blameworthiness. Although this could have been a crime of opportunity, the circumstances also suggested advance planning. [21] There was only one mitigating factor of substance, namely, the absence of a criminal record. Mr. Ali was released on bail for the offence in December 2020 and complied with those terms. There was no demonstrated history of a propensity for violence. The judge found it likely that Mr. Ali would comply with court orders in the future. However, at the same time, she noted that she knew: little about his background and [knew] nothing about his motivation to commit the crime. [She knew] nothing about good prospects in the community, other than his landlord [would] allow him to return to his house. [She knew] of no unfortunate personal circumstances, other than that he is a recent refugee to Canada, that make him a sympathetic offender in that sense. There is no suggestion that drugs or alcohol were involved in this matter. Mr. Ali's prospects for rehabilitation were a "complete unknown". At the time of sentencing, he "appear[ed] to completely lack any motivation to pursue studies or seek employment." [22] Both the Crown and the defence provided the judge with case authorities to assist in determining a fit sentence. She concluded that "something in the mid‑provincial range" would be appropriate. [23] Ultimately, the judge decided that a sentence of 11 months' imprisonment was a fit sentence, with credit for two months' pre‑sentence custody. She considered a conditional sentence to be the "most appropriate" sentence on the bases that it has punitive aspects, would likely deter Mr. Ali and others of like mind as long as it contained strict conditions, and it could be "used for him to become engaged with his community" through work service or meeting with a conditional sentence supervisor. A conditional sentence would also assist Mr. Ali in "recognizing the harm he has done to the [victim] and allow rehabilitation, if that is even necessary." The judge found that having Mr. Ali serve his sentence in the community would not endanger the public. Discussion Standard of Review [24] Sentences attract considerable deference on appeal. Appellate intervention is only justified where the appellant establishes an error in principle that impacted the sentence or the sentence is shown to be demonstrably unfit: R. v. Lacasse, 2015 SCC 64 at paras. 41-44. [25] Errors in principle include errors in law, failures to consider relevant factors, and the erroneous consideration of an aggravating or mitigating factor. A demonstrably unfit sentence is a sentence that constitutes a "clearly unreasonable" departure from the fundamental principle of proportionality: Lacasse at paras. 53-55. [26] Where the test for appellate intervention is satisfied, the appeal court will perform its own sentencing analysis with deference to any factual findings made by the sentencing judge that are not tainted by legal error: R. v. Friesen, 2020 SCC 9 at paras. 27-29; Parranto at paras. 29, 30, 36 and 122. Application of Standard to this Case [27] The Crown seeks appellate intervention on three bases. [28] First, it says a conditional sentence for robbery using a weapon is prohibited by s. 742.1(e)(iii) of the Criminal Code: 742.1 If a person is convicted of an offence and the court imposes a sentence of imprisonment of less than two years, the court may, for the purpose of supervising the offender's behaviour in the community, order that the offender serve the sentence in the community, subject to the conditions imposed under section 742.3, if (e) the offence is not an offence, prosecuted by way of indictment, for which the maximum term of imprisonment is 10 years, that (i) resulted in bodily harm, (ii) involved the import, export, trafficking or production of drugs, or (iii) involved the use of a weapon; [Emphasis added.] [29] The Crown contends that the constitutional declaration in R. v. Chen, 2021 BCSC 697, does not apply here. Chen struck the provision that precludes a conditional sentence for offences with a maximum of life imprisonment (s. 742.1(c)); however, that ruling did not engage s. 742.1(e)(iii). The Crown says that s. 742.1(e)(iii), properly interpreted, prohibits a conditional sentence for any offence prosecuted by indictment that involves the use of a weapon and carries a maximum imprisonment of 10 years or more. This would include robbery involving a weapon. As such, s. 742.1(e)(iii) functions as a secondary proscription against a conditional sentence for Mr. Ali's offence. [30] The Crown's second complaint is that the judge erred in principle by giving insufficient weight to the sentencing principles of denunciation and deterrence. Third, it says the sentence is demonstrably unfit. [31] Mr. Ali agrees with the Crown that a conditional sentence was not available because of s. 742.1(e)(iii). On that basis, he supports allowing the appeal. However, he asks that his sentence be reduced to the amount of time he has now served, plus probation. Alternatively, Mr. Ali seeks a custodial sentence of less than six months, followed by probation. He acknowledges that any such sentence would be "at the low end of the range," but says it is justified. Mr. Ali can seek a reduction of sentence without filing his own appeal on the authority of Hill v. R., [1977] 1 S.C.R. 827, 1975 CanLII 38. [32] I am not persuaded this would be an appropriate case in which to consider the statutory interpretation issue raised by the Crown. [33] First, at sentencing, the Crown's position on whether s. 742.1(e)(iii) precluded a conditional sentence was equivocal. The judge reasonably understood from the Crown's submissions that a conditional sentence was available at law and she did not provide a substantive analysis of the issue. Second, given Mr. Ali's position in the appeal, we would not receive full argument on the issue. Third, the Supreme Court of Canada is scheduled to hear an appeal in March 2022 that is likely to provide interpretive and constitutional guidance on s. 742.1. The federal government has also reintroduced what is now known as Bill C‑5, An Act to amend the Criminal Code and the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act, which seeks to narrow the proscription against the use of conditional sentences. That too may resolve the issue raised by the Crown. See the discussion in R. v. Merkel, 2021 BCCA 445 at paras. 92-93. [34] Finally, and in any event, I am satisfied this appeal should be allowed on the ground that a nine‑month conditional sentence is demonstrably unfit. It is therefore not necessary to address the interpretation issue or the alleged error in principle. Appellate Intervention Is Justified [35] The judge appropriately described Mr. Ali's offence as a very serious robbery. She also found he is an offender with a high degree of moral blameworthiness. The robbery involved a weapon and a threat of bodily harm. Mr. Ali attempted to avoid detection and discarded evidence as he fled the scene. He traumatized a young woman who was entitled to expect that her schooling environment would be safe, and his conduct profoundly disrupted her life. [36] Mr. Ali was a considerable distance from his home when he committed the offence. On the facts, he had no connection to the university campus and no reason to be there. He had a weapon with him in contravention of a court order, he used it at night, and he forced the victim to withdraw money while it must have been obvious to him that she was distraught and afraid. Nonetheless, he carried on. As the judge noted, there are features of the case consistent with premeditation. [37] At the time of sentencing, there was minimal mitigation weighing in Mr. Ali's favour. He had no criminal record, but could not claim the mitigating benefit of a guilty plea. Nor were there any mental health, substance use or cognitive issues providing an explanatory context for the choices he made or that properly enabled a finding of diminished moral blameworthiness. There was an indication of financial and emotional hardship since he arrived in Canada, but that background was not fleshed out in any way, at Mr. Ali's behest. Importantly, there was no established evidentiary link between Mr. Ali's personal circumstances and his motivation for the offence. The judge also acknowledged that she had no basis on which to meaningfully assess Mr. Ali's rehabilitative prospects. [38] Respectfully, it is difficult to understand how, in those circumstances, a nine‑month conditional sentence is "consistent with the fundamental purpose and principles of sentencing": Criminal Code, s. 742.1(a). There was little, if anything, before the judge that attenuated the gravity of the offence or the purposefulness and intentionality of Mr. Ali's violent conduct. [39] Instead, I agree with the Crown that the principles of denunciation and deterrence loomed large in this case: Criminal Code, ss. 718(a)-(b). To give those principles meaningful expression, as well as the aggravating factors and Mr. Ali's moral culpability, a conventional prison term was required. [40] As noted by the majority in Marks at para. 26: "Robbery with a weapon will generally attract a custodial sentence." There will, of course, be cases in which extenuating circumstances or diminished moral blameworthiness call for something other than that, but this is not one of them. [41] In my view, the sentence imposed here markedly departs from sentences imposed in like circumstances. Although I appreciate that no two cases will be exactly alike, I find the following decisions instructive. [42] In R. v. Owens, 2001 BCCA 465, the 21‑year‑old appellant was sentenced to 18 months' conventional imprisonment for a robbery using a starter pistol. With an accomplice, he robbed a gas station attendant. They wore balaclavas, threatened the attendant and fled with cigarettes, cash and personal items belonging to the attendant. The attendant was hit on the head with the pistol, necessitating stitches. Mr. Owens pleaded guilty, had a youth court record and was on probation. However, by the time of sentencing, he was abstaining from drugs and alcohol; had reconciled with his family; was in a training program; and was volunteering in his community. The 18‑month sentence was upheld on appeal. [43] In R. v. McPherson, 2003 BCCA 183, the 22‑year‑old appellant committed a robbery using a screwdriver as a weapon. He approached a 16‑year‑old outside of a convenience store, brandished the screwdriver, threatened the victim and forced him to withdraw $30 from an ATM. There was no physical harm, but the victim was emotionally traumatized. Mr. McPherson was intoxicated. The Court described the offence as "an act of stupidity to obtain money to continue drinking" (at para. 5). Mr. McPherson received the equivalent of 27 months' imprisonment with one year of probation. The sentence was upheld on appeal. Mr. McPherson had a criminal record. However, by the time of sentencing, he had taken rehabilitative steps. [44] In Marks, referred to earlier, this Court upheld a 12‑month conventional term of imprisonment for robbery. Mr. Marks entered a gas station, robbed the owner and his son of cigarettes, and pulled out what appeared to be a black handgun. He threatened to kill the victims. He was later apprehended at his home. He confessed. The gun was an imitation. Mr. Marks pleaded guilty. He was 18 at the time of the offence, with no criminal record. He expressed remorse and wrote a letter of apology to the victims. He suffered from a medical condition that led to him being bullied. He came into contact with a negative peer group and was threatened when he tried to disassociate. The robbery was likely motivated by threats to pay drug debts. He was on bail for approximately three years prior to sentencing and, during that time, he "made significant efforts to turn his life around" (at para. 10). Notwithstanding the considerable mitigation, the majority upheld the 12‑month prison term. [45] With reference to cases such as Owens, McPherson and Marks, the sentence sought by the Crown in this appeal is a proportionate sentence and, although at the lower end given the gravity of Mr. Ali's conduct, it is defensible with reference to the generally accepted approach to robbery using a weapon, including for first‑time offenders. It gives meaningful effect to the principles of denunciation and deterrence, both specific and general. As aptly noted in R. v. Reid, 1990 CanLII 1154 (B.C.C.A.): The use of threats of death or grave bodily injury for the purposes of robbery, or any other unlawful purpose, whether or not the person making the threats intends, or has the means, to carry them out, is rightly regarded as particularly reprehensible in our society. [Emphasis added.] [46] Mr. Ali is not a youthful offender. His offence included multiple aggravating features. The mitigation was nowhere near as substantial as in Marks. The judge made a finding of high moral blameworthiness. In light of that finding, there was nothing about the circumstances of the offence, or the background of Mr. Ali as presented to the judge, that justified a conditional sentence. Nor is the sentence that Mr. Ali proposes on appeal a fit sentence. It is far too lenient given his moral culpability. [47] I recognize that Mr. Ali's conviction carries collateral immigration consequences. His counsel has advised that because of the inadmissibility finding, Mr. Ali is likely subject to a deportation order. I also appreciate that if there is a right of appeal from the deportation order, it may be rendered meaningless by a custodial sentence of six months or more: Tran v. Canada (Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness), 2017 SCC 50 at para. 7. [48] However, the weight assigned to collateral consequences depends on the facts of the case. Collateral consequences are relevant to the determination of fitness; however, they cannot detract from the Court's responsibility to impose a sentence that gives sufficient recognition to the seriousness of the offence and the offender's moral blameworthiness: R. v. Pham, 2013 SCC 15 at para. 14. A court cannot impose (or substitute on appeal) an "inappropriate and artificial" sentence "to avoid collateral consequences which may flow from a statutory scheme or from other legislation, thus circumventing Parliament's will": Pham at para. 15; R. v. Gonzales, 2016 BCCA 436 at para. 30. In my view, that is what would happen here, were Mr. Ali to receive a sentence of less than six months' imprisonment. Disposition [49] For the reasons provided, I am of the view the nine‑month conditional sentence and one year of probation unreasonably departs from the fundamental principle of proportionality. [50] As such, I would grant the Crown leave to appeal, allow the appeal from sentence, set aside the conditional sentence order, and substitute an 18‑month conventional term of imprisonment, followed by two years of probation. A probation order of that length is justified given the unknown risk presented by Mr. Ali, the inability to assess his rehabilitative prospects, and the need for community-based supervision to protect the public. [51] Consistent with the determination of the sentencing judge, Mr. Ali would be given two months' credit for his pre‑sentence custody. This means that as at the date of the original sentence, Mr. Ali would have had 16 months' imprisonment to serve. [52] As explained in R. v. Nguyen, 2008 BCCA 252 at para. 53, when a custodial sentence is imposed by an appeal court in substitution for a conditional sentence, the offender will receive credit for the time spent on the conditional sentence. However, that time will not count towards statutory remission. As a result, the offender is likely to be released later than had the conventional prison term been imposed at sentencing. See also R. v. Andrew, 2008 BCCA 141. [53] Mr. Ali has served just under three months of his conditional sentence. As I understand it, for every portion of a month served, an incarcerated offender generally receives 15 days' remission. Accordingly, I would assign Mr. Ali a further 45 days' credit to reflect the time spent on his conditional sentence. [54] The sentence that would run from November 16, 2021, is therefore 14.5 months' imprisonment. The terms of the two‑year probation order would be the same as those imposed by the Court below. [55] WILLCOCK J.A.: I agree. [56] FISHER J.A.: I agree. [57] WILLCOCK J.A.: Leave is granted to the Crown to appeal the sentence, the appeal is allowed, the conditional sentence order is set aside, and a sentence of custodial imprisonment of 14.5 months running from November 16, 2021 is imposed after giving the offender the credit described in the reasons for judgment. That will be followed by a two‑year probation order on the terms set out by the Court below. Mr. Ali will surrender himself to the sheriff's office forthwith.
