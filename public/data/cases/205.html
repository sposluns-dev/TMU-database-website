Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXXX XXXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of Hearing 24 November 2010 Date(s) de l'audience Place of Hearing Lieu de l'audience Date of Decision 15 December 2010 Date de la décision Panel Tribunal Counsel for the Frank Goldberg Conseil(s) du / de la / des Claimant(s) demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer N/A Agent(e) des tribunaux Designated N/A Représentant(e)(s) Representative(s) Désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXXX XXXXX (the "claimant"), a citizen of Russia, claims refugee protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (the "Act" or "IRPA").1 Allegations [2] The claimant's husband died in Russia under suspicious circumstances while the claimant was visiting her daughter in Israel in XXXX 2003. After that colleagues at work with the XXXX started treating her with hostility and whispering anti-semitic remarks. The claimant lost her job in XXXX 2005 and was unable to secure good employment. She visited her daughter in Israel for 6 months in 2007. When she returned to Russia she started being harassed by skinheads, and was tripped and mocked by them in XXXX 2007. On XXXX, 2008, the claimant was returning home and her way was blocked by skinheads who beat her. Bystanders came to her aid and she was taken to the hospital. The claimant's son was beaten on XXXX, 2008. When she made reports to the police, a police officer informed the claimant that the police did not have the capacity to deal with the skinheads. On XXXX, 2008, the claimant was beaten again, and had to be hospitalized for 2 days. On XXXX, 2008, she came to Canada to stay with friends. In XXXX 2009, the claimant's son in Russia was targetted in a hit and run accident. When the claimant was told she was unable to get visa extensions for her stay in Canada, she made a refugee claim on December 21, 2009. Determination [3] I find that the claimant is not a Convention refugee as she does not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Russia. I also find that the claimant is not a person in need of protection in that her removal to Russia would not subject her personally to a risk to her life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. I find that there are no substantial grounds to believe that her removal to Russia would subject her personally to a danger of torture. Analysis [4] The claimant's identity as a citizen of Russia is established by her testimony and the supporting documentation filed, namely her Russian passport.2 [5] The determinative issue in this claim is the availability of an Internal Flight Alternative (IFA) for the claimant. I find that the claimant has a viable IFA in the city of Yekaterinburg. This issue was raised with the claimant both before and at the hearing. [6] The IFA test has two prongs, namely the panel must be satisfied on a balance of probabilities that (1) there is no serious possibility of the claimant being persecuted or, on the balance of probabilities, in danger of torture or subjected to a risk to life or cruel and unusual treatment or punishment in the IFA and (2) that conditions in that part of the country are such that it would not be unreasonable, in all the circumstances, including those particular to the claimant, for her to seek refuge there.3 These are my reasons for believing that a viable IFA exists for the claimant. [7] The first question is whether the claimant would find safety in the city of Yekaterinburg. The claimant herself is not Jewish. She believes that she was targeted by skinheads in her home city of XXXX and was taunted with anti-Semitic insults because her daughter had married a Jewish man and had moved to Israel. Even though the daughter was now divorced, she still lived in Israel. The claimant believed that when she visited her daughter, rumours in the town connected her to Israel and she subsequently faced targeting by the skinheads. Since that time her son and his family members, who still live in XXXX, have also been subject to a violent attack. [8] I find that the claimant's situation in fearing anti-Semitic acts is a localized issue for her, because her troubles are based on information being spread in her community about her daughter and about her past travels to Israel. While this situation is certainly very disturbing to the claimant and to her son, there is no evidence that such targeting by association would happen to her in another city. Documents confirm that there continues to be recorded incidents of racially motivated attacks against Jews in Russia,4 and vandalism against Jewish synagogues or other buildings.5 However, I have not found any other evidence of anti-Semitism against non-Jews based on a family member marrying a Jew or based on a person's having made a trip to Israel. Although a skinhead group in XXXX appears to have labelled and bullied the claimant on this basis, there is no reason to believe that a perception of the claimant as being Jewish would be carried with her to another city in Russia. [9] This finding is borne out by the claimant's testimony about the time when she lived in Moscow, from XXXX 2006 until XXXX 2007. She lived with a friend there while taking a course, and she testified that she had no problems in Moscow and felt really good there. [10] I also note that the 2009 United States Department of State Report indicates that there has been a trend toward a decrease in violent attacks against Jews, while other prejudice accounted for more recent skinhead attacks. This report states that "skinhead violence continued to be a serious problem. Skinheads primarily targeted foreigners, particularly Asians and individuals from the Northern Caucasus, although they also expressed anti-Muslim and anti-Semitic sentiments."6 This document also reports societal violence against the Roma, dark-skinned persons in general, and against non-Orthodox religious groups such as evangelical and Pentecostal Christians. The claimant herself is fair-skinned and her faith is listed as Orthodox. When asked, she said that she does not believe that she was targeted by skinheads on any of these other bases, only the perceived connection to Jews. Therefore, because the claimant does not have any of the risk factors noted in documentation, I find that she would be safe from skinhead attacks if she were to move to a different city such as Yekaterinaburg. [11] The question in the second prong is whether it would be reasonable in the claimant's circumstances for her to relocate within Russia and seek refuge in Yekaterinaburg. [12] The claimant stated that she would be unable to get a new residence card because of the Russian "propiska." She stated that without propiska she would not be able to get a job. The claimant was not able to clearly explain to me what steps she would have to take to be able to legally reside in a different city. She seemed to indicate that she would need enough money to purchase property, but that she wouldn't be able to do that without first securing propiska. Documentary evidence indicates that the Soviet Union's system of propiska is actually no longer in effect in Russia, having been replaced by a system of registration, but that many Russians continue to use the term propiska to informally refer to this system.7 This document notes that Russian laws provide for the freedom to choose one's place of residence, although there are practical difficulties for some people to register. [13] I do not find that the particular problems noted in this document relate to the claimant. The laws give 90 days in which to cancel one's previous registration and register one's new location. There is discrimination against ethnic and racial minorities in obtaining permanent registration, but this does not apply to the claimant's profile. Moscow is noted as a more difficult place to secure registration than other districts. The claimant herself stated that her friend-with whom she lived in Moscow-did not have a large enough residence for the claimant to be able to register as a permanent resident if she again lived with her friend. For this reason I proposed Yekaterinaburg to the claimant. [14] Yekaterinburg is a city with a population of 1,332,300.8 Documentation indicates that "information about registration restrictions in Yekaterinburg was scarce among the sources consulted by the Research Directorate."9 I find that if there were significant barriers to registration in Yekaterinaburg, there would likely be some information to that effect among the seventeen sources consulted in this research, some of which included independent human rights groups and personal interviews. Furthermore, the UK Operational Guidance Note concurs that "all adults in the Russian Federation are issued with internal passports, which they must carry while travelling, and they are expected to register with the local authorities within 90 days of their arrival in a place. The current advice from the Foreign and Commonwealth Office is that any returnee who is the holder of a valid Russian passport (indicating former residence on Russian territory) should be able to resettle in any of a number of regions in the Russian Federation, even after a prolonged absence."10 This document indicates that if an individual is not in fear of mistreatment by state authorities, then in general it is not unduly harsh to internally relocate in Russia. [15] I find that this evidence indicates that-although there may be some difficulties and challenges in getting permanent registration in a new city-for a person like the claimant who is not a member of a group subject to discrimination and who has some resources from her job in Canada, she will be able to relocate to a new region within Russia. [16] The claimant also stated that she would likely be able to get a private job, such as the XXXX position she currently has in Canada, if she were to live in a city such as Yekaterinaburg. However, she previously held a job in the XXXX sector, and if she were to get a job in the private sector she would be unable to get the pay level, the benefits, or the pension that she had previously been entitled to in the public service. This appears to also be her concern about her son, who does not have a high enough income to support his family because he has a private sector job. The claimant would not be able to make as much money as a XXXX in Russia as she can make in her job in Canada. She is concerned that she would not be able to send money to assist her son and his family as she is doing now. In addition, the claimant also indicated that she does not have any friends or relatives in Yekaterinaburg or any city in Russia other than XXXX and her one friend in Moscow. [17] These are significant challenges. However, Canadian case law sets a very high threshold for what makes an IFA unreasonable in all the circumstances, and the hardship associated with dislocation and relocation is not the kind of undue hardship that renders an IFA unreasonable.11 Furthermore, the fact that a claimant might be better off in Canada, physically, economically and emotionally than in a safe place in her own country is not a factor to consider in assessing the reasonableness of the IFA.12 [18] Once the issue of an IFA has been raised, the onus is on the claimant to show that he or she does not have an IFA.13 The claimant did not provide evidence to indicate that there would be a risk to her in the IFA from skinheads or other people who are bigoted against those with family connections to Jews. Her testimony regarding challenges of registration does not fulfill the burden of proof in demonstrating that she would be unable to move to Yekaterinaburg, considering the documentary evidence which indicates that there is a system allowing for freedom of movement in Russia. [19] I have also considered whether there is an application of the Chairperson's Guidelines for Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-related Persecution,14 noting that the Gender Guidelines highlight that the circumstances which give rise to women's fear of persecution are often unique to women, and that religious, economic, social and cultural factors may be relevant in determining the reasonableness of an IFA for a woman fearing gender-related persecution. In this case, I do not find that the claimant provided evidence to indicate that the harm she fears is based on her gender, considering that she testified that her son faces similar targeting in XXXX. My above consideration of the reasonableness of an IFA for the claimant does take into account her personal circumstances, and I do not find that the claimant faces gender-related difficulties in moving within Russia, such that a move would be considered unreasonable in her situation. [20] Having found that the claimant can avail herself of an IFA in connection to the risk of persecution according to section 96 of IRPA, based on her perceived affiliation to Jewish people, I have considered whether there is any residual evidence to support a claim for refugee protection. The reasoning regarding the availability of an IFA applies to the claimant's situation with regard to a claim to be a person in need of protection according to section 97 of IRPA as well. There is nothing else in the evidence - whether personal or documentary - that would provide a well-founded fear of persecution or a foundation for establishing a personal risk to life, or cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or danger of torture for the claimant pursuant to section 96 or section 97 of the Act. CONCLUSION [21] For the foregoing reasons, I conclude that XXXX XXXXX is not a Convention refugee and not a person in need of protection and I therefore reject her claim.
