Motifs et décision Person(s) who is(are) the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX XXXX Personne(s) en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, Ontario Appel instruit à Date of decision June 29, 2015 Date de la décision Panel Roslyn Ahara Tribunal Counsel for the person(s) who is(are)the subject of the appeal Arthur I Yallen Barrister and Solicitor Conseil(s) du (de la/des) personne(s) en cause Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX, (the Appellant) who is a citizen of Russia, is appealing the decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) denying her claim for refugee protection. She has not submitted new evidence in support of her appeal nor is she requesting an oral hearing. The Appellant is, however, asking the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) to substitute a positive determination, or in the alternative, to remit the matter to a newly-constituted panel of the RPD. DETERMINATION [2] The RAD determines that the Appellant is a Convention refugee, pursuant to section 111(1)(b) of the IRPA. Background [3] The Appellant's fears are based on her religion and ethnicity as a Caucasian Jew. As a XXXX XXXX in Russia, she described how she and her family have experienced anti-Semitism in Russia. [4] In XXXX of 2014, the Appellant was attacked verbally by individuals, one of them being a client of her XXXX, and when the Appellant attempted to reason with them, she was beaten up. She alleges that she sought medical help and also complained to the police, however they denied existence of any anti-Semitic incident. She contacted the Prosecutor's Office, however they did not take her complaint, as there was no refusal from the police. [5] She alleges on XXXX XXXX, 2014 that she and her sister were both attacked by anti-Semitic young men. The Appellant required medical intervention. When the police refused to investigate the incident, the Appellant decided to come to Canada in XXXX 2014 and she filed an inland claim in December 2014. ROLE OF THE RAD [6] Counsel submits that the RAD that it must conduct an independent assessment of the entire record. The RAD has taken guidance from the Federal Court in Huruglica,1 wherein Justice Phelan states, in paragraphs 54 and 55, of the foregoing decision. [54] Having concluded that the RAD erred in reviewing the RPD's decision on the standard of reasonableness, I have further concluded that for the reasons above, the RAD is required to conduct a hybrid appeal. It must review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of whether the claimant is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. Where its assessment departs from that of the RPD, the RAD must substitute its own decision. [55] In conducting its assessment, it can recognize and respect the conclusion of the RPD on such issues as credibility and/or where the RPD enjoys a particular advantage in reaching such a conclusion but it is not restricted, as an appellate court is, to intervening on facts only where there is an "overriding and palpable error." [7] While there has been some conflict in the case law from the Federal Court regarding the standard of review to be applied by the RAD when reviewing RPD decisions, Justice Martineau in Alyafi2 has said that, in the face of this conflict, the Federal Court should allow the RAD to apply either approach as long as the question of the scope of the appellate review of the RPD's decisions has not been settled by a final judgment by the Federal Court of Appeal or the Supreme Court of Canada. The RAD has applied the reasoning as set out in Huruglica.3 COUNSEL'S SUBMISSIONS * Did the RPD err in its assessment of state protection? [8] The RPD found the determinative issue to be state protection. [9] The RPD accepted as credible that the Appellant had experienced various degrees and instances of anti-Semitism and racism in Russia. The Appellant submitted a police report from XXXX 2014, as well as a police response to an incident in which she was assaulted in XXXX 2014. The RPD noted that the police investigation indicated unidentified individuals due to national hatred is not being pursued "due to lack of constituent elements of the crime". The RPD found that that this particular incident was not pursued due to a lack of identified assailants. The RPD further considered the fact that the Appellant testified that in each situation she described it was different assailants whom she could not identify. In totality, the RPD found that the violence described by the Appellant was consistent with what is in the documentary evidence, violence that is xenophobic and situational. [10] The RPD noted that the majority of the Jews living in Russia reside in Moscow and St. Petersburg and the Jewish community is well established with Jewish universities, Jewish Sunday and day schools, synagogues and community centres.4 [11] The RPD noted Article 282 of the Criminal Code which prohibits actions aimed at the incitement of hatred or enmity, as well as abasement of dignity of a person or a group of persons on the basis of sex, race, nationality, language, origin, attitude to religion, as well as affiliation to any social group, if these acts have been committed in public or with the use of mass media.5 However, it did not indicate that there are criminal prosecutions of hate-based crimes and sentences for xenophobic propaganda increasing in 2013.6 [12] The RPD acknowledged the growing number of hate-based crimes and sentiments being expressed in the public sphere against a variety of minority groups. However, the RPD found that this did not rebut the presumption of state protection. It made this finding based on investigations and prosecutions which are still taking place, hate-based crimes being enshrined in the criminal code. It further noted that human rights agencies have called on the Russian government and its agencies to task on such issues7 and there exists various levels of legislative and judicial intervention. The RPD cites the US DOS report which indicates the various entities responsible for law enforcement at all levels of government; the police forces and the Ombudsman as well as the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR) with respect to alleged violations by the state of human rights. [13] The RPD concluded that the Appellant's profile as a XXXX would provide her access to and knowledge of the internal complaints mechanisms as well as knowledge of the tools to leverage the state in cases where police were not responding. However, she insisted that she did not have faith in the police. The RPD found that the Appellant had the obligation to pursue her concerns further if she felt that she was not getting adequate protection. [14] The Appellant submits in her Affidavit that the RPD made a finding with respect to higher authorities from whom she could have sought state protection. Specifically, she mentions the Human Rights Ombudsman in Moscow and submits that the question of why she had not approached them was never put to her. Furthermore, she submits that had this question been put to her, she would have responded, as a XXXX XXXX, that this office only makes recommendations; however it is up to the police and prosecutor's office to decide these complaints. [15] It is submitted by the Appellant that the RPD accepted that she had been attacked in XXXX and XXXX 2014 and she received no response from the police after reporting these attacks. The Appellant argues that following the XXXX 2014 attack, she went to a higher authority, namely, the Prosecutor's Office, however they refused to provide any assistance to her. In XXXX 2014, the Appellant submits that she was detained by the police and dared to question the police's authority to detain her without reason, and believes this occurred due to her Jewish faith. [16] It is argued that although the police report from the XXXX 2014 attack states in terms of the investigation "inappropriate due to the lack of constituent elements of a crime', that this decision was reached despite medical evidence confirming her injuries. The Appellant questions why she would go to the Prosecutor's office following the XXXX 2014 attack when she had no success following the XXXX 2014 attack. [17] Despite the foregoing, which the RPD accepted, the RPD found that the Appellant had not rebutted the presumption of state protection, which in the Appellant's submissions, was erroneous. [18] It is argued by the Appellant that the legal test for state protection is whether or not it is adequate at the operational level. It is insufficient to say that there are laws in place and they have some prosecutions. [19] It is further submitted by the Appellant that the RPD found the Appellant to be credible, and therefore, the only issue was the availability of state protection. The RPD acknowledges anti-Semitism as being a serious problem and that the police do not always investigate attacks or they treat them as hooliganism rather than as hate crimes. In XXXX 2014, it is submitted that the police themselves incited hatred for the Appellant's Jewish faith. [20] It is argued that the transcript reveals that the RPD acknowledges that the number of racially motivated crimes far exceeds the number of convictions, but there are prosecutions happening. In other words, the Appellant submits that in acknowledging the foregoing, it indicates that the level of state protection is not adequate at an operational level. [21] It is submitted that the RPD failed to consider the level of democracy in Russia and the Appellant's reasonable belief that state protection would not be forthcoming. [22] With respect to the level of democracy in Russia, the Appellant argues that the RPD fails to do an analysis on the level of democracy, which it had to do to assess what types of efforts the Appellant had to have undertaken to rebut the presumption of state protection. The Appellant refers the RAD to the Board's National Documentation Package (NDP): Freedom House's 2014 report, Russia, Freedom in the World 2014. It is submitted that currently Russia is not a democracy and as such the presumption of state protection is not a high one. Accordingly, it is argued that the RPD applied too high a standard on the Appellant to seek help from higher state authorities. [23] The efforts by the Appellant to seek police protection with no success, it is argued, are evidenced in her XXXX and XXXX 2014 attacks, her detention by the police in XXXX 2014 and her family's dealing with the police. In light of the foregoing, the Appellant argues that had the RPD properly considered the Appellant's efforts in light of the level of democracy, it would not have reached the same conclusion, and therefore, the RPD made a reviewable error. [24] With respect to the issue of why the police did not assist the Appellant, the Appellant argues that the RPD erred when it stated that the police failed to investigate the XXXX 2014 attack due to a lack of identified assailants and not because of the Appellant's Jewish ethnicity or religion.8 This statement by the RPD, it is argued contradicts the statement by the RPD that "while anti Semitic beliefs held by even the police officers is possible, and in the case of the Appellant, credible", is at odds with its earlier findings that the Appellant's ethnicity didn't play a role in the police's refusal to assist the Appellant. It is further argued that the statement on the police report "due to the lack of constituent elements of a crime", does not translate into being able to identify the attackers by name and provide the police with their addresses. It is submitted that the police did not indicate that they could not locate or find the attackers otherwise the report would have stated so. RAD's FINDINGS [25] With respect to the issue of the Appellant not being questioned about the role of the Human Rights Ombudsman in Moscow, on this issue, the RAD does not concur that it was necessary to confront the Appellant prior to noting this government entity in the RPD's decision. Having said that, the issue is the rebuttal of state protection and what efforts the Appellant made in this regard, in proportion, as argued by the Appellant, to the level of democracy in Russia. [26] Documentary evidence indicates that the Russian Federation has a highly centralized, weak multi-party political system dominated by President Vladimir Putin. The bi-cameral Federal Assembly consists of a directly elected lower house (State Duma) and appointed upper house (Federation Council). Presidential elections in March 2012 featured accusations of government interference and manipulation of the electoral process. Security forces generally reported to civilian authorities, however, in some areas of the Northern Caucasus, civilian authorities did not exercise full control over security forces. Security forces throughout the country committed human rights abuses.9 [27] The most significant human rights problems during the year involved: 1. Restrictions of Civil Liberties: The government continued its crackdown on dissent that began after Vladimir Putin's return to the presidency. The government selectively employed the law on "foreign agents," the law against extremism, and other means to harass, pressure, discredit, and/or prosecute individuals and entities that had voiced criticism of the government, including nongovernmental organizations (NGOs), independent media outlets, and the political opposition. The Office of the Prosecutor General, Ministry of Justice, Federal Security Service, tax authorities, and other agencies carried out inspections of hundreds of NGOs suspected of being "foreign agents." 2. Government Discrimination against Racial, Ethnic, Religious, and Sexual Minorities: The country adopted several laws that discriminated against lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender (LGBT) persons, including a ban on the so-called propaganda of nontraditional sexual relations to minors, which effectively criminalizes public expression and assembly for anyone who would advocate LGBT equality. The government continued to use laws against extremism to prosecute some religious minorities and made "offending the religious feelings of believers" a criminal offense. Authorities in many cities also discriminated against ethnic minorities, arbitrarily detaining thousands of migrant workers amid a wave of anti-immigrant sentiment. Laws, actions, and official rhetoric restricting the rights of the LGBT community, migrants, and other minorities coincided with a marked increase in violent attacks against these groups. Other problems reported during the year included: allegations of torture and excessive force by law enforcement officials, life-threatening prison conditions, interference in the judiciary and the right to a fair trial, restrictions on freedom of speech and press, restrictions on free assembly and association, restrictions on religious freedom of some religious minorities, electoral irregularities, widespread corruption, societal and official intimidation of civil society and labor activists, violence against women and limits on the rights of women in certain regions, trafficking in persons and limitations on workers' rights.10 [28] The government failed to take adequate steps to prosecute or punish most officials who committed abuses, resulting in a climate of impunity. Rule of law was particularly deficient in the North Caucasus, where conflict among government forces, insurgents, Islamist militants and criminal forces led to numerous human rights abuses, including killings, torture, physical abuse, and politically motivated abductions. [29] The RAD concurs with the Appellant that the RPD failed to consider the level of democracy when it found that the Appellant had not rebutted the presumption of state protection. The RAD concurs with the Appellant that in light of a democracy that is certainly in question, the presumption of state protection is a lower one. [30] More importantly, however, the RAD finds that the RPD found the Appellant to be credible. Yet, the RAD finds that it did not accept that she had rebutted the presumption of state protection. She had been to both the police and the Public Prosecutor's office in XXXX 2014, she had been to the police in XXXX 2014 and she had not gone to the Public Prosecutor's office on that occasion as they did not assist her previously. She was then detained and questioned by the police in July 2014, to which she attributes as anti-Semitic action on the part of the police. [31] The RAD has considered the Appellant's argument with respect to the contents of the police report emanating from the XXXX 2014 attack. "inappropriate due to the lack of constituent elements of a crime". The RAD after reviewing the transcript concurs with the Appellant that the police did not consider the Appellant's physical injuries resulting from this attack. Moreover, the RAD concurs with the Appellant that the words "due to the lack of constituent elements of a crime" do not translate into being able to identify the attackers by name and provide the police with their address. The RAD, quite frankly, does not know the meaning of the foregoing words. CONCLUSION [32] The RAD finds that the only issue in this claim is state protection. The RAD finds that the Appellant has, in fact, rebutted the presumption of state protection, given the level of democracy that exists in Russia today. [33] Accordingly, the RAD finds that the Appellant is a Convention refugee, pursuant to section 111(1)(b) of the IRPA. (signed) "Roslyn Ahara" Roslyn Ahara June 29, 2015 Date 1 Huruglica Bujar v.
