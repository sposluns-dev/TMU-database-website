XXXX XXXX) XXXX XXXX Personne(s) en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, Ontario Appel instruit à Date of decision May 31, 2016 Date de la décision Panel Milton Israel Tribunal Counsel for the person(s) who is(are)the subject of the appeal Elyse Korman Conseil(s) du (de la/des) personne(s) en cause Designated Representative(s) XXXX XXXX Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX (the "principal Appellant"), XXXX XXXX (the "female Appellant") and XXXX XXXX (the "minor female Appellant") are citizens of China and permanent residents of Argentina. XXXX XXXX (the "minor male Appellant") is a citizen of Argentina. They appeal decisions of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) denying their claims for refugee protection. The Appellants have not submitted new evidence, and they have not requested an oral hearing. They request that the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) set aside the determination of the RPD and substitute a decision that the Appellants are Convention refugees or persons in need of protection. Alternatively, the Appellants request that the matter be remitted to the RPD for reconsideration by a differently constituted panel. [2] The principal Appellant was appointed the designated representative for the two minor Appellants. DETERMINATION [3] Pursuant to section 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD confirms the determination of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. The appeals are dismissed. Background [4] The Appellants alleged that they fear persecution in China because they strongly oppose family planning officials' demand for their sterilization if they were to return to China. [5] The Appellants also fear persecution in Argentina as a result of harassment and threats by the general population of native Argentinians and the refusal of police to help them. The Appellants alleged the police sought bribes instead. [6] The Appellants also alleged that the minor Appellants have been subject to discrimination and bullying from schoolmates because of their Chinese ethnicity. They alleged as well that the teacher criticized and teased the minor female Appellant, and school officials refused to take these acts seriously. The Issues [7] The panel indicated that the determinative issues in regard to these claims are national identity (section 1E of the Refugee Convention) and credibility as to their fear of persecution in China and Argentina. [8] The Appellants submit the issues in this appeal are the following: (a) Whether the panel erred in finding there is no nexus between the Appellants' fear and a Convention ground; and (b) Whether the panel erred in finding that the Appellants failed to rebut the presumption of state protection. The Role of the RAD [9] While the IRPA sets out grounds for appeal as well as possible remedies, it does not specify the standard by which the RAD is to review the decision of the lower tribunal. [10] The RAD now has the benefit of Federal Court jurisprudence which speaks to the appellate tribunal's role in relation to that of the RPD. [11] In this regard, the Court, in Huruglica, considered the appropriate relationship between the RAD and the RPD.1 In Huruglica, the Court determined that the RAD is to conduct a "hybrid appeal". It is to review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of the Appellant's refugee claim, deferring to the RPD only where the lower tribunal enjoys a particular advantage in reaching a conclusion. Where the RAD's assessment departs from that of the RPD, the RAD must substitute its own determination. [12] The Court determined that, in conducting its assessment, the RAD can recognize and respect the conclusion of the RPD on such issues as credibility and/or where the RPD enjoys a particular advantage in reaching such a conclusion. The RAD is, however, not restricted, as an appellate court is, to intervening on the facts only where there is a "palpable and overriding error". [13] A recent decision of the Federal Court of Appeal2 has confirmed the central elements of Huruglica. The Court held that the RAD should use the correctness standard with respect to questions of fact, law and mixed fact and law. The RAD is to intervene when the RPD is wrong in fact, law and mixed fact and law. The Court further stated that there may be cases where the RPD enjoys a meaningful advantage over the RAD in making findings of fact and mixed fact and law, because they require an assessment of the credibility or weight to be given to the oral evidence it hears. The Court stated as well that an appeal to the RAD is not a true de novo proceeding. It does not start anew, and the original decision is not ignored in all respects. [14] The RAD will review all of the evidence in the RPD's record and come to its own independent assessment of whether the Appellants are Convention refugees or persons in need of protection. Article 1E [15] The Minister of Citizenship and Immigration (MCI) submits that the Appellants are either permanent residents or citizens of Argentina; they are entitled to return to that country, and therefore the Appellants fall under the Article 1E exclusion.3 The panel noted that the Appellants did not contest these facts. [16] The panel found that the Appellants are entitled to return to Argentina. As the Appellants have made claims against Argentina, the 1E country, the panel considered this before applying the exclusion. The panel indicated that, if required, it would consider the claim for refugee protection against China. [17] The panel found that the Appellants have not established that there is a serious possibility of persecution on a Convention ground or that, on a balance of probabilities, they would be personally subjected to a risk to their lives or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a risk of torture if they were to return to Argentina. In this context, the panel further found the Appellants are excluded from refugee protection in accordance with section 98 of the IRPA. The panel found as well that the minor male Appellant has not established his claim against Argentina, his country of nationality. The panel therefore found the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. Credibility [18] The panel found the Appellants to be credible witnesses and accepted that incidents did occur as set out in the principal Appellant's Basis of Claim (BOC) form. However, the panel found there was no evidence on which it could conclude that the Appellants were specifically targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity. [19] The panel noted that the Appellants stated in their BOC that there was discrimination by native-born Argentinians and also verbal abuse, threats, harassment, and also a robbery in 2009. There was as well a false police accusation that they sold beer to a minor. The Appellants also stated that the police provided no help and asked for bribes. The Appellants stated that both the police and members of the public indicated their Chinese ethnicity was the source of their problems. [20] The panel reviewed the evidence and found the incidents of harassment and verbal abuse by members of the public in XXXX 2011, XXXX 2012 and XXXX 2012 appeared to be random occurrences. The panel found nothing in the evidence on which to conclude that the Appellants were specifically targeted based on their race. The panel acknowledged that the Appellants were subjected to slurs based on their Chinese ethnicity but found it reasonable to conclude, due to the randomness of the incidents, that anyone, including other ethnic minorities, would be subject to similar treatment, including derogatory references to their ethnicity. [21] The panel found, in regard to the XXXX 2015 incident, when a stranger called, demanded money and threatened the safety of the minor Appellants if the money was not paid, that this is not persecution but rather a criminal incident. The panel cited country documentary evidence that indicates "kidnapping for ransom is relatively rare and mostly affects wealthy Argentines".4 The panel further found that there is no indication that criminals specifically target people of Chinese ethnicity in Argentina. The panel found as well that it is reasonable to conclude that the Appellants were not specifically targeted because of their race and that members of other ethnic communities who ran businesses would be similarly subjected to abuse from customers who felt they had not received the service they expected. [22] The panel acknowledged that the problems experienced in school by the minor Appellants were discriminatory and unacceptable, but it found that they do not amount to persecution, as such treatment could be experienced by a member of a different ethnic minority. The panel cited country documentary evidence indicating that discrimination on the basis of race is prohibited by law and "the government generally enforced these prohibitions".5 The panel noted that no evidence was provided that the Appellants brought these matters to the attention of higher school authorities. [23] The panel indicated it considered the documentary evidence they provided concerning discrimination against ethnic minorities and foreigners, the targeting of Chinese XXXX by Argentina mafia and the joint efforts by Chinese and Argentine police to recover money taken from XXXX owners.6 The panel also noted that one of these sources concerns problems with police protection, language barrier, lack of police investigation and the unwillingness of victims to speak out.7 [24] The panel noted that, while it considered this documentation, it preferred country documentary evidence in the National Documentation Package (NDP), including Amnesty International, Freedom House and the U.S. Department of State, Argentina, 2014 Crime and Safety Report. The panel noted that there were no references in these sources indicating specific targeting of Chinese people by either the police or the general public in Argentina.8 The panel further noted that, while there were references to discrimination against women and children in matters such as child abuse, sexual exploitation and forced marriages and also anti-Semitism and discrimination against persons with disabilities and indigenous people in Argentina, there were no examples in these reports of serious harm or persecution faced by ethnic Chinese in Argentina. [25] The panel noted that country documentary evidence indicated that crime is a serious problem in Argentina, but there is no indication that ethnic Chinese are particularly targeted. The panel further noted this evidence indicates that favourite targets for robbery are banks and businesses dealing in cash or high-value merchandise.9 On the basis of the evidence noted above, the panel concluded that the motivation for robbery and attempted extortion was economic and neither social nor racial. The panel found that the Appellants' evidence did not establish a nexus with a Convention ground as it relates to robbery and attempted extortion. The panel further found that harassment and abuse by members of the general public do not constitute a nexus to a Convention ground, nor does the behaviour of the minor Appellants' schoolmates and teachers constitute persecution. Even when these acts are viewed cumulatively, the panel found they were insufficient to bring them within the range of persecution. [26] The panel found the Appellants did not provide clear and convincing evidence that they sought the protection of the state. The Appellants' evidence indicates they decided in many instances not to involve the police. The panel also found they could have approached higher authorities at the police stations to report requests for bribes by police and insisted that reports be taken. The panel cited country documentary evidence that indicates that federal security forces conduct investigations of alleged police abuse and that authorities have investigated, detained, prosecuted and convicted officers involved.10 [27] The Appellants submit that, even if the Appellants were not targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity, which the Appellants deny, they still fall within the scope of s. 96 because of the actions and motivations of the Argentine police. The Appellants further submit that the evidence is clear that the police refused to help the Appellants because they were Chinese. The Appellants note that the panel found their evidence credible in this regard. The Appellants also submit that the police demanded bribes but failed to assist them even when they paid. The Appellants submit as well that the police stole merchandise, and this was common for Chinese-owned businesses in Argentina. [28] The Appellants submit that by refusing to help or protect them, the police, as agents of the state, were complicit in the crimes against the Appellants. The Appellants further submit that their fear of persecution is linked to a Convention ground because the Argentinian state was complicit in the crimes and because this complicity was rooted in the Appellants' Chinese ethnicity. The Appellants argue that the panel erred in law by failing to appreciate this reality and considering only the motivations of the criminals who targeted the Appellants. [29] The Appellants submit that the panel erred in finding no evidence to establish that they were targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity. The Appellants note that the panel found the facts of their encounters with customers to be credible. [30] The Appellants note in this regard an XXXX 2011 incident when an angry customer said that all Chinese were thieves and should leave Argentina. The police were called but said that there was nothing they could do. The Appellants further note a XXXX 2012 incident in which a customer refused to be served by the female Appellant and said all Chinese people were thieves and should leave Argentina. In XXXX 2012, a man broke the door of the Appellants' XXXX and demanded food. He also said that "Chinese pigs" should leave Argentina. In XXXX 2013 a customer smashed a glass bottle and yelled "Chinese are cheaters". He also threatened the female Appellant. The police refused to help. The Appellants also submit they were aware of instances where criminal organizations targeted Chinese children for kidnapping, and Chinese businessmen were killed by gangsters for failing to pay protection money. [31] The Appellants argue that it is unclear how the panel could accept these allegations but conclude there was no evidence that the Appellants were specifically targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity. [32] The Appellants submit that the panel erred in finding that, despite the fact they were subjected to racial slurs, these incidents were not persecutory because anyone, including other ethnic minorities, would be subject to similar treatment. The Appellant argues that the mere fact that the agents of persecution may not be racist only against Chinese people does not mean they are not motivated by race, and therefore there is no nexus to the Convention. The Appellant cites James Hathaway, The Law of Refugee Status in which it is stated: "In sum...it is sufficient if the Convention reason or ground accounts in part or a contributing factor in creating the risk of being persecuted."11 The Appellants submit that the panel erred in law by requiring that they had to establish that their Chinese ethnicity was the sole or dominant motivation of the agents of persecution. The Appellants further submit that this is not the correct legal test for establishing nexus. [33] The Appellants submit the same argument applies to the treatment of the minor Appellants in school. The Appellants argue that the fact that other ethnic minorities might experience the same treatment is irrelevant. The Appellants further argue that the question is whether the minor Appellants were harassed and discriminated against because of their Chinese ethnicity. [34] The Appellants submit that the panel did not find that they faced only a generalized risk in Argentina. Rather, the panel found that there is no nexus to race because the incidents they faced have been experienced by other ethnic groups in Argentina, and the incidents were random. The Appellants further submit that the panel's findings in this regard support their position that there is a nexus to a Convention ground on the basis of race. The Appellants argue if the incidents noted above were motivated, even in part, by race, then they fall within the scope of s. 96 regardless of whether members of other ethnic minorities could have been subjected to the same acts. [35] The RAD has reviewed and assessed all of the evidence in the record concerning the Appellants' allegation that they are targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity and are at risk of persecution in Argentina. The RAD acknowledges, in the context of the country documentary evidence cited above, that race and foreigner identity are the basis for widespread prejudice and discrimination in Argentina. The RAD further acknowledges that such behaviour has been directed against ethnic Chinese as well as other groups. The RAD notes in this regard that the panel found credible the Appellants' allegations of experiencing a number of incidents involving racial slurs. [36] The RAD finds that when racism is alleged as the basis for a claim, the allegation must be considered in the context of a s. 96 analysis. The RAD further finds, in regard to the case at bar, that, in addition to the credibility of the allegation, the issue to be considered is whether the acknowledged prejudice and discrimination rises to the level of persecution. [37] The RAD finds that the three incidents of harassment and verbal abuse during the three years the Appellants operated a XXXX, while unacceptable, do not rise to the level of persecution. No one was injured, and the incidents apparently passed quickly. The RAD notes that the Appellants' XXXX was robbed in 2009, but no evidence was disclosed indicating that they were targeted because it was a Chinese business. The RAD further notes that, in regard to the attempted extortion in a phone call in 2015, there is no evidence that the call was motivated by the Appellants' Chinese ethnicity. In addition, as noted by the panel, both of these incidents were criminal in nature and therefore do not have a nexus to a Convention issue. [38] In regard to the discriminatory behaviour experienced by the minor Appellants at school, the RAD has reviewed the evidence and agrees with the panel that, while unacceptable, this does not amount to persecution. The RAD notes that no evidence was disclosed indicating the minor Appellants were denied access to school and an education. In addition, no evidence was disclosed indicating that higher school officials were contacted concerning the alleged actions of teachers. [39] The RAD notes that the Appellants submit that, even if the Appellants were not targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity, which they deny, they still fall within the scope of s. 96 because of the actions and motivations of the Argentine police. The Appellants further submit that the evidence is clear that the police refused to help the Appellants because they are Chinese and that they demanded bribes. [40] The Appellants submit as well that the panel erred in finding that they failed to rebut the presumption of state protection. The Appellants note that the panel accepted the fact that they went to the police on several occasions, but the police refused to help and they blamed Chinese people for causing trouble. The Appellants further note that the police entered their shop and took merchandise without paying for it. The Appellants submit as well that, on several occasions, the police asked for bribes and, even when paid, there was no help. The Appellants argue that the panel based its finding on the argument that they could have approached higher authorities and also on country documentary evidence that indicates that Argentinian federal authorities are able to conduct internal investigations into such abuses and dismiss people accused of human rights violations. [41] The Appellants submit this finding is unsustainable. The Appellants argue the mere fact authorities have this ability to investigate violations does not mean that the Appellants would have been assisted had they approached higher authorities. The Appellants further argue that their evidence that it is known in the Chinese community in Argentina that the police will not help Chinese residents in Argentina was found to be credible by the panel. The Appellants also note that the US Department of State report indicates that there are reports of torture by federal and provincial police, the use of excessive force, arbitrary arrest and detention and official corruption. [42] The Appellants submit that the panel's finding that they did not make sufficient efforts to approach the state for protection is not tenable. The Appellants argue that, in light of past experience with the police and the general feeling among Chinese Argentinians that the police will not help them, the Appellants had good reason to mistrust the police and to believe that approaching the police would be futile. [43] The RAD notes that, although the panel accepted the credibility of the allegations concerning the incidents described in their BOC, it did not accept the Appellants' submission that it is known in the Chinese community in Argentina that the police will not help Chinese residents. The RAD notes evidence below that Argentine and Chinese police have jointly combated the so-called Chinese "mafia" that preys in particular on Chinese XXXX owners. [44] The RAD has reviewed the Appellants' evidence regarding their experience with the Argentinian police. The RAD notes that, while the Appellants stated they were told by the police that Chinese always brought trouble to them, the police did respond to the robbery in XXXX 2009. Although apparently the robbers were not caught even though the Appellants paid a bribe to the police, there is no clear evidence that their failure to resolve the case was due to the ethnicity of the victims. [45] The RAD notes that the Appellants called the police as a result of the first incident of harassment and racial slurs in their XXXX. The police responded but indicated there was nothing they could do. It is unclear from the evidence whether the person who harassed them with racial slurs was still in the XXXX or whether she had left. It is also unclear whether the Appellants could identify her and whether there was anything the police could do if this person had only expressed herself in a particularly nasty way. The Appellants stated in their BOC that, after this experience, they did not bother to report this kind of behaviour. [46] The RAD notes that the Appellants stated that a police officer extorted a bribe from them by suggesting they had broken the law regarding selling alcohol to a minor. The RAD further notes that no evidence was disclosed that they were targeted in this regard because of their Chinese ethnicity. [47] In regard to the XXXX 2015 attempted extortion and threat, the RAD notes that no evidence was disclosed that the Appellants were targeted because of their Chinese ethnicity. The RAD further notes that the Appellants did not contact the police in this regard, even though they were advised to do so by the Chinese embassy. Country Documentary Evidence [48] The RAD notes that country documentary evidence, also cited by the panel, indicates that the authorities in Argentina generally maintain effective control over the security forces. There is, however, evidence of crimes committed by the police. While members of the security forces have been convicted of crimes, others have engaged in abuses with impunity. It is also noted that cases of corruption occurred in some security forces. The most frequent abuses included extortion of, and protection for, those involved in drug trafficking and prostitution. As well, the evidence indicates that "weak institutions and an often ineffective and politicized judicial system undermined systemic attempts to curb corruption".12 [49] The RAD notes, as did the panel, that this evidence indicates that there are specific groups that suffer discrimination and societal abuse, including women, Jews and indigenous people. There is however no mention of ethnic Chinese as being one of these groups. [50] The RAD notes that the Appellant provided documentation and submitted that the panel appeared to give no weight to this documentation because items in the NDP cited by the panel provided no evidence that Chinese Argentinians were targeted for abuse. The RAD notes that the panel did consider the Appellants' evidence but placed more reliance on the NDP documentation. The RAD further notes that the panel stated that there were no examples of serious harm or persecution faced by ethnic Chinese in Argentina in the Appellants' documentation. The panel also noted that the documents provided by the Appellants were written by unknown writers "whose objectivity about the contents of the articles are unknown".13 [51] The RAD has reviewed the documents provided by the Appellants and notes that they indicate that discrimination against foreigners is common in Argentina, and there are divisions between classes and races. One article cites a study by the National Institute Against Discrimination, Xenophobia and Racism, which states that Bolivians were the number one target followed by Peruvians and Paraguayans. The article notes that Chinese, Koreans and black people are not on the list, but the prejudice concerns them as well and is reflected in "the everyday vernacular of people here in Buenos Aires". The article further notes that the relationship between Argentinians and Chinese seems based on mistrust and misunderstanding. Also noted is the use of nicknames such as "gringo" for whites reflects the "cavalier attitudes to race that are on display in Buenos Aires", but it is further noted that "the people who have used them clearly do not identify or think of these words as at all racist".14 [52] An article concerning the Chinese community in Buenos Aires notes that there is mistrust and misunderstanding and discrimination is widespread. Also noted is the range of businesses in Buenos Aires in which Chinese-Argentinians are involved. The writer notes that Chinatown not only provides services for the Chinese community, but serves as well as an introduction to Chinese culture for non-Chinese local residents. It is further noted that mainland Chinese are still arriving in Argentina and investing in that country. It is noted as well that "neighbourhood XXXX that cater to the local Argentine community...have become...ubiquitous in the city". Although these markets have become "very popular with busy urban families, many Argentines do not trust them".15 [53] A third article was disclosed by the Appellants, while noting that xenophobia and discrimination remain problematic, Chinese culture "has become a part of modern Argentine society". It is further noted that in the context of China's rise to power, there is a continued increase of students studying Chinese in Argentina. The article concludes that, although Chinese immigrants are not always socially accepted, "there is no denying that their significant presence provides economic and cultural promises for the country's future.16 [54] There are as well two articles concerning the operations of Chinese "mafia" in Argentina.17 These articles indicate that there is a joint operation between Chinese and Argentine police and that $1.6 million taken from XXXX has been recovered. It is also indicated that these "mafia" groups are involved in extortion, and Chinese XXXX owners refuse to report to the police. [55] The RAD finds, on the basis of its review and assessment of all the documentary evidence disclosed by both the Appellants and the Board, that while discrimination and prejudice exist in Argentina, there is insufficient evidence that it is particularly directed against ethnic Chinese. In addition, while the evidence concerning the police in Argentina is clearly mixed, there is as well insufficient evidence that the police refuse to provide assistance to ethnic Chinese. [56] The RAD notes that the principal Appellant moved to Argentina in 1999 and became a permanent resident in 2003. He opened a XXXX in 2007 and subsequently opened a XXXX in 2012. While any one of the experiences noted in the Appellants' BOC is unfortunate and unacceptable, the RAD agrees with the panel that they appear to reflect random incidents rather than targeted attacks on the Appellants for any reason, including their Chinese ethnicity. [57] The RAD finds, on the basis of the country documentary evidence cited above, that the failure of the police to provide, at times, an adequate response and their apparent sharing of racist and discriminatory views is a generalized risk, apparently broadly shared in Argentinian society, including in particular: immigrants from nearby South American countries, Jews, women, indigenous people and also Chinese. [58] The RAD concludes, on the basis of its review of all of the evidence in the record, including the country documentary evidence cited above, that the panel did not err in finding that the Appellants have not established that there is a serious possibility of persecution on a Convention ground or that, on a balance of probabilities, they would be subjected to a risk to their lives or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a risk of torture if they were to return to Argentina. DISPOSITION [59] Pursuant to section 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the determination of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. The appeals are dismissed. (signed) "Milton Israel" Milton Israel May 31, 2016 Date 1 Huruglica, Bujar v. M.C.I. (F.C., no. IMM-6362-13), Phelan, August 22, 2014, 2014 FC 799. 2 M.C.I. v.
