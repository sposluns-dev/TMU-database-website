Overview [1] The backdrop for this application is a university at a time of high tension, when conflict in Palestine and Israel dominated political dialogue on campus. Attacks by Hamas on Israel on October 7, 2023, and Israel's responses, have significant international political and humanitarian implications and have given rise to expressions of support, condemnation, opinion and concern from individuals, institutions and governments around the world. [2] In the university context, expression on challenging and divisive matters is encouraged and differing views are expressed in a range of ways. A speaker's right to participate in that dialogue is protected both constitutionally and statutorily. As in other areas of life, there may nevertheless be legal limits or effects from that speech, including those found in the law of defamation. At issue for this Court is a fundamental legal and social tension between the value of free expression and the consequences the law may impose on potentially harmful speech. [3] This case specifically involves expression in the form of a sticker imprinted with the logo and name of the University of British Columbia Social Justice Centre that states, "I ❤️ Hamas" (the "Sticker"). The Sticker was designed to appear as though it was published by the UBC Social Justice Centre (the "UBC SJC"), but was in fact created by Adam Dobrer, a UBC student who claims the Sticker was meant to lampoon the UBC SJC. Mr. Dobrer was a past employee and, at the time of the Sticker's publication, was engaged by Hillel BC Society ("Hillel") to carry out certain projects. The plaintiff, Matthew Cheesman, was a coordinator and active member of the UBC SJC when the Sticker was published. [4] Mr. Cheesman and the UBC SJC filed a lawsuit asserting that Mr. Dobrer, and through him his employer Hillel, defamed them through publication of the Sticker. The claim by the UBC SJC was later discontinued. [5] In advance of any determination on the merits of the claim, the defendants brought an application to have the action dismissed pursuant to s. 4 of the Protection of Public Participation Act, S.B.C. 2019, c. 3 [PPPA]. [6] I am allowing the application to dismiss the action. In brief, I conclude that Mr. Cheesman's claim does not have a reasonable prospect of success. The substance of the Sticker could be found to be defamatory, in that it could cause damage by diminishing a person's reputation in the eyes of a reasonable person. However, in this case, the Sticker does not sufficiently refer to the plaintiff, Mr. Cheesman, and accordingly his claim that it has harmed him personally does not have substantial merit. I do not find that it is appropriate in this case to order damages or full indemnity costs, as was sought by the defendants. II. Factual Background A. Matthew Cheesman [7] The plaintiff, Matthew Cheesman, was a student at UBC in 2023, and a member and leader of the UBC SJC. Mr. Cheesman first became involved with the UBC SJC at the beginning of his university studies. He was an active member of the organization throughout his time at UBC, including acting as a coordinator of the group. [8] The UBC SJC is a group formed under the Alma Mater Society, UBC's student union (the "AMS"). Such groups are funded through student fees. The UBC SJC does not have formal membership, but it is open to all students, and part of its mandate is to engage in research, outreach and action on social justice issues. [9] The UBC SJC does not have a formal or hierarchal organizational structure. It has a constitution, but it is not strictly followed. The UBC SJC is organized by thematic sub-groups representing the work on which they are focused. Topics addressed include local issues like housing and drug policy, and international issues including Palestine solidarity. The UBC SJC participates in public discourse on these topics at in-person events, and on social media and traditional media. Mr. Cheesman observed that there are typically 30-40 actively involved members, around 10 of whom are elected coordinators of three main subject-area sub-groups: housing justice, harm reduction, and international solidarity. The coordinators carry out leadership roles relating to the subject areas, but do not constitute a board of directors or executive. The total membership number may have been up to 200, based on online records. The membership of the UBC SJC is fluid, and participants' involvement waxes and wanes depending upon new and graduating students joining or departing, and individuals' level of interest and capacity. [10] Mr. Cheesman's work with the UBC SJC was focused on Palestinian solidarity. His involvement as a coordinator included organizing a coalition of campus groups to lobby the AMS and UBC to boycott and divest from Israel. [11] In his role as coordinator, Mr. Cheesman spoke publicly on behalf of the UBC SJC. He was an advocate on this issue in mainstream media and the student newspaper. He attended and spoke publicly at events advocating for divestment, and represented the organization on campus in a range of contexts. He was not identified in social media on behalf of the UBC SJC and there was a loose policy at the UBC SJC not to attribute social media posts to any one member. B. Hillel BC [12] Hillel is a non-profit organization and a society constituted under the Societies Act, S.B.C. 2015, c. 18. It has a mandate to promote Jewish life on university campuses in BC, with a view to enabling Jewish young adults to explore their identity and relationship with Israel in a safe, pluralistic and inclusive community. Hillel is located on campus, but it is not a UBC organization or club. Hillel, too, participates in public discourse by making statements and hosting events promoting its perspectives. C. Adam Dobrer [13] Mr. Dobrer was employed by Hillel from July 15, 2022, to August 15, 2023. During this time, Mr. Dobrer worked on various initiatives including hosting workshops and seminars about Israel and supporting students to engage in discussions on campus about Israel. His employment concluded in the summer of 2023 and a replacement for his position was hired. [14] Hillel engaged Mr. Dobrer to assist with specific on-campus projects arising from the October 7 attack by Hamas against Israel. Hillel submits Mr. Dobrer was engaged to assist Hillel in better understanding "anti-Israeli sentiment on campus, terrorist elements operating on campus, and to assist students in advocating for themselves." [15] Hillel terminated its relationship with Mr. Dobrer on November 19, 2023, after his publication of the Sticker. D. The Sticker [16] The Sticker, what it expresses and about whom, is at the heart of this case. [17] Mr. Dobrer printed 500 copies of the Sticker, which is circular in format with a white background. All words on the Sticker are fully capitalized, including, "I ❤️ HAMAS", and the caption, "UBC SOCIAL JUSTICE CENTER". The text is in black while the heart image is in red, with a silhouetted figure superimposed on the heart in black. This figure appears to be a symbol used by the UBC SJC in their logo. [18] Hamas is a Palestinian militant group declared to be a terrorist organization by the Government of Canada. [19] Mr. Dobrer distributed 100 copies of the Sticker to others. On or about November 17, 2023, copies of the Sticker were posted on the UBC Vancouver campus. Photos of the Sticker were taken and posted to social media, where they were reposted by accounts with large numbers of followers. [20] On social media, the image of the Sticker was posted and reposted, with captions revealing that readers genuinely attributed the Sticker to the UBC SJC. Vitriolic messages were posted in response to the sticker calling for student members of the UBC SJC to be expelled, deported, charged, or victimized. Messages posted online threatened to locate and share personal information of the members of the UBC SJC. [21] Hillel released a public statement calling the stickers "offensive" and asserting that Mr. Dobrer's actions did not reflect Hillel's values. Hillel's public statement, while disavowing the Sticker, did not clarify that the Sticker was not published or created by or linked to the UBC SJC or anyone involved with the UBC SJC, including Mr. Cheesman. [22] Mr. Cheesman attested that the publication of the Sticker caused him significant distress, fear and confusion, even before it was known who had published it. He worried deeply about the effect of the Sticker on his reputation and particularly how he was viewed by his peers and those he respects, as he is someone who does not justify or condone violence. He was afraid of being caricatured as a terrorist sympathiser in a context that calls for nuance. [23] He also attested that he witnessed the online response to the Sticker, with highly connected social media participants decrying the UBC SJC's supposed position. These individuals also suggested that the UBC SJC was glorifying terror and were pure evil, and Mr. Cheesman was disturbed by their threats to expose members' personal information or addresses. [24] Mr. Cheesman attested that the publication of the Sticker resulted in significant anxiety, fear and stress, and that this affected him at school, at home, and in his community. He experienced distress in personal relationships, including with his Jewish friends and their families. People continued to ask him if he was associated with the Sticker. He attested that it was particularly concerning to him that the Sticker, which appeared to be attributed to the organization he was part of, caused harm to the Jewish student body or caused Jewish students to feel unsafe. He opposes antisemitism and dedicates his work to promoting equity and opposing oppression and discrimination, so this false attribution of the Sticker was upsetting to Mr. Cheesman. E. Procedural Background [25] Mr. Cheesman and the UBC SJC commenced this suit by notice of civil claim on February 7, 2024. The claim alleges the Sticker is defamatory, and seeks injunctive relief and damages. [26] Mr. Dobrer filed an amended response to civil claim on June 14, 2024, which admits he published the Sticker, and that it was placed at public locations around UBC's Vancouver campus, including on posters advertising UBC SJC events. Hillel filed a response to civil claim on April 12, 2024, asserting that Hillel had no involvement or prior knowledge of the Sticker, and denying liability and vicarious liability. [27] The defendants plead that the Sticker is not defamatory, and is not defamatory of Mr. Cheesman. In the alternative, both defendants plead the defence of fair comment and further assert that no damages arise from the publication of the Sticker. [28] The defendants also pleaded that the UBC SJC, as an unincorporated association, cannot maintain a lawsuit in defamation. On May 28, 2024, the claim by the UBC SJC was discontinued. [29] The defendants each filed an application under s. 4 of the PPPA for dismissal of the action. Under s. 5 of the PPPA, until the application under s. 4 is concluded the parties cannot take further steps in the litigation. III. Legal Framework: Defamation and the PPPA A. Tort of Defamation [30] Mr. Cheesman's action is for defamation, which is a personal action to claim damages for harm to an individual's reputation. [31] At the core of defamation law are "two competing values: freedom of expression and the protection of reputation. Each is essential to maintaining a functional democracy": Hansman v. Neufeld, 2023 SCC 14 [Hansman SCC] at para. 1. The legislature has determined it is necessary to protect speech or expression made on matters of public interest from strategic litigation against public participation ("SLAPP") and to this end has established the framework we see in the PPPA. [32] While s. 2(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms [Charter] provides the broad and vital protection of speech from state-imposed limits, it nevertheless infuses the private law of defamation in Canada. It is the PPPA, like other similar provincial statutes, that provides protection to individuals engaging in speech on public interest matters from lawsuits that may give rise to damages due to allegations of personal harm. I note that although reference was made to s. 2(b) by all parties in their submissions, this matter does not require consideration of Mr. Dobrer's freedom of expression under the Charter as this is a dispute between two private individuals, not an individual and the state. [33] To succeed in a claim in defamation, Mr. Cheesman must prove: a) the expression in question was published, by being communicated to at least one person other than the plaintiff; b) the expression was defamatory in that it would tend to lower the reputation of the plaintiff in the eyes of a reasonable person; and c) the expression referred to the plaintiff. See Neufeld v. Bondar, 2025 BCCA 51 at para. 16 [Bondar], citing Simán v. Eisenbrandt, 2023 BCSC 379 at para. 43 [Simán BCSC]; Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61 at para. 28. [34] The first element is self-explanatory, and in this case is not an issue as both defendants admit that Mr. Dobrer published the Sticker. [35] The second element of defamation, which assesses whether the Sticker is defamatory in its content, requires viewing the expression from the perspective of a reasonable person. The case law has described this person as someone "who is reasonably thoughtful but not excessively sensitive or fragile": Hudson v. Myong, 2020 BCSC 517 at para. 105, citing Weaver v. Corcoran, 2017 BCCA 160 at paras. 68-69 [Corcoran]; Color Your World Corp. v. Canadian Broadcasting Corp. (1998), 1998 CanLII 1983 (O.N.C.A.). It is an objective assessment, or a 'common sense approach'. A court must avoid seizing upon the worst possible meaning as well as restrain from interpreting the words in a mild or inoffensive way to relieve defendants from liability: Simán BCSC at para. 49. Statements that merely threaten a plaintiff's 'wished-for reputation or pride', or that are simply rude, abusive, or insulting, will not be seen as defamatory: Bondar at para. 42, citing Weaver v. Ball, 2020 BCCA 119. [36] A statement may be defamatory literally (directly), inferentially (indirectly), or by legal innuendo (based on extrinsic facts): Corcoran, at paras. 71-72. In this case, Hillel argues the Sticker is "cartoon-style". On this point, case law has held that a "reasonable man of ordinary intelligence knowing that cartoons are not to be taken literally - but are to be considered as rhetorically making a point by symbolism, allegory, satire and exaggeration" may not derive a literal meaning from a cartoon: Vander Zalm v. Times Publishers, 1980 CanLII 389 (B.C.C.A.) at para. 86. [37] The third element of a defamation claim is that the expression must refer to the plaintiff; in other words, reasonable people acquainted with the plaintiff would reach the conclusion that the statements in question refer to the plaintiff: Rooney v. Galloway, 2024 BCCA 8 at para. 100; Butler v. Southam Inc., 2001 NSCA 121 at para. 29. [38] A statement or publication may still be defamatory of an individual even if they are not identified by name: Rooney at para. 101. Rather, the expression "may reasonably be found to refer to the plaintiff in light of the surrounding circumstances": Butler at para. 39. Butler advises that the assessment focuses on the 'intensity of suspicion' about whether the expression in question referred to the plaintiff, and that this exercise involves a focus on the link between the impugned expression and the reputation of the individual plaintiff: Butler at paras. 56, 59. [39] Individual harm through group defamation is possible, and requires assessment of whether statements made about a group may reasonably be determined to be defamatory of individual members. In Bou Malhab v. Diffusion Métromédia CMR Inc., 2011 SCC 9 at paras. 58-78, the SCC provides a non-exhaustive list of factors to assist in this analysis, with no single factor being determinative. These factors are addressed below. B. Section 4 of the PPPA [40] The PPPA targets strategic lawsuits against public participation, or "SLAPP" suits. The "archetypal" SLAPP suit involves a powerful or wealthy plaintiff using litigation to silence criticism from a comparatively under-resourced defendant: Hansman SCC at para. 47; 1704604 Ontario Ltd. v. Pointes Protection Association, 2020 SCC 22 at para. 2 [Pointes]. [41] The PPPA does not, however, demand that a claim fit the archetype to have application. The defining feature of a SLAPP suit is that the underlying action aims to silence or deter a speaker, and suppress debate on matters of public interest, rather than to remedy harm suffered by the plaintiff: Hansman SCC at para. 48; Simán v. Eisenbrandt, 2024 BCCA 176 at para. 32 [Simán BCCA]; Galloway v. A.B., 2021 BCSC 2344 at para. 48; Hobbs v. Warner, 2021 BCCA 290 at para. 6. Such suits give rise to the above-noted tension between competing public interests: the interests of justice and the need to address disputes on their merits, and the interests of individuals and the public in protecting freedom of expression. [42] British Columbia joined Ontario and Quebec in passing legislation to mitigate the harmful effects of SLAPP litigation. The PPPA is modeled on the November 2015 legislation passed in Ontario, namely, the Ontario Protection of Public Participation Act, 2015, S.O. 2015, c. 23. Accordingly, the Supreme Court of Canada's guidance on that legislation is authoritative: see, e.g., Pointes and Bent v. Platnick, 2020 SCC 23. BC's PPPA has been considered by the Supreme Court of Canada in Hansman SCC, and by our Court of Appeal in Simán BCCA; Neufeld v. Hansman, 2021 BCCA 222; and Hobbs. [43] Mr. Dobrer's and Hillel's applications are brought pursuant to s. 4 of the PPPA, which provides: Application to court 4 (1) In a proceeding, a person against whom the proceeding has been brought may apply for a dismissal order under subsection (2) on the basis that (a) the proceeding arises from an expression made by the applicant, and (b) the expression relates to a matter of public interest. (2) If the applicant satisfies the court that the proceeding arises from an expression referred to in subsection (1), the court must make a dismissal order unless the respondent satisfies the court that (a) there are grounds to believe that (i) the proceeding has substantial merit, and (ii) the applicant has no valid defence in the proceeding, and (b) the harm likely to have been or to be suffered by the respondent as a result of the applicant's expression is serious enough that the public interest in continuing the proceeding outweighs the public interest in protecting that expression. [44] Section 4 of the PPPA provides a two-stage, multi-part, burden-shifting analysis. Justice Horsman helpfully and concisely summarized the task of the court under s. 4 in Simán BCCA at paras. 32-47, and I rely on and adopt that articulation entirely. [45] Section 4 of the PPPA requires addressing the following issues. 1. Section 4(1) [46] First, the applicant/defendant must show, on a balance of probabilities, that the proceeding arises from expression, and that this expression relates to a matter of public interest, pursuant to s. 4(1)(a) and (b). The applicant bears the burden under s. 4(1) as described in Simán BCCA: [35] Section 4(1) of the PPPA places a threshold burden on the applicant (defendant) to demonstrate, on a balance of probabilities, that: (i) the proceeding arises from an expression made by the applicant, and (ii) the expression relates to a matter of public interest. If the applicant fails to meet this burden, then the action will not be dismissed: Pointes at paras. 21-23. The words "matter of public interest" in s. 4(1)(b) of the PPPA are to be given a "broad and liberal interpretation": Pointes at para. 26. The expression must be assessed "as a whole", and it must be asked whether "some segment of the community would have a genuine interest in receiving information on the subject": Pointes at para. 27, citing Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61 at paras. 101-102. 2. Section 4(2)(a)(i) [47] If the applicant meets the burden under s. 4(1), the burden shifts to the respondent/plaintiff to demonstrate there are grounds to believe the action has substantial merit, pursuant to s. 4(2)(a)(i). The Supreme Court of Canada articulates this burden in Pointes: [49] [F]or an underlying proceeding to have "substantial merit", it must have a real prospect of success -- in other words, a prospect of success that, while not amounting to a demonstrated likelihood of success, tends to weigh more in favour of the plaintiff. In context with "grounds to believe", this means that the motion judge needs to be satisfied that there is a basis in the record and the law -- taking into account the stage of the proceeding -- for drawing such a conclusion. This requires that the claim be legally tenable and supported by evidence that is reasonably capable of belief. [50] Importantly, this standard is more demanding than the one applicable on a motion to strike, which requires that the claim have some chance of success under the "plain and obvious" test (Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., 1990 CanLII 90 (SCC), [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959). It is also more demanding than requiring that the claim have a reasonable prospect of success, which is a standard that this Court has also used to animate the "plain and obvious" test (R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42, [2011] 3 S.C.R. 45, at paras. 17-20). In light of the existence of a record, the substantial merit standard calls for an assessment of the evidentiary basis for the claim - this is why the claim must be supported by evidence that is reasonably capable of belief. This is consistent with the APR's references to "substantive" merit, which inherently calls for an assessment of the basis or evidentiary foundation for a claim. I reiterate, however, that a claim with merely some chance of success will not be sufficient to prevail. Nor will a claim that has been merely nudged over the line of having some chance of success. A real prospect of success requires that the claim have a prospect of success that, while not amounting to a demonstrated likelihood of success, tends to weigh more in favour of the plaintiff. See also, Simán BCCA at paras. 39-40. 3. Section 4(2)(a)(ii) [48] If the claim is found to have substantial merit, the respondent/plaintiff must then demonstrate there are grounds to believe there is no valid defence to the claim. This burden is described in Simán BCCA: [41] The applicant (defendant) must "put into play" the defences they intend to present. Once this is done, the burden is on the responding party to demonstrate there are grounds to believe there is "no valid defence" to the proceeding. The word "no" is absolute, and the corollary is that if there is any valid defence to the proceeding then the proceeding should be dismissed: Pointes at para. 58. This mirrors the inquiry on substantial merit. Both criteria entail an assessment by the application judge of the strength of the claim or any defences as part of an overall assessment of the prospect of success of the underlying claim: Pointes at para. 59. [42] Accordingly, the structure of the "no valid defence" analysis is: (i) the responding party must satisfy the judge that there are grounds to believe that the defences have no real prospect of success, (ii) this requires a showing that there are grounds to believe the defences do not tend to weigh more in favour of the defendant, (iii) this means there must be a basis in the record and the law--taking into account the stage of the proceeding--to support a finding that the defences do not tend to weigh more in favour of the defendant: Bent at para. 103. 4. Section 4(2)(b): [49] Lastly, the respondent/plaintiff must show that the harm they suffered, or will suffer, due to the applicant/defendant's expression, is serious enough that the public interest in continuing the proceeding outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression. The final stage of the s. 4 analysis is described as follows in Simán BCCA: [43] As a final hurdle, s. 4(2)(b) of the PPPA requires the responding party to satisfy the judge that the likely harm they have suffered, or will suffer, due to the defendant's expression is sufficiently serious that the public interest in permitting the proceeding to continue outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression. This provision serves as a "robust backstop" for the judge to dismiss even technically meritorious claims if the public interest in protecting the expression outweighs the public interest in allowing the proceeding to continue: Pointes at paras. 53, 62. In Pointes, the Court described the weighing exercise as the "crux" or "core" of the analysis because it engages directly with the overarching concerns that anti-SLAPP legislation seeks to address by assessing the public interest and public participation implications: Pointes at paras. 61-62; Hansman at para. 58. [44] As a prerequisite to the weighing exercise contemplated by s. 4(2)(b), the responding party must show two things: (i) the existence of harm, and (ii) the fact that the harm was suffered as a result of the applicant's expression: Pointes at para. 68; Hansman at paras. 67-68. Either monetary or non-monetary harm may be relevant to the existence of harm under the first criterion. There is no threshold requirement for the harm to be sufficiently worthy of consideration: Pointes at paras. 69-70. [45] However, the magnitude of the harm becomes relevant at the weighing stage of the analysis, when the judge must determine whether the harm is sufficiently serious that the public interest in allowing the underlying proceeding to continue outweighs the public interest in protecting the speech: Pointes at para. 70. Accordingly, while the presumption of damages in defamation law can establish the existence of harm, it cannot establish that the harm is "serious": Hansman at para. 67. As explained in Hansman: [67] To hold otherwise would be to presumptively tip the scales in favour of the plaintiff in defamation cases and effectively gut the weighing exercise. Rather, to succeed on the weighing exercise, a plaintiff must provide evidence that enables the judge "to draw an inference of likelihood" of harm of a magnitude sufficient to outweigh the public interest in protecting the defendant's expression (Pointes, at para. 71; Bent, at para. 154). Presumed general damages are insufficient for this purpose, as are bare assertions of harm. [Emphasis added in Simán BCCA.] [46] Even where the actual harm suffered by the responding party is serious, s. 4(2)(b) of the PPPA also requires some evidence that enables the judge to infer a causal link between the defendant's expression and the harm suffered: Pointes at para. 71; Hansman at para. 68. Evidence of a causal link between the expression and the harm will be especially important where the harm may have been caused by sources other than the defendant's expression: Pointes at para. 72. It should be borne in mind that at this stage, the plaintiff cannot be expected to provide a fully developed damages brief, and the application judge is not required to make definite findings of causation: Pointes at para. 115; Hansman at para. 65. [47] The evaluation of the public interest in protecting the defendant's expression is informed by s. 2(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, which "grounds the level of protection afforded to expression in the nature of the expression": Pointes at para. 77. The closer the defendant's expression is to the core values underlying freedom of expression, the greater the public interest in protecting it: Pointes at para. 77. The public interest weighing exercise is not simply an inquiry into whether the proceeding has the hallmarks of a SLAPP: Pointes at para. 79. [Underline emphasis in original.] [50] An application made under s. 4 also permits the court, on its own motion or as requested by the applicant, to award damages against the respondent should it be found that the respondent brought the proceeding in bad faith or for an improper purpose: PPPA, s. 8. [51] Additionally, if a dismissal order is made under s. 4, the applicant is, by default, entitled to full indemnity costs under s. 7 of the PPPA, unless such an order is "inappropriate in the circumstances": Mawhinney v. Stewart, 2023 BCCA 484 at paras. 44, 49. [52] In this application, Mr. Dobrer requests costs on a full indemnity basis and damages, while Hillel requests only the former. IV. Issues to Be Determined [53] Based on the legal framework outlined above, the issues in this case are as follows: a) Should the applications under the PPPA be allowed: i. Is the Sticker a form of expression; and if yes, does its subject matter, being the UBC SJC's views on the Israel-Palestine conflict, relate to a matter on which some segment of the community would have a genuine interest in receiving information? ii. If yes, is there a basis in the record and the law to conclude that Mr. Cheesman's claim in defamation against either, or both, Mr. Dobrer and Hillel, has substantial merit? iii. If yes, can Mr. Cheesman demonstrate there are grounds to believe there is no valid defence to the proceeding; in this case, the defence of fair comment, being the only defence put into play by both defendants? iv. If yes, is the harm alleged to be suffered by Mr. Cheesman serious enough that the public interest in allowing his defamation claim outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression (i.e., the Sticker)? b) Was Mr. Cheesman's claim advanced in bad faith or for an improper purpose; and if so, should damages be ordered against him? c) Finally, is an order for costs on a full indemnity basis against Mr. Cheesman inappropriate in the circumstances? V. The Parties' Arguments A. Section 4(1) [54] The applicant bears the burden under s. 4(1). [55] All parties agree the Sticker constitutes expression relating to a matter of public interest. B. Section 4(2)(a)(i) [56] The burden shifts to the plaintiff/respondent under s. 4(2). The first question is whether the plaintiff's claim has substantial merit under s. 4(2)(a)(ii). [57] Mr. Dobrer asserts that Mr. Cheesman's claim that the Sticker is defamatory faces substantial hurdles and the evidentiary foundation weighs more in favour of the defendants. He argues that, in determining whether the Sticker is defamatory, the court must consider the context of its publication. Specifically, he says the fact it was published to an audience familiar with the UBC SJC and their pro-Palestinian social justice advocacy following October 7th means the Sticker would represent an "obvious incongruity so infused with satire that a reasonable reader would not conclude a literal message was being conveyed." [58] Hillel, in arguing that the Sticker was not defamatory, says that the meaning conveyed was exaggerated and satirical, and although it may have an offensive meaning, this does not necessarily mean it was defamatory. On these bases, Mr. Dobrer and Hillel say there is no substantial merit to the claim that reputational harm occurred as a result of the Sticker. [59] Mr. Dobrer and Hillel further argue the sticker does not name Mr. Cheesman directly, by name or by description. Moreover, in the context of the UBC SJC, a heterogenous group lacking formal structure, the meaning of the Sticker is generalized and does not cause any individual reputational harm. [60] Mr. Dobrer disputes Mr. Cheesman's position that as a coordinator of the UBC SJC he was particularly referenced. Rather, Mr. Dobrer says that Mr. Cheesman was one of a dozen people who occupied coordinator positions and was deliberately not referred to on social media. Hillel further argued that Mr. Cheesman did not provide any evidence that anyone reached the conclusion that the Sticker referred to him. [61] In addition to the above, Hillel argues there is no merit to Mr. Cheesman's claim against them as there is no basis for vicarious liability. Hillel submits that Mr. Dobrer was an independent contractor, was not working for Hillel in any capacity related to the Stickers, and that Mr. Cheesman has not provided a factual basis for Hillel's vicarious liability. Instead, Hillel submits, a proper understanding of the entire relationship between Hillel and Mr. Dobrer, which included Hillel's lack of substantial control over how, when or where he did his work, and how the structure of Mr. Dobrer's work was more in the nature of a contractor, reveals that Hillel should not be liable for the conduct of Mr. Dobrer in the circumstances. [62] Mr. Cheesman submits that from an ordinary person's review of the Sticker, it is defamatory. It appears on its face to be expression by the UBC SJC and its leaders. Its natural and ordinary meaning, being that the UBC SJC supports Hamas, speaks to the character of the leaders of the UBC SJC, suggesting they "are the kind of people who would create stickers declaring their support [for a terrorist organization] and post them up around campus". Mr. Cheesman submits that those who saw the Sticker online or on campus would have done so without necessarily having any additional context and it could not be assumed to have been satirical. [63] Mr. Cheesman further submits the Sticker refers to him, being a member of a group of people defamed by the Sticker. Mr. Cheesman argues this is indicated by a number of factors including: that Mr. Cheesman is a prominent member of the UBC SJC on this issue in particular; the Sticker appears to have come directly from the UBC SJC so it would be believed to be the work of leadership; and, given his role in speaking on behalf of the UBC SJC, the Sticker would be connected to him. [64] Mr. Cheesman addresses the vicarious liability argument and asserts that the relationship between Hillel and Mr. Dobrer should be understood as an employer-employee relationship, given that Mr. Dobrer was enmeshed in Hillel's functions as an organization, using its contacts and e-mail address, and because Hillel substantially controlled Mr. Dobrer's work. He argues that the fact that Mr. Dobrer was called a contractor during the period the Sticker was published is irrelevant and that, looked at functionally, Mr. Dobrer was in fact identically positioned during that time as when he was named an employee. Mr. Cheesman argues that the publication of the Sticker was sufficiently related to the conduct Hillel authorized Mr. Dobrer to engage in, and that connection justifies imposing vicarious liability. C. Section 4(2)(a)(ii) [65] The plaintiff has the burden to show the defendant has raised no valid defence. Mr. Dobrer and Hillel raise the defence of fair comment in their pleadings. In Hansman SCC, the Supreme Court describes the defence of fair comment, and the court's role in analysing this defence, as [95] aim[ing] to keep the equilibrium in defamation law between two competing values: the protection of individual reputation from unwarranted attack, on one side, and the free debate "that is said to be the 'very life blood of our freedom and free institutions'" on the other (WIC Radio, at para. 1, quoting Price v. Chicoutimi Pulp Co. (1915), 1915 CanLII 66 (SCC), 51 S.C.R. 179, at p. 194). The task of courts in interpreting the defence is to reconcile these two values, not to prefer one over the other (WIC Radio, at para. 2). [66] To succeed in establishing fair comment, the defendant must establish, with a view to the full context of the expression: a) the comment is on a matter of public interest; b) the comment is based on fact; c) the comment, though it can include inferences of fact, must be recognisable as comment; d) the comment must satisfy an objective test: whether any person could honestly express that opinion on the proved facts; and e) even though the comment satisfies the objective test, the defence may be defeated if the plaintiff proves the defendant was actuated by express malice. See Rooney at para. 106, citing Hansman SCC at para. 96; Grant at para. 31; WIC Radio Ltd. v. Simpson, 2008 SCC 40 at para. 28. [67] To constitute fair comment, a factual basis for the expression must be indicated within the publication itself or the facts must be notorious: Hansman SCC at para. 99. There is no requirement that the facts to which the expression relates confirm the truth of the comment. The expression must relate to facts on which it is based but the defence is not a measure of the fairness of the comment. The purpose of requiring the facts underlying the comment to be known is to ensure that a reader can make up their own mind: Hansman SCC at para. 100. [68] Mr. Dobrer asserts that the Sticker meets each element of the fair comment defence. He says a reasonable reader would see the sticker as "lampooning the [UBC SJC] because the 'Palestinian resistance' it supported was the October 7th violent attack on Israeli civilians perpetrated by Hamas". He says that an assertion that the UBC SJC aligned itself too closely with Hamas is an opinion that the UBC SJC loves or admires Hamas. Mr. Dobrer says that although the Sticker's content conveys potentially offensive criticism and judgment, the pith and substance of it is commentary. [69] Mr. Dobrer asserts there is a factual basis for the commentary, relying on: a) "provocative" public statements published to the world on the UBC SJC's social media channel; b) a series of pro-Palestinian rallies and on-campus events organized, promoted, supported, or engaged in by the UBC SJC between October 7th, 2023, and November 17th, 2023; c) the UBC SJC being one of the main advocacy clubs on campus; and d) the UBC SJC's public social media account maintaining the above-mentioned statements and promotional literature. [70] Mr. Dobrer submits that these facts have been otherwise known or are so notorious to visitors at the UBC Vancouver campus, "such that the audience would have been able to make up their minds as to the merits of Mr. Dobrer's critique." He further submits the audience "would have been steeped in the atmosphere" on campus following October 7th, one that included the UBC SJC's posts and events. Mr. Dobrer submits that based on the UBC SJC's statements and conduct, a person could have honestly held the opinion offered by the stickers. [71] Mr. Dobrer takes issue with Mr. Cheesman's plea that the Sticker's statement was made with malice and asserts there is no allegation of fact raised that could support a plea of express malice. [72] Meanwhile, Hillel BC argues the defence of fair comment is met because the stickers were: a) readily recognisable as comment on a matter of public interest; b) based on notorious facts known to students and others on the UBC Vancouver campus; and c) expressed an opinion that, on an objective standard, a person could honestly hold. [73] Hillel BC describes the sticker as being "cartoon style", a riff on the UBC SJC's 'heart' logo, paired with the 'I [heart] New York' motif. They say this sticker would be understood by reasonable readers to express an opinion, criticism, or judgment, or another valid purpose fulfilled by political satire. Therefore, they conclude, the sticker did not convey a statement of fact, but a criticism or judgment. [74] Mr. Cheesman responds that the defence of fair comment does not have a real prospect of success. [75] First, Mr. Cheesman argues the facts on which the opinion is based were not known to the audience. They are not in the Stickers themselves, nor were they so notorious the audience would have sufficient information to decide the fairness, or unfairness, of the alleged comment. Mr. Dobrer's own knowledge and interpretation of the facts upon which he relied was insufficient to establish their notoriety. General on-campus and online audiences in particular would not have the basic knowledge or sufficient context about the UBC SJC to reach their own conclusions on the comment. The notorious fact, Mr. Cheesman says, is that Hamas is listed as a terrorist organization by the Canadian government; that Hamas conducted the October 7th attack; and unarmed civilians were harmed, abducted, and murdered in that attack. He argues that the defence of fair comment requires "more than the mere availability of the facts to the audience: they must be stated within the expression itself," and submits that no such facts are included in the expression, and this is fatal to the defence. [76] Second, Mr. Cheesman argues that many of the facts on which the defendants allege the opinion is based were false, taking issue particularly with: a) the defendants' characterization of the UBC club Solidarity for Palestinian Human Rights ("SPHR") being a 'sister organization' to the UBC SJC; b) their characterization of the Divestment Coalition and its activities; c) claims about UBC SJC's alleged role in a Vancouver Art Gallery protest characterized as inflammatory; and d) a claim the UBC SJC intended to 'storm' a UBC student union council meeting. [77] Essentially, Mr. Cheesman says there is no evidence to support Mr. Dobrer's characterization of the facts, and these cannot form the basis for a fair comment defence. He says Mr. Dobrer's opinion is based on an "incorrect perception that the [UBC] SJC is responsible for all pro-Palestine activity on campus". [78] Third, Mr. Cheesman submits the stickers were not recognisable as comment. He says the stickers were a hoax and were reasonably perceived by the audience as a genuine expression by the UBC SJC and Mr. Cheesman of their support for terrorism. He says there is nothing to indicate the stickers were satirical or inauthentic. Instead, the first-person phrasing and the UBC SJC logo gave the appearance of being a real expression by UBC SJC members, which is how an ordinary person without special knowledge would reasonably perceive them. Mr. Cheesman points to evidence that Mr. Dobrer himself testified that, in another context, the Sticker would not be recognizable as an expression of a lampooning opinion. In this testimony, Mr. Dobrer stated he genuinely believed that if someone else placed the Stickers on Hillel property, the meaning would be threatening to Jewish or pro-Israel students. [79] Fourth, Mr. Cheesman argues no one could honestly express the impugned opinion on proven facts. He says Mr. Dobrer has failed to point to any comments or actions by the UBC SJC or Mr. Cheesman that they are in favour of Hamas. Indeed, Mr. Dobrer testified he does not think the UBC SJC actually loves or supports Hamas, just that they foolishly aligned themselves with Hamas' propaganda. [80] Finally, Mr. Cheesman argues that malice may be presumed in this case, and in the alternative, there are factual grounds to believe Mr. Dobrer was motivated by malice. Mr. Cheesman challenges the credibility of Mr. Dobrer's claim he wanted to anonymously criticize the SJC and its student leaders for their stance on Palestine, and did so using fraudulent stickers because he was afraid to put his name to this criticism. Mr. Cheesman points to previous expressions made by Mr. Dobrer criticizing the UBC SJC and other groups in his own name. Moreover, Mr. Cheesman says that if Mr. Dobrer wished to remain anonymous, he could have shared his criticism in other, legitimate ways without impersonating the UBC SJC. Mr. Cheesman argues Mr. Dobrer's aim was to provoke a negative reaction aimed at the UBC SJC and its leadership, and that to publish a comment with the dominant purpose of injuring the plaintiff is malice and thus defeats the defence of fair comment. D. Section 4(2)(b) [81] The final stage of the s. 4 analysis remains the plaintiff's burden, requiring Mr. Cheesman to satisfy the court that the likely harm he has or will suffer due to the defendant's expression is sufficiently serious that the public interest in permitting the proceeding to continue outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression. [82] Mr. Dobrer argues the claim should be dismissed because it also does not satisfy the public interest hurdle. He says that Mr. Cheesman's reputation was not harmed by the Sticker, which did not identify him. And although he may have subjectively felt harassed, this harm and the harm to his interpersonal relationships may have been the product of the positions he was taking rather than the Sticker. Mr. Dobrer argues that Mr. Cheesman knew the topic was highly charged and controversial, and the UBC SJC publicly advocated a view that some disagreed with. [83] Mr. Dobrer claims that certain circumstances around the commencement of the lawsuit, which involve evidence of the support of the National Council of Canadian Muslims ("NCCM") for Mr. Cheesman's claim, are relevant. The NCCM was of the view the Sticker was Islamophobic, and that Mr. Cheesman and the UBC SJC were victims of that Islamophobia. NCCM contacted Mr. Cheesman and ultimately agreed to indemnify him in bringing the claim. Based on this, Mr. Dobrer alleges that "what is really going on here is an attempt by a non-party to have another instigate a weak lawsuit for political and strategic gain." [84] Lastly, Mr. Dobrer relies on several factors in support of his argument that the public interest justifies dismissing the action. In particular, he argues about the importance of his free expression rights and the vital public interest in counter-speech on geopolitical issues on campus. He also asserts the Sticker made a contribution to the dialogue on campus in response to the UBC SJC's pro-Palestinian statements. [85] Hillel argues the Sticker deals with valuable political speech. They say this litigation is not about limiting Mr. Cheesman's free speech rights, it is about Mr. Dobrer's right to respond to the UBC SJC "without the threat of civil liability". Hillel echoes Mr. Dobrer's position that Mr. Cheesman himself has not suffered personal harm. Being an activist on controversial topics, he cannot complain of reputational harm as the result of others disagreeing with him. Hillel also submits that Mr. Cheesman has not provided sufficient evidence of such harm on a prima facie basis, and that if his reputation has suffered it may be caused by his affiliation with the UBC SJC's positions. [86] Hillel argues the court should consider the risk of a chilling effect on political speech including satire on campus. In addition, Hillel claims there is a public interest in protecting Mr. Dobrer's expression, which Hillel characterizes as counter-speech motivated by the defence of a vulnerable or marginalised group. [87] Hillel submits that Mr. Cheesman's claim bears indicia of a SLAPP suit, including: a) the lawsuit is about punishing Hillel and raising political awareness; b) Mr. Cheesman is not actually pursuing a damages award against Mr. Dobrer, but against Hillel; c) the NCCM is facilitating the lawsuit; d) the damage to Mr. Cheesman's personal reputation, which is denied, is nominal; and e) permitting the continuance of this proceeding will have broader chilling effects. [88] Hillel echoes Mr. Dobrer's position that 'what is really going on here' is the NCCM is using Mr. Cheesman as a proxy to pursue strategic litigation against Hillel. [89] Mr. Cheesman submits that the harm he suffered is serious enough that the public interest in continuing the proceeding outweighs the public interest in protecting the Stickers. He refers to case law that states that accusations of supporting terrorism are serious and damaging, and on their own are sufficient to establish serious harm for the purposes of an anti-SLAPP application even absent specific evidence of harm, and to give rise to a presumption of malice and significant damages: Lascaris v. B'nai Brith Canada, 2019 ONCA 163 at para. 40; Paramount v. Kevin J. Johnston, 2019 ONSC 2910 at para. 70. [90] Mr. Cheesman describes the serious harm he suffered as including an onslaught of abusive backlash online; straining of his interpersonal relationships; and fear and anxiety. Mr. Cheesman submits the defendants are arguing for an unjustifiably high bar for him to establish harm that would justify the dismissal of a s. 4 application. [91] Mr. Cheesman denies the defendants' claim that the harm he experienced was caused by his activism, not the Sticker specifically. There is no evidence, he submits, that the online vitriol that occurred was because of Mr. Cheesman's expressions of his activism about Israel and Palestine, or that anyone posting knew about Mr. Cheesman or the UBC SJC. Instead, they were reacting to the Sticker. He was aware that not everyone agreed with him, but other than responses to the Sticker, people were not attacking him or giving him cause for concern about his reputation. He further says that his activism long predated the change in his relationships with his friends and their parents. Mr. Cheesman argues there is a significant difference between legitimate criticism and disagreement, and defamatory publication. He says he "worked hard to cultivate and express a nuanced perspective", but these stickers damaged that specific reputation. [92] Mr. Cheesman also says that Hillel's and the UBC SJC's statements made after the fact have not remediated his reputation, as these do not include having apologized or even clearly expressed that the Sticker did not come from the UBC SJC. [93] In response to the defendants' arguments that his activism opened him up to responses like the Stickers and the subsequent reaction, Mr. Cheesman submits that engaging in the public debate on controversial issues does not permit an "open season" on his reputation or disproportionate or gratuitous counter-speech. Mr. Cheesman describes Mr. Dobrer's actions as both disproportionate and gratuitous, saying he "created a hoax that scared community members and directed vitriol at [Mr.] Cheesman and other [UBC] SJC members". Mr. Cheesman asks the court not to accept that, by wading into a sensitive and political public debate, an individual therefore accepts the stress, fear, and anxiety that arises when targeted by a defamatory hoax. [94] Mr. Cheesman further says that his motivation in this litigation (i.e., to stand up for his right to express his views on Palestine) is not suspect or improper, nor it is punitive or retributory against Mr. Dobrer. Mr. Cheesman's subjective reasons for this litigation are irrelevant, as the case is still aimed at remediating the harms he suffered for Mr. Dobrer's defamatory statements. It is not Mr. Cheesman's freedom of expression that has been impacted, but his credibility as an activist: he seeks to repair his reputation so he may be taken seriously as an activist, given this defamation. [95] Mr. Cheesman submits that conversely there is little public interest in protecting the stickers, because they are not close to the core value of free expression, arguing: "[a] hoax is by its very nature contrary to the goal of truth-seeking." He says Mr. Dobrer created the stickers to appear genuine, not actually as a 'lampoon' or a satirical comment about the UBC SJC. Those who saw these stickers reacted as though student activists at UBC had shown support for a terrorist organization, and did not serve a truth-seeking function; they "created an online furor that was not based on reality." He argues they also were contrary to the s. 2(b) value of political participation, as a hoax does not encourage open political participation, it is meant to silence opponents. Mr. Cheesman says this lawsuit is not about chilling free expression rights but about Mr. Dobrer's "choice to use a hoax to direct vitriol at his political opponents". [96] Finally, Mr. Cheesman addresses the 'what is really going on' arguments advanced by the defendants. He argues this question is not about conspiracy theories, but whether the case is truly a SLAPP suit. More specifically, he says that the NCCM's support of his lawsuit does not suggest anything suspicious or improper, as the NCCM is not the litigant nor is this a proxy claim by the NCCM against Hillel. He says the defendants are mischaracterizing the evidence about the NCCM's role in this litigation. Furthermore, Mr. Cheesman submits there is nothing wrong with the NCCM connecting with a non-Muslim student activist in a context relevant to Islamophobia. Rather, it is a way for a litigant of limited means to pursue their rights in court, and non-profit organizations commonly support test cases relevant to their mandate. A non-profit funding a piece of litigation that they agree with in principle does not make them the 'real litigant'. VI. Hillel BC's Standing [97] Mr. Cheesman raised the issue of Hillel's standing to bring an application under s. 4. Mr. Cheesman submits that Hillel may not simultaneously plead they did not make the impugned expression and have standing under the PPPA to apply to dismiss the claim. On this point, Mr. Cheesman relies on Christman v. Lee-Sheriff, 2023 BCCA 363 at paras. 64-65, where our Court of Appeal determined that the PPPA does not permit the applicant to meet the burden under s. 4(1)--that the proceeding arises from expression made by the applicant--if they simultaneously deny having made the expression. [98] There the court held that the defendant, who denied making the impugned expression, failed to meet the burden of proof under s. 4(1) and so the PPPA application could proceed no further: [65] Thus, as the moving party or applicant, Mr. Christman failed to meet the initial burden of proof under s. 4(1) if he denied making the expression. Therefore, his application regarding the Presentation Slander Allegation must fail at the outset. There is no "expression" to consider. [69] Mr. Christman's submission is also at odds with the purpose of the PPPA. Mr. Christman contends that the moving party in the PPPA application can meet their burden even if they actually deny making the expression at all in the underlying defamation action. This submission urges a myopic and artificial focus on the PPPA application without the broader context of the litigation, and therefore the purposes of the PPPA itself. The PPPA demands that the impugned expression be considered in relation to the public interest (Pointes, at para. 82). More specifically, anti-SLAPP legislation urges courts to strike a balance between the value of free expression and a plaintiff's right to combat slanderous, libelous, or defamatory expressions via litigation (Pointes, at para. 64). Mr. Christman's non-admission of the expression at issue means that one essential ingredient of the two-part weighing exercise he asks us to conduct is missing. If Mr. Christman denies having made one of the impugned expressions, there is simply no "public participation in the debates of the issues of the day" for the PPPA to protect (Neufeld, 2021 BCCA 223, at para. 3). Accordingly, Mr. Christman cannot seek the protection of the PPPA for that expression. [70] Again, the PPPA's purpose is to filter out defamation suits where the intention is to silence or intimidate others. The proposition that one may benefit from the protection of the PPPA, bearing its legislative purpose in mind, while simultaneously denying having ever made the impugned expression, is irreconcilable with that purpose. It is illogical for an individual to claim the defamation suit against them is a silencing attempt, while purporting never to have uttered the expression in the first place. [99] Mr. Cheesman acknowledges that Christman is distinguishable to a degree, as in that case the applicant denied there was any expression to consider. Here, Hillel acknowledges the expression and its source, Mr. Dobrer, but denies vicarious liability. Mr. Cheesman argues, however, that the specific finding in Christman applies, particularly that an applicant cannot satisfy the burden under s. 4(1)(a) to establish the proceeding arises from an expression they made without pleading they made that expression. [100] Hillel submits the facts in this case are notably different and do not engage with the rationale for the finding in Christman. In particular: a) the content of the publication at issue (the Sticker) is well-established; b) the author of the publication at issue has admitted publishing it; and c) Hillel does not deny the existence of a relationship with Mr. Dobrer at the material time but says rather its limited relationship does not and cannot attract vicarious liability. [101] Hillel further argues that Christman cannot be read broadly to deny persons who employ consultants or contractors from bringing PPPA motions, on facts where the expression is admitted, thereby depriving them of the benefits of the PPPA and forcing them to proceed to trial. They say that employers of employees or independent contractors must be permitted to apply under the PPPA when the expression is admitted yet deny liability for publication. [102] Given my ultimate conclusion that the action should be dismissed as being without substantial merit (which arises from Mr. Dobrer's application in any event of Hillel's standing), it may not be strictly necessary to determine Hillel's standing. I offer the following reasons for why I would permit Hillel's standing in this case nevertheless. [103] While I agree with Mr. Cheesman that the core purpose of the PPPA is to protect defendants facing attempts to silence their participation in public discourse, in the present case, I would find that Hillel has standing to bring the application. [104] The allegation of vicarious liability against Hillel supports Hillel's standing. Although Hillel denies it published the Sticker itself, it admits that its former employee and contractor, Mr. Dobrer, did publish the Sticker. Essentially, Mr. Cheesman's claim asks that--for liability purposes--the court find that Hillel stands in the place of Mr. Dobrer and is in that sense responsible for his conduct. In my view, where Hillel has been brought in to stand in a position of responsibility for liability, and Mr. Dobrer's conduct is suggested to be attributable to Hillel, it should not be disentitled from participating in the statutory process available under the PPPA had it been alleged to be directly rather than vicariously liable. [105] To exclude the employer from the PPPA's framework, and force the parties to trial separately on the issue of vicarious liability, may also be inconsistent with the purposes of the legislation, which include provision of a summary process for disposition of alleged SLAPP suits. In any event this would be inefficient in that it would require Hillel to bring a separate motion to strike or other summary application to dismiss the claim, and result in duplication of proceedings. [106] I also conclude that denying standing in this case would not align with the rationale underlying Christman. This case is not one where there is simply no expression admitted that amounts to "public participation in the debates of the issues of the day" for the PPPA to protect: Christman at para. 69. I was not taken to any authority that applies Christman to deny standing in a similar situation. In the specific facts of this case, it would not be in the interests of justice to read Christman to apply so broadly as to deny standing to Hillel where the expression is admitted but liability is contested. Finally, since the application by Hillel is advanced in parallel with the application by Mr. Dobrer, there is little utility in dismissing the application based on standing. In the circumstances, I would have permitted Hillel to make submissions on the application as an affected party, and I conclude that in any event I would exercise my discretion to grant standing for Hillel's application to proceed. VII. Decision [107] In summary, my decision respecting the application of s. 4 of the PPPA is as follows. [108] The Sticker is expression contemplated under s. 4(1)(a, b) of the PPPA. Accordingly, the analysis moves to the next step. [109] Under s. 4(2)(a)(i) Mr. Cheesman has not demonstrated there are grounds to believe the claim has substantial merit because the expression in the Sticker is insufficiently connected to the plaintiff himself. I do not agree with the defendants that there is no substantial merit to the assertion that the Sticker was defamatory in nature. However, the question of identification is essential, and s. 4(2)(a)(i) is ultimately determinative of this matter. I therefore allow the applications on the basis that the claim in defamation does not have substantial merit. [110] I do not need to make any additional determinations under s. 4(2)(a)(ii) or 4(2)(b). [111] In the circumstances I do not order damages under s. 8, and I conclude that full indemnity costs are not appropriate in the circumstances, for reasons expressed below. A. Section 4(1) [112] This action by Mr. Cheesman against Mr. Dobrer and Hillel arises from expression (the Sticker) and relates to a matter of public interest (the conflict in Israel and Palestine). The parties agree on this question and I concur that the application meets the threshold hurdle under s. 4(1). The analysis therefore proceeds to the questions under s. 4(2). B. Section 4(2) [113] At the second stage, set out in s. 4(2), the burden of proof shifts to the plaintiff. At this point, the plaintiff must satisfy the court that a) there are grounds to believe the action in defamation has substantial merit; b) the defendant has no valid defence; and c) that the public interest in permitting the proceeding to continue outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression. If the plaintiff is unable to satisfy the court that it has met this burden, the application will be granted and the action dismissed: PPPA, s. 4(2)(a) and (b); Pointes at para. 18. [114] There are several elements of the burden under s. 4(2)(a) that have been expanded upon by our courts, including in Simán BCCA. [115] The standard for "grounds to believe" under 4(2)(a)(i) has been found to mean "the existence of a basis in the record and the law--taking into account the stage of litigation--for reaching the belief or conclusion that the legislated criteria have been met": Simán BCCA at para. 37; Pointes at paras. 36, 39, 49. Such a 'basis' exists "if there is any basis in the record and the law--provided it is legally tenable and reasonably capable of belief--to support a finding of substantial merit and the absence of a valid defence": Simán BCCA at para. 37; Bent at para. 88. [116] The standard for "substantial merit" under s. 4(2)(a)(i) is defined as meaning the action must have a "real prospect of success--in other words, a prospect of success that tends to weigh more in favour of the plaintiff": Pointes, para 49. The Supreme Court in Pointes concluded that this requires the claim be "legally tenable and supported by evidence that is reasonably capable of belief." para. 49. [117] The standard for "real prospect of success" is higher than a 'plain and obvious' test on a motion to strike; it must be more than an arguable case: Simán BCCA at para. 40, citing Pointes at para. 50. 1. Elements of a Defamation Claim [118] It bears restating the essential elements required for a claim in defamation in assessing whether Mr. Cheesman's claim has substantial merit. To make out a claim that a statement is defamatory, a plaintiff must demonstrate: a) the expression would tend to lower the plaintiff's reputation in the eyes of a reasonable person; b) the impugned expression in fact referred to the plaintiff; and c) the impugned words were published, in that they were communicated to at least one person other than the plaintiff. See Bondar at para. 16; Grant at para. 28. [119] Since it is admitted that the Sticker was published, the burden is on Mr. Cheesman to demonstrate his claim in defamation against the defendants has a real prospect of success under the first two branches of the inquiry stated above. a) Is the Sticker Defamatory? [120] Mr. Cheesman argues the Sticker has a defamatory meaning in that the content would tend to lower the subject's reputation in the eyes of a reasonable person. He says the natural and ordinary meaning of the Sticker is that the UBC SJC supports Hamas, a terrorist organization, and that the Sticker suggests the leaders of the UBC SJC are the types of people who would create stickers declaring that support and publishing them. He argues the Sticker would lead persons reasonably acquainted with him to believe the Sticker referred to Mr. Cheesman and held the view expressed in the Sticker. [121] As noted above, the objective assessment of whether a statement is defamatory requires looking at the impugned expression through the lens of a reasonable person. This person is reasonably thoughtful and informed, but not excessively sensitive or fragile: Hudson at para. 105. [122] Mr. Dobrer submits that the context of the Sticker demonstrates it should not be found to have a defamatory meaning. He argues it is informative that the timing of the Sticker's publication was shortly after the October 7 attack, and in the period in which the UBC SJC had published commentary describing the events as "Palestinian resistance". He describes the UBC SJC's advocacy as "provocative, pro-Palestinian social justice offensive organized, promoted and staged by the [UBC] SJC on UBC's Vancouver campus". [123] In this context, Mr. Dobrer says the Sticker "represents an obvious incongruity so infused with satire that a reasonable reader would not conclude a literal message was being conveyed." He submits that such a reasonable reader would understand the Sticker to be satire and not literal. As such, no reputational harm could occur. [124] I disagree with this aspect of Mr. Dobrer's submission. It is not clear on its face, nor in the evidence of how the Sticker was understood, that there is an "obvious" incongruity plainly or even subtly conveying that the message of the Sticker was not literal. The Sticker is designed to appear as though it was made by the UBC SJC, and there is no context whatsoever in the publication that suggests otherwise. It is not clear to me from the record that, even if a reasonable reader possessed the contextual knowledge of current events on campus, or even if they were knowledgably placed within the ongoing dialogue at UBC about the Israel-Palestine conflict, there could be an indication the Sticker was inauthentic or satirical. [125] Accordingly, I cannot agree there is no reasonable prospect of success for Mr. Cheesman's claim on the basis that the content of the expression is not defamatory. It is not apparent that the audience for the Sticker, being anyone on campus who saw it, and anyone online who reviewed a post or re-post of the Sticker, would have sufficient or any context to know the UBC SJC is an organization that condemns violence and advocates for human rights, not a terrorism-supporting organization on campus, and that the facial meaning of the Sticker is contradictory. [126] I further disagree with the defendants' argument that it is obvious that the Sticker is satirical or lampooning the UBC SJC. The context of the Sticker, unlike a political cartoon, published in a newspaper and appearing as a cartoon, does not bear any indication of being satirical. It is mimicry: it appears to have the name and logo of the UBC SJC in a format used by the organization. Without anything further, in the context of a very active debate on campus regarding these issues, it is not apparent to me that a reader would perceive any satirical form of expression in the Sticker, which resembles more a prank or a hoax than a message lampooning the UBC SJC. The evidence suggests the heated discourse at the time was such that it would not have been surprising for even a reader who was reasonably informed to conclude that an organization supporting Palestinian resistance may also publish a seemingly extreme statement like, "I ❤️ Hamas". [127] I agree with Mr. Cheesman that an ordinary person with no special knowledge would likely believe the Sticker is genuine and would thus infer that UBC SJC members created and posted the Sticker. That belief would lead a reasonable person to conclude that members of the UBC SJC support a terrorist organization and would create stickers indicating their support for it. This would diminish the reputation of the person or people referred to. Mr. Cheesman has thus shown there are grounds to believe the Sticker is defamatory. b) Does the Sticker Refer to Mr. Cheesman? [128] Mr. Dobrer and Hillel argue that Mr. Cheesman's defamation claim cannot succeed because the Sticker does not name or otherwise identify Mr. Cheesman. I conclude this position has purchase. In Bou Malhab v. Diffusion Métromédia CMR Inc., 2011 SCC 9, the Court states, "[i]n any action in defamation, injury is proved if the plaintiff satisfies the judge that the impugned comments are defamatory, that is, that an ordinary person would believe that they tarnished the plaintiff's reputation": para. 57 (emphasis added). The Sticker does not on its face name Mr. Cheesman, the only plaintiff. Accordingly, it falls to Mr. Cheesman to demonstrate that it would reasonably be understood to refer to him. [129] Mr. Cheesman argues his role in the organization as a leader and public-facing participant would lead to the conclusion that the Sticker referred to him. Mr. Cheesman also argues the Sticker refers to him as a member of a limited class of people to whom the Sticker refers. [130] A defamatory statement that does not specifically reference the plaintiff may be found to refer to the plaintiff if it "may reasonably be found to refer to the plaintiff in the light of surrounding circumstances": Mainstream Canada v. Staniford, 2012 BCSC 1433 at para. 125, citing Butler at para. 39. [131] At common law, an actionable defamatory statement must have been "of and concerning" the plaintiff. Statements may be defamatory without specifically identifying a plaintiff. If they do not refer to a plaintiff by name, they can be found to be "of and concerning" the plaintiff in light of surrounding circumstances: Casses v. Canadian Broadcasting Corporation, 2015 BCSC 2150 at paras. 334-335. [132] In determining whether the plaintiff may be said to have been personally injured by defamation expressed in relation to a group, the court must look to factors that would tend to indicate the personal reputation of the individual plaintiff has been harmed: Bou Malhab at paras. 48, 64. [133] The Supreme Court of Canada in Bou Malhab set out a range of non-exhaustive considerations used to determine whether personal injury has been sustained, warning that none of the factors is determinative on its own, and regard must be given to the particular facts in issue: paras. 48, 58. The focus of the inquiry is on the "intensity of suspicion" that the expression relates to the plaintiff, in the mind of a sensible person: Butler at paras. 56-60; Bou Malhab at para. 64. [134] The factors outlined by the court in Bou Malhab at paras. 58-78 include: a) The size of the defamed group to which the individual plaintiff belongs. The larger the group, the harder it is for a plaintiff to prove personal injury. There is no maximum size, and the inquiry remains whether the statement was of and concerning the plaintiff, and to what degree a reasonable person would suspect the comments relate to the plaintiff. b) The nature of the group. Generally, the more homogenous the group is, and the more formally organized, the easier it will be to show that the injury attaches to a member of the group. The more heterogenous, the more implausible an allegation of personal injury. If a group's members are identifiable in the community, it will be easier to prove personal injury. c) The plaintiff's relationship with the group. A person who is well recognized as a member of a group is more likely to experience damage to reputation because of comments about the group. The specific positioning of the plaintiff within the group may make it more or less plausible that the comment in question can be understood to relate to them personally. d) The real target of the defamation. The characteristics of the expression can be considered to determine the real target of the speech. Its specificity or the generality may influence whether the expression could give rise to personal injury, and it will be more difficult to show that a vague statement has an injurious character than one that is precise. e) The seriousness or extravagance of the allegations. Expression that is more inflammatory or serious may be more likely to prove personal injury; however, if it is exaggerated or excessive it may have less credence. f) The plausibility of the comments. A more convincing allegation will make it easier to connect expression with a member of a group personally. Implausible allegations will be more readily brushed aside. g) Extrinsic factors. The courts have also considered the medium of the comments and their context, which can cause expression to be attached to certain individuals and defame them personally. [135] In considering these factors, as well as the Supreme Court of Canada's direction to carry out a review of the general context, being the "best approach for identifying personal attacks camouflaged behind the generality of an attack on a group," I conclude that the Sticker, though capable of defamation in its substance, does not sufficiently attach to Mr. Cheesman: Bou Malhab at para. 79. I conclude that Mr. Cheesman has not shown there are grounds to believe a reasonable person would connect the expression to him, giving rise to a personal harm. [136] The non-exhaustive factors listed above each have a degree of application to this case. Although I do not address each separately, my assessment is that cumulatively, Mr. Cheesman's claim that the Sticker is 'of and concerning' him does not have a real prospect of success. [137] The Sticker refers to the UBC SJC generally. Although Mr. Cheesman had a central role in the organization, and even a public-facing role, the evidence does not suggest he was the face or name associated with the organization, such that speech about the UBC SJC was understood to be speech about Mr. Cheesman. The UBC SJC tended to conduct its communications without attribution to a particular member, and Mr. Cheesman himself did not use his name in relation to social media posts by the organization. While it is not determinative of a reasonable person's interpretation, the evidence also did not disclose that Mr. Cheesman was referred to in responses by social media posters to the Sticker. [138] The size and nature of the group, and Mr. Cheesman's relationship with it, are prominent considerations in this case. It is true that Mr. Cheesman was a committed and active coordinator within the UBC SJC and had public-facing duties. He was one of approximately 12 coordinators and at least 30 active members. The group did not have an executive or board that had a public facing role that would lead a viewer to attach a defamatory statement about the group to the individual leaders. [139] The real target of the allegedly defamatory statement is a dominant factor in this case. Here the Sticker targets the UBC SJC as an organization. It is of course comprised of its members, but the aim of the Sticker, on its face, appears to be to depict the group and its efforts qua organization as extremist and philosophically affiliated with terrorism. In my view it does so without more specifically targeting members or leaders of the group and identifying them as having those allegiances. The medium of the Sticker presents a message at a high level of generality, and so does not suggest the expression refers to particular identifiable individuals and thus result in personal injury. [140] The evidence shows the significant contributions he has made to the UBC SJC, and the involved and central role he performed as a coordinator. The extent of his efforts and the genuineness of his commitment to the work is without doubt. Despite his role as a leader in the organization, it is not apparent to me that, on a standard of substantial likelihood of success, Mr. Cheesman could demonstrate the Sticker was speech made in reference to him such that it could be found to have caused him reputational damage. [141] In summary, the content of the Sticker was of too high a level of generality to attach to Mr. Cheesman personally, and he has not provided evidence to show there is a real prospect of success for this element of the claim. That is, Mr. Cheesman has not shown grounds to believe that a sensible person, observing the Sticker, would understand it refers to Mr. Cheesman. Accordingly, I cannot conclude that his claim has substantial merit, and the action will be dismissed on application of PPPA s. 4(2)(a)(i). [142] As my conclusion under s. 4(2)(a)(i) is determinative of these applications, this ends the analysis. I do not need to decide the issue of vicarious liability. I also do not need to decide whether the fair comment defence raised by the defendants is insufficient to provide a valid defence in the proceeding. Nor do I need to carry out the analysis set out in s. 4(2)(b) respecting whether the harm suffered by the plaintiff as a result of the expression is serious enough that the public interest in continuing the proceeding outweighs the public interest in protecting the expression. I offer no obiter conclusions on these issues. C. Damages and Costs [143] The PPPA permits a court to make an order for damages pursuant to s. 8, where it is found that the respondent brought the proceeding in bad faith or for an improper purpose. This section "represents an effort to separate out a subset of SLAPP [suits]" (emphasis in original), cases where bringing the proceeding goes beyond an attempt to limit expression and includes efforts to intimidate or infliction of harm on the defendant: Reynolds v. Deep Water Recovery Ltd., 2024 BCSC 1922 at para. 7, citing Todsen v. Morse, 2022 BCSC 1341 at para. 198. See also, Hobbs v. Warner, 2019 BCSC 2196, rev'd on other grounds 2021 BCCA 290; United Soils Management Ltd. v. Mohammed, 2019 ONCA 128. [144] Section 7 of the PPPA, meanwhile, has been interpreted to provide full indemnity costs to the applicant by default, unless this order would be inappropriate in the circumstances. Finding such an order inappropriate is a discretionary decision. Many factors may inform this decision, including but not limited to SLAPP indicia, the motivations of the parties, and the conduct of the parties. See Mawhinney at paras. 44-45, 49. [145] Mr. Dobrer and Hillel argue that to order damages in this case would mark this Court's disapproval of the claim and what Mr. Dobrer describes as a distortion of the litigation process. They claim, further to their arguments under s. 4(2)(b) that the Court ought to "scrutinize what is really going on", that the action is brought for some improper purpose. The defendants ask that I characterize Mr. Cheesman's claim as being in bad faith and as a party meddling in private litigation to punish the defendants. [146] As the considerations for damages include the indicia of a SLAPP suit, I note here that the archetypal SLAPP suit contains the following characteristics: a) a history of the plaintiff using litigation or the threat of litigation to silence critics; b) a financial or power imbalance that strongly favours the plaintiff; c) a punitive or retributory purpose animating the plaintiff's bringing of the claim; and d) minimal or nominal damages suffered by the plaintiff. See Pointes at para. 78, citing Platnick v. Bent, 2018 ONCA 687, aff'd 2020 SCC 23, at para. 99. [147] On the evidence before me, I do not find that Mr. Cheesman's claim can be characterized as an archetypal SLAPP suit, nor do I find that it was brought in bad faith or for an improper purpose. [148] Considering the SLAPP indicia referenced above I would not conclude that the evidence shows Mr. Cheesman used litigation, or the threat of it, to silence his critics. There is also no evidence of a financial or power imbalance strongly favouring the plaintiff; rather, the evidence shows that Mr. Cheesman would not have been able to bring this claim without the assistance of the NCCM. [149] As for the question of whether the purpose of the action is punitive or retributory, I find this characteristic is not present. [150] The defendants characterize Mr. Cheesman's purpose in bringing this lawsuit as improper and in bad faith, and ask this Court to look at "what is really going on". I do not agree with the defendants' position in this regard. To the extent that I am required to assess "what is really going on" in the litigation, I have reviewed the arguments and evidence relied on by the parties and conclude, on the record before me, there is nothing apparently insidious about this litigation. The fact that I find it should be dismissed under the PPPA does not undermine this finding. [151] That the action is defective does not mean that it is brought for an improper purpose, which is the test under s. 8. It goes without saying that in most cases one party loses, but a party who loses is not by definition a bad faith actor. The action was commenced by the UBC SJC as co-plaintiff, an allegedly improper party, and I have found the remaining party, Mr. Cheesman, does not have sufficient connection to the expression to sustain the claim. These features may be serious and ultimately incurable flaws, but they do not necessarily depict a suit brought for an improper purpose. [152] The defendants here are particularly concerned about Mr. Cheesman's evidence that NCCM had a role in his pursuit of the litigation, specifically that the NCCM have agreed to indemnify him for adverse costs awards. The evidence discloses that the NCCM viewed the Sticker as having an Islamophobic message and that Mr. Cheesman and the UBC SJC were affected by Islamophobia due to its publication. [153] Counsel for Mr. Dobrer relied heavily on an interview Mr. Cheesman gave to a local publication when arguing what the "real purpose" of the litigation was. In that interview, Mr. Cheesman commented that goals of the litigation included to curtail intimidation tactics and remind people of the conflict between Israel and Palestine. [154] Although he asked about this very subject during cross-examination on his affidavit, counsel for Mr. Dobrer declined to take the court to the sworn testimony of Mr. Cheesman that provided reasons for pursuing litigation which expressly included the aim of clearing his reputation. This omission was not of assistance to the Court's effort to make a decision on the basis of a reasonably accurate assessment of the record. [155] Mr. Cheesman testified that without the support of the NCCM he would not have been able to pursue the claim, because he did not have the funds to do so. However, he also testified that the rationale for doing so with the financial support that was offered was in order "to clear our names" and "set the record straight that I and the [UBC] SJC do not love Hamas because of the deluge of threats that we received as a result of the stickers. That's why the lawsuit exists." I would not conclude that the fact that an anti-Islamophobic non-profit organization agreed to support an individual and organization who felt they had suffered harm by being falsely portrayed as terrorist-sympathisers diminishes the genuineness or character of the litigation. Whether or not the NCCM supported Mr. Cheesman's litigation does not change the nature of the litigation. The evidence suggests Mr. Cheesman had various reasons for pursuing litigation, some for personal vindication and remedy for reputational harm, others related to his political commitments. [156] As noted above, the PPPA is intended to address 'strategic' lawsuits against public participation because this type of action has the effect of quashing important public discourse. In Hansman SCC, the Supreme Court of Canada described a SLAPP as not having the goal of a legal victory, "but a political one: to intimidate and suppress criticism with the threat of costly litigation": para. 46. The court further stated that a key feature of a SLAPP is "the strategic use of the legal system to silence contrary viewpoints": Hansman SCC at para. 46. The fact that Mr. Cheesman expresses that his litigation has political facets, does not render the litigation legally problematic in this case. Mr. Cheesman's political motivation expressed in relation to the suit was one of vindication, not silencing--he wanted to clear his name and that of the UBC SJC and ensure that the perspective they advanced was able to remain one that could be heard on campus. [157] Mr. Cheesman's claim, in my view, cannot be described as one intended to "suppress debate". Without finding what the intention of the Sticker may have been or the value of its expression, on its face it did not appear to be an individual's significant contribution to debate in the meaning understood by the PPPA. The PPPA is a legislative instrument intended to protect the vital right of free expression of individuals whose rights are curtailed by forces larger and more powerful than themselves. Individuals should be able to speak out on matters of public interest. They should be able to do so without unjustified limits imposed on them by the state. While individuals are to be accountable for their false or harmful publications, they should also be able to express themselves without litigation being used coercively to suppress speech rather than protect individual reputation. [158] Here the speech was not attributed to its author, and was made by a person who did not intend to take credit for it, much less champion the message (although later properly admitted to publishing the Sticker). The speech was made in the guise of expression by the UBC SJC itself and was created in a manner that could reasonably be expected to fool an audience into thinking it was genuine. This does not mean that a defamation claim would succeed in relation to the Sticker. It does, however, weigh in favour of the Plaintiff's argument that his action is not motivated by the desire to suppress opposing viewpoints, but by the proper aims of a defamation suit, being vindication of harms to reputation caused by perceived disinformation. [159] Although a claim in defamation does not have to be 'archetypal' for the PPPA to apply, as it does in this case, not every claim subject to the PPPA should be viewed in the same light. Having reviewed the evidence relied on, it is my view that the defendants' arguments about the role or participation of the NCCM were misplaced and ultimately unhelpful. [160] Lastly, while there is an argument to be made that Mr. Cheesman's damages claim is minimal or nominal, it would be premature to make that assessment, and I make no finding as to that SLAPP characteristic here. This factor does not alter my ultimate determination that Mr. Cheesman's claim was not brought in bad faith or for an improper purpose. Therefore, I decline to order damages. [161] As for the defendants' requests for costs on a full indemnity basis, I do not consider this an appropriate case for full indemnity costs. In Hobbs v. Warner, 2021 BCCA 290, the Court of Appeal considered the circumstances in which such an award might be considered inappropriate, and offered the following guidance: [104] Section 4 applications will fall along a continuum ranging from lawsuits that have strong indicia of a true SLAPP to those where the competing interests of reputation and public expression are finely balanced. In those cases where there are strong indicia of a SLAPP, a full indemnity award may well be appropriate. The full indemnity starting point is clearly intended to disincentivize such litigation. On the other hand, when the proceeding bears little resemblance to a SLAPP but the public interest in protecting the expression leads to a dismissal of the action, it might well not be fair and reasonable to award full indemnity costs. [162] I reiterate my findings respecting the SLAPP indicia here. In particular, I repeat that the record suggests that Mr. Cheesman's motivation in bringing this lawsuit was one of vindication, not one aimed at suppressing debate relating to matters of public interest. These factors militate against ordering full indemnity costs against Mr. Cheesman. [163] Therefore, I conclude that this is not an appropriate case in which full indemnity costs should be ordered. In Simpson v. Rebel News Network Ltd., 2022 BCSC 1160 at para. 99, Justice Milman found that although the plaintiff had failed to meet her burden under s. 4(2)(a) of the PPPA, it did not follow that she had nothing to complain about in how she was treated. I would similarly find that Mr. Cheesman raised a valid concern about the Sticker, and although I have concluded that his suit could not succeed, it was not entirely meritless. The parties will bear their own costs. VIII. Conclusion [164] I have found Mr. Cheesman's claim does not have substantial merit. If the Sticker had named him or more closely identified him, I may have concluded differently. Although I would not describe this claim as an archetypal SLAPP, the legislation nevertheless applies. I am allowing the applications under s. 4(2)(a)(i) and Mr. Cheesman's claim is dismissed. I decline to order full indemnity costs or damages. The parties will bear their own costs. "E. Sigurdson J."
