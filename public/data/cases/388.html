Gallagher Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Me Michael Bossin Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant désigné Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS AND DECISION [1] Ms. XXXX XXXX, who is a 68-year-old citizen of Ukraine, is appealing a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) rejecting her claim for refugee protection. The Appellant has requested to present new evidence and has not requested an oral hearing. DETERMINATION OF THE APPEAL [1] With respect to the RPD's determination on country conditions applied to the Appellant's allegations, which is the determinative issue in this claim, for a number of reasons the RPD concluded that the Appellant could return to Ukraine, and specifically to either Kherson or Mykolayiv, and that living there she would not face a serious risk of persecution or at risk as understood in s. 97 of IRPA. I am in agreement with this assessment, therefore, pursuant to ss. 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) confirms the determination of the RPD, namely, that Ms. XXXX XXXX is neither a "Convention Refugee" pursuant to s. 96 of IRPA nor a "person in need of protection" pursuant to s. 97 of the IRPA. BACKGROUND [2] The Appellant, who is a retired Russian speaking individual of Bulgarian ethnicity, alleges in her BOC Narrative and in her testimony that she fears Ukrainian Nationalists and Nationalist militias who seek to drive Russians from the area where she lives in Belgorad-Dniestrovski, Odessa Oblast, Ukraine. [3] The Appellant, who possessed a multiple entry visitor's visa to Canada, left Ukraine to arrive in Canada in XXXX 2014. The Appellant requested Canada's protection in June 2014. The RPD denied this claim in a decision dated September 23, 2014, whereupon this decision was appealed by submitting a Notice of Appeal, which was received by the RAD on October 22, 2014. SUBMISSIONS [4] The Appellant argues that the RPD's analysis includes errors related to its determination that an Internal Flight Alternative (IFA) exists in Kherson or Mykolayiv, which are cities in nearby Oblasts. The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in its understanding of the IFA test. The Appellant also argues that the RPD ignored evidence and did not recognize that the Appellant does not have the resources or capacity to move to Kherson or Mykolayiv because of the administrative and financial constraints that she would face. [5] In terms of an outcome from this appeal, the Appellant seeks to have the RAD overturn the RPD decision and recognize the Appellant as a convention refugee. Alternatively, the Appellant seeks to have the file returned to the RPD for reconsideration by a different panel. NEW EVIDENCE [6] The Appellant has requested to provide new evidence related to the sale of the Appellant's existing property in Belgorad-Dniestrovski and the purchase of an apartment or condominium in Kherson or Mykolayiv (Appellant's Record, pp. 65-79). Based in section 104(4) of IRPA, I accept this material because I accept that the Appellant could not have reasonably provided it earlier. ROLE AND FUNCTION OF THE RAD [7] With respect to the role and function of the RAD in reviewing the RPD decision, Justice Phellan in Huruglica1 states that the RAD "must review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of whether the claimant is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection". Justice Phellan also indicates that there is no need for the RAD to enunciate a 'standard of review' and suggests that while the RAD might show some deference on a credibility assessment and/or where the RPD enjoys a particular advantage in reaching such a conclusion, in other areas, such as an assessment of country conditions evidence, the RAD need not be deferential. [8] In Njeukam,2 Justice Locke references Huruglica and suggests that "Except in cases where the credibility of a witness is critical or determinative or when the RPD has a particular benefit from the RAD to draw a specific conclusion, the RAD must not give any deference to the analysis of the evidence made by the RPD". [9] Following Huruglica and Njeukam, I conclude that this appeal, which turns on a question of mixed law and fact, specifically the assessment of IFA and country conditions, I must provide my own assessment of the evidence and reach my own conclusion. ANALYSIS Core Issue: Did the RPD reach an acceptable conclusion related to country conditions in Ukraine to effectively address an IFA for the Appellant? [10] The determinative issue identified by the RPD in this case is that the Appellant would have an IFA in either Kherson or Mykolayiv. In reviewing the evidence, I must agree that either of these cities would be reasonable in the particular circumstance of the Appellant. [11] A first question identified was whether the RPD identified the "wrong test" by using the word "impossible" at one point in its IFA analysis (Appellant's Memorandum, paragraph 26). [12] I note that the RPD wrote: In addition, the panel considers the claimant's conclusion that she could not sell her apartment to be pure speculation. Furthermore, this is not supported by any documentary evidence. The panel does not believe that it would be impossible for the claimant to sell or rent her apartment. As the owner of her condominium, the claimant has a cashable asset. She also receives a monthly pension. She failed to demonstrate that it would be impossible for her to live elsewhere or that her right to housing would be violated if she had to live in one of those cities (RPD Decision, paragraph 27). [13] In this case, I view the second use of the term 'impossible' in the last sentence as a rhetorical use of the term in the sense that it parallels the Appellant's use of the term with respect to the sale of her condominium. This would have been clarified if the RPD had used apostrophes around the second use of 'impossible'. I note that the RPD clearly understood the second prong test for an IFA when it sets out that the RPD must "assess whether it is objectively unreasonable for the claimant to seek refuge by moving to another part of the country before claiming refuge (sic) protection abroad" (RPD Decision, paragraph 17). I conclude that the RPD did not error in this area. [14] With respect to the first prong, the Appellant's evidence is that she has been targeted by individuals in Belgorad-Dniestrovski that seek to force her out. I note that the RPD found the Appellant credible based on the Appellant's testimony and corroborative evidence. At the same time, I find the RPD is correct to note that the documentation would support a conclusion that the Appellant's alleged circumstance is an anomaly. The RPD notes a April 2014 review of the situation by a UN High Commission for Human Rights Special Rapporteur that sets out: The claimant also failed to demonstrate that she would encounter problems because of her Bulgarian origin, and the documentary evidence does not mention any problems specific to Bulgarians. Furthermore, Document 3, Tab 13.7, states: I met with representatives of communities including those who identify as ethnic Armenians, Azerbaijanis, Bulgarians, Crimean Tatars, Gagauzis, Germans, Greeks, Hungarians, Moldovans, Poles, Roma, Romanians, Russians, Ruthenians, Slovaks, Vietnamese and members of Jewish communities. I also met with those who identify clearly as Ukrainian to seek their views. The overwhelming majority of those minority and other representatives whom I consulted in all locations visited described harmonious inter-ethnic and inter-faith relations and a legislative and policy environment that is conducive to the protection of their rights, including cultural rights. Most minority representatives described to me conditions of non-discrimination in all spheres of life and acknowledged that violence, intimidation or aggression against them on the basis of national, ethnic or religious belonging are rare, even in these times of heightened tension (RPD Decision, paragraph 30). [15] I note that the Appellant does not challenge this assessment of the RPD instead focusing on the second prong of the IFA analysis related to the reasonableness of a relocation. According to the Appellant, "In this case, the Appellant takes no issue with the RPD's finding that she would not face a serious possibility of persecution in either Kherson or Mykolayiv. Rather, she takes issue with the RPD member's finding that it would not be unreasonable for her to seek refugee (sic) in those cities" (Appellant's Memorandum, paragraph 25). [16] In its decision, the RPD appears to view these areas, including Belgorad-Dniestrovski, Kherson and Mykolayiv as having been stressed by recent events but that these are areas where the major ethnicities and language groups generally get along. I would add that Ukraine is a democracy faced with the challenge of the machinations of Russia in the Crimea and Donbas regions. In the Donbas region, there is now open combat between Russian insurgents (and clandestine Russian regular troops) supported by Moscow and regular Ukrainian forces supported by Nationalist militias including Right Sector. I see nothing in the documentation which suggests or implies that there is any significant separatist or terrorist activity in the areas in question in this file (Kherson Oblast, Odessa Oblast, Mykolaiv Oblast). [17] In looking through the documentation, the one area where there has been recurring violence is Odessa (RPD Record, pp. 33-34). Here there has been a breakdown of trust after the riots in May 2014, which left many dead including many Russian nationals who were possibly provocateurs. This in turn led to further demonstrations (Appellant's Record, p. 115). In response, there has been very close international scrutiny of these events and efforts to 'deescalate tensions' (RPD Record, p. 42). It should be noted that the confrontations that have occurred are usually between Russian supporters, who maybe of Russian ethnicity, and Ukrainian Nationalists often associated with paramilitary militias such as the Right Sector. [18] Although the Appellant may speak Russian, it is very hard to see how Ukrainian Nationalists would view her as a threat. The fact is that she is an older retired Russian speaking woman of Bulgarian ethnicity. This is not a profile of an individual who would normally be viewed as someone who would be at risk. It is undoubtedly the case that cities like Belgorad-Dniestrovski, Odessa, Kherson and Mykolayiv have large numbers of older, retired woman who speak Russian but who are not ethnically Russian, who are not viewed as a threat by anyone. In the case of Belgorad-Dniestrovski, the majority of its population speaks Russian even though only a quarter of its population is ethnically Russian.3 If anything, the allegations of the Appellant, supported by corroborative evidence provided by the Appellant, must be viewed as anomalous in that there is little if anything in the RPD documentation or in the reference documentation provided by the Appellant, which would suggest that anyone of the Appellant's profile is at risk of being targeted in Ukraine. [19] It should also be kept in mind that all of these oblasts in this general area (Kherson Oblast, Odessa Oblast, Mykolaiv Oblast) contain a large percentage of Russian speakers, of which many, like the Appellant, are also not of Russian ethnicity. I also note that there is a very open media in this area and the UN is closely monitoring human rights conditions. I must conclude that the targeting allegedly faced by the Appellant is not consistent with the documentation and I note that in the Appellant's case, she and her family would be able to research and review Ukrainian and Russian language resources from the region. Regardless, they have provided little to nothing that supports a specific allegation that a retired Russian speaking woman of Bulgarian ethnicity would be targeted by Ukrainian nationalists in Belgorad-Dniestrovski. In fact, the Appellant provided nothing media related specific to Belgorad-Dniestrovski. Overall, I do not find it an error on the part of the RPD to conclude that the Appellant would be safe, if she were to move to Kherson or Mykolayiv. [20] With respect to the second prong of the IFA analysis and relocation, I must first note that the Appellant is now in Canada living with her son. If there is any risk to a return to Belgorad-Dniestrovski, then she should not be expected to return there. In this case, her property must be considered lost. The loss of this property, or the potentially of recovering some of its worth, is not a necessary consideration in the second prong of the IFA analysis. [21] The Appellant focuses attention on the fact that the RPD did not make specific reference to the statement of Professor XXXX XXXX who questions whether a relocation to Kherson or Mykolayiv is reasonable as understood as "realistic, attainable" (Appellant's Memorandum, paragraph 37). I note the RPD did make reference to documentation related to the freedom of movement in Ukraine and the requirement to register to take up residence (RPD Decision, paragraph 26), and I do not view it to be an error to not address each piece of evidence. [22] In reviewing Professor XXXX's arguments, I note he identifies two challenges facing the Appellant. The first is that the Appellant must meet the administrative requirement of registering her relocation to Kherson or Mykolayiv in order to retain retirement benefits and citizen entitlements. According to Professor XXXX, this requires that the Appellant cancel her previous residency registration to acquire a new one in Kherson or Mykolayiv. Given that Mr. XXXX XXXX, her former husband and father to her son, Mr. XXXX XXXX, was involved in securing documents (Appellant's Record, p. 69), it would seem reasonable that he would represent the Appellant in her effort to cancel her previous residence registration. I do not see in this claim that the Appellant is alleging that state actors are seeking to persecute her, therefore, this should be administratively possible. [23] With respect to the second element identified by Professor XXXX, the Appellant must secure a "long-term housing arrangement" in order to register with authorities in the IFA destination of Kherson or Mykolayiv. Professor XXXX suggests that possibly, this might even require a "substantial bribe". According to Professor XXXX, "Ms. XXXX lacks the resources for this risky approach" (Appellant's Memorandum, paragraph 34). I also note that the Appellant has provided evidence that an average three bedroom apartment in Kherson would cost $44,000 US to purchase (Appellant's Record, p. 70). [24] In reviewing this situation, it seems clear that the problem facing the Appellant is that she is a retired woman who requires a loan to purchase an apartment and get on with her life while retaining access to Ukraine's various entitlements. I note that she has a son in Canada who likely has paid for the Appellant travel to Canada on several previous trips so perhaps he might be able to help. Overall, I must conclude that this is not a case where Canada's international commitment to grant protection aaccording to 96 and 97(1), to those fleeing risk or persecution can be evoked. CONCLUSION [25] For all these reasons, I confirm the determination of the RPD, namely, that Ms. XXXX XXXX is neither a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection". [26] Therefore, the appeal is dismissed. Stephen J. Gallagher Stephen J. Gallagher April 23, 2015 Date /cl 1 Huruglica v. M.C.I. (2014) FC 799. 2 Njeukam v. M.C.I.
