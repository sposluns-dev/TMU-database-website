January 4, 2023 [1] THE COURT: These are my oral reasons for judgment in respect of a notice of application filed on March 21, 2022 by the following defendants in this civil proceeding: What The Fungus, Thorsten Clausen, Brian Callow, King Campbell, Faith Arroylo Callow, Arlene Fenrich, Clayton Fenrich and Garnet Valley Holdings. The remaining defendants, namely the Agricultural Land Commission and the Corporation of the District of Summerland, did not appear at the hearing of this application. [2] As these are oral reasons for judgment, in the event a transcript is ordered I reserve the right to edit these reasons for judgment for style and grammar and to include citations for any case law, rules, or statutes not expressly referred to. However, the substance of my decision will not change. [3] The notice of application filed by the above-named defendants seeks the following relief: 1. That the Amended Notice of Civil Claim be struck, and the action be dismissed as against the applicants, What The Fungus Mushrooms, Thorsten Clausen, Brian Callow, King Campbell, Faith Arroylo Callow, Arlene Fenrich, Clayton Fenrich, and Garnet Valley Holdings Ltd. 2. In the alternative, paragraphs 4, 6, 19, 21, 22, 36-39 of Par 1 [of] the Amended Notice of Civil Claim, paragraphs 1, 5, 6 and 9 of Part 2 of the Amended Notice of Civil Claim, and paragraphs 1, 2, 6, 7, 8, 9 and 20 of Part 3 of the Amended Notice of Civil Claim be struck. 3. In the alternative, paragraphs 15 and 16 of Part 1 of the Amended Notice of Claim be struck. 4. In the [further] alternative, paragraphs 8 and 188 of Part 1 and paragraph 13 of Part 3 of the Amended Notice of Claim be struck. 5. In the [further further] alternative, paragraphs 7, 33, 70-88, 92-100, 126 and 194 of Part 1 of the Amended Notice of Civil Claim, paragraphs 12, 89, 90 and 91 of Part 2 of the Amended Notice if Civil Claim and paragraphs 10, 11, 12, 16 and 20 of Part 3 of the Amended Notice of Claim be struck. 6. Special costs. 7. In the alternative, costs at Scale C. Background [4] The litigation and the underlying dispute between these parties has a rather complex history. [5] This particular action was commenced back on October 24, 2019. Before turning to the chronology of the litigation itself, I will somewhat briefly summarize the underlying factual matrix, keeping in mind that for the purposes of this application I am not making any findings of fact but simply noting that the factual matrix is relevant to understanding the application to strike the pleadings. [6] The plaintiff, Vicki Besler, is the owner of certain real property located at 18816 Garnet Valley Road in the District of Summerland. The plaintiff, Ms. Besler, is the mother of the other defendants, Bradley Besler and Darren Besler. The Besler property, as I will refer to it, neighbours the property located at 18420 Garnet Valley Road. They share a property line. [7] The 18240 Garnet Valley property is owned by the defendant, Thor Clausen. Thor Clausen operates a business on this property which is formally incorporated as the defendant, Garnet Valley Holdings Ltd., but operates under what I understand is the tradename of "What the Fungus Mushrooms" or possibly just "What the Fungus". [8] For the purposes of this application, I shall repeat again, I need and do not make findings of fact. However, it is very apparent from the record before me that significant neighbourly conflict arose between these parties and this conflict remains unresolved. Further, to put the scope of the conflict into some perspective, it reached the point of criminal proceedings involving the plaintiffs, Bradley and Darren Besler. Litigation History [9] As noted, this proceeding was commenced back in October of 2019. That was more than three years prior to the hearing of this application before me. [10] In February of 2021, Mr. Justice Gomery of this court held a case planning conference. The key order arising from that conference is that the plaintiffs were ordered to amend their notice of civil claim by June 1, 2021, that being some six months after the case management conference. While on its face this was a very generous timeline to amend, it was during a time when the court's operation was restricted due to the COVID‑19 pandemic and I expect, although not expressly stated obviously in the order, that this was somewhat of a factor in making the case management order and setting that timeline. The February 2021 order of Mr. Justice Gomery also included other terms including, amongst other things, a deadline for the delivery for a response to demand for particulars. [11] The amended notice of civil claim was ultimately not filed until November 10, 2021. It is lengthy to say the least. Having regard to the amendments, which include both additions and redactions, it spans some 68 pages. Accompanying this, the defendants did make a demand for particulars as contemplated by the case management order of Mr. Justice Gomery. The response to that demand for particulars is some 33 pages. Although there is some duplication, the pleadings as augmented by the response to demand for particulars, which also form part of the pleadings and would be included in the trial record, are thus almost 100 pages in length. [12] At present, and this may have been clear from my above comments, the pleadings are prolix and will ultimately be very challenging for the trier of fact to understand when this matter proceeds on its merits in the event that the parties cannot resolve it outside of the court process. This is a key concern as pleadings are fundamental to the trial process. Amongst other things, pleadings set the parameters for relevance of evidence and must set out the material facts which are sought to be proven. Bradley Besler, who represents himself and the other two plaintiffs but is a notably sophisticated and articulate self-represented litigant, effectively acknowledged as much during his oral submissions. [13] The question for this court, as I will come back to, is whether the plaintiffs should be given yet further opportunity to amend the pleadings or whether, as sought by the defendants, the action should be dismissed or certain paragraphs as enumerated above be struck. Overview of the Claims [14] Upon a scrutiny of the pleadings as they presently stand, they allege claims based on the following torts: negligence and defamation. I will briefly summarize the claims currently made in the pleadings relating to these torts as best I can having regard to my earlier comments about the prolix nature of the pleadings as they presently stand and the challenges that presents for the court in understanding what is actually being pled. Negligence [15] Violation of certain bylaws of the defendant, the District of Summerland, including construction of structures without proper building permits and failure to comply with zoning bylaws. Defamation [16] The claims in defamation consume much of the amended notice of civil claim. These include allegations of defamation of the plaintiffs to the Regional District, the RCMP, the District of Summerland, the media and other agencies including WorkSafeBC and the B.C. Mental Health Authority. [17] Even with the response to demand for particulars, it is hard to discern from the pleadings the core elements necessary to plead the tort of defamation. Those core elements are, simply stated, (a) a statement was made that would have the effect of lowering the reputation of the claimant or claimants in the eyes of a reasonable person, (b) a statement or statements refer to the claimant or claimants, and (c) the statement or statements were published to at least one other person. [18] There are, of course, defences to defamation such as truth, fair comment and qualified privilege. However, the obligation rests on the defence to plead those defences. The obligation of the plaintiffs is generally to plead the necessary facts and particulars to found the claim for defamation. These propositions are effectively trite but I will make specific reference to the Supreme Court of Canada decision in Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61, at paragraphs 28 and 29, which was cited by counsel for the applicant defendants and confirms these propositions. [19] In addition, defamation together with liable and slander is somewhat unique in that the Supreme Court Civil Rules [Rules] have specific provisions regarding how pleadings alleging this tort must be drafted. Specifically, I refer to R. 3‑7(21) of the Rules. It says: Particulars in libel or slander (21) In an action for libel or slander, (a) if the plaintiff alleges that the words or matter complained of were used in a derogatory sense other than their ordinary meaning, the plaintiff must give particulars of the facts and matters on which the plaintiff relies in support of that sense, and (b) if the defendant alleges that, insofar as the words complained of consist of statements of fact, they are true in substance and in fact, and that insofar as they consist of expressions of opinion, they are fair comment on a matter of public interest, the defendant must give particulars stating which of the words complained of the defendant alleges are statements of fact and of the facts and matters relied on in support of the allegation that the words are true. In other words, it is a reciprocal obligation to have very precise pleadings in matters that invoke this rule. However, the plaintiff (or plaintiffs) are the party who commences the action and so the obligation on the defence is secondary to the obligation on the plaintiff (or plaintiffs). We thus do not put the cart before the horse. [20] I am going to reference to two specific cases in this regard, the first being Gosal v. Gill, 2018 BCSC 2132. This matter subsequently went to the Court of Appeal and was reversed in part. However, I am referring to the proposition from the judgment of this Court which stands for the proposition that the prevailing theme in the law of defamation is that at the time a party makes the defamatory allegations he or she must have the ability to support those allegations based on their knowledge at that time, even if that support is provided by particulars obtained after the pleadings have been filed, or if provided by material that was not in their possession at the time of the alleged defamatory statement. [21] In addition, I refer to E.B. v. British Columbia (Child, Family and Community Services), 2021 BCCA 47, which stands for the proposition that to meet the requirements of the rule I have referenced, namely R. 3‑7(21), the material facts should generally include the publication alleged to be defamatory, the specific words at issue, the time and place of the publication, the manner of the publication, and the recipient. The plaintiff is required to set out the exact words, unless those words are only knowable through further steps in the litigation process. Harassment and Conspiracy [22] There is also a reference in Part 1 of the amended notice of civil claim to harassment, namely paragraphs 15 and 16 as presently drafted. However, there is no relief sought on this basis. In relation to this tort, I have ultimately concluded that the reference to harassment is simply made in the part of the overall factual narrative and that the tort of harassment is not actually being specifically pled at present. [23] Similarly, there is reference at paragraphs 8 and 188 of the amended notice of civil claim to claim conspiracy. Again, there is no relief sought based on the tort of conspiracy, and as such I find that this reference is again part of the factual matrix and that the tort of conspiracy is not being specifically pled. [24] An additional challenge that arises is as to whether or not the tort of conspiracy to commit the tort of defamation is actually viable at law. However, I am choosing not to weigh into that argument on the basis that I do not conclude that on the basis of the pleadings, as presently drafted, that there is actually a claim based on the tort of conspiracy but rather simple reference to it in passing in the pleadings. [25] If these two above-noted torts were specifically pled, the amended notice of civil claim is wildly deficient to set forth the material facts and the relevant legal basis, as well as failing to seek any substantive relief sought. The Relevant Rules [26] Rule 3‑1(2)(a) of the Rules requires that a notice of civil claim must set out a concise statement of material facts giving rise to the claim. [27] Rule 9‑5(1) of the Rules provides that at any stage of the proceedings the court may order that pleadings be struck out in whole or in part on grounds that (a) they disclose no reasonable claim or defence as the case may be; (b) they are unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious; (c) they may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding; or (d) they are otherwise an abuse of the process of the court. [28] The applicants here are seeking that the amended notice of civil claim be struck on the basis of R. 9‑5(1)(a), (b), (c) and (d), as well as, as indicated above, seeking the dismissal of the claim ultimately as against the applicant defendants. The Law with Respect to Striking Pleadings [29] The Supreme Court of Canada in Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., 1990 S.C.R. 959, established key principles relevant to any application brought pursuant to R. 9‑5(1)(a) (albeit considering its predecessor at that time, R. 19(24)(a)) as follows: a) Assume the facts in the pleading can be proven; b) Ask the question, is it plain and obvious that the plaintiff's claim discloses no reasonable cause of action in the sense that it is certain to fail because it contains a radical defect; c) Is there a chance the plaintiff might succeed? The plaintiff should not be driven from the judgment seat; d) The length and complexity of the issue, the novelty of the cause of action, and potential strength of the defendant's case should not prevent the claim from proceeding. [30] A fundamental consideration on any application to strike a pleading under R. 9‑5(1)(a) is whether the pleading can be preserved by an amendment: International Taoist Church of Canada v. Ching Chung Taoist Association of Hong Kong Limited, 2011 BCCA 149. [31] The determination of whether to permit an amendment rather than to strike the pleading involves the exercise of judicial discretion that may require consideration as to the degree to which the pleadings are deficient, the extent to which the deficiencies may be addressed by an obvious or straightforward amendment, the apparent merit of the claim that may be made out with amendment, and the prejudice that may be incurred by dismissing the claim: Jones v. Bank of Nova Scotia, 2018 BCCA 381. [32] Ideally where a party seeks to amend to avoid having their pleadings struck, a draft pleading should be provided to the court. Again, I refer back to the decision in E.B. v. British Columbia, namely the 2019 BCSC 2200 decision. [33] In order to succeed under either R. 9‑5(1)(b) or (c), the applicant must demonstrate it is "plain and obvious" that the respondent's pleading offends either or both provisions: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, [1999] CarswellBC 2111. [34] In that same case, namely Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, Justice Romilly, as he then was, summarized many of the principles applicable to an analysis under subrules (b) and (c). These principles include: [47] Irrelevancy and embarrassment are both established when pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is being pleaded: . . . An "embarrassing" and "scandalous" pleading is one that is so irrelevant that it will involve the parties in useless expense and will prejudice the trial of the action by involving them in a dispute apart from the issues: . . . An allegation which is scandalous will not be struck if it is relevant to the proceedings. It will only be struck if irrelevant as well as scandalous: . . . A pleading is "unnecessary" or "vexatious" if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or does not advance any claim known in law: . . . A pleading that is superfluous will not be struck out if it is not necessarily unnecessary or otherwise objectionable: . . . A pleading is "frivolous" if it is obviously unsustainable, not in the sense that it lacks an evidentiary basis, but because of the doctrine of estoppel: . . . [35] I am going to refer to further cases which were not cited by counsel, but which I think are particularly relevant. [36] The first is Rodrigues v. Tribe, 2016 BCSC 1233. This case stands for the proposition it is not for the defendants or this court to try and construct a possible legal path to relief when plaintiffs have failed to do so in their pleadings and when the notice of civil claim does not allege facts that would support the relief they have asked for. The fact that the plaintiffs are not unrepresented by counsel in this case, and have always been unrepresented by counsel, does not relieve them from the responsibility of providing the court and the defendants with intelligible pleadings that meet the minimum requirements of the Rules. [37] The last case I will refer to is Lam v. Macdonald, 2018 BCSC 281. Lam stands for the proposition that the burden on the defendant in applications to strike is very high and the court should exercise its discretion to strike in only the clearest of cases. The pleadings should be read generously, particularly where a plaintiff is self-represented, to allow for inadequacies due to drafting deficiencies. However, it is not the court's role to give advice to self-represented plaintiffs about how to cure the deficiencies in their pleadings or how to properly present their legal claims. Rather, it would be unfair to grant indulgences to a self-represented party that would interfere with another party's right to know the case to be met. Again, this last statement underscores the core of this application, what is the case to be met? Application of the Law to These Pleadings [38] As I have made clear, these pleadings cannot stand as presently stated. However, I am ultimately, after some deliberation, not satisfied that they are unable to be cured. Specifically, I am not satisfied that this is a Lam v. MacDonald "clearest of the case" situation warranting striking of the pleadings at this juncture. [39] Negligence and defamation/liable/slander are not novel causes of action. There are also some material facts that can be gleaned from the existing prolix pleadings which would support claims for either or both torts. I am by no means saying that these torts can be proven on the requisite standard of proof at trial, but that is not the issue before me on an application to strike the pleadings, as I have just recited in the law above. The law, as indicated, supports a conclusion that upon a consideration of all of those factors, that the plaintiffs here should not "be driven from judgment seat" at this juncture of the proceeding. Conclusion [40] I am thus not going to grant any of the orders sought by the applicant defendants in the above-noted March 20, 2021 notice of application. I am instead going to exercise my discretion afforded to me under the Rules to make the following order: a) By March 3, 2023, the plaintiffs must file and serve a further amended notice of civil claim which complies with the Supreme Court Civil Rules, including the relevant rules with respect to liable and slander which I have referred to in these reasons for judgment. [41] That amended notice of civil claim shall be what I will describe as a "fresh claim", which simply amends the existing amended notice of civil claim as Appendix A. [42] I am making this somewhat unusual order for two reasons, one of which is that I think it is important for the ultimate trier of fact to know the background of the pleadings. That is the reason why in the normal course pleadings are struck out or blacklined and it is clear what the amendments are. On the other hand, I am not going to saddle the trial judge with what may then become a 150-page or so pleading wherein they have to try and decipher between what has been added, what has been redacted, and what has been included. [43] Specifically, what I am contemplating, and this will be what the order is, is that it will be a clean amended notice of civil claim and the existing amended notice of civil claim which was before me for the purposes of this application will remain intact and simply included as Appendix A to that new fresh pleading. [44] Upon receipt of this further amended notice of civil claim, which as indicated must be provided by no later than March 3, 2023, unless the parties all agree otherwise, all the defendants remain at liberty to make a further application to strike some or all of the pleadings if they still do not comply with the Rules. Stated another way, this issue is not res judicata as to the pleadings in this matter, but the plaintiffs are being given the opportunity as contemplated in Lam v. Macdonald to bring them into compliance with the Rules. Costs [45] Costs are awardable at the discretion of the presiding justice. Even though I have not granted the relief sought by the application defendants, the reason I have not done so is that the plaintiffs effectively concede that the pleadings need amending. A request to amend and if necessary an application to amend could have been brought in advance of the hearing of this application which was filed back, as noted, in March of 2022, some approximately nine months before the hearing of the application before me. I do appreciate there were likely some delays in getting the matter heard due to court restrictions and COVID, but there was ample opportunity to have at least presented the court with some form of amended pleading. The failure to do so is a concern but ultimately, as indicated, I have concluded that the plaintiff should have one more opportunity to amend their pleadings. [46] As such, while I am dismissing the application, I am granting the application defendants their costs of this application in any event of the cause but not payable forthwith. [47] Those costs shall be assessed on the basis of this matter being of ordinary difficulty and on the basis that this application including both the hearing and the attendance for reasons for judgment consumed one full day. There will of course be the necessary disbursements. [48] As a final housekeeping matter, I will not be returning the application record binders or the briefs of authorities as I have marked them up, so they will be disposed of. [49] Those are my reasons for judgment. "Hardwick J."
