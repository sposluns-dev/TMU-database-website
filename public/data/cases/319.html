April 21, 2016 [1] THE COURT: The accused is before me today for sentencing. He pled guilty to seven weapons-related offences arising out of events that occurred on January 31, 2014. [2] There are four counts relating to four separate firearms, of possessing those firearms knowing that he was not the holder of a licence permitting him to possess them, contrary to s. 92(1) of the Criminal Code. There are three counts under s. 95(1) of the Criminal Code. Two of them are for possessing an unloaded prohibited or restricted firearm with readily accessible ammunition, without holding appropriate authorizations. The final count under s. 95 is for possessing a loaded prohibited or restricted weapon, and in that particular case, it was a Walther .32 calibre handgun. [3] To assist me in the sentencing, I have been provided with a signed statement of admissions of fact which the Crown read into the record, signed by counsel for Mr. Sears, a presentence report that was ordered and prepared, and a Forensic Psychiatric Services Commission assessment report. There are actually two psychiatric reports, only one of which was generated by virtue of actually seeing Mr. Sears. [4] I also have been provided with a collection of reference letters and some certificates with respect to the two handguns that are the subject of various other charges. Those certificates indicate that dating back into the early 1990s, Mr. Sears did hold a valid registration certificate for those firearms. [5] The circumstances of these offences are set out in the admissions of fact which I will read from for context. Mr. Sears was at the time living in the Lavington area of Coldstream with his wife. They have had a relationship for more than 30 years. Also in that residence was Mrs. Sears' mother, who lived in a suite in the basement. [6] On January 30th, the accused had indicated that he was intending to kill himself while Mrs. Sears watched. At that time, one of the firearms at issue in these charges, a sawed-off .22 long rifle calibre Winchester Model 67, was lying on the bedroom floor. That firearm was ultimately removed. [7] On January 31st, at about noon, the RCMP attended pursuant to a request to check on the well-being of Mr. Sears' wife, Lorraine Sears. The events at issue unfolded from there. [8] The police met Mr. Sears at the rear exit of the residence. He was intoxicated by a combination of alcohol and prescription medication, specifically Ativan. It is, I understand it, a benzodiazepine. He did not have any weapons at that point. [9] He indicated to the police that he needed help with his alcohol addiction. He did act somewhat unusually, which progressed. He became suspicious of the police, and asked them whether they had firearms under their coats. He told them that he was an expert martial artist who could kill the members with one strike if he wanted by pushing their noses up into their brains. He began adopting a fighting posture, putting his hands up in a "karate" pose. [10] His mother-in-law emerged at one point, and indicated she had not seen the accused in that condition before, and that she did not know him to be violent. The accused consumed more alcohol. At one point, he went into the bedroom, laid down facing away from the police. They noted a black leather object on a bedside table. They could not see his hands. They realized the object could be a gun holster. The accused rolled over and had one of the handguns in his hand. The police responded by taking cover. When they looked next, the accused had dropped the handgun to a position on a pillow about 12 inches from his head. [11] The police were able to acquire the handgun. That was the Walther semiautomatic handgun .32 auto calibre with a live round in the chamber and several additional rounds in the magazine. [12] Subsequently the police noted an open closet in which there was a wooden gun case that was unlocked. In that was a Galesi -- if I am pronouncing it correctly -- Model 9 semiautomatic handgun .25 auto calibre. There was a variety of ammunition also located there, including ammunition for that handgun. [13] In a wooden gun case there was a Model AK-47/22 semiautomatic rifle with .22 long rifle calibre. It was stored with a magazine with nine rounds. [14] The accused had purchased the Winchester and the AK‑47 from a storage locker auction sometime around 1990. He had a valid firearms acquisition certificate at the time, but that had lapsed. He had purchased the Galesi, a collector's item, and the Walther, both the handguns, at a gun shop at approximately the same time. He had registration certificates, or renewal certificates, or replacement certificates on May 2, 1991, that were not kept current. [15] The presentence report sheds some further light on the overall circumstances. Mr. Sears was born January 29, 1957 in Saskatchewan into a large family. He is the oldest of ten children. The family moved to B.C. many years ago. The accused began using marihuana at about age 15. By the age of 17, he was using alcohol and drugs, and described himself as being a mess for the next eleven years. He was in a treatment centre at the age of 28, and was clean and sober for ten years. He then met his current wife at AA meetings. They remained sober for some time thereafter, but then began consuming, and he realized that his alcohol consumption was out of control. The two did not drink together because of their inability to avoid conflict when drinking. [16] He indicates to the probation officer who prepared the report that, on the date of these incidents, he had consumed eight bottles of wine, a 40‑ounce bottle of vodka, two Ativan pills and then a mickey of vodka. Mrs. Sears indicated she had a prescription for 40 Ativan pills, and it was her belief that Mr. Sears had consumed all of them over a short period of time. [17] Despite all of that history, Mr. Sears has been employed for some 13 years at what often is referred locally as the Okanagan Brewery. Sleeman is his actual employer. His supervisor spoke to the probation officer who prepared the presentence report, and gave very positive comments. One of the reference letters, such as it is, simply confirms his employment at that location. [18] Mr. Sears acknowledged to the probation officer that he is emotionally unstable when he consumes alcohol. [19] I am told that he has been sober since this incident. [20] The psychological assessment found no psychiatric condition and no diagnoses arose from that. [21] Mr. Sears has no related criminal history. He does have prior convictions of an unrelated nature, the most recent of which is 1981, and those have minimal relevance here. [22] He has filed a collection of reference letters, primarily from family members, who speak highly of Mr. Sears. A sister, who is not one of the authors of the reference letters, reports that in her view, Mr. Sears' relationship with Lorraine Sears, his wife, is a toxic one. To some extent, it would appear Mr. Sears and Mrs. Sears endorse that, at least when they are drinking. [23] Based on the pre-sentence report and reference letters, Mr. Sears appears to have a good work ethic and has a number of positive attributes. [24] The presentence report does allude to the fact that Mr. Sears does not agree with Canadian gun laws. The inference one might take is that the reason his permitting and licencing had lapsed and manner of stowing the weapons in the way he did is, to some extent at least, a product of that, but that is not a conclusion that I specifically draw, nor is it germane to my sentencing here. [25] In all of the circumstances, the Crown argues that a three to three-and-a-half year sentence is appropriate. The defence argues that a sentence of something less than two years is appropriate, and more specifically the defence position is that a conditional sentence is appropriate. [26] There have been many recent and important judicial comments on firearms offences in Canada. Most recently that has arisen in the context of Parliament's creation of mandatory minimum penalties for firearms offences. The constitutional validity of those minimums came before the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Nur, 2015 SCC 15. The opening sentence in the judgment of the Chief Justice simply says this: "Gun-related crime poses grave danger to Canadians" -- a sentiment that is germane to what Parliament has undertaken and the sentencing that has been carried out by courts in this province and across the country. [27] The specific issue before the Supreme Court of Canada in Nur were those mandatory minimums which were ultimately held to be unconstitutional. The Supreme Court of Canada was, however, careful to stress the need for the courts to treat gun-related offences very seriously. With respect to the s. 95(1) offences, the court said this at paragraph 82: Section 95(1) casts its net over a wide range of potential conduct. Most cases within the range may well merit a sentence of three years or more, but conduct at the far end of the range may not. At one end of the range, as Doherty J.A. observed, "stands the outlaw who carries a loaded prohibited or restricted firearm in public places as a tool of his or her criminal trade. ... [T]his person is engaged in truly criminal conduct and poses a real and immediate danger to the public". At this end of the range -- indeed for the vast majority of offences -- a three-year sentence may be appropriate. A little further along the spectrum stands the person whose conduct is less serious and poses less danger; for these offenders three years' imprisonment may be disproportionate, but not grossly so. At the far end of the range, stands the licensed and responsible gun owner who stores his unloaded firearm safely with ammunition nearby, but makes a mistake as to where it can be stored. For this offender, a three-year sentence is grossly disproportionate to the sentence the conduct would otherwise merit under the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Code. [28] It is that observation, or those conclusions, that ultimately led to the mandatory minimums being struck down. [29] Numerous cases have followed. I should say that, in that case, Mr. Nur received a four-year sentence which was not disturbed through the appeal process. The circumstances were that a young man had gone into a community centre in a neighbourhood of Toronto and advised a staff member he was afraid of someone who was waiting outside to get him. The staff member saw someone outside who looked threatening. The neighbourhood had very high levels of crime. Gun violence was a serious problem. The supervisor put the community centre on lockdown and called the police. When the police attended, they saw four men standing outside. Mr. Nur was among them. When the police approached, the men scattered. Mr. Nur was ultimately arrested. He had thrown something to the ground. What the police found was a .22 calibre semiautomatic with an oversized ammunition clip. There were 23 bullets in the clip and one in the chamber. [30] Mr. Nur, who was 19, was attending high school. He was performing well and hoped eventually to attend university. He had part-time jobs and volunteered in the community. He was viewed by teachers and past employers as having considerable potential, one describing him as an exceptional student and athlete who exceled in the classroom and on the basketball court, an incredible youth with unlimited academic and great leadership skills. As I say, in that case, the four-sentence was not disturbed. [31] Numerous cases have followed. Important among them is our Court of Appeal's decision in R. v. Holt, 2015 BCCA 302. There, Madam Justice Newbury, for the Court, said, at paragraph 18: In my view, the possession of a loaded firearm does pose a serious threat, and a growing one, to communities across Canada. There is no reason why British Columbia courts should take this threat less seriously than other Canadian courts now do. [32] She then refers to a quote from the Supreme Court of Canada in Nur, and says at paragraph 19: In my view, the fact a 40-month sentence was upheld almost without challenge in the Nur appeals, the comments of courts in other recent appellate decisions, but most especially the proliferation of guns in the Lower Mainland generally, indicate that a re‑alignment of the "range" is in order in British Columbia. I would say that Mr. Holt falls at the bottom end of the "criminal" range, and that a sentence of close to three years is called for. I am also persuaded that the sentence of 18 months' imprisonment was unfit given the seriousness of the offence. A 30‑month sentence was imposed. [33] The facts being referred to by Madam Justice Newbury are set out earlier in the decision, at paragraph 3 and 4. At para. 3 she says: [3] Mr. Holt was apprehended on December 4, 2011 for a driving infraction by the Burnaby RCMP. He was driving his truck and had a female passenger with him. They had both spent the evening consuming alcohol and smoking crack cocaine. The officer who had stopped them arrested Mr. Holt for possession of drugs and carried out a search of the truck incidental to the arrest. He found a backpack containing a loaded .22 calibre revolver and a box containing 43 cartridges capable of being discharged in the gun. [34] Mr. Holt was 66, unemployed, had no record. He was a heavy drug user. [35] I refer next to the decision of R. v. Brereton, 2015 BCSC 1553, a decision of a judge of this court, Mr. Justice MacKenzie, who had the benefit there of the decision of the Court of Appeal in Holt. There, the police had acquired a search warrant for the residence. I should say that the sentencing decision there was actually for a co‑accused, Mr. Guy. The residence was rented to Mr. Guy and Ms. Brereton. They did indeed find a quantity of drugs, including 77 and a half grams of powdered cocaine. [36] In an open closet, there was a non-functioning firearm, two air guns, a set of brass knuckles, as well as a sawed-off 12-gauge and a .357 Magnum. Ammunition which matched the Magnum was in a bedroom closet. [37] Mr. Guy was 33 years of age, grade 10 education, with what was described as a tragic upbringing. He had been sentenced in 2008 to a four-month conditional sentence for possession of cocaine, and was released and on bail in respect of 2010 drug charges at the time of the offences in question. [38] In an effort, in my reading of the case, to reconcile the authorities, Mr. Justice MacKenzie said, at paragraph 37: [37] While the court in Nur has said that in the vast majority of cases, including the so-called "outlaw" cases described by Doherty J.A., a three-year sentence may be appropriate, it has also held that for offenders whose conduct is less serious and dangerous than the typical case, it may be appropriate to impose a sentence of less than three years. [39] Reference is then made to the Court of Appeal decision in Holt, again picking up on the language from Nur of the so-called "outlaw" carrying a loaded or prohibited and restricted weapon. [40] Ultimately, the court observed, at paragraph 69, the guns were in a closet. They were not loaded. The ammunition was accessible. The court said that Mr. Guy's conduct was less serious and posed less danger than the category of offence where the offender possesses a prohibited or restricted firearm in a public place in furtherance of his criminal activity, and further: [70] Notwithstanding these particular circumstances, I am bound by the general observations of the court in Holt, and Madam Justice Newbury's determination as to the low end of the range for offences under 95(1). [41] Ultimately, Mr. Justice MacKenzie imposed a 26‑month sentence of imprisonment. [42] I find the authorities do distinguish between those so-called outlaw or true criminal scenarios where a person is possessing a prohibited or restricted weapon in some manner in furtherance of a criminal activity, whether it be associated with drugs, whether it be as in the circumstances of Nur, and thus the range set out in Holt is subject to a consideration of those sorts of features. [43] Here there are a number of troubling aspects with respect to the circumstances; the loaded firearm with a round in the chamber; the number of weapons; the conduct of Mr. Sears. They are, however, distinguishing features from the types of so-called outlaw or criminal conduct in cases in the context in which I have just referred to it. Mr. Sears was not furthering any other criminal activity, was in public, and accordingly, there is greater latitude here in terms of the range of sentence at the lower end that might be available. [44] It is apparent that alcohol, in combination with prescription drugs -- not his, I should say -- have played a significant role in the overall circumstances. Given his lack of any relevant criminal history, the reference letters, his employment in these general circumstances, in my view, the appropriate sentence is 25 months. [45] Mr. Sears did spend four months in custody before he was released on bail and counsel agree that he should receive credit for that at a ratio of one-and-a-half to one, and I will give him credit for six months. Therefore the actual sentence globally will be 19 months. Mr. Clerk, for clarity, it will be 19 months on all counts, concurrent. I do not see any reason to distinguish one from the other. [46] I turn next to the question of whether or not this is an appropriate circumstance for the sentence to be served in the community. Crown agree that there is no specific statutory impediment to the sentence being served conditionally, provided I am satisfied that doing so would ought not endanger the safety of the community and would be consistent with the fundamental purposes and principles of sentencing set out in ss. 718 to 718.2. [47] I am very much alive to the fact that, with firearms offences, deterrence, both specific and general, is an important consideration. Having said that, Mr. Sears has, it must be noted as well, been on restrictive bail conditions for an extended period of time, and there is no indication that there have been any problems. The courts have said frequently that conditional sentences can have a deterrent effect, in part through the stringent conditions. [48] In all of the circumstances, I am prepared to make a conditional sentence order. It will be for a period of 19 months. The terms and conditions are as follows. [49] MR.
