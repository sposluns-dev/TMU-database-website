Yallen Barrister and Solicitor Conseil(s) du (de la/des) demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du (de la) ministre [1] XXXX XXXX (the claimant) has made an application for protection pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA).1 ALLEGATIONS [2] The claimant alleges that he is a citizen of Ukraine and that he is at risk because of his Jewish ethnicity and heritage. He alleges that the Ukrainian authorities did not protect him from persecution and if he were to return they would still not protect him from the nationalists and anti-Semites. [3] In 1999, the claimant left Ukraine and went to the United States (US) on a visitor's visa. He alleged that he contacted a lawyer to assist him in converting his visitor's visa to one that permits him to work and deal with his sponsorship. He alleged that his lawyer who turned out to be a criminal lied to him. The lawyer eventually was arrested and jailed but in 2007, the claimant was deported from the US. [4] The claimant alleges that on his return to Ukraine he noticed that nationalism had become more rampant and in 2008 he was physically assaulted by members of Ukrainian National Assembly - Ukrainian People's Self-Defence (UNA-UNSO). In XXXX 2009, members of UNA-UNSO attacked his home, broke his windows, vandalized his home and shouted anti-Semitic slurs. A week later his car was vandalized, windows broken, tires slashed and abusive graffiti was scraped and painted on it. The claimant moved to XXXX city in hope that members of UNA-UNSO would leave him alone but in XXXX 2010 he was attacked by three men. [5] The claimant alleges that he returned home to assist his mother with his sick father but in XXXX 2010 his car was again vandalized. In XXXX 2011, he was attacked by a group of nationalists and beaten up. The claimant alleged that he moved back to XXXX and continued his efforts to leave Ukraine. Fearing for his life and safety he left Ukraine on XXXX XXXX, 2012 and claimed refugee status on February 24, 2012. [6] The claimant alleges that he cannot return to Ukraine, where the authorities are unable to protect him from nationalists, etc. DETERMINATION [7] The panel finds that the claimant is not a Convention refugee, as he does not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Ukraine. Nor is he a person in need of protection, as his removal to said country would not subject him personally to a risk to life, or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or to a danger of torture. ANALYSIS [8] The claimant's personal identity and status as a citizen of Ukraine is established on a balance of probabilities.2 Credibility and Well-founded Fear [9] With regard to credibility, the panel is guided by the Federal Court of Appeal, which has ruled that testimony given under oath is presumed to be true, unless there is a valid reason to doubt its truthfulness.3 The assessment the panel must use to test the truth of a story of a witness is that it be in harmony with the preponderance of probabilities, which a practical and informed person would readily recognize as reasonable in that place and in those conditions.4 Furthermore, the panel cannot be satisfied that, "the evidence is credible or trustworthy unless satisfied that it is probably so, not just possibly so".5 [10] The panel, in considering the credibility of the claimant, made an assessment of all the evidence, both oral and documentary.6 The evidence was also assessed as a whole, so it could be treated in a consistent manner.7 However, not every piece of evidence will be referred to, but those the panel finds relevant to its decision.8 Even if a piece of evidence is not referred to, the panel carefully considered it as part of the evidence.9 The panel is entitled to make reasonable findings based on implausibility, common sense and rationality, and may reject evidence if it is not consistent with the probabilities affecting the case as a whole. [11] The claimant testified that he was physically attacked multiple times, beaten, threatened and called anti-Semitic names, his home and car were vandalized and anti-Semitic graffiti scraped and painted on them because of his Jewish background by nationalists and other anti-Semitic individuals. The panel does not believe that he was a victim of persecution and that the authorities did not provide help. The panel believes and so finds on a balance of probabilities that the claimant made the story up that he was persecuted by nationalists and anti-Semitic individuals and that despite seeking help from the authorities they were unwilling and unable to provide him help. The claimant has used a basic set of facts, namely, "Ukraine Jews see alleged beating of Jewish man as symptom of mounting nationalism"10 and "Investigators in Lviv are looking into claims that two policemen assaulted and urinated on a Jewish man as part of an anti-Semitic attack, Ukraine media reported"11 and "Ukraine and the POLITICS OF Anti-Semitism: The West Upholds Neo-Nazi Repression of Ukraine's Jewish Community",12 and through manipulation of these facts concocted a story of personal persecution. [12] In arriving at this conclusion, the panel notes that the claimant testified that he was attacked several times but he sought medical attention twice. Once in XXXX 2010 at a government institution and the second time at a private clinic in XXXX 2011. He testified that after the XXXX 2011 incident "I started thinking about leaving the country". Since the evidence before the panel is that the claimant is Jewish and he testified that he started thinking about leaving Ukraine in XXXX 2011, he was asked if he had considered going to Israel. He replied, "At that point no, I was looking for any option [panel's emphasis] to leave the country. My sister and wife reside in the States so I wanted to leave as soon as possible". This information that he was looking for any chance or opportunity to leave Ukraine as soon as possible because his wife and sister were in the US, in the view of this panel is very important. Because it shows the lengths he would go to make sure if he could not be in the US, he would at least be as close as possible to his wife until his sponsorship application from his wife is finalized and he could return to the US to reunite with his family. Bear in mind he was deported from the US in 2007 and XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX followed him from the US to Ukraine to marry him on XXXX XXXX, 2007.13 According to the claimant's father, the claimant's wife has "started the sponsorship process" but because he was deported from the US it is taking them "very long".14 [13] Thus, the panel finds on a balance of probabilities that the claimant has come up with a concocted story that he was being persecuted because he is a Jew, so he could get closer to where his wife is until the sponsorship is complete which undermines his credibility of a subjective fear. For example, seeing that the claimant said that after the XXXX 2011 violent attack against him he started thinking about leaving Ukraine, but as the panel notes he didn't leave until XXXX XXXX, 2012 - four to five months hence - he was asked why he didn't leave Ukraine before. He replied, "I didn't have plans to leave the country. I had plans to return to the US to my family and continue with my living. After the last incident when I was beaten badly I decided to leave but I was told that if I go to the police I would disappear". The panel finds his explanation unsatisfactory. Bear in mind the evidence before this panel is that as early as in the spring of 2008 he was having problems in Ukraine because of his race/religion. For example, he was attacked and physically assaulted by a group of UNA-UNSO members who told him that they would make sure that he left Ukraine because Jews were not welcomed.15 This was followed by other incidents in XXXX 2009, a week later, as well as in XXXX 2009, XXXX 2010 and in XXXX 2010. He also testified that he personally made three complaints to the police. He was specifically asked if he had gotten any help from the police at any time and he said "No". Given the alleged problems he was having in his country, the panel finds on a balance of probabilities that if indeed he was being persecuted, he would have left before. He didn't and the panel draws a negative inference. [14] In addition, the claimant testified that he came to Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2012 but he claimed refugee status on February 24, 2012. He was asked why he didn't claim refugee at the airport and he said "Because I was told by people who helped me that I had to stay for two weeks in Vancouver to study". Then he added "I don't see much difference in filling out documents up on arrival or later". The panel finds the claimant's explanation unsatisfactory which further undermines his credibility of a subjective fear. First he didn't say what he was going to study in Vancouver for two weeks that would prevent him from making a claim at the airport other than he was told by 'people' that he had to. But his explanation that he did not see any difference in filling out documents upon his arrival at the airport or doing it one month later seems to suggest that he doesn't take the refugee process seriously or have a basis of subjective fear. For example, delay can indicate whether or not a claimant has a basis of subjective fear. Where there is no reasonable excuse for the delay, an inference of evasion going to credibility is often warranted.16 [15] If the claimant was really being persecuted in his country on a balance of probabilities he would have claimed refugee status as soon as he arrived. After all he is experienced in travelling and an intelligent man with fourteen years education17 and several years working experiences. [16] In the Singh,18 decision Mr. Justice Shore said in part: 'There's a well-established principle to the effect to any person having a well-founded fear of persecution should claim refugee protection in Canada as soon as he or she arrives in the country if that is his or her intent. On this point the Federal Court of Appeal has already concluded that delay in claiming refugee protection is an important factor which the Board may take into consideration in its analysis. Such a delay indicates a lack of subjective fear of persecution since there is an assumption to the effect that a person having a well-founded fear of persecution will claim refugee protection at the first opportunity. Accordingly in conducting its assessment the Board is entitled to take into consideration the applicant's delay in claiming refugee protection.' [17] In the Nijjar,19 decision, Mr. Justice de Montigny declared: 'It is trite law that the delay in submitting your refugee protection claim while not decisive remains a relevant element that the Tribunal may take into account in assessing both the statements and the actions and deeds of a claimant The claimant knew upon his arrival that he was only authorized to stay in Canada for a specific and limited period of time. Under these circumstances it was reasonable to expect that he regularize this status as soon as possible if he truly feared for his life and physical integrity.' [18] Similarly in the case of Assadi Nasser-Eddin,20 the court held: "Failure to immediately seek protection can impugn the claimant's credibility including his or he testimony about events in his country of origin". [19] While the delay in filing may not be extremely long and the claimant may well be within the time granted for him to remain in Canada, his explanation is unreasonable which undermines his credibility of a subjective fear. The panel therefore determines that the claimant's delay of roughly a month in seeking Canada's protection is not consistent with subjective fear of persecution in Ukraine. [20] The Federal Court has ruled that the claimant must subjectively fear persecution, and this fear must be well founded in an objective sense. Thus, a lack of evidence going to the subjective element of the claim is in itself sufficient for the claim to fail.21 This appears to be so even when there is evidence that an objective basis for the fear exists.22 [21] The Federal Court has also found that, where a claimant is found to be lacking in credibility, the panel can legitimately find that there is no basis of subjective fear for the claim. In such cases, it is very difficult, if not impossible, to find credible evidence of a claimant's subjective fear, notwithstanding the existing evidence of human rights violations in the claimant's country.23 Despite the documentary evidence,24 the oral testimony and counsel's oral submissions, this panel finds no basis of a subjective fear in this claim. State protection The principles of state protection [22] Countries are presumed to be capable of protecting their citizens unless the state is in a complete state of breakdown. The onus is then on the claimant to provide clear and convincing proof that the authorities of said state are unable or unwilling to protect him or her.25 [23] Countries are not expected to afford perfect protection to all at all times. The fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens does not justify a claim in countries where there are military, police and civilian authorities in place, which are making serious efforts to protect their citizens.26 [24] The more democratic a country, the more difficult it is for the claimant to establish that all avenues of protection have been exhausted.27 The Federal Court has ruled that state protection needs to be adequate rather than effective per se.28 A state, then, requires a legislative and procedural framework to provide protection, and its authorities must be able and willing to implement law and procedure.29 [25] In this case, the claimant has the burden of providing clear and convincing evidence that he cannot obtain adequate state protection in Ukraine. Is Ukraine in a state of breakdown? [26] The panel takes judicial note that Ukraine is faced with a number of problems at the present time: * In February 2014, Russian forces moved into the Crimean peninsula. In March, Crimea held a referendum and decided by 97% to join Russia. The Ukrainian forces withdrew from Crimea shortly afterwards. * In April 2014, pro-Russian forces occupied government buildings in eastern Ukraine. Ukraine launched an anti-terrorist operation against the separatists. Pro-Russian sentiment and separatist activity is strong in the regions of Luhansk and Donetsk. The Ukrainian authorities have responded with military force. [27] The claimant hails from XXXX town, XXXX territory. He has also lived in XXXX territory. [28] The United Nation High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) reports that internal displacement has affected more than 10,000 individuals in the country. Most of them hail from Crimea and eastern Ukraine.30 [29] The panel finds that Ukraine is not in a complete state of breakdown and notes that Ukraine has sent its troops to the eastern part to restore order, and held a presidential election in May 2014. These occurrences demonstrate that Ukraine is still in control of its territories (except Crimea, Luhansk and Donetsk). Ukraine is a republic with a presidential-parliamentary system of government... The October 2012 elections for the 450-seat parliament and parliamentary by-elections in December did not meet international standards or transparency. Authorities maintained effective control over the security forces. Security forces committed human rights abuses.31 [30] The panel takes judicial notice of the following: In February 2014, then-President Viktor Yanukovych disappeared and fled to Russia. In May 2014, Ukraine held a presidential election. Petro Poroshenko was elected as president. [31] The United States Department of State Report for Ukraine for 2013 states the following: The most serious human rights problem during the year was increased government interference with, and pressure on, media outlets, including government tolerance of increased levels of violence toward journalists. The second major human rights problem was intensified pressure on civil society, nongovernmental organizations (NGOs), and civic activists. The third major problem was the practice of politically motivated prosecutions and detentions.... Other human rights problems included: abuse of persons in custody, including beating and alleged torture of detainees and prisoners; harsh and unhealthy conditions in prisons and detention facilities; an inefficient and corrupt judicial system; inadequate provisions for asylum seekers; pervasive corruption in all branches of government; societal violence against women and abuse of children; societal discrimination against and harassment of ethnic minorities; trafficking in persons; discrimination and violence against lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender (LGBT) persons; discrimination toward persons with HIV/AIDS that endangered their prospects for treatment; limitations on workers' right to strike; and forced labor. The government generally did not prosecute security officials who committed abuses, especially against prisoners, members of minority groups, and civic activists. Human rights observers often criticized prosecutions for corruption as selective and politically motivated. Impunity was a problem throughout the government.32 More principles of state protection [32] A contextual approach is required when assessing the availability of state protection and determining whether the claimant has rebutted the presumption of state protection.33 The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) should take into consideration the personal situation of the claimant, the particular risk alleged, the agent of persecution, and the country conditions.34 [33] The adequacy of state protection cannot rest on the subjective fear of the claimant. A finding of adequate state protection is premised on an objective assessment that must be made independently of whether the claimant possesses a subjective fear.35 [34] The protection need not be perfect as no state can guarantee perfect protection.36 The claimant seeks state protection [35] The claimant testified that he made three reports to the police but said that he didn't receive any help from them. However, he presented a police/medical forensic report37 which suggests that the police did take action. For example, it reads, "In accordance with the referral of the senior district police officer of XXXX Regional Branch of the Ministry of Internal Affairs of Ukraine in XXXX district, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX from XXXX XXXX XXXX.2010....". While the document states that the claimant sustained different kinds of injuries the panel notes that there is no mention of who caused the injuries but that they were the result of blunt objects. The medical report38 also did not mention the cause of the injuries but mentions that the claimant was referred for a forensic examination. The panel finds that if indeed the injuries were as a result of violence that was racially motivated, on a balance of probabilities it would have said so. For example, counsel presented several internet articles which highlighted some of the problems facing Jews in Ukraine. For example, "Jews feel increasingly targeted by nationalists"39 and a 29 year old Jewish businessman, Mr. Dmitry Flekman was beaten by two detectives.40 The assault of Mr. Flekman was publicly addressed at a conference by the president of the Ukrainian Jewish Committee, Mr. Feldman who is also a Ukrainian parliamentarian. [36] The claimant alleged that he was attacked and beaten up by several uniformed members of UNA-UNSO in 2008 and as recently as in XXXX 2011, he received threatening letters from this group and was again attacked by nationalists and beaten into unconsciousness. This is in addition to his beating in XXXX 2010. While the panel believes that in XXXX 2010 the claimant sustained injuries to his body, it finds on a balance of probabilities that it had nothing to do with his Jewish ethnicity. The panel finds on a balance of probabilities that the claimant was not attacked and beaten because of his Jewish ethnicity in Ukraine. For example, one of the internet articles presented by counsel reads "Anti-Semitic assaults are rare in Ukraine".41 The panel prefers the documentary evidence over the claimant's testimony since they are drawn from a wide range of publicly accessible documents from reliable non-government and government organizations. [37] The claimant testified that he was attacked and assaulted in XXXX 2011 which was what caused him to decide to leave the country. He didn't present any police report, he explained that he was warned not to report it or they would kill him. He testified that he sought medical treatment at a private clinic and while he didn't present a medical report from that clinic, he presented a medical report42 from another physician. It states "According to the claimant he was beaten up a few days ago and received first medical aid at a private institution". Again there is no mention of the reason he was beaten up. But even more importantly, the report goes on to say "The patient does not mention the details of the beating. Refuses hospitalization".43 Even if the claimant didn't mention the details of the beating, the fact that it is stated as a beating denotes criminal activity which requires the doctor to report it to the police. For example, [M]edical institutions and law enforcement agencies are obligated to cooperate. The OSI report refers to Order No. 63 of 1993 of the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Ministry of Health, dated 11 June 1993, and states that managers of hospitals, clinics, and other medical institutions are obligated to inform law enforcement authorities when treating people with injuries that appear to be caused by "illegal actions". A report by the West Ukrainian Centre "Women's Perspectives," a non-governmental organization (NGO) based in Lviv which promotes gender equality, corroborates this information. Citing Order No. 307/105 of the Ministry of Internal Affairs and the Health Ministry, dated 10 May 1993, the report states that "heads of medical institutions must ensure 'the police are informed about all cases of persons with shot and knife wounds and other bodily injuries turning for medical assistance, if there are reasons to believe that they have been sustained as a result of a crime'."44 [38] There is no evidence that this alleged attack and assault were reported to the police which suggests to this panel that the incident did not take place. In examining the evidence before it, and given the credibility problems of the claimant, the panel assigns very little weight to this medical report. A medical opinion is only as valid as the truth of the facts on which the opinion is based. If a panel does not believe the underlying facts, it may discount a medical report in light of that finding.45 [39] But if the claimant was indeed attacked and beaten the several times he said he did and had gone to the local police and made three reports as he said he did, and the police didn't help as he says they didn't, he still has a responsibility to exhaust all of the recourses available to him domestically before claiming refugee status.46,47 For example, approaching the prosecutor's office to complain against the local police failure to help him. The claimant was asked if he sought help from any higher authorities. He replied "There was a procedure if you want to go to a higher authority you had to get a report from the police and if you don't get that report you can't go to the higher authority". He was asked who informed him of this procedure. He said that he went to the prosecutor's office and they told him that he needed a police report. The panel rejects the claimant's allegation that there is such a procedure in place. A search of the documentary evidence before this panel refutes the claimant's allegation. For example, "While some people submit complaints about their ill-treatment to the Prosecutor's Office or the police itself, many do not as they have little faith that the existing system can deliver justice; some fear repercussions for complaining".48 There is no mention that to make a report to the prosecutor's office one needs first to get a report from the police. It is clear that one can make a report either to the police directly or the prosecutor's office. Also, the claimant's own document indicates that in the case of Mr. Flekman "the Lviv Prosecutor's Office has opened a criminal investigation based on the medical report that show his injuries could only be caused with a blunt object".49 [40] Documentary evidence indicates that while the judicial system is inefficient and corrupt, law enforcement forces were generally effective.50 It was further indicated that there were cases of abuse, and not all cases were properly investigated.51 In regard to this claim, no evidence of abuse by the police was disclosed and, as noted above, there was a forensic medical report in regards to the XXXX 2010 incident. [41] There are an estimated 103,600 Jews in Ukraine, although community leaders estimate the number of persons with Jewish heritage to be over 370,000.52 There were no reports of religious persecution by the government, and the Jewish community is represented in the All-Ukraine Council of Churches and Religious Organizations (AUCCRO).53 On October 16, 2012, the largest Jewish community centre in the world was opened in Dnipropetrovsk, and national and international leaders attended the ceremony.54 The new facility included a synagogue, a community centre, and a Holocaust museum and research centre. While there remain reports of societal abuses and discrimination, including cases of anti-Semitism reflected in vandalism, graffiti and assaults, there have also been court trials in regard to anti-Semitic offenses.55 [42] The documentary evidence is mixed in regards to the availability of state protection. Country documentary evidence makes clear that there are societal abuses and discrimination in Ukraine based on religion, including anti-Semitism.56 The panel finds, however, in the context of the country documentary evidence cited above, and as well in the context of the police response to the one incident in terms of the police forensic medical document noted above, that adequate state protection is available to the claimant in Ukraine, and it was available to the claimant when he resided in Ukraine. [43] The panel further finds, on a balance of probabilities and in the context of country documentary evidence and findings and negative inferences noted above, that while it is clear that the claimant could experience harassment and prejudice in Ukraine there is only a mere possibility that he would experience attacks that rise to the level of persecution. [44] The panel therefore finds, on a balance of probabilities and in the context of the cumulative findings and negative inferences noted above, that the claimant has failed to satisfy his onus of establishing a serious possibility that he would be persecuted, or that he would be subject to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a risk to his life, or a risk of torture by any authority in Ukraine. CONCLUSION [45] The claimant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. The Refugee Protection Division therefore rejects his claim. (signed) " Edward C. Robinson " Edward C.
