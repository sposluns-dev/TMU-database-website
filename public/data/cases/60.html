Conseil(s) du (de la/des) demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Designated Representative(s) XXXX XXXX Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du (de la) ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX, (the principal claimant) XXXX XXXX, (the female claimant), XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, collectively (the claimants) seek protection in Canada pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA).1 [2] Pursuant to Rule 55 of the Refugee Protection Division Rules, the claims were heard jointly as the claimants are a husband, wife and their two children. As well, pursuant to section 167(2) of the IRPA, the principal claimant was designated the representative for the minor claimants. ALLEGATIONS [3] The claimants have set out their allegations of persecution in the narrative portions of their Personal Information Forms, (PIF).2 The principal claimant comes from a Jewish family, while the female claimant is Ukrainian. They allege that due to their actual or perceived Jewish nationality they have been subjected to discriminatory and harassing conduct from Ukrainians generally, the business partner of the principal claimant's father as well as from relatives of the female claimant. ISSUES [4] The issues central to the determination of the claims are, the claimant's personal identity and their identities as Jewish persons, credibility and state protection. Identity [5] The panel had before it copies of the travel documents used by the claimants to travel to, and enter Canada, namely their Ukrainian passports. The panel also had before it a number of Ukrainian identity documents including birth certificates, marriage certificate and the principal claimant's military book all of which satisfactorily establishes the claimants' personal identities and their identities as citizens of Ukraine.3 The Claimant's Jewish Identities [6] The principal claimant testified to his Jewish heritage. He stated that while not technically Jewish he was raised in a Jewish family by his grandmother who was Jewish. As such, the family followed Jewish traditions observing the Sabbath and were known in the village as a Jewish family. He stated that the festival of Purim was his favourite because of its carnival like atmosphere. The female claimant is not Jewish, however, the claimants allege that by marrying a Jewish man she, too, is perceived as being Jewish. She testifies that she observes the Sabbath and brings it in by saying prayers and lighting the candles, this is the only time that she covers her hair. [7] In Canada, the principal claimant and the son have further embraced their Jewish heritage and have attached themselves to the XXXX XXXX Jewish Cultural and Educational Network, where they study under Rabbi XXXX XXXX. In his letter of support, RabbiXXXX XXXX states that the principal claimant and his son attend the synagogue on a regular basis and that the female claimant sometimes accompanies them.4 Rabbi XXXX alluded to the difficulties presented to the family by the fact that the female claimant is an agnostic, but notes that she is supportive of the principal claimant and their son. [8] The son, XXXX, demonstrated good knowledge of Jewish rituals and their significance when asked. The panel finds, on a balance of probabilities that, in Ukraine, given the particular circumstances of the principal claimant, namely growing up in a small town or village with a Jewish grandmother, and observing the Sabbath, the family is perceived, or would be perceived, as being Jewish. The panel also finds that given the current involvement of the principal claimant and the son with the XXXX XXXX Centre that it is likely that if returned to Ukraine they would seek out similar Jewish organisations. This would likely cause all four family members to be viewed as Jewish. Credibility [9] The claimants allege that they were attacked on three separate fronts. First, the principal claimant alleged that he was the subject of anti-Semitic abuse during his childhood. The abuse was made worse because of a significant birth defect. He alleged that it was futile to complain about the abuse because no redress was forthcoming. Things, however, took a turn for the worse after his father returned to Ukraine in XXXX 2011. During this visit the principal claimant attended a synagogue with his father. This was when the anti-Semitic threats increased. At this time the claimants were living in XXXX. However, the synagogue that the principal claimant went to was in XXXX. [10] The panel considered the likelihood of it becoming known in the principal claimant's hometown that he had attended a synagogue in XXXX and came to the conclusion that it was slim. The panel also considered the impact publication of his attendance at a synagogue would have made in XXXX. The panel concluded that while the principal claimant may have told his friends that he had gone to the synagogue, however, given that XXXX was where he was born and raised, and also given his claim that he is from a Jewish family, the panel is not persuaded that his attendance at a synagogue would have generate any additional anti-Semitic behaviour towards him. The Father's Business Partner [11] The principal claimant stated that his father used to own a XXXX business in XXXX. Prior to emigrating from Ukraine, the principal claimant's father concluded an agreement with his business partner, by which agreement, the business partner was to have made payments to the principal claimant. In breach of the agreement, the business partner never made these payments. When his father returned to Ukraine, the principal claimant informed his father that his former business partner had defrauded him by reneging on the agreement to pay him part of the profits from the XXXX business. The principal claimant alleged that when he and his father confronted his former business partner, the partner refused to allow him to look at the books until his own accountant arrived. When they protested, the business partner abused them using anti-Semitic rhetoric and ordered them to leave his office. He also told the father to return to the US and to take the principal claimant with him. In essence, the former business partner had stolen his father's XXXX business. [12] After they reached home, in XXXX, five men attacked the principal claimant and his father. He states that the men were connected to the former business partner. His opinion is based on the fact that during the attack their attackers referred to the business and abused them as Jews. The principal claimant alleged that both he and his father required medical attention, however, when they tried to report the incident, the police were not cooperative. Instead, the police accused them of provoking the attack and advised his father to return to the United States as soon as possible. [13] The claimants exhibited a copy of a lease agreement and a copy of a joint venture agreement. The lease agreement relates to premises leased by the principal claimant's father and his partner in order to carry on a business. The joint venture agreement chronicles the agreement between the parties. However, it appears to be silent regarding paying money to the principal claimant.5 Therefore, it is not clear to the panel what the terms of the agreement regarding paying money to the principal claimant were, nor is it clear that there was ever such an agreement. [14] The claimants submitted a copy of a medical report6 to substantiate the principal claimant's claim that he and his father suffered a beating at the hands of persons connected to the father's former business partner. The medical report appears to substantiate that the principal claimant had injuries that were consistent with him having been beaten. The medical report cannot, however, reliably state who beat the claimant or their motives for doing so. However, when the panel asked him, the principal claimant stated that he formed the opinion that the five men, who were strangers to him, were linked to the former business partner because they told his father to forget about the XXXX business. [15] The principal claimant testified that even after the beating he tried to get the former business partner to honour his agreement with his father. However, he refused to do so. Instead, he threatened the principal claimant and his children. The threats worried him. [16] The panel is cognizant of the statement in Maldonado,7 that when a claimant swears to the truth of certain, this creates a presumption that they are true unless there is valid reason to doubt their truthfulness. In other words, a claimant's sworn testimony gives rise to a rebuttable presumption of truthfulness. In the present case, the panel found that aspects of the principal claimant's testimony about the beating and his later interaction with the former business partner were not plausible thereby undermining his credibility. [17] The panel finds that it was not plausible that the principal claimant would continue to approach the former business partner if he was of the view that the business partner had caused him and his father to be beaten up. Having made this finding, the panel finds on a balance of probabilities, that if the claimant was beaten up as he claimed, the beating had nothing to do with his father's former business partner. In any event, there being a written agreement between the parties, the panel finds that there were other avenues open to the claimant's father to enforce the terms of the agreement. [18] Counsel for the claimants submitted that the motive for the attack was anti-Semitic rather than "corporate". The panel disagrees. Based on the evidence before her the panel finds that if it was true that the father's former business partner caused the principal claimant and his father to be attacked and beaten, the attack was connected more to the his actions in defrauding the principal claimant's father and less to their Jewish identity. The true motive then would have been to warn them off of making any claims to the business. Any anti-Semitic slurs were icing on the cake, so to speak. The Assault of XXXX 2011 [19] The principal claimant alleged that he suffered a second serious attack in XXXX of 2011. In this incident he was beaten by relatives of his wife and other anti-Semitic persons. The principal claimant stated that this assault stemmed from the attempts of his wife's relatives to cause her to separate from him. He testified that from the outset they were against the relationship because they hated Jews. They were opposed to the marriage and had never ceased their campaign to get her to leave him. Even the birth of a child did not soften their stance. After the son was enrolled in school, one of the wife's relatives who was a teacher at the school encourage the other children to beat him and call him names. [20] In her testimony, the female claimant testified that the husbands of her sister and her aunt were among the attackers. She knew this because they were not disguised. [21] The claimants were able to provide a copy of a medical report that they claim supports their account of the second beating.8 The panel makes the same observations about this second report as it did of the first. [22] The principal claimant testified that in addition to this beating, the family received harassing telephone calls and threats regardless of who answered the phone. The callers urged the family to leave Ukraine and promised to make their lives difficult if they remained. He linked this harassment to a general increase in anti-Semitic incidents in Ukraine. He testified that the trend was for such incidents to increase and that many Jewish families are choosing to leave because their businesses are being expropriated and it is impossible to work. He, however, was working at an architectural and design bureau until he left Ukraine. [23] The panel asked the principal claimant if there was any other reason why the family left Ukraine. He responded that a lack of state protection prompted his decision to leave as they had offered no protection to him as a Jewish person. He testified that on the two occasions that he sought police protection he was met with nonchalance and inaction. Although the police officers told him that they were not going to deal with the matter, when he attempted to follow up, they refused to tell him whether they had investigated the beating. They also laughed at the claimant. They then kicked him out of the police station. These actions displayed an an anti-Semitic attitude. [24] Also, when he was asked, the principal claimant responded that he made one attempt to take his complaint further. He said he went to the prosecutor's office to complain, but that this effort met with no success. The prosecutor's office advised him to forget about the incidents, maintain a low profile, or leave the country. This indifference did little to ease the principal claimant's fears and caused him to lose faith in the ability or willingness of the police to protect him and his family. [25] The claimants did not submit any police reports. In response to their counsel, the principal claimant explained that this was because the police did not actually take a report. Their interest vanished once they were told the reason for the attacks. This testimony is inconsistent with his former testimony that he made two complaints to the police. [26] He also testified that in XXXX 2011 he was hospitalised for XXXX days and that upon being released, he went to the police station to pursue the matter further. Later on, someone telephoned his home. That person told him they knew that he had gone to the police and that should he persist things would get worse for him. The prosecutor's office told him that it could not proceed with this matter without a police report. [27] The claimant testified that he met with a similar response from the district police when someone drew a swastika on his garage. The officer to whom he made his complaint told him to clean it, or paint it, and that he (the principal claimant) would never find that person who did it. [28] The principal claimant made arrangements to leave the Ukraine shortly after the XXXX beating. [29] The panel considered the claimants' allegations and the testimony of the principal and female claimants as to the identity and motive of the perpetrators of the XXXX beating. The panel finds that if this beating occurred in the circumstances the claimants describes, it was possible that there was an anti-Semitic motive to the beating. However, not only is this an insufficient basis on which to ground the claim, the claimants must also establish that state protection would not be forthcoming. STATE PROTECTION [30] The claimants allege that should they return to Ukraine state protection would not be available to them because they are Jewish, or in the case of the female claimant, married to a man who is Jewish and therefore perceived as being Jewish. The claimants provided in a plethora of documentary evidence that, in their view, support their claims. The claimant's documentary evidence highlights a number of incidents that have an anti-Semitic basis. [31] However, the totality of the documentary evidence before the panel disclosed two conflicting opinions concerning the situation of Ukrainian Jews. The Board's documentary evidence9 largely painted a picture where there was little overt discrimination towards Jews, while the claimant's documentary package painted a starkly different version.10 For example, the Report of the Special Rapporteur on minority issues, Mission to Ukraine, observes: - The overwhelming majority of the minority and other representatives consulted by the Special Rapporteur on minority issues during her visit to Ukraine described a history of harmonious inter-ethnic and interfaith relations and a legislative, policy and social environment that was generally conducive to the protection of their rights, including cultural and linguistic rights. Nevertheless, minority issues have become highly politicized as the situation of political and social unrest has emerged in some regions since February 2014. That threatens to create and widen fractures along national, ethnic and linguistic lines and undermine peaceful coexistence if not quickly resolved. An end to violence and constructive consultations on minority rights must be primary objectives for all stakeholders.11 [32] The report observed that Ukraine has a wide range of religions, belief groups and religious freedoms and the rights of religious minorities are protected in practice. Nevertheless, given the climate of political and social unease, it is particularly necessary for the authorities to guard against any human rights violations, including acts of violence, intimidation, threat or abuse targeted at individuals or groups based on their religion. Some incidents of concern have been reported in the context of the tense social and political environment since February 2014.12 [33] The report also noted that in commenting upon the position of Jews within the society, Jewish representatives stated:13 - Jewish representatives reflected that they were well integrated, enjoyed their rights as a religious minority and that anti-Semitism, discrimination and violent incidents were rare. They generally expressed satisfaction at the extent of their minority rights protection. However, some incidents were reported in the context of the unrest that had put Jewish communities on alert. In February, the Giymat Rosa Synagogue in Zaporizhia, near Kyiv was firebombed. In early April 2014, a Holocaust memorial in Odesa was vandalized with Nazi graffiti. On 19 April, the Nikolayev Synagogue was firebombed causing minor damage. Representatives expressed concern about adequate protection measures. One leader stated: "No proper police are in place; ordinary people are carrying arms." Some anti-Semitic graffiti was also reported in the Autonomous Republic of Crimea. Of concern to the Jewish community was the distribution in Donetsk by men wearing balaclavas of leaflets calling on Jews to register with the pro-Russians, pay a tax, or leave. The leaflets bore a stamp reportedly of the self-proclaimed "People's Republic of Donetsk", although it declared the leaflets to be a hoax. It remains unclear who was behind the leaflet. One leader of the Jewish community mentioned an incident in which neo-Nazi graffiti, allegedly signed by the Right Sector, had been painted on the walls of a synagogue in Odessa. Right Sector representatives denied involvement and reportedly helped remove the graffiti. A Jewish leader stated: "Politicians are playing the 'Jewish card'" and that the incidents were intended to inflame tensions and concerns amongst Jewish communities for political ends. While incidents remain rare, they nevertheless indicate a potential rise in manifestations of anti-Semitism, which must be monitored closely. The lack of implementation of the rule of law in some localities provides an environment in which far right groups have undoubtedly increased their activities and such anti-Semitic incidents may become more pronounced. Such incidents have created anxiety and should be investigated as crimes aggravated by hatred. Anti-Semitism must be acknowledged by the Government and measures should be taken to prosecute according to the law any person or group alleged to have committed or incited anti-Semitic acts. [34] Under the heading "Anti-Semitism" the United States, Department of State Reports acknowledged that harassment of ethnic and religious minorities occurred, but that attacks against Jews were rare. The section noted that: - According to census data and international Jewish groups, an estimated 103,600 Jews lived in the country, constituting approximately 0.2 percent of the population. According to the Association of Jewish Organizations and Communities (VAAD), there were approximately 300,000 persons of Jewish ancestry in the country, although the number may be higher. Before Russian aggression in eastern Ukraine, according to VAAD approximately 30,000 Jewish persons lived in the Donbas. Jewish groups estimated between 10,000 and 15,000 Jewish residents lived in Crimea before Russia's attempted annexation.14 [35] The US DOS goes on to state that:15 - Jewish community leaders reported that societal anti-Semitism was low, and authorities took steps to address problems of anti-Semitism when they arose. Institutional anti-Semitism was rare, and VAAD stated that attacks were isolated and carried out by individuals rather than organized groups. VAAD claimed that negative attitudes towards Jews and Judaism continued to be low, although some individuals espoused anti-Semitic beliefs. VAAD believed that some attacks were provocations meant to discredit the government. In September the Jewish pilgrimage to the Uman burial site of Rabbi Nachman took place without significant incidents. On December 21, however, unknown individuals vandalized the site with a pig's head and blood. Authorities opened an investigation into the incident and immediately condemned it. In July authorities named a street in Kyiv after former Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists (OUN) leader, Stepan Bandera. In response according to press reports, more than 20 Ukrainian Jewish groups published a statement condemning, as a form of Holocaust denial, the naming of streets for leaders of the OUN and the Ukrainian Insurgent Army (UPA),. Some international scholars also objected. At the same time, authorities also named a street in Kyiv in honor of Janusz Korczak, a Polish-Jewish writer who had died in Auschwitz. According to the National Minority Rights Monitoring Group (NMRMG) supported by the Euro-Asian Jewish Congress and VAAD, one case of suspected anti-Semitic violence was recorded during the year, compared to one case of anti-Semitic violence in 2015, four such cases in 2014, and four in 2013. The NMRMG identified 18 cases of anti-Semitic vandalism during the year, as compared to 22 in 2015 and 23 in 2014. Graffiti swastikas continued to appear in Kyiv and other cities. On January 13, arsonists damaged a Jewish cemetery in Kolomiya, following similar attacks in 2015. On March 4, unknown persons set fire to a wreath left by the Israeli minister of justice at the Babyn Yar memorial. On April 15, vandals defaced a monument to the Holocaust in Cherkasy. In May, on Israel's national Memorial Day for the Holocaust, an unknown group of persons burned an Israeli flag at the Babyn Yar memorial. There were reportedly several anti-Semitic incidents targeting the memorial during the year. [36] The report also noted that according to Jewish community leaders, "Senior government officials and politicians from various political parties continued efforts to combat anti-Semitism by speaking out against extremism and social intolerance and criticizing anti-Semitic acts. On September 29, the government held a commemoration ceremony marking the 75th anniversary of the Babyn Yar massacre, during which 33,771 Jews were killed in two days during the Nazi German occupation.16 [37] By contrast, the claimant's documentary evidence package universally pointed to a situation that was fraught with danger for Jews living in Ukraine. [38] In her submissions, Counsel for the claimants argued that the documentary evidence supported the claimants' allegations. She cited among others, a January 18, 2018 article17 that accused the Ukrainian authorities of backtracking on human rights issues and failing in its Human rights commitments. She also referred to the beating of Pediatric Neurosurgeon, Alexander Duchovsky.18 Counsel linked this beating to that inflicted on the principal claimant and she argued that similar incidents were occurring throughout Ukraine. [39] Counsel for the claimant also alluded to the defacing of Jewish monuments, including a Hannukiyah in Kiev in December 2017 and a monument in Ternopol. As well she alluded to an incident that occurred to the Rabbi in Lvov and she argued that the large number of documents that the claimants submitted indicate that a real threat to Jews exists in Ukraine. Counsel for the claimant also submitted that the documents support the claimants' position that the police do not act on the anti-Semitic nature of the complaints, but instead simply put it down to hooliganism. [40] In assessing the availability of state protection, the panel prefers the Board's documentary evidence because it has been culled from a wide variety of credible sources whose raison d'etre is to chronicle the human rights situation. Thus, the Boards' documentary evidence draws not only from the US DOS report but also from reports of NGOs such as Amnesty International, Freedom House and Human Rights Watch.19 The claimant's documentary evidence package does contain some of these sources, but is largely culled from internet sources which may or may not be reliable. [41] In any event, there is little independent evidence before the panel that could allow it to find that the claimants have met their onus to establish that there was no state protection available to them. The claimants are motivated to remain in Canada, and it is not clear to the panel why these claimants could not obtain a report from the police for the incident when they were taken to the hospital in XXXX 2011. The panel understands from numerous hearings that, in these situations the police do make a report. Whether they act on it is another matter. [42] Furthermore, this is a case where the claimants were able to identify two of the attackers, therefore, the panel is not persuaded that the police would refuse to act in such a case. Accordingly, the panel concludes that the claimants have not met their onus to establish, by means of credible evidence, that state protection would not be forthcoming to them. [43] Therefore, in the circumstances, the panel finds that the claimants are not Convention refugees. Are the Claimants Persons in need of Protection? [44] Notwithstanding its finding that the claimants are not Convention refugees, the panel turned its mind to the question of whether they meet the definition of persons in need of protection. [45] Section 97 of the IRPA establishes that protection could be extended to foreign nationals who are not Convention refugees in certain circumstances. The section states in part: 97 (1) A person in need of protection is a person in Canada whose removal to their country or countries of nationality or, if they do not have a country of nationality, their country of former habitual residence, would subject them personally o (a) to a danger, believed on substantial grounds to exist, of torture within the meaning of Article 1 of the Convention Against Torture; or o (b) to a risk to their life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if * (i) the person is unable or, because of that risk, unwilling to avail themself of the protection of that country, * (ii) the risk would be faced by the person in every part of that country and is not faced generally by other individuals in or from that country, * (iii) the risk is not inherent or incidental to lawful sanctions, unless imposed in disregard of accepted international standards, and * (iv) the risk is not caused by the inability of that country to provide adequate health or medical car20 [46] Section 97 (1) (b) requires that there be a risk to life or of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if the person is unable, or because of that risk, unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country and that the risk would not be faced by the person in every part of the country. The definition also requires that the risk not be a generalised risk, or be inherent to lawful sanction, or due to lack of medical services in the country. [47] The stumbling block to the claimants' case is that the risk must be faced throughout the country. Ukraine is a large country, and the claimants have not established that they would be at risk of attack from the female claimant's relatives throughout the territory of Ukraine. For this reason, the panel finds that the claimants do not meet the definition of "a person in need of protection" under Section 97 of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [48] The claimants, XXXX XXXX, XXXX XXXX, XXXX XXXX and XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX applied for protection in Canada pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act. For the reasons set out above the panel finds that they are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. [49] The claims are rejected. (signed) "H. Ross" H.
