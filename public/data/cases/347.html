Gong v. O'Neill

2022 BCCA 359 Gong v. O'Neill COURT OF APPEAL FOR BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Gong v. O'Neill, 2022 BCCA 359 Date: 20221018 Docket: CA48499 Between: Quan Gong Appellant (Plaintiff) And P. O'Neill Respondent (Defendant) Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Horsman (In Chambers) On appeal from: An order of the Supreme Court of British Columbia, dated July 29, 2022 (Gong v. O'Neill, 2022 BCSC 1696, Vancouver Docket S2110068). Oral Reasons for Judgment The Appellant, appearing in person: Q. Gong Counsel for the Respondent: C. Armstrong Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia October 18, 2022 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, British Columbia October 18, 2022 Summary: The appellant applies for leave to appeal an order of a chambers judge, dismissing her application to set aside the order of a Master. Held: Application for leave to appeal dismissed. The appellant seeks to overturn a decision of a Master, adjourning an application to dismiss her civil claim against an arbitrator. Her appeal has no merit, has no practical utility as the hearing of the dismissal application is already underway, and it can only serve to unduly hinder the progress of the action. HORSMAN J.A.: [1] The appellant, Quan Gong, seeks leave to appeal the order of a chambers judge issued on July 29, 2022, dismissing her application to set aside the order of a Master. The reasons of the chambers judge are indexed at 2022 BCSC 1696. Background [2] This appeal arises out of a civil action that Ms. Gong filed against the defendant, P. O'Neill. Mr. O'Neill acted as an arbitrator for the Residential Tenancy Branch. He was the arbitrator on a residential tenancy dispute between Ms. Gong and her landlord. Ms. Gong is dissatisfied with Mr. O'Neill's decisions. In addition to filing a civil action against him, Ms. Gong also filed a petition for judicial review of his decisions. [3] In February 2022, Mr. O'Neill filed an application seeking to have the civil action against him struck on the basis that Ms. Gong's pleadings does not disclose a claim, and is frivolous, vexatious, and an abuse of the court process (the "Dismissal Application"). The Dismissal Application was originally scheduled for February 23, 2022. The hearing date was adjourned on a number of occasions due, at least in part, to Ms. Gong's refusal to provide her availability. [4] The parties appeared before Master Robertson on June 7, 2022, on Ms. Gong's application for an order that the date for the hearing of the Dismissal Application be adjourned once again. By this point, Ms. Gong had not filed response materials to the Dismissal Application. Master Robertson ordered that the Dismissal Application be adjourned to August 30, 2022, on a peremptory basis, and that Ms. Gong provide response materials by August 2, 2022. [5] On July 15, 2022, Ms. Gong filed a notice of application seeking, among other things, an order setting aside Master Robertson's order of June 7, 2022, and an order prohibiting certain staff at the law firm representing Mr. O'Neill from communicating with Ms. Gong. Ms. Gong filed further notices of application on July 18, 2022, and July 25, 2022, seeking the same or similar orders. [6] On July 19, 2022, Ms. Gong filed a notice of appeal of Master Robertson's order. The appeal was not filed within 14 days of Master Robertson's order, as required by Rule 23-6(8.1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009. The chambers judgment [7] On July 29, 2022, the parties appeared before the chambers judge on Ms. Gong's application to set aside Master Robertson's order. Although not entirely clear from the record, it appears that the matter scheduled for hearing on that day was Ms. Gong's application of July 15, 2022, or possibly her duplicative application of July 18, 2022. Ms. Gong's primary argument before the chambers judge was that Master Robertson's order gave her insufficient time to prepare for an August hearing date. [8] The chambers judge characterized Ms. Gong's application to set aside Master Robertson's order as "fundamentally an appeal of Master Robertson's order", which could not be brought by way of notice of application: Chambers decision at para. 8. In noting that Ms. Gong had, in fact, filed a notice of appeal, the chambers judge stated: [8] The plaintiff has filed a notice of appeal, although late. She will need leave of the court to allow her appeal to be heard. No transcript is before me, nor is there a statement of argument on her application. Even if I were inclined to convert today's notice of application to an appeal, the materials before me are insufficient and so I could not decide the appeal on that basis. [9] Accordingly, the chambers judge dismissed Ms. Gong's application to set aside Master Robertson's order on procedural grounds, while expressly noting that "her notice of appeal can go ahead": Chambers decision at para. 9. The chambers judge further held there was no basis in law for the order sought prohibiting employees of the law firm representing Mr. O'Neill from communicating with Ms. Gong. Therefore, she dismissed the entirety of Ms. Gong's application: Chambers decision at paras. 10-11. Events after the chambers judgment [10] There are events that occurred after the chambers judgment that are relevant to the present application. [11] On August 30, 2022, the date set by Master Robertson's order for the hearing of the Dismissal Application, Ms. Gong filed another application seeking an order prohibiting certain employees of the law firm representing Mr. O'Neill from communicating with her, and also prohibiting the service of documents on her by email. [12] The parties attended judges' chambers for the Dismissal Application on August 30, 2022. However, the application was not heard due to insufficient court time. The judge presiding in chambers ordered that the Dismissal Application and Ms. Gong's August 30, 2022 application both be set for hearing for one full day on September 12, 2022, on a peremptory basis. [13] The applications were also not heard on September 12, 2022, due to insufficient court time. They were rescheduled for October 12, 2022. [14] There is a dispute between the parties as to how the October 12, 2022 date came to be set. I do not need to resolve that dispute for the purpose of this application. [15] The parties did appear before Forth J. on October 12, 2022. Counsel for Mr. O'Neill advises that Forth J. heard Mr. O'Neill's submissions on the Dismissal Application, and then adjourned the application to complete on November 15, 2022 to provide Ms. Gong time to prepare a response. I understand that Ms. Gong may take issue with this description of what occurred on October 12, 2022. However, she agrees that the parties are scheduled to appear before Forth J. on November 15, 2022. The appeal [16] Ms. Gong filed a notice of appeal of the July 29, 2022 chambers decision on August 26, 2022. She filed an application book for this leave to appeal application on September 26, 2022. Legal framework [17] A party must seek leave to appeal if the order being appealed is a limited appeal order: Court of Appeal Rules, B.C. Reg. 120/2022, s. 12(a) [Rules]. Limited appeal orders include an order that grants an adjournment (Rules, s. 11(e)) and an order of a Supreme Court judge granting or refusing an appeal from an order referred to in s. 11(e) (Rules, s. 11(g)). The order of Master Robertson was one in which an adjournment was granted, and the order of the chambers judge was one in which she dismissed Ms. Gong's request to set aside the Master's order. As the chambers judge discussed, the request was fundamentally an appeal: Chambers decision at para. 8. Therefore, leave to appeal is required in this case. [18] Determining whether leave to appeal should be granted requires consideration of the following criteria, set out at para. 10 in Goldman, Sachs & Co. v. Sessions, 2000 BCCA 326 (Chambers): (1) whether the point on appeal is of significance to the practice; (2) whether the point raised is of significance to the action itself; (3) whether the appeal is prima facie meritorious or, on the other hand, whether it is frivolous; and (4) whether the appeal will unduly hinder the progress of the action. [19] The above factors are "all considered under the rubric of the interests of justice": Vancouver (City) v. Zhang, 2007 BCCA 280 at para. 10 (Chambers). Even if the above four criteria are met, leave may still be denied if it would be against the interests of justice to do so: Movassaghi v. Aghtai, 2010 BCCA 175 at para. 27 (Chambers). Discussion [20] I have read Ms. Gong's application materials, and listened to her submissions this morning. I understand her arguments to be that: (1) she should have been entitled to apply to set aside Master Robertson's order by way of notice of application; (2) the submissions of Mr. O'Neill's counsel to the chambers judge were incorrect, but taken at face value; (3) the advice that staff at the Supreme Court registry gave to Ms. Gong about scheduling was incorrect; and (4) Master Robertson's order was unreasonable, as it imposed a timeline for the filing of response material that was impossible for Ms. Gong to meet. [21] In my view, the factors relevant to the assessment of an application for leave to appeal strongly militate against the grant of leave in this case. (1) Significance to general practice [22] This appeal concerns a discretionary interlocutory order by a chambers judge dismissing an application to set aside a Master's order granting an adjournment on certain terms. The chambers judgment does not raise any issue of general importance to the practice of law. (2) Significance to the action itself [23] The proposed appeal has no significance to the action itself. The order of the chambers judge did not decide the Dismissal Application, and did not even decide the merits of Ms. Gong's appeal of Master Robertson's order. The chambers judge expressly stated that Ms. Gong was free to pursue her appeal. [24] Furthermore, subsequent events have undermined any possible argument that this appeal has some practical significance to the action. The fact is that the Dismissal Application was not heard on August 30, 2022, and is now presently scheduled to complete on November 15, 2022. There is no utility to an appeal of an interlocutory order dismissing an application to set aside an order directing an August 30, 2022 hearing date, when the August 30, 2022 hearing date has already been adjourned. In light of subsequent events, the proposed appeal is effectively moot. (3) Merits of the appeal [25] There is no merit to this appeal. The chambers judge was clearly correct to hold that Ms. Gong could not appeal Master Robertson's order by way of a notice of application. I see no prospect that the order of the chambers judge would be reversed by a division of this Court. Furthermore, as noted, since the August 30, 2022 hearing date did not proceed, there is no practical utility to the appeal in any event. (4) Unduly hinder the progress of the action [26] Given the history of this proceeding, there is risk that the proposed appeal could unduly hinder the progress of the action. Indeed, it is difficult to see what purpose can be served by this appeal at this point, other than to delay the action. Conclusion [27] I conclude it would not be in the interests of justice to grant Ms. Gong leave to bring an appeal that has no merit, no practical utility, and can only serve to unduly hinder the progress of the action. [28] I therefore dismiss Ms. Gong's application for leave to appeal, with costs to Mr. O'Neill. "The Honourable Madam Justice Horsman"
