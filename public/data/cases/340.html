Watamaniuk v. Government of Canada

2017 BCSC 2052 Watamaniuk v. Government of Canada IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Watamaniuk v. Government of Canada, 2017 BCSC 2052 Date: 20171114 Docket: S-S-186875 Registry: New Westminster Between: Mark David William Watamaniuk Plaintiff And Gov't of Canada in respect of the Royal Canadian Mounted Police, Gov't of Canada in respect of the Canada Revenue Agency, Province of British Columbia in respect of the Student Loans Dep't, Province of British Columbia in respect of the Attorney General's Department, the Province of British Columbia in respect of the Ministry of Education, City of Vancouver in respect of the Vancouver Police Department (VPD), City of Vancouver in respect of the Office of the Mayor and City Hall Defendants Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Crawford Reasons for Judgment Appearing on his own behalf: M. Watamaniuk Counsel for the City of Vancouver: K. Liang Counsel for the Government of Canada: B. Ward Counsel for the Province of British Columbia: A. Glen Place and Date of Trial/Hearing: New Westminster, B.C. September 26, 2017 Place and Date of Judgment: New Westminster, B.C. November 14, 2017 Introduction [1] On January 11, 2017, Mr. Watamaniuk filed a Notice of Claim. [2] As can be seen by reference of the style of cause, he names the Federal Government with respect to the RCMP and the Canada Revenue Agency (the "CRA"), the Provincial Government with respect to the "Student Loans Department", the Attorney General's Department, and the Ministry of Education, and last, the City of Vancouver with respect to the police department and "the Office of the Mayor & City Hall". [3] Under Claim Relief are some 27 paragraphs seeking a variety of court orders against not only the defendants, but many non-parties including the Hotel Vancouver, The British Columbia College of Surgeons and Physicians, The Law Society, various crown counsel, the BC Securities Commission, Simon Fraser University, a law firm and various individuals. [4] Mr. Watamaniuk filed a Notice of Application on February 1, 2017, seeking a variety of orders, which I will exemplify by paragraph 1. It reads: "That the RCMP provide the names of all their personnel who are being, or who have been serviced sexually by the prostitutes MM, AR, LJ, KW, SH, LT and MO". [5] Then follow 24 proposed orders: the RCMP accept and process criminal charges for various assaults on the plaintiff in 2005; a murder attempt in 2005; an assault in 2007; that a dentist be charged regarding a murder attempt against the plaintiff in 2002; that various members of a fraternity be charged for assault; that certain alleged thieves return property of the plaintiff; that an order be made in an estate; that the CRA and the Province of BC stop seeking to collect unpaid student loans from the plaintiff; that charges be laid against various Vancouver Police chiefs; and the city mayor's office annul the plaintiff's alleged 1997 marriage. [6] Responses were filed by the Attorney General of BC on February 9, 2017, the Attorney General of Canada on the same day, and the City of Vancouver on September 5, 2017. Collectively, they seek to strike the statement of claim as being contrary to Rule 9-5(1) which reads: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1)At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [7] As well, the three applicants seek the dismissal of the application for the various orders summarized above. Mr. Watamaniuk [8] Mr. Watamaniuk advised the court that he is homeless and does not have a residence and inferred that he is not living in British Columbia. His only point of contact would be his email address. [9] If I can summarize his address to the court, it was that as a result of the various assaults he alleges from his university days through his marriage and up to date, he is alleging a conspiracy between various women he describes as whores or prostitutes, the federal police and the Vancouver Police, the physicians and dentists who he believes are part of a Jewish cult and various others who have conspired to assault him, steal from him and deprive him of his ability to make a living, and therefore he seeks recourse. Standing [10] Although Mr. Watamaniuk had not served all three government defendants, I am satisfied that they have standing: see the Attorney General Act, R.S.B.C. 1996 c. 22, s. 2(2)(b)(c) and Noddle v. Canada, 2016 BCSC 607, p 2-4, citing Montgomery v. Makhdoom, 2015WL10713244 B.C.S.C unreported 21 April 2015. [11] On the same basis, the Attorney General of Canada has standing in this action. [12] The City of Vancouver was served, and although they take the position that the Vancouver Police Department, the Mayor's Office and City Hall are not legal entities, the City has status. The City of Vancouver is a municipal corporation and it creates a police board pursuant to the Police Act R.S.B.C. 1996 c. 367, and finances the police department. [13] Under s. 20 of the Police Act, the city can be held jointly and severally liable in tort for actions of police committed in the course of their duties. Common Arguments [14] The defendants argue Mr. Watamaniuk's Notice of Claim must be struck, either because it discloses no reasonable cause of action, has no prospect of success or is certain to fail: see Regina v. Imperial Tobacco Ltd., 16 D.L.R. (3d) 470, 64 C.P.R. 3. [15] The applicants argue there is no concise statement of material facts to support any particular allegation. They say the pleadings are opaque, convoluted, confusing, and lacking in specifics or particulars such that no party can sensibly respond. As well they argue the pleadings are frivolous, vexatious and embarrassing, and for those reasons alone, should be struck. The Attorney General for British Columbia [16] Counsel submitted that with respect to crown counsel, they alone enjoy a statutory discretion as to proceeding with charges: see the Crown Counsel Act, R.S.B.C. 1996 c. 87 s. 2. Crown counsel are protected by s. 3(2)(a) of the Crown Proceeding Act, R.S.B.C. 1996 c. 89; as well, see the Supreme Court of Canada's comments in Krieger v. Law Society, 2002 SCC 65 at para. 46, and R. v. Anderson, 2014 SCC 41 at para. 44. [17] Counsel noted the plaintiff's request for trial transcripts does not require a court order because transcripts can be obtained on request. [18] The claims or remedies regarding the BC College of Physicians and Surgeons, the Law Society and the BC Securities Commission all fail as they are not parties and there is no legal basis for the relief sought. As to seeking compensation for a victim of crime, counsel suggests Mr. Watamaniuk should consider the Crime Victim Assistance Act, S.B.C. 2001, c.38. Attorney General for Canada [19] With respect to the RCMP, Mr. Watamaniuk alleges various prostitutes and members (named and unnamed) of the RCMP and various Vancouver Police, crown counsel, fraternity members, a doctor, a dentist, American Jews and others at various times attempted to murder him, assaulted him, drugged him, and one woman simulated a marriage, all contrary to various sections of the Canadian Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46 [the Criminal Code]. [20] Counsel noted that the plaintiff sought orders that the RCMP be held liable for their repeated acts of vandalism and damage caused to his property including two motor vehicles; that the Government of Canada issue him a passport; that the Government of Canada issue him a firearms acquisition certificate; and that the CRA be enjoined from garnishing activity regarding his student loan; he sought injunctions against various prostitutes and that an RCMP officer "not being serviced sexually buy [sic] any of the above whores, not aligned with anyone aligned with the Jewish cult" investigate and verify the misconduct alleged in his statement of claim. [21] Counsel submitted the statement of facts in the Notice of Claim was prolix, incoherent, and a collection of conclusory statements of alleged criminal offences. Counsel pointed out that the Criminal Code definitions of criminal offences do not create civil claims, and on that basis, the claims should be struck: see D.L.H v. M.J.M., 2011 BCSC 1228 at para. 63. [22] Even the paragraphs that might suggest a battery still lack material elements and factual content as to make the allegation meaningless: Latkin v. Vancouver (City), 2013 BCSC 2270 at para. 15. [23] Nor is there relief connected to the allegations, e.g., as to the gun licence, passport, or relief from garnishment. [24] The plaintiff's legal basis was said to be based on ss.6 and 7 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, Part I of the Constitution Act, 1982, being Schedule B to the Canada Act 1982 (U.K.), 1982, c. 11, "precedents established in the Zehaf-Bibeau case", and other precedents and legislation stated in the claim, but seem unrelated to the other paragraphs in the claim. [25] Counsel noted that if a civil claim is made against the RCMP, the proper defendant is the Minister of Public Safety and Solicitor General. Civil liability can be found against an RCMP officer acting in the course of their duty and the federal Crown: see the Police Act ss. 11, and 14(2), and the Crown Liability and Proceedings Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-50. [26] However, the pleading does not identify a federal crown servant by name nor plead material facts to support a reasonable cause of action against the federal servant. [27] As to the application against the CRA regarding his student loans, no material facts are alleged. The orders sought against the RCMP, e.g., provide a list of the RCMP members, fails on the basis that the court does not order document creation: see Eichhorst v. Eichhorst, 2010 BCSC 1555 at para. 47. Nor can the court order the RCMP lay charges. It is noted that power lies with crown counsel. The City of Vancouver [28] The City of Vancouver submitted that the Notice of Claim is a series of unintelligible conclusory statements against the police and others for wrongful actions (drugging, a feigned marriage, theft, murder, attempted murder, assault, home invasion and conspiracy). [29] While allegations of fact are presumed true, it is clear that allegations based on assumptions and speculation are not: see Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083 at para. 19. [30] Further, the Notice of Claim fails to specify issues of fact or law. Rather than providing concise statements of material fact, the wording is conclusory, convoluted, confusing, and the lack of specifics makes it impossible for the City to respond or for the court to regulate the trial process: Stoneman v. Denman Island Local Trust Committee, 2010 BCSC 636 at paras. 27 and 29. [31] The City notes in the Notice of Claim at part one, paragraph 5, the allegation that two former chief constables restricted "filing criminal charges against [Watamaniuk's] assailants and perpetrated additional crimes related to the administration of justice"; at paragraph 21 the Vancouver Police stalk and harass the plaintiff, "each time stealing his keys, copying the same and using them to steal from the plaintiff"; and at part two, paragraph five, Mr. Watamaniuk alleges the police should be held liable for "the current value of the property stolen by the whore O ". [32] The relief sought in the Notice of Claim regarding the City is (1) the Vancouver Police accept criminal charges against (several persons) and they be "made to stand trial in Canada"; (2) that O's clients (members of the City Police) be charged for crimes relating to the administration of justice; (3) the City annul Watamaniuk's marriage; (4) the City be held liable for property stolen by X; (5) that the City issue an injunction against the whores and VPD clients; and (6) the whores provide their client lists. [33] Other orders were sought against various non-parties such as the Hotel Vancouver, a law firm, various individuals, Simon Fraser University and ICBC. [34] The City agreed crown counsel enjoys the sole discretion to bring charges. As to his marriage, that would be governed by the Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp) and such a claim should be commenced in a civil family proceeding. [35] Further the City described the plaintiff's claim as not advancing any claims known to law, that they were argumentative, speculative, lacking specific material facts, offensive, and inflammatory. Counsel noted a myriad of inflammatory allegations against women, a doctor, a dentist, a lawyer, and fraternity members, none of whom were parties: see Strata Plan 3259 v. Sze Hang Holding Inc., 2015 BCCA 424. In oral submission reference was made to Madam Justice Garson's description of Ms. Jensen's claim in Jensen v. Jackman, 2010 BCCA 6 at para. 26: 26 The precise nature of the claims Ms. Jensen advances is unclear. Her proposed statements of claim are, as I have already said, prolix. It is not for the Court to articulate for a litigant, from such materials, a comprehensible and legally cognizable cause of action. Pleadings must enable the court, within a reasonable time in review of the pleading, to find in those pleadings the cause of action, and the material facts upon which the cause of action is based. Ms. Jensen's proposed pleadings are an abuse of the court's process because they do not meet these minimum requirements of pleadings. In saying this, I recognize that Ms. Jensen is self-represented and may be entitled to some latitude in meeting the requirements of pleadings, but where, as here, the pleadings do not satisfy the function of pleadings at all, they are an abuse of the process of the court. Discussion [36] Mr. Watamaniuk is self-represented. On a reading of his claim, it is evident he has not considered the Supreme Courts Rules requirements of a claim in the British Columbia civil courts, nor the laws of limitation, i.e., that the passage of time may cause a claim to expire: the Limitation Act, S.B.C. 2012 c.13. The Supreme Court Civil Rules, [the Rules] R. 3-1(2) reads; (2) A notice of civil claim must do the following: (a) set out a concise statement of the material facts giving rise to the claim; (b) set out the relief sought by the plaintiff against each named defendant; (c) set out a concise summary of the legal basis for the relief sought; (d) set out the proposed place of trial; (e) if the plaintiff sues or a defendant is sued in a representative capacity, show in what capacity the plaintiff sues or the defendant is sued; (f) provide the data collection information required in the appendix to the form; (g) otherwise comply with Rule 3-7. [37] It is not intended that evidence be pleaded. Rather, pleadings must be summary in nature, setting out the material facts that give rise to the claim, counter claim or defence, or relate to matters raised by the claim. Material facts are those essential to formulate each cause of action or defence: see Johnson Estates v. Johnson, 2016 BCSC 1388 at para. 43-46, and Homalco Indian Band v. British Columbia, [1998] 25 C.P.C. (4th) 107 at p. 110. [38] Rule 9-5 requires pleadings disclose a cause of action and not be unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, nor prejudice, embarrass or delay a fair trial, see the Supreme Court of Canada in Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959, and Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, [1999] 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 at para. 47, where the court held that: Irrelevancy and embarrassment are both established when pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is being pleaded: Gittings v. Caneco Audio-Publishers Inc. (1987), 17 B.C.L.R. (2d) 38 (B.C.S.C.). An "embarrassing" and "scandalous" pleading is one that is so irrelevant that it will involve the parties in useless expense and will prejudice the trial of the action by involving them in a dispute apart from the issues: Keddie v. Dumas Hotels Ltd. (1985), 62 B.C.L.R. 145 at 147 (B.C.C.A.). An allegation which is scandalous will not be struck if it is relevant to the proceedings. It will only be struck if irrelevant as well as scandalous: College of Dental Surgeons of B.C. v. Cleland (1968), 66 W.W.R. 499 (B.C.C.A.). A pleading is "unnecessary" or "vexatious" if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or does not advance any claim known in law: Strauts v. Harrigan, [1992] B.C.J. No. 86 (Q.L.) (B.C.S.C.). A pleading that is superfluous will not be struck out if it is not necessarily unnecessary or otherwise objectionable: Lutz v. Canadian Puget Sound Lumber and Timber Co. (1920), 28 B.C.R 39 (C.A.). A pleading is "frivolous" if it is obviously unsustainable, not in the sense that it lacks an evidentiary basis, but because of the doctrine of estoppel. [39] The intent of the Rules is that the claimant's pleadings disclose to a defendant the facts giving rise to the claim against the defendant so he may sensibly respond. [40] Collectively, the defendants argue the alleged claim is factually unclear. Various people are named in the pleading but not named as defendants. Precise dates and locations are lacking. To a lawyer knowing alleged torts are usually governed by a six year limitation, many of the allegations would seem to be barred: see the Limitation Act. [41] Criminal charges in British Columbia are left to the prosecutor's discretion, and private charges are also governed by prosecutorial supervision: see the Crown Counsel Act. [42] As well, relief is sought against non-parties, e.g., against the College of Physicians and Surgeons and the Law Society. Orders are sought for transcripts which are obtained usually by request. The suggestion that the plaintiff requires an x-ray can be obtained by him seeing his doctor and getting a doctor's requisition. As to the plaintiff's student loan and garnishment proceedings, there are insufficient pleadings, and the Rules require that if a non-party is to be effected by court action, notice must be given to the non-party: see R. 7-1(18). [43] A reading of the plaintiff's claim, and the application materials he has filed, supports the arguments of counsel for the defendants. [44] Mr. Watamaniuk's claim is a collection of alleged assaults starting in the 1980s at his university fraternity, and continued forms of harassment to the present day. In terms of pleadings giving rise to a cause of action, in many aspects it lacks material facts of any specificity in terms of location, date or other particulars. In many aspects, it lacks proper legal foundation. It is a collage of alleged events occurring over decades. There is use of inappropriate language, and inferences of racism. The mish-mash of relief sought makes any possibility of revising and amending Mr. Watamaniuk's claim impossible. There are claims that on their face would be time-barred, and would fall. There are claims against non-parties that would fall. There are claims that cannot succeed such as the exercise of prosecutorial discretion that must fall. Claims against the parties named have no realistic chance of success on the face of the pleadings. It is not for the court to rewrite Mr. Watamaniuk's claim. His Notice of Claim is, with its scatter gun allegations, confusing and prolix. I find the pleadings irrelevant, scandalous and embarrassing and thus contrary to Rule 9-5 (1)(b) and (c). [45] In sum then, I order the plaintiff's claim be struck, the plaintiff's application is dismissed; the defendants' applications are allowed and the defendants are entitled to their costs. "Crawford J."
