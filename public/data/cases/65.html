[3] The claimants fear racist and Hungarian guards in Hungary. They are targeted because they are Jewish in a predominantly Christian state. [4] The principal claimant XXXX XXXX XXXXXX was threatened, watched and physically attacked, on account of his faith. He was called filthy names and demeaned. [5] The claimant turned to the police on repeated occasions. Since there was a lack of evidence, they could not help him. [6] The principal claimant was threatened at work. He was spat upon. He was threatened with serious bodily injury if he were to report this to the police. [7] A Star of David was painted on the wall where he lived. Later it was carved into. [8] The persons the claimants fear are ordinary Hungarians and skinhead groups. [9] The principal claimant was fired from a job after a customer told his employer that he had given work to a filthy Jew. [10] The principal claimant was hit by something hard on his way home from work. The persons called him a dirty Jew and threatened to cut off his private parts. His family would be gassed. [11] Another time cigarettes were put out on his hand. [12] The principal claimant has suffered psychological abuse. He has received for his stress and panic disorder. He has suicidal thoughts. [13] Even in Budapest he was abused. Rocks were thrown through the window at him. He was attacked with flammable liquids. He was injured on the upper body and arms as a result. [14] The claimant marched against fascism. Those who marched were attacked by racists in plain view of the police. DETERMINATION [15] I find the claimants are not Convention refugees in that they do not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Hungary. I also find that the claimants are not persons in need of protection in that their removal to Hungary would not subject them personally to a risk to their lives, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, and there are no substantial grounds to believe that their removal to Hungary will subject them personally to a danger of torture. ANALYSIS [16] Your identity as nationals of Hungary is established by your testimony and copies of your passports. [17] I have considered whether a viable internal flight alternative exists for you, as this issue was raised with you both before and at the hearing. On the evidence before me, I find there is no serious possibility that you would be persecuted or that you would be subjected, on a balance of probabilities, to a danger of torture, or a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment in Budapest, for the following reasons. [18] The documents submitted in this case, including those in Exhibit 6 that were submitted today, indicate there is rising anti-Semitism in Hungary.3 Anti-Semitism is increasing in Hungary. This is however, only one part of the analysis of whether the claimants have a well-founded fear of persecution if they are perceived to be Jewish, or whether they face an objective fear of harm in Hungary and especially in Budapest pursuant to section 96 of the Act. [19] The United States Department of State report and its Inter-Religious Freedom report, both in the NDP, summarize well all the documents contained in the documentary evidence. They indicate that the government of Hungary generally respected religious freedom and practice. There are increased reports of societal abuses or discrimination based on religious affiliation, belief or practice, suggesting an increase of anti-semitic rhetoric in a climate of political friction and economic uncertainty. Extremist groups grew in size and number, staging anti-Semitic public events, and a far right-wing political party, Jobbik, grew in popularity. Jobbik is an openly anti-semitic party with anti-semitic positions. [20] At the same time, the government and politicians from all parliamentary parties continue in their efforts to combat anti-Semitism by speaking out against extremism and social intolerance. The government has distanced itself from the Jobbik party. It describes Jobbik as unacceptable, extremist, aggressive and anti-democratic.4 [21] On April 19, 2009, more than 10,000 persons, including the prime minister and high-level politicians from every parliamentary party, participated in a march of a living torchlight procession commemorating the 65 anniversary of the Hungarian holocaust. Officials go to annual holocaust remembrance days in Hungary and so forth. The prime minister, senior government officials and representatives of other parties routinely counter various far-right demonstrations by participating in demonstrations supporting tolerance and religious freedom.5 [22] Now, looking at anti-Semitic acts in Hungary and the government's response to them, again I am referring to the Religious Freedom report, what has been done to Jews or those who say they are Jews in the last few years, there were several incidents where public Jewish places were defaced, including putting pig's feet on a holocaust sculpture and demonstrations denying the holocaust. At several of the events where there were anti-Jewish statements, police ordered investigations of the events, as it is illegal to have hate speech which could foment attacks against Jews in Hungary. [23] On December 23, 2008, neo-Nazi protestors disrupted a Hanukkah celebration in Budapest by shouting anti-semitic epithets not far from the outdoor ceremony. Another example, far extremists threw acid and animal feces at patrons at the Budapest Jewish Theatre on September 28, 2008. There has been a lot of vandalism or disruption of Jewish and Christian property in the country as a whole. [24] In its entire report issued on October 26, 2009, the Inter-Religious report does not list one Jew who was attacked, unless that person was with many others in organized Jewish communal life. [25] I also examined closely the March 11, 2010, United States Department of State Report.6 It stated that anti-semitism has not increased over the last year. There was one report on June 30, 2009, where three men beat a 27-year-old Jewish man after asking if he was a Jew. The prime minister asked the minister of justice and law enforcement to supervise and accelerate the investigation and to present proposals on how to avoid similar incidents. On December 23, the police arrested the two persons, one age 16 and one 18, in connection with that attack. The prime minister has appealed to the police for increased vigilance regarding racist attacks against Jews and other minorities. [26] In your evidence in Exhibit 5, I found one incident where individuals were attacked outside a Jewish communal setting. On March 30, 2010, unknown persons threw stones into an apartment window in Budapest where Jews were celebrating Passover. Police are investigating this incident.7 [27] On page 69 of Exhibit 5, it states that violence against individuals is rare, although hatred of Jews is present in Hungary. [28] Thus I find, in looking over all the documents submitted in this case, that anti-semitism is present in Hungary and growing in Hungary. There is a lot of anti-Semitic rhetoric of Jobbik and individuals. But violence is extremely rare against individuals, and where there is a risk or more than a possibility of risk of harm this takes place in public Jewish settings such as memorials, synagogues, et cetera. [29] The principal claimant, Mr. XXXX XXXX XXXXXX, claims many incidents of violence in XXXX. This may be the case, but it is evidence that is completely at odds with the country documentation as to anti-Semitic acts toward individuals. So I find that there may be other reasons for why the claimant was attacked. But be that as it may, I find, based on the objective evidence in this case, the claimants would not be at serious risk of persecution or harm in Budapest, for the following reasons. [30] The claimants in their lives almost never attend public Jewish events and ceremonies. They practice together, alone. In Canada, where they do not have any fear, in the almost two years they have been here, they have never set foot in a synagogue or gone to any other event in the Jewish community in these two years. Their only public forays have been to a messianic church8 over the last two months. The claimants say they did not understand that the messianic church or synagogue was a Christian place. The principal claimant understands English well, and I do find it astounding that the only "Jewish" place that the claimants have attended in the last two years in Canada is a place that believes in Jesus, in Jesus' resurrection and so forth. So as I am finding, the claimants do not go to Jewish communal places, even when they can do so, without any fear in Canada. [31] The only incidents of attacks on individual persons in Budapest are being fully investigated by the police. So not only are these attacks rare, there is state protection in Budapest, as well. The only evidence is that the government takes these attacks very seriously. And so for this reason, I do not find that the claimants would face persecution or an objective risk of harm in Budapest pursuant to sections 96 and 97. There is state protection as indicated besides very few persecutory acts toward individual persons in Budapest. [32] Having considered the conditions of Budapest and all the circumstances in this case, including those particular to you, I find it is not objectively unreasonable for you to seek refuge in Budapest. You have worked in various occupations over the years and all of those types of work are available in Budapest, as in all large cities. It is not objectively unreasonable for you to live there. Conclusion [33] And for the foregoing reasons, I conclude that you XXXX XXXX XXXXXX and XXXX XXXXXX, are not Convention refugees and not persons in need of protection, and I therefore reject your claims.
