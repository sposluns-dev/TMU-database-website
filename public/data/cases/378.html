McDermott Tribunal Counsel for the persons who are the subject of the appeal Yasin A Razak Conseil des personnes en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] I dismiss this appeal. [2] XXXX XXXX (Principal Appellant) and XXXX XXXX (Associate Appellant) are common-law partners and citizens of Poland. The Appellants fear persecution in Poland because of their Roma ethnicity. The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) found that the Appellants had not rebutted the presumption that adequate state protection exists for them in Poland. The Appellants argue that the RPD unfairly drew negative inferences against the Principal Appellant and made errors and unreasonable findings with regards to the issue of state protection. The Minister intervened at the RPD, but has not done so here. [3] The Appellants are relying on new evidence but have not requested an oral hearing. [4] The determinative issue in the appeal continues to be state protection. DECISION [5] I dismiss the appeal. The RPD was correct in finding that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. NEW EVIDENCE The evidence presented by the Appellants is rejected [6] According to the Law,1 I can only accept evidence that: (i) arose after the RPD decision; or (ii) was not reasonably available at the time of the decision; or (iii) that the Appellants could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to bring to the RPD before the decision. [7] If the evidence meets one or more of these requirements, I must decide if the evidence is new, credible and relevant (the Singh/Raza factors) before I can accept it.2 Balkaninsight.com article "Threats to Human Rights Growing in Central, Southeast Europe - HRW | Balkan Insight3 [8] This article is dated January 13, 2022, which is after the RPD decision and therefore meets the statutory requirement. Turning to the Singh/Raza factors, although the article is credible, the discussion regarding Poland is limited to control over the judiciary, smearing journalists and human right's activists critical of the government and migrants entering Poland. None of these are applicable to the Appellants. I find that the article is not relevant in the appeal, and I do not accept it into evidence. Associated Press article "Tusk says hacking marks crisis of democracy in Poland"4 [9] This article is dated December 28, 2021, which is after the RPD decision and therefore meets the statutory requirement. Turning to the Singh/Raza factors, the article is credible. However, the article deals with accusations of surveillance of three persons associated an opposition party. I find that the Appellants have failed to demonstrate that the article is relevant in the appeal, and I do not accept it into evidence. Nytimes.com article "Opinion | Poland Could Be the Future of Europe"5 [10] This article is dated December 29, 2021, which is after the RPD decision and therefore meets the statutory requirement. Turning to the Singh/Raza factors, the article is the opinion of the two authors and describes the Polish government's attempts to exert Polish sovereignty at the expense of (European) continental cohesion. While the document may be credible, there is no mention of the treatment of Roma or the treatment of Polish citizens by the police. I find that Appellants have failed to establish that the article is relevant in the appeal, and I do not accept it into evidence. The Week.com article "Le Pen, Orban and other European populist leaders meet in Warsaw to discuss EU concerns"6 [11] This article is dated December 4, 2021, which is after the RPD decision and therefore meets the statutory requirements. Turning to the Singh/Raza factors, I have no reason to doubt the credibility of the article. However, the article makes no mention of the situation for Roma in Poland. I find that the Appellants have failed to establish that the article is relevant in the appeal, and I do not accept it into evidence. US News article: "Poland's Authorities Back Far-Right March Banned by Courts7; FAIR.org article: "Cable News' No.1 Host Flirts With Fascism"8; jta.org article: "Death to Jews, Polish nationalists shout at rally while burning book about Jews in Poland"9; Brookings.edu article: "When security in Europe conflicts with democracy in Poland"10; The Times of Israel article: "Polish president denounces antisemitism at nationalist rally as 'treason'"11; CBS17.com article: "Poland arrests 3 in connection to antisemitic demonstration"12; nbcnews.com article: "How the European Union is struggling to contain its 'illiberal' democracies"13 [12] These articles all pre-date the RPD decision and all were reasonable available prior to the RPD decision. The Appellants argue that they could not reasonably have been expected to have provided the documents since the deadline to provide documents in support of their claim had passed, the claims had been heard, and the matter adjourned while the claimants awaited a decision. [13] With respect, there is no merit to the Appellants' argument. It is true that the RPD will frequently allow claimants to submit additional documents post-hearing and will usually set a deadline for their submission. By setting a deadline, the RPD is agreeing that it will not finalize its decision until that deadline has passed. In the case at bar, there was nothing mentioned at the second hearing about submitting additional documents, although the RPD did state he would provide a written decision, "...hopefully... in around two or three weeks." However, irrespective of whether the RPD agrees in advance, RPD Rule 4314 allows claimants to make an application to provide additional documents to the RPD at any time after the RPD hearing is completed but before the RPD issues its decision. The Appellants did not do this. I understand that after three weeks had passed, the Appellants would reasonably have assumed that the RPD decision was imminent, however this does not change the fact that Rule 43 would have allowed them to submit these additional documents to the RPD prior to its decision. I find that the Appellants have not established that they could not reasonably have been expected to have submitted the documents to the RPD prior to its decision and therefore the articles do not meet the statutory requirement. [14] Even if the articles did meet the statutory requirement, they all fail the Singh/Raza factor of relevance. None of the articles make any reference to the situation for Roma in Poland or of how police in Poland treat complaints from Roma. [15] I do not accept the articles into evidence. ANALYSIS [16] My role is to look at all the evidence and decide if the RPD made the correct decision.15 The credibility findings of the RPD are not relevant in the appeal [17] Although the Appellants agree that the credibility findings made by the RPD are not relevant or determinative to the issue of state protection, they do argue that the RPD was unreasonable in drawing negative inferences against the Principal Appellant. [18] As the determinative issue in the appeal is state protection, it is not necessary for me to address the Appellants' arguments regarding credibility. The RPD did not err in its analysis of state protection [19] The RPD found that the Appellants have not rebutted the presumption of adequate state protection existing for them in Poland and that this was determinative of their claim. The Appellants argue that the RPD placed improper emphasis on the efforts of the Polish government, appeared to downplay the situation for Roma in Polish society and was selective in its reading of the objective country condition evidence. Relevant jurisprudence on state protection [20] In Ruszo16, at paragraph 29, the Federal Court stated: "[29] It is settled law that absent a complete breakdown of state apparatus, it should be presumed that a state is capable of protecting its citizens. Moreover, "[t]he more democratic the state's institutions, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all the courses of action open to him or her. However, in all cases to which the presumption applies, the burden is upon an applicant for refugee protection to demonstrate, with clear and convincing evidence, the state's inability to provide adequate protection. This burden must be discharged on a balance of probabilities." (internal references omitted) [21] In Poczkodi17, at paragraph 35, the Federal Court outlined the principles regarding state protection from the prevailing jurisprudence as follows: "[35] ...that there is a presumption of adequate state protection, the onus rests on the refugee claimant to provide clear and convincing evidence that state protection is not adequate for them, that state protection need not be perfect, that local failures alone do not demonstrate inadequate state protection, and that a claimant must make reasonable efforts to access state protection - which are proportional to the level of democracy in the state before seeking the protection of another country." The RPD erred in establishing the level of burden the Appellants faced in rebutting the presumption of state protection [22] The RPD found that the Appellants had a high burden to show that they would not be able to receive state protection. The Appellants argue that the RPD focussed on the fact that Poland is a "functioning democracy". [23] There is some merit to the Appellants' argument. While it is clear from Ruszo and Poczkodi that the level of democracy is relevant to the analysis of state protection, it is not the sole factor to be considered. There is some objective evidence of Polish police misconduct. Although in most cases, authorities took action against the police officers involved, I can understand why some people might be reluctant to engage with the police in Poland. I therefore find that the Appellants have a medium burden to show that they would not be able to receive state protection. The RPD did not err in finding that the Appellants failed to meet their burden [24] The RPD found that the Appellants had failed to meet their burden that state protection would not be available to them in Poland. The Appellants argue that the RPD failed to regard the totality of the evidence and the special circumstances of the Appellants. [25] The RPD did not err and the Appellants' arguments must fail. The Appellants' own testimonies are that neither of them ever approached the police regarding the treatment they encountered. The Principal Appellant testified18 that he had not reported anything to the police, explaining that it would not make any difference. When the RPD asked what (information) source he had for this or whether he had any experience with this, the Principal Appellant replied no, but then stated from other people in the area. The Associate Appellant testified19 that she had never been to the police in Poland regarding the issues she faced. The Federal Court has been clear that claimants must make reasonable efforts to access state protection. Considering that the Appellants do not allege that the police are an agent of persecution and there is no evidence before me that the Appellants have had any negative interactions with the police in Poland, I find that a reasonable effort would have included at least making a complaint to the police regarding the treatment they, or their daughter, received. Their explanations, that others had told them that it would make no difference, or they would be called names, do not satisfactorily explain the Appellants' decisions not to go to the police. [26] The Appellants rely on Kozlowski20 and Dawidowicz21 in the appeal. These decisions both fault the RPD for not taking into account the circumstances of the claimants. However, I find both cases distinguishable as the claimants had either alleged that the police were an agent of persecution or had made complaints to the police without result. In the case at bar, neither Appellant has alleged that the police are an agent of persecution or ever approached the police for protection. [27] The Appellants also rely on a Refugee Appeal Division decision22 from 2019. However, that case is also distinguishable as the claimants in that decision had made at least one report to the police, regarding an arson. [28] As part of my independent assessment, I reviewed the National Documentation Package for Poland, both the one available to the RPD and the most recent version, dated October 29, 2021. There are three references to Roma interactions with the police in Poland. The first23 refers to an August 2016 incident where a Roma man was abducted and robbed by police officers. The second24 is a statement that Roma are subjected to ethnic profiling by law enforcement officers. The third25 is a report that law enforcement authorities in Poland often treat Roma as thieves and criminals. I find that these instances are local failures and are not sufficient to demonstrate inadequate state protection. [29] I find that the Appellants have failed to demonstrate, with clear and convincing evidence, the state's inability to provide adequate protection for them in Poland, on a balance of probabilities. CONCLUSION [30] I dismiss the appeal and confirm the decision of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. (signed) "Charles A.
