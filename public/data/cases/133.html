Positions of the Parties [20] The position of Bonaparte and Chief Antoine on this application is that the allegations made against them do not sound in defamation. Defamation is the only cause of action that is disclosed in the notice of civil claim, but the plaintiffs do not plead that Bonaparte or Chief Antoine uttered defamatory words or otherwise made defamatory statements. [21] Bonaparte and Chief Antoine assert that no material fact is pleaded that could result in a finding that, even if proven as alleged, Bonaparte or Chief Antoine defamed the plaintiffs. [22] Bonaparte and Chief Antoine argue that the plaintiffs have not pleaded a recognized cause of action against them. There is no action of failing to stop another in the absence of a legal duty to do so. The law of defamation, Bonaparte and Chief Antoine argue, simply does not go that far. [23] They argue that the function of pleadings is to clearly define the issues of fact and law to be determined by the court, but the plaintiffs have failed to do that. They rely upon Homalco Indian Band v. British Columbia (1998), 25 C.P.C. (4th) 107, [1998] B.C.J. No. 2703 (S.C.); Troup v. McPherson (1965), 53 W.W.R. 37, [1965] B.C.J. No. 19 (S.C.); Delaney & Friends Cartoon Productions Ltd. v. Radical Entertainment Inc., 2005 BCSC 371 [Delaney]. Law Striking Pleadings [33] Rule 9-5(1) provides as follows: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, [ ] and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [34] Any material fact is essential to formulate a cause of action because if a material fact is omitted, a cause of action is not effectively pleaded: Delaney at para. 9; Micka v. Oliver & District Community Economic Development Society, 2008 BCSC 1623 at para. 9. [35] On an application brought pursuant to Rule 9-5(1), the court is to consider the case on the basis that the facts alleged are true. That is, this inquiry does not engage any of the evidence adduced by the parties, and the case is taken at its highest for the plaintiff: Canadian Bar Assn. v. British Columbia, 2008 BCCA 92 at para. 37, leave to appeal ref'd, [2008] S.C.C.A. No. 185. [36] The test for striking out a notice of civil claim for disclosing no reasonable cause of action is "whether it is plain and obvious, assuming the facts pleaded to be true, that the pleading discloses no reasonable cause of action": Grosz v. Royal Trust Corporation of Canada, 2020 BCSC 128 at para. 58. [37] Where facts are pleaded on assumptions or speculation that are incapable of proof, they are not assumed to be true. The plaintiff "must plead assertions of fact which would establish the essential elements of a successful claim if proven": Grosz at para. 59.The principles governing an application to strike were discussed by the Supreme Court of Canada in Odhavji Estate v. Woodhouse, 2003 SCC 69: [15] An excellent statement of the test for striking out a claim under such provisions is that set out by Wilson J. in Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc. , [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959, at p. 980: . . . assuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it "plain and obvious" that the plaintiff's statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action? As in England, if there is a chance that the plaintiff might succeed, then the plaintiff should not be "driven from the judgment seat". Neither the length and complexity of the issues, the novelty of the cause of action, nor the potential for the defendant to present a strong defence should prevent the plaintiff from proceeding with his or her case. Only if the action is certain to fail because it contains a radical defect . . . should the relevant portions of a plaintiff's statement of claim be struck out . . . The test is a stringent one. The facts are to be taken as pleaded. When so taken, the question that must then be determined is whether there it is "plain and obvious" that the action must fail. It is only if the statement of claim is certain to fail because it contains a "radical defect" that the plaintiff should be driven from the judgment. See also Attorney General of Canada v. Inuit Tapirisat of Canada, [1980] 2 S.C.R. 735. [38] The test on an application under Rule 9-5(1) is whether it is plain and obvious that the reasonable cause of action has been pleaded: Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959: [33] Thus, the test in Canada governing the application of provisions like Rule 19(24)(a) [now 9-5(1)] of the British Columbia Rules of Court is the same as the one that governs an application under R.S.C.O. 18, r. 19: assuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it "plain and obvious" that the plaintiff's statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action? [ ] [39] Rule 9-5 codifies the court's power under its inherent jurisdiction to stay matters where the notice of civil claim discloses no reasonable cause of action: Hunt. [40] A leading case on Rule 9-5(1) is R. v. Imperial tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42 [Imperial Tobacco], propositions from which were summarized in Rose v. British Columbia Life & Casualty Company, 2012 BCSC 1296: [3] The recent and leading case of R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada, 2011 SCC 42, [2011] 3 S.C.R. 45, confirmed the following propositions in relation to the former Rule 19(24)(a): (i) A claim will only be struck if it is plain and obvious, assuming the facts pleaded to be true, that the pleading discloses no reasonable cause of action (at para. 17); (ii) The power to strike claims that have no reasonable prospect of success promotes efficiency in the conduct of the litigation and contributes to more effective and fair litigation (at para. 19); and (iii) The motion to strike is a tool that must be used with care, as the law is not static and actions previously were deemed hopeless may in the future succeed. Therefore, it is not determinative that the law has not yet recognized the particular claim. In its analysis the court must be generous and err on the side of permitting a novel but arguable claim to proceed to trial (at para. 21). [41] A claim will only be struck if it is plain and obvious, assuming that the facts pleaded are true, that the pleading discloses no reasonable cause of action: Hunt at 980; Imperial Tobacco at para. 17. Another way of putting the test is "that the claim has no reasonable prospect of success": Imperial Tobacco at para. 17. [42] In Imperial Tobacco, the Court noted that "[t]he power to strike out claims that have no reasonable prospect of success is a valuable housekeeping measure essential to effective and fair litigation. It unclutters the proceedings, weeding out the hopeless claims and ensuring that those that have some chance of success go on to trial": para. 19. [43] The Court also noted in Imperial Tobacco that "the motion to strike is a tool that must be used with care. The law is not static and unchanging": para. 21. Consequently, it is not determinative that the law has not yet recognized a particular claim. [44] In Imperial Tobacco, the Court held the following in relation to the third proposition: [21] [ ] The law is not static and unchanging. Actions that yesterday were deemed hopeless made tomorrow succeed. [ ] The history of our law reveals that often new developments in the law surface on motions to strike or similar preliminary motions, like the one at issue in Donoghue v. Stevenson, [[1932] A.C. 562]. Therefore, on a motion to strike, it is not determinative that the law has not yet recognized the particular claim. The court must rather ask whether, assuming the facts pleaded are true, there is a reasonable prospect that the claim will succeed. The approach must be generous and err on the side of permitting a novel but arguable claim to proceed to trial. [45] In Dempsey et al. v. Envision Credit Union et al., 2006 BCSC 750 at para. 17, the Court helpfully summarized general propositions from the authorities on when pleadings will be struck: [17] In summary, a pleading will be struck out if: (a) the pleadings are unintelligible, confusing and difficult to understand (Citizens for Foreign aid Reform [Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 36 C.P.C. (4th) 266 (B.C.S.C.)]); (b) the pleadings do not establish a cause of action and do not advance a claim known in law (Citizens for Foreign aid Reform, supra); (c) the pleadings are without substance in that they are groundless, fanciful and trifle with the Court's time (Borsato v. Basra, [[2000] B.C.J. No. 84 [S.C.]); (d) the pleadings are not bona fides, are oppressive and are designed to cause the Defendants anxiety, trouble and expense (Borsato v. Basra, supra); (e) the action is brought for an improper purpose, particularly the harassment and oppression of the Defendants (Ebrahim v. Ebrahim, 2002 BCSC 466). [46] There is no question that striking pleadings is draconian. For that reason, the court must carefully consider whether the pleading can be preserved by amendment: Tenshi Seafood Ltd. v. Ocean Run Seafood Canada Ltd., 2021 BCSC 2075 [Tenshi]. In Tenshi, Justice Forth held: [16] The plaintiff concedes that, prior to going to trial, it will be required to make amendments to the notice of civil claim to provide proper notice to the defendants of the claim that they must meet. The plaintiff suggests that this is a situation where it is not able to provide full particulars of the allegations prior to discovery. I accept that the discovery process may provide more particulars of the allegations, but I do not accept that the plaintiff has included all reasonably available particulars of the defamation. The first step is that the plaintiff has to reveal all the particulars within its knowledge: The Catalyst Capital Group Inc. v. Veritas Investment Research Corporation, 2017 ONCA 85 at para. 25. [17] I will heed the direction of the Court of Appeal that "when there is an attack on a pleading as showing no reasonable claim or defence the court is well advised to first consider whether the pleading can be preserved by amendment": International Taoist Church of Canada v. Ching Chung Taoist Association of Hong Kong Limited, 2011 BCCA 149 at para. 28. [47] Justice Forth in Tenshi went on to hold as follows: [21] The striking of a pleading is a draconian remedy: Shoolestani v. Ichikawa, 2017 BCSC 1589 at para. 55. However, the plaintiff cannot evade its responsibility to prepare and file a proper pleading. If it fails to do so, the ultimate remedy is the striking of the pleading. Defamation [48] The law is clear that a plaintiff is required to set out the exact alleged defamatory words in pleadings: Central Minera Corp. v. Lavarak, 2001 BCSC 349 at para. 12. A plaintiff cannot rely on a vague general statement of the defamatory words: Fraser v. Central Ready-Mic Ltd., 1999 CanLII 2066, [1999] B.C.J. No. 2061 (QL) (B.C.S.C.), at para. 12. [49] The law of defamation protects a person's reputation from defamatory falsehoods. A publication is considered defamatory if it tends to lower the plaintiff's reputation in the estimation of reasonable persons in the community. In Weaver v. Corcoran, 2017 BCCA 160 at para. 62, the Court of Appeal articulated the function of the law of defamation as follows: [62] The function of defamation law is to protect and vindicate reputation from harm that is unjustified. A good reputation fosters one's sense of self-worth and, as an aspect of personality, is related to the innate worthiness and dignity of the individual, an underlying value of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. Once tarnished, good repute is hard to regain, with sometimes devastating consequences, particularly in a professional context. However, its protection must be balanced and reconciled with the Charter guarantee of freedom of expression, a recognised pillar of modern democracy. [ ] [Citations omitted.] [50] To succeed on a defamation claim, a plaintiff must prove, on a balance of probabilities, that the impugned words: (1) were defamatory; (2) referred to the plaintiff; and (3) were published, in that they were communicated to at least one person aside from the plaintiff: Weaver at para. 70; Grant v. Torstar Corp., 2009 SCC 61 at para. 28. Where the plaintiff establishes these elements, falsity and damage are presumed and the onus shifts to the defendant to advance a defence in order to escape liability. [51] To prove the third element, publication, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant has, by any act, conveyed the defamatory meaning concerning the plaintiff to a third party, who has received it: Weaver at para. 86. In Weaver, our Court of Appeal commented that "entirely passive acts" are not "publication": [86] Finally, the plaintiff must prove publication. It must be established that the defendant has, by any act, conveyed the defamatory meaning concerning the plaintiff to a third party, who has received it. Traditionally, any act which transferred defamatory information to a third person was considered publication. However, in the modern age this has been modified to exclude entirely passive acts, such as some forms of referencing or hyperlinking of defamatory material. Where the acts at issue merely transmit information in a content-neutral way, without expression, adoption or endorsement, they are generally not considered publication: Crookes at paras. 16, 21, 30, 48. [Emphasis added.] [52] In Crookes v. Wikimedia Foundation Inc., 2011 SCC 47, although the Court wrote three separate concurring opinions, all justices of the Supreme Court of Canada agreed that only deliberate acts can lead to liability: Pritchard v. Van Nes, 2016 BCSC 686 at para. 93. After reviewing the reasons of Justice Deschamps in Crookes, Justice Saunders in Pritchard concluded that "a deliberate action can encompass failure to act to remove the defamatory material once actual knowledge or constructive knowledge has been made out": at para. 98. [53] In Pritchard, after a thorough review of decisions dealing with third party liability in defamation for comments made on one's social media account, such as Facebook or another internet-based platform, Saunders J. formulated the following test for establishing liability for third party defamatory material: (1) actual knowledge of the defamatory material posted by a third party; (2) a deliberate act that can include inaction in the face of actual knowledge; and (3) power and control over the defamatory content: Pritchard at para. 108. After these elements are established, it may be said that the defendant has adopted the third-party defamatory material as their own: Pritchard at para. 108. Discussion [54] The plaintiffs' claim for defamation against Bonaparte and Chief Antoine rests on an argument that Bonaparte and Chief Antoine were present when the alleged defamatory statements were uttered and did not intervene to stop those statements, which was also a failure of Bonaparte and Chief Antoine to abide by Bonaparte's zero-tolerance policy against harassment, violence, bullying, racism, and other forms of abuse. Due to their failure to intervene to stop the statements, the plaintiffs argue that Bonaparte and Chief Antoine are liable in defamation. [55] The question arising from this is whether accepting the plaintiffs' argument would be the kind of incremental change in the law in which a novel claim can go forward, as discussed by the Court of Appeal in BNSF Railway Company v. Teck Metals Ltd., 2016 BCCA 350 at para. 55. [56] It is plain and obvious that the claim in defamation against Bonaparte and Chief Antoine cannot succeed as a matter of law on the pleading as framed. The Court is to err on the side of permitting a novel claim to proceed at this stage. On this basis, I find that, at this stage, the claims advanced against Bonaparte and Chief Antoine have no reasonable prospect of success. [57] The law of defamation is far from imposing and creating an action for failure to act in a situation where a person has no control over the slander being made, particularly in a situation like the present case where aspects of the defamation pleaded occurred in the community. [58] Defamation clearly requires a deliberate act and, in this situation, a slander. The question remains: could a person or an entity like a Band be liable in defamation where it had no control over the person saying the slander or be legally responsible for the actions of the person making the statement? In my view, the answer is no. [59] First, I do not see how Bonaparte or Chief Antoine could be said to have "published" defamatory remarks by not intervening. This is an integral element of the tort of defamation. [60] Second, the Supreme Court of Canada has made it clear that liability for defamation requires a deliberate act. No such deliberate act is pleaded or alleged here. Although Pritchard concludes that a deliberate act can arise from a failure to remove defamatory material once actual knowledge or constructive knowledge has been made out, I fail to see how a person or entity can be said to have deliberately acted by not intervening to stop a third person from uttering defamatory statements or by not correcting the untruths. This is not akin to a failure to remove defamatory material. [61] Third, while Canadian courts have accepted some forms of third-party liability in defamation, the manner has not been set out in the pleadings as they stand. The plaintiffs have failed to demonstrate how those forms of third-party liability would apply here or could be extended to meet these circumstances. Whether the law of defamation could extend to include the kind of third-party liability contemplated here on the existing case law is tenuous. The test formulated by Saunders J. in Pritchard was based on decisions dealing with written statements posted on online forums. Counsel before me were asked in the hearing if there was caselaw to support the position being advanced in the notice of civil claim respecting the claim that no action taken by Bonaparte and Chief Antoine in respect of the slander should result in damages, but no caselaw has been provided despite the court seeking further input on the Pritchard case. [62] Having found that the claims, as framed, have no reasonable prospect of success, the question becomes whether the plaintiff should be permitted to conduct discoveries to conclusively ascertain whether defamatory statements were made. [63] The plaintiffs now assert that they require discovery to conclusively rule out whether Bonaparte and Chief Antone defamed the defendants by slander. On that basis, I will not strike the pleadings at this time. In the event a claim arises in slander against Bonaparte or Chief Antoine, the plaintiffs have leave to amend. Following discovery, the defendants can renew this application or bring a fresh application. DISPOSITION [64] The application to strike the plaintiff's claims against Bonaparte and Chief Antoine pursuant to Rule 9-5(1)(a) is dismissed pending the conclusion of the discovery process with leave for the defendants to renew any applications arising after that process. In the event a claim arises in slander against Bonaparte or Chief Antoine, the plaintiffs have leave to amend. "Maisonville J."
