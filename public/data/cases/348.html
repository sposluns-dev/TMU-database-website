Salager v. Dye & Durham Corporation

Dye & Durham Corporation, 2017 BCSC 470 Date: 20170323 Docket: S149765 Registry: Vancouver Between: Serge Salager Plaintiff And Dye & Durham Corporation Defendant Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Pearlman Reasons for Judgment Counsel for the Plaintiff: N.T. March 23, 2017 INTRODUCTION [1] By their notice of application filed December 5, 2016, the defendants Dye & Durham Corporation, OneMove Online Systems Inc. and Matthew Proud applied for a declaration that Harris and Company LLP ("Harris") is in a conflict of interest as between the defendant Dye & Durham and the plaintiff, Mr. Serge Salager; an order removing Harris as counsel of record for the plaintiff; and an interim and permanent injunction enjoining Harris and any lawyer associated with Harris from acting for the plaintiff or against Dye & Durham in relation to the subject matter of this action. [2] At the conclusion of the hearing of March 10, 2017, I dismissed the defendants' application, with reasons to follow. These are my reasons. BACKGROUND FACTS [3] The plaintiff is a businessman and the former Chief Executive Officer of OneMove Technologies Inc. [4] At the material times, the defendant Matthew Proud was the director of OneMove Technologies Inc. and OneMove Online Systems Inc. (collectively "OneMove"), and Mr. Salager's successor as Chief Executive Officer of OneMove Technologies Inc. [5] In 2014, Harris brought an action on behalf of Mr. Salager and his companies against OneMove and Mr. Proud for wrongful dismissal and breach of a stock option agreement. [6] In late August 2014, the parties settled that action by executing a mutual release and settlement agreement. [7] On December 16, 2014, the plaintiff, again represented by Harris, commenced this action against OneMove and Mr. Proud, by which he claims damages and other relief for defamation and breach of the settlement agreement. [8] For its part, OneMove has counterclaimed against Mr. Salager for damages for defamation and breach of the settlement agreement. [9] Between May 2005 and September 2015, the defendant Dye & Durham Corporation intermittently sought and received legal advice from Harris relating to employment law and human resources related matters. [10] On or about March 10, 2016, OneMove Technologies Inc. announced it had acquired 100 per cent of Dye & Durham. [11] After Harris learned of the acquisition, it determined that it would no longer provide legal advice to Dye & Durham, but did not communicate that decision to Dye & Durham until October 2016. [12] On July 1, 2016, OneMove Technologies Inc. amalgamated with Dye & Durham. [13] On October 18, 2016, the defendants' solicitors wrote to Harris, stating they were instructed that Dye & Durham was a current employment law client of Harris and asserting that firm was in a conflict of interest by virtue of the amalgamation, and that Dye & Durham did not consent to Harris's continued representation of the plaintiff in this action. [14] On October 28, 2016, Mr. Geoffrey Litherland of Harris wrote to Dye & Durham as follows: Further to our call today, as discussed, I do not believe we have ever had a written retainer agreement with Dye & Durham. However, as you know, we have not done any work for Dye & Durham since about September 2015, and, as I advised, once I discovered that OneMove had purchased Dye & Durham in or about March 2016, I considered that we would no longer be acting for Dye & Durham as we act for a party who has adverse in interest to OneMove. I confirm our understanding that Dye & Durham will not be using the services of Harris and Co. given these circumstances. [15] On November 10, 2016, Harris responded to the defendants' solicitor's correspondence of October 18, 2016. Harris stated that it had done no recent work for Dye & Durham, and that the firm had closed its file and had informed Dye & Durham that it was no longer a client. Harris maintained there was no conflict in its continuing representation of Mr. Salager in this action. [16] After Harris refused to terminate its representation of the plaintiff, the defendants brought this application. [17] On December 16, 2016, the Honourable Madam Justice MacNaughton ordered a stay of proceedings pending the hearing of this application. [18] On January 9, 2017, the parties consented to an order of the Registrar substituting Dye & Durham as a defendant in this action in place of OneMove Technologies. POSITIONS OF THE PARTIES The Defendants' Position [19] The defendants contend that Harris has a disqualifying conflict of interest because it had a continuing retainer with Dye & Durham from 2005 until at least October 28, 2016. The defendants say that following the amalgamation of OneMove Technologies Inc. and Dye & Durham, that conflict precluded the law firm from representing Mr. Salager in this action. [20] Alternatively, the defendants submit Harris has received confidential information from Dye & Durham during its retainer concerning matters closely related to the subject matter of this action. The defendants say that Harris, through its provision of employment and human resources related advice to Dye & Durham, has acquired confidential information concerning that defendant's internal strategy and executive decision-making. [21] The defendants argue that Harris has breached the "bright line" rule, which prohibits a law firm from concurrent representation of clients who are adverse in interest without the informed consent of both clients, and that disqualification is necessary both to prevent prejudice to the defendants and to maintain public confidence in the administration of justice. The Plaintiff's Position [22] The plaintiff submits that Harris had no continuing retainer with Dye & Durham and denies the law firm concurrently represented Mr. Salager and Dye & Durham. [23] Further, the plaintiff contends that Harris received no confidential information from Dye & Durham relevant to this action and that accordingly, there is no risk that any information acquired by Harris will be used in these proceedings to the prejudice of Dye & Durham. The plaintiff says that a reasonably informed person, apprised of the facts, would be satisfied there is no risk of the misuse of confidential information. The plaintiff submits the application should be dismissed. ISSUES [24] The following issues arise for determination on this application: (a) Did Harris concurrently represent clients who were adverse in interest without first obtaining their consent? (b) Did Harris receive confidential information attributable to its solicitor and client relationship with Dye & Durham relevant to this action, and if so, is there a risk that information will be used to the prejudice of the defendants? (c) Is there a disqualifying conflict of interest in this case? DISCUSSION AND ANALYSIS (a) Did Harris concurrently represent clients who interests are adverse? [25] In Canadian National Railway Co. v. McKercher LLP, 2013 SCC 39, [2013] 2 S.C.R. 639, McLachlin C.J., for the Court, stated the "bright line rule" at para. 27: [27] In Neil, this Court (per Binnie J.) stated that a lawyer may not represent a client in one matter while representing that client's adversary in another matter, unless both clients provide their informed consent. Binnie J. articulated the rule thus: The bright line is provided by the general rule that a lawyer may not represent one client whose interests are directly adverse to the immediate interests of another current client -- even if the two mandates are unrelated -- unless both clients consent after receiving full disclosure (and preferably independent legal advice), and the lawyer reasonably believes that he or she is able to represent each client without adversely affecting the other. [Emphasis in original; para. 29] [26] The Court emphasized, at paras. 28 and 29, that the rule expressly applies to both related and unrelated matters, and that where applicable, the bright line rule prohibits concurrent representation. [27] As the Court went on to explain at para. 32: However, Neil and Strother make it clear that the scope of the rule is not unlimited. The rule applies where the immediate legal interests of clients are directly adverse. It does not apply to condone tactical abuses. And it does not apply in circumstances where it is unreasonable to expect that the lawyer will not concurrently represent adverse parties in unrelated legal matters. The limited scope of application of the rule is illustrated by Neil and Strother. This Court found the bright line rule to be inapplicable to the facts of both of those cases, and instead examined whether there was a substantial risk of impaired representation: Neil, at para. 31; Strother, at para. 54. [28] The bright line rule does not apply in exceptional circumstances where it would be unreasonable for the client to expect that its law firm will not act against it in unrelated matters. In determining whether the client had a reasonable expectation that the firm would not act against it in unrelated matters, the court conducts a case-by-case assessment taking into account such factors as the nature of the relationship between the law firm and the client, the terms of the retainer and the types of matters involved: McKercher at para. 37. [29] Where a conflict exists, and there is a need to prevent the misuse of confidential information, disqualification will generally be the appropriate remedy: McKercher at para. 62. [30] However, to give rise to a risk of misuse, the information "must be capable of being used against the client in some tangible manner". A "bald assertion that the past relationship has provided the solicitor with access to litigation philosophy" will not suffice: McKercher at para. 54. [31] Ms. Constance Fenyo, who was the corporate operations officer for Dye & Durham when she swore her affidavit in support of the defendants' application on November 29, 2016, deposed that from approximately 2005 to the present, Dye & Durham has engaged Harris to provide legal services on matters relating to human resources and employment law. She swore that Harris has provided advice to Dye & Durham on the following matters: (a) employment matters generally; (b) employee and executive termination and disputes arising as a result thereof; (c) just cause for dismissal; (d) strategy in severance negotiations; (e) settlement and related agreements; and (f) issues relating to disability accommodation. [32] Ms. Fenyo also deposed that Dye & Durham sought advice from Harris on an intermittent basis, usually over the telephone, and that it was not uncommon for a year or more to lapse between occasions when Dye & Durham sought Harris's advice. [33] At paragraphs 6 through 9 of her affidavit, Ms. Fenyo stated: 6. Throughout the Service Period, members of D & D [Dye & Durham] have periodically phoned Geoff Litherland and other lawyers at Harris to obtain advice on HR Issues. Harris has typically provided that advice promptly, usually on a same-day and ongoing basis. 7. To the best of my knowledge, Harris has never requested a money retainer from D & D. 8. At all times during the Service Period, I have been part of the executive decision-making body at D & D. Advice provided by Harris as a component of the Legal Services has influenced significant decisions that I been involved in regarding employment matters. 9. On several occasions, I have been present during conference calls with counsel with lawyers from Harris in which open discussions relating to legal strategy in relation to HR Issues took place. [34] Ms. Fenyo also deposed that Harris's provision of legal services put lawyers at Harris in direct contact with senior employees of Dye & Durham. [35] At paragraph 11 of her affidavit, Ms. Fenyo deposed: Accordingly, it is my belief that Harris' role as a provider of the legal services has allowed Harris to access confidential information regarding matters of internal strategy and decision-making at D & D, and more specifically, the ways in which D & D's executive decision-making body approaches legal disputes as well as the tactics employed by D & D to minimize legal liability. For these reasons, it is my view that Harris' continued retainer for the plaintiff in this proceeding prejudices D & D's interest in this litigation. [36] Ms. Fenyo also acknowledged that Harris was last engaged by Dye & Durham in September 2015 to provide advice on termination procedures in order to minimize the risk of wrongful dismissal lawsuits. [37] Ms. Fenyo has recently retired from Dye & Durham. [38] Mr. Geoffrey Litherland, the partner at Harris who most frequently dealt with Dye & Durham, has sworn that Dye & Durham first contacted Harris to obtain advice relating to employment matters in May and June 2005. Mr. Litherland does not believe that he provided any advice to Dye & Durham from July 2005 until July 2011. [39] Based on his review of the firm's accounting records, Mr. Litherland identified 12 occasions between August 2011 and September 2015 when Harris provided employment related advice and rendered accounts to Dye & Durham. [40] Mr. Litherland also deposed that Harris never had a written retainer agreement with Dye & Durham; never represented Dye & Durham in any litigation; and never provided advice on litigation strategy to Dye & Durham. Further, Mr. Litherland has sworn that he is unaware of any litigation strategy or litigation philosophy held by Dye & Durham; that he has no recollection of ever meeting in person with any Dye & Durham personnel in order to provide legal advice; and that Harris usually provided advice over the phone or by e-mail in a summary fashion regarding discrete employment issues. [41] At paragraphs 15 and 16 of this affidavit, Mr. Litherland stated: 15. Harris has not provided any legal advice to Dye & Durham since September of 2015. 16. After I learned that OneMove Technologies Inc. had purchased Dye & Durham in or about March 2016, I considered that Harris would no longer provide advice to Dye & Durham as Harris was acting for a party adverse to OneMove Technologies Inc. [42] As I have previously discussed, on October 28, 2016, Mr. Litherland informed Dye & Durham that Harris would not be in a position to provide any future legal advice to Dye & Durham after its acquisition by OneMove Technologies Inc. [43] Both Ms. Fenyo and Mr. Litherland agree, and I find, that Harris provided no legal advice to Dye & Durham after September 2015. [44] I also find that in 2005, and between August 2011 and September 2015, Dye & Durham intermittently engaged Harris for occasional legal advice concerning employment law and human resource related matters. Harris had no written retainer agreement with Dye & Durham and never represented that defendant in any litigation. On each occasion when Harris provided advice, the law firm rendered an account, which Dye & Durham promptly paid. [45] Counsel for the defendants referred to an exchange of e-mails between Dye & Durham's in-house counsel, Mr. Grant McLaney and Mr. Litherland on September 3, 2015 as demonstrating a continuing solicitor and client relationship between Harris and Dye & Durham. On September 3, 2015, Mr. McLaney e-mailed Mr. Litherland to request a detailed version of his firm's August 31, 2015 invoice. Mr. Litherland responded as follows: Hi Grant, We can definitely provide you a more detailed style of account going forward. For the August account, the information has been purged from Esilaw so we'd have to manually re-create it, but I have attached a copy of our pre-bill which I believe provides the info your accounting dept needs. The figures on this page do not include the taxes and disbursements on our fees. My hourly rate is $415. As well, my assistant is Barbara Borchardt. I have cc'ed her on this e-mail so you have her contact info. I hope this meets your needs. Geoff [46] In my view, this was, at most, a representation by Harris that the law firm was willing to provide further legal advice to Dye & Durham at Mr. Litherland's hourly rate of $415, and that the law firm was prepared to accommodate Dye & Durham's request for detailed accounts. [47] In September 2015, Dye & Durham sought advice from Mr. Litherland on an employment law matter. Harris provided that advice and rendered a detailed account at Mr. Litherland's hourly rate. Dye & Durham made no further request for Harris's services, either before or after the amalgamation of July 1, 2016. Dye & Durham made no promise to retain Harris on a continuing basis and never paid the firm a monetary retainer. [48] In short, in 2005, and again between August 2011 and September 2015, Dye & Durham engaged Harris from time to time as and when it required employment law advice on discrete matters. At the time of the amalgamation, Harris had provided no advice to Dye & Durham for approximately 10 months. [49] I find that when OneMove Technologies Inc. and Dye & Durham merged, Harris was not concurrently providing legal advice or representation to Mr. Salager and Dye & Durham. Accordingly, the bright line rule has no application in the circumstances of this case. [50] Further, I find this is one of those exceptional cases where it would be unreasonable for Dye & Durham to expect that Harris would not act against it on an unrelated matter. [51] At the time of the amalgamation, Harris was engaged in ongoing litigation for Mr. Salager, in which the plaintiff advanced claims in defamation and breach of contract. The conflict between the interests of Mr. Salager and Dye & Durham only arose as a result of the amalgamation. This was not a case where the law firm accepted a retainer to act for a client who they knew, from the outset, to be adverse in interest to Dye & Durham. More importantly, the intermittent nature of the legal services provided by Harris to Dye & Durham, which involved the law firm responding from time to time to telephone or e-mail requests for legal advice on employment related matters, and the absence of any ongoing retainer agreement rendered unreasonable any expectation of Dye & Durham that Harris would not act against it in an entirely unrelated matter. (b) Did Harris receive confidential information attributable to its solicitor and client relationship with Dye & Durham relevant to this action, and if so, is there a risk that information will be used to the prejudice of the defendants? [52] The second ground on which the defendants seek the disqualification of Harris is that the law firm received confidential information from Dye & Durham relevant to this action. [53] In MacDonald Estate v. Martin, [1993] S.C.R. 1236, Sopinka J., for the majority, at para. 45, held that in determining whether there is a disqualifying conflict of interest, two questions must be answered: (1) Did the lawyer receive confidential information attributable to a solicitor and client relationship relevant to the matter at hand? (2) Is there a risk that it will be used to the prejudice of the client? [54] Sopinka J. continued, at para. 46: In my opinion, once it is shown by the client that there existed a previous relationship which is sufficiently related to the retainer from which it is sought to remove the solicitor, the court should infer that confidential information was imparted unless the solicitor satisfies the court that no information was imparted which could be relevant. This will be a difficult burden to discharge. Not only must the court's degree of satisfaction be such that it would withstand the scrutiny of the reasonably informed member of the public that no such information passed, but the burden must be discharged without revealing the specifics of the privileged communication. Nonetheless, I am of the opinion that the door should not be shut completely on a solicitor who wishes to discharge this heavy burden. [55] In Brookville Carriers Flatbed GP Inc. v. Blackjack Transport Ltd., 2008 NSCA 22, Cromwell J.A., (as he then was) addressed the question of when retainers are "related" at paras. 50 to 52: [50] Whether two retainers are related must be considered in light of the underlying purpose of the inquiry. In the MacDonald Estate analysis, the focus is on protection of the client's confidential information. In that context, two matters will be sufficiently related to trigger the principle if, as Goudge, J.A. put it in Chapters at para. 30, "... it is reasonably possible that the lawyer acquired confidential information pursuant to the first retainer that could be relevant to the current matter." The issue is not so much whether the subject-matter of the two retainers is the same, but whether confidential information learned in one would be relevant to the other. Ultimately, the "overriding policy" must be "that the reasonably informed person would be satisfied that no use of confidential information would occur.": MacDonald Estate, p. 1260. [51] Under the principle relevant here, that concerning acting against a former client in a related matter, the focus is different. As the cases and commentators show, the scope of this duty is very limited absent confidential information being at risk. This broader continuing duty of loyalty to former clients is based on the need to protect and to promote public confidence in the legal profession and the administration of justice. What is of concern is the spectre of a lawyer attacking or undermining in a subsequent retainer the legal work which the lawyer did for the former client or of a lawyer effectively changing sides by taking an adversarial position against a former client with respect to a matter that was central to the previous retainer. Basinview is an example of the former: the new retainer involved the lawyer attacking or attempting to undermine the very legal services provided to the former client. Harris and Chiefs of Ontario are examples of the latter: the new retainer involved attacks on the honesty and integrity of the former client in relation to exactly the same sort of matters as the lawyer acted to defend in the previous retainer. In either type of case, the relationship between the two retainers must be very close so that the lawyer in the new retainer is attacking or undermining the value of the legal work provided to the former client or effectively changing sides in a matter that was central to the previous retainer. [52] It is important, in my view, that this principle not be applied too broadly. One must not lose sight of the important right of parties to retain and instruct the counsel of their choice or of lawyers to earn a living free of undue restriction. Moreover, one must not ignore the possible strategic use of applications to disqualify counsel. As Binnie, J. pointed out in Neil at para. 14, "[i]f a litigant could achieve an undeserved tactical advantage ... by ... using 'the integrity of the administration of justice' merely as a flag of convenience, fairness of the process would be undermined." [56] Recently, in Stewart v. Stewart, 2016 BCSC 2256 at paras. 41 and 42, Abrioux J. provided a succinct statement of the applicable standard for a disqualification application: [41] The applicable standard is that of a reasonably informed person, apprised of the facts: MacDonald Estate at para. 53. In making this determination, there should be a cautious approach to disqualification applications. The court should interfere with a party's right to representation by counsel only in clear cases: Manville Canada v. Ladner Downs (1992), 63 B.C.L.R. 102 (S.C.) [Manville (S.C.)] at para. 51; Merrick v. Rubinoff, 2013 BCSC 2352 [Merrick] at para.14. [42] Both this court and the Court of Appeal have provided additional direction on the circumstances in which a disqualification application should succeed: (a) to demonstrate that the current action is a related matter, there must be more than "a mere assertion of similarity": LS Entertainment Group Inc. v. Wong, 2000 BCSC 1789 [LS Entertainment] at para. 53; (b) "given the drastic nature of the remedy there should be compelling and cogent evidence which provides a sufficient connection between the retainers": LS Entertainment at para. 54 citing Moffat v. Wienstein (1996), 135 D.L.R. (4th) 298 (Ont. Civ. Div.) at 326; (c) disqualification ought only to occur where there is evidence of "real or actual mischief". A "mere perception or appearance of a conflict of interest is not sufficient to find a disqualifying conflict of interest". Instead, the court must have regard to reality rather than perceptions and appearances: Maedou Consulting v. 0887455 BC Ltd., 2015 BCSC 2009, para. 43; LS Entertainment at para. 70; Manville (S.C.) at paras. 45-49; (d) even where the MacDonald Estate requirements are met, disqualification is a discretionary remedy that is equitable in nature. Disqualification of the lawyer can only be justified if it prevents the imposition of a greater injustice on the party applying for the disqualification than that suffered by the party whose counsel is disqualified: Manville (S.C.) at para. 51; Merrick at para. 23; and (f) generally, courts in this province have taken a cautious approach in ordering the removal of counsel contrary to the wishes of the client, thereby acknowledging the right of a litigant to the choice of his or her own counsel: Coutu v. Jorgensen, 2004 BCCA 400 at para. 31. [57] Here, Harris no doubt received confidential information from time to time between 2005 and September 2015 related to the employment law matters on which Dye & Durham sought its advice. However, the defendants have not shown that confidential information is relevant to Harris' retainer by Mr. Salager. The plaintiff's claims in this action for defamation and breach of the settlement agreement are unrelated to the employment law and human resource issues on which Harris advised Dye & Durham. This is not a case where the law firm seeks to attack or undermine the work it performed for Dye & Durham. Further, because Harris last provided legal advice to Dye & Durham 10 months before its acquisition by OneMove Technologies Inc., the law firm could not have obtained any confidential information from Dye & Durham specifically related to this action. [58] Relying upon Ms. Fenyo's affidavit, the defendants contend that Harris acquired confidential information concerning Dye & Durham's internal strategy and decision-making, and particularly, the manner in which Dye & Durham's "executive decision-making body approaches legal disputes", as well as its tactics for minimizing legal liability. The defendants have offered no more than a bare assertion that the past relationship provided Harris with access to Dye & Durham's strategic and tactical decision-making processes. As the Court held in McKercher at para. 54, that is insufficient to establish that the firm acquired confidential information capable of being used against the client in some tangible manner. [59] Further, Ms. Fenyo is no longer a member of the executive of Dye & Durham. Any insight Harris has gained concerning the decision-making of Mr. Proud arises through its representation of Mr. Salager, rather than Dye & Durham. [60] I conclude that Harris has not received confidential information attributable to its solicitor and client relationship with Dye & Durham that is relevant to this action, and that a reasonably informed person apprised of the facts would be satisfied that there is no risk of the misuse of confidential information. (c) Is there a disqualifying conflict of interest in this case? [61] It follows from my earlier findings that Harris has no disqualifying conflict of interest in this case. CONCLUSION [62] The application of the defendants is dismissed. [63] The plaintiff will have the costs of this application, at Scale B, as costs in the cause. "PEARLMAN J."
