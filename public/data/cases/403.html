Dalrymple Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre [1] XXXXX XXXXX (the claimant), a citizen of the People's Republic of China (PRC), is claiming refugee protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act. [2] The claimant's initial hearing was scheduled for September 30, 2009. The claimant was ill and counsel provided a brief medical note from the XXXXX Medical Centre indicating that the claimant was suffering from XXXXX XXXXX. A Show Cause hearing to proceed was scheduled for November 25, 2009. [3] The panel finds for the following reasons, cause has been shown to proceed with the hearing. The claimant provided a medical note from Dr. XXXXX XXXXX of the XXXXX Medical Centre indicating that he had seen the claimant on XXXXX, 2009 and at that time, the claimant was suffering from XXXXX XXXXX and was not fit to attend the hearing of September 30, 2009. ALLEGATIONS [4] The claimant alleges the following. His friend, XXXXX XXXXX, began to spread the Gospel to the claimant at the beginning of XXXXX 2006. The claimant began to attend a house church on XXXXX XXXXX, 2006 and attended every two weeks until XXXXX, 2007. The service was being held at the claimant's home and the claimant was serving as lookout. The claimant observed the Public Security Bureau (PSB) approaching and notified the organizer. The claimant left the location immediately and went into hiding at the farm of his uncle. Because the service had been held in their home, the claimant's wife also went into hiding, leaving their son in the care of the claimant's parents. The PSB continued to look for the claimant and with the assistance of his uncle, a smuggler was found to help the claimant to leave China. The claimant began his travels to Canada on XXXXX, 2007. He arrived in Toronto on June 15, 2007 and made his claim for refugee protection on the same date. DETERMINATION [5] For the following reasons, the panel finds that the claimant has not satisfied the burden of establishing a serious possibility that he would be persecuted if he returned to China. He is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. IDENTITY [6] The panel finds that the claimant is a citizen of the People's Republic of China. The claimant provided photocopies of identity documents, including his Chinese resident identity card1 and household register2 to establish his citizenship. ANALYSIS [7] The claimant bases his need for protection on his fear of persecution as a member of a house church practicing Christianity in China. [8] The issues to be considered are: * Are there issues of credibility? * Was the claimant practicing Christianity as a member of a house church in China? * Does he practice Christianity in Canada? And * Does he face a serious possibility of being persecuted, arrested or imprisoned by the Chinese authorities if he returns to China? Are there issues of credibility? [9] The determinative issue in regard to this claim is the credibility of the claimant concerning his subjective fear of persecution as a member of an unregistered house church in China and as a genuine practicing Christian in Canada whose profile puts him at risk of persecution should he return to China. For the following reasons, the panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant is not a credible witness in this regard. [10] The panel is aware that none of the credibility concerns raised may be sufficient on their own to negate this claim. However, the cumulative effect of all of them is that the panel does not have sufficient credible and trustworthy evidence upon which to base a determination that the claimant is a Convention refugee. As the Court of Appeal stated in Sheikh:3 Even without disbelieving every word [a claimant] has uttered, a...panel may reasonably find [that]...a general finding of a lack of credibility on the part of the [claimant] may conceivably extend to all relevant evidence emanating from his testimony. [11] Evidence that is not contradicted may be rejected if that evidence does not accord with the probabilities affecting the case as a whole.4 In this respect, the British Columbia Court of Appeal stated in Faryna v. Chorny:5 In short, the real test of the truth of the story of a witness in such a case must be its harmony with the preponderance of the probabilities which a practical and informed person would readily recognize as reasonable in that place and in those conditions. [12] The panel was also guided by the words of Mahoney, J.A. in Orelien:6 ...one cannot be satisfied that evidence is credible or trustworthy unless satisfied that it is probably so, not just possibly so. [13] For the following reasons, the panel finds that there are issues with the claimant's credibility. The panel finds that it is not plausible for the claimant not to know the name or the country of issue on the passport he was traveling with. He testified that he did not know the name on his passport and was never asked by the authorities in any of the countries he traveled through, including not being asked any questions upon his arrival in Canada. He also provided this information during his June 16, 2007 interview with Canada Border Service Agency (CBSA).7 [14] The claimant testified and provided information to CBSA that he was not aware of the countries he traveled through on his way to Canada. He testified that he did not understand the announcements and could not read the information on the boarding pass. According to the Port of Entry (POE) notes,8 the claimant was not forthcoming with information regarding the documents with which he traveled to Canada. He did not have a passport nor did he have a boarding pass or luggage tags to document which airline he travelled on. For the following reasons, the panel finds that it is not plausible for the claimant not to know the names of the countries he traveled through on his trip to Canada. The claimant has twelve years of formal education9 and traveled to Canada on commercial airlines. With boarding and landing announcements, printed materials on the aircraft, boarding passes and logos on uniforms and the plane itself, the panel finds that the claimant would have to be aware of the country he was leaving and the destination of the flights. With the assistance of a Mandarin interpreter, the claimant also told the CBSA officer that he spent two weeks in one country he traveled through but did not know the name of the country. When asked why, the claimant told the panel that the snakehead never allowed him to leave the place he was staying in. In response to a question from the CBSA officer, "Who travelled with you from China?", the claimant answered, "I travelled alone." When asked what he did with his passport, the claimant said, "I gave it to him", referring to the snakehead. When reminded that he had said he had travelled alone, the claimant then said, "I threw it away." The panel draws a negative inference from these inconsistencies. Was the claimant practicing Christianity as a member of a house church in China? [15] The panel relied on the oral testimony of the claimant in the absence of specific documentary evidence regarding the claimant's attendance at a house church in China. For the following reasons, the panel finds that much of the claimant's solid religious knowledge was learned after his arrival in Canada. [16] The claimant joined the house church on XXXXX XXXXX 2006, meetings were held two times per month and the house church was discovered on XXXXX, 2007. Therefore, if the claimant had attended every service during his tenure with the church, he would have attended no more than eight services. During his POE interview on June 16, 2007,10 the claimant demonstrated a limited understanding of the principles and practices of Christianity. In answer to the officer's question, "What is Christianity?", the claimant responded, "Jesus". When asked to tell the officer about Jesus, the claimant replied, "He was born in a Jewish country in Bethlehem in a manger." He then told the officer, "He was born on December 25 but I don't know which year. He was born in BC. If you believe in Jesus you will have eternal life. You will wash off all your sins. You go to heaven to wash off the sin of your whole life. Old testament there are 39 Holy Scripts." [18] For the following reasons, the panel finds that the claimant's testimony at the hearing demonstrated a solid knowledge and understanding of the principles and practice of Christianity. He was able to identify the two parts of the Bible as the Old and New Testaments. He described the Old Testament as being the story of creation of the world and its population of humans and animals, the identification of the Jews as the chosen people of God and the Covenant that God made with the Israelites. The claimant described the New Testament as the word of Jesus and the examples he established as the ways to live ones life as a Christian. The claimant also properly responded that Jesus is not mentioned by name in the Old Testament, however, the Old Testament refers to the Messiah on a number of occasions. The claimant was also able to identify and correctly describe the meanings of Holy Communion, Easter, Christmas and the Pentecost, the story of which is found in the Book of Acts. The claimant also recited the Lord's Prayer correctly. [19] For the following reasons, the panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant only attended the house church in China for a brief period of time. He joined the house church XXXXX, 2006 to XXXXX, 2007 and services were held every two weeks. He provided a typical description of the house church he attended, indicating that it had fifteen members and had precautions in place to avoid discovery, which included meeting in two locations, meeting in the evenings and the posting of lookouts. The claimant provided a general description of the raid on the night the house church was discovered and a general description of his escape. Does the claimant practice of Christianity in Canada? [20] For the following reasons, the panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant practices Christianity in Canada. In his letter of XXXXX, 2009,11 Pastor XXXXX XXXXX writes that the claimant has been attending XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX since XXXXX 2007. The pastor goes on to say that the claimant has been an active member of the Sunday worship services, Saturday Bible Study and annual church retreat camp. As well, the claimant makes regular financial contributions to the church. According to Pastor XXXXX, the claimant has also assumed responsibility for the church maintenance group and has headed up a number of projects, including washroom-plumbing replacements. [22] The claimant also provided a letter of XXXXX, 2009 from Reverend XXXXX XXXXX 12 attesting to the claimant's regular attendance at XXXXX XXXXX since XXXXX, 2007. The claimant provided a Certificate of Baptism13 indicating that Reverend XXXXX baptized him on XXXXX, 2007. As well, the claimant provided photographs of the baptism ceremony.14 Does the claimant face a serious possibility of being persecuted if he returns to China? [23] The panel considered whether the claimant would face a serious possibility of being persecuted if he returns to China and practices Christianity in an unregistered church. The claimant is from Fujian province and has lived and worked there all of his life. The panel notes the documentary evidence states: ...the Executive Secretary of the Hong Kong Christian Council commented that Fujian and Guangdong have "the most liberal policy on religion in China, especially on Christianity" (Executive Secretary 1 Sept. 2005a). In his travels, the Executive Secretary has met with local authorities who, he said, usually tolerate activities of unregistered Christian groups (ibid.). While authorities are of a more tolerant nature in rural areas than in urban centres, they would usually take steps to discourage religious activity if it had a link to groups from outside China (ibid.). The Executive Secretary stated that he is aware of a number of unregistered churches that have been allowed to function for years (ibid.). Though he has received a few reports of arrests of Catholic priests in the years 2003 to 2005, the Executive Secretary noted that overall, Christians in Fujian and Guangdong "enjoy one of the most liberal polic[ies] on religious freedom in China." (ibid.).15 [24] The panel also notes that though there were arrests of Christians documented in 2005, 2006, 2007 and 2008, none of these arrests were noted as being in Fujian province. In 2005, there were reports of house church crackdowns in the provinces of Shanxi, Henan, Hubei, Jiangxi and Jilin and in May 2005, Chinese officials coordinated raids on 100 house churches in Jilin province [northeast China], arresting approximately 600 Christians.16 During the year, there were also reports of similar incidents in Hebei, Xinjiang, Beijing and Shanghai.17 The U.S. 2007 International Religious Freedom Report18 specifically reviewed arrests of Christians in China in 2006 and 2007 and noted arrests of Christians in Beijing, Shandong province, Xinjiang province, Liaoning province, Henan province, Jiangsu province, Anhui province, Zhejiang province, Szechuan province, Jiangxi province, Hebei province, Guanguxi province, and Shaanxi province. No mention is made of arrests of Christians in Fujian province in 2006 and 2007. [25] The panel has reviewed the data in the most recent articles19 in the exhibits covering 2008 and notes arrests of Christians in Xinjiang province, Guizhou province and Henan province, but none in Fujian province. The Annual Report of Persecution by the Government on Christian Churches within Mainland China20 very specifically notes the arrests and incidents of persecution of Christians in China from January 2007 to December 2007 and none of these incidents occurred in Fujian province where the claimant is from. This report noted that during 2007, the number of Christians persecuted in all of China was 788 and that the number of Christians arrested and detained was 693, of which 415 were church leaders, which accounts for 59.9% of all people arrested and 52.6% of all people persecuted. The panel reviewed documentary evidence submitted and the only mention of arrests of lay Christians in Fujian province was in 2002 when 20 unregistered Catholics were arrested while attending Catechism classes.21 [26] The panel considered all of the documents submitted regarding the situation for Christians in China, specifically the articles and reports documenting arrests and persecution and finds, on a balance of probabilities, that if there were arrests of lay Christians in Fujian province after 2002, there would be some documentation of these arrests. [27] The panel considered the claimant's testimony that he was a member of an underground church that was raided and that he is now wanted by the PSB who have continued to go to his home in China looking for him. The British Home Office Report notes that 31.4% of Chinese nationals aged sixteen and older are religious22 and that there are forty to seventy million Protestant Christians in China with only ten million practicing their religion in registered churches.23 That report also states that prayer meetings and Bible study groups held among friends and families are legal and need not register with authorities.24 The United States Department of State Report25 notes that house churches were more likely to have problems when the membership grew, arranged for regular use of facilities or forged links with other groups. There is no persuasive evidence that the claimant's group did any of these things. His group was small, consisting of a constant membership of fifteen including him and during their gatherings they would recite and discuss Bible passages and pray. The panel considered the claimant's description of his house church and considered its location in Fujian province where there have been no reported arrests of lay Christians since 20 Catholics were arrested in 2002. [28] The panel reviewed the documentary evidence and finds the likelihood of an individual member of an unregistered church being harassed by government authorities in Fujian province is so small so as to conclude that there is not a serious possibility that this claimant would be persecuted should he return to China. Therefore, the claimant does not have a well-founded fear that he would be persecuted even if he were to continue to worship in an unregistered church. Were the claimant to be found by the authorities, the country documentation referred to before indicates that as a member (not a leader) of an unregistered church, there is no serious possibility that the claimant would be persecuted. The same documentation indicates that in the claimant's province of Fujian, it may well be the case that his unregistered church would not be disrupted at all. [29] The panel therefore finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant would be able to practice his religion in an unregistered church if he were to return to his home in Fujian Province in China and that there is not a serious possibility that he would be persecuted for doing so. [30] On the basis of the totality of the evidence and the cumulative findings, the panel finds that the claimant has not satisfied his burden of establishing a serious possibility that he would be persecuted or that he would be personally subjected to a risk to his life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or a danger of torture by any authority in the People's Republic of China. CONCLUSION [31] In this particular claim, having considered the documentary evidence, the claimant's testimony and submissions, the panel finds that the claimant has failed to satisfy the burden of establishing that there is a serious possibility that he would be persecuted, or that he would be subjected personally to a danger of torture or a risk to his life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment by any authority in the People's Republic of China. [32] Having considered all the evidence, the panel finds that the claimant is neither a Convention refugee under section 96, nor a person in need of protection within the meaning of subsections 97(1)(a) or (b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act. Therefore, the Refugee Protection Division rejects his claim. "Stephen E. Rudin" Stephen E.
