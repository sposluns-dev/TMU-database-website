Motifs et dÚcision Person who is the subject of the appeal XXXX XXXX XXXX Personne en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, ON Appel instruit / entendu Ó Date of decision June 11, 2020 Date de la dÚcision Panel Linda Hart Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Cemone Morlese Barrister and Solicitor Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A ReprÚsentant(e) dÚsignÚ(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX XXXX (the Appellant), is a citizen of Hungary. He fears persecution in Hungary due to his Roma identity. He is appealing a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD), rejecting his claim for refugee protection. DETERMINATION [2] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes its determination that the Appellant is a Convention refugee. BACKGROUND [3] The Appellant alleged before the RPD that he experienced discrimination, harassment and segregation in school because he is Roma. He alleged that the area where he resided was frequently attacked by Guardists and skinheads, but the police failed to help. He was often intimidated by Guardists and skinheads. The Appellant resided with his grandparents in Miskolc and they were evicted from their home by the city. The Appellant was homeless and eventually moved to uncle's residence prior to coming to Canada. He has alleged that his family did not receive proper healthcare because they are Roma and that he was unable to find employment because of his Roma identity. He fears being unemployed and homeless should he return to Hungary. [4] The Appellant's application for refugee protection was heard on XXXX XXXX, 2018. In a written decision dated June 29, 2018, the RPD rejected the claim finding that the Appellant's personal circumstances did not rise to the level of persecution and that he had not demonstrated that he would face persecution upon return to Hungary. [5] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in determining that the discrimination he experienced did not amount persecution; erred in concluding state protection is available for Roma people; erred by focusing on the past and not assessing the forward-looking risk of persecution; erred by failing to conduct a section 97 analysis; and failed to consider the successful refugee protection claims of his family members. The Appellant also argues that there was a reasonable apprehension of bias because the RPD refused to allow some of his country conditions documentation into evidence. ANALYSIS [6] The RAD finds that the determinative issue in this appeal is whether the Appellant would experience discrimination amounting to persecution should he return to Hungary and whether state protection would be available to him. Experiences in Hungary RPD Findings [7] The RPD found that the Appellant had not established that the discrimination he experienced in Hungary rose to the level of persecution. They RPD found that the Appellant had not established with reliable and trustworthy evidence that he would be unable to work in Hungary, that he was denied an education and medical services, or that he was evicted from his home. [8] The RPD found that there was insufficient evidence that the Appellant experienced discrimination in his education given that he was accepted into trade school and had never complained about the poor treatment he received while at school. [9] The RPD considered the Appellant's evidence regarding the time he went with a fellow trade school graduate for a job interview and that the other person got the job because he was Hungarian. The RPD noted the Appellant's testimony that both students were recommended for the job by a trade school instructor and that he had written in his narrative that he was refused the job due to a lack of experience, as he was 17 years old at the time. The RPD noted that the Appellant did not seek any redress or file any complaint. [10] The RPD found that the Appellant's allegation that his grandmother received improper healthcare which led to her death, was not credible. The RPD considered that the Appellant had provided no documentation regarding the cause of death. [11] The RPD found that it was difficult to assess why the Appellant and his family were evicted from the resident in Miskolc, as he did not provide any documentation from the city regarding the lease or eviction. [12] The RPD found that there were avenues open to the Appellant to seek redress for the discrimination he alleged, such as the Labour Court or the Equal Treatment Authority (ETA). The RPD noted that the ETA fined a hospital for its treatment of a Roma woman in 2016 and fined the city of Miskolc $1800 in 2015 for subjecting residents of a segregated area to the threat of homelessness and discriminated against them because of their social status, financial situation and Romani origin. Appellant's Arguments [13] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in determining that the discrimination he experienced in Hungary did not amount to persecution. He submits that the basis for his claim was severe discrimination on cumulative grounds. He submits that the RPD member failed in her analysis of the specific areas of discrimination and by not performing a cumulative assessment of the discrimination. [14] The Appellant argues that the Roma minority in Hungary faces serious challenges in several areas such as education, employment, housing, healthcare and being in public places. [15] The Appellant submits that the RPD disregarded his sworn evidence regarding his lack of a proper education because he did not provide corroborating documents. He argues that the discriminatory treatment he received in education placed him at an incredible disadvantage from an early stage of life, as without the proper education he was unable to find stable employment, proper housing or healthcare. [16] The Appellant argues that his Roma ethnicity contributed to an employer's decision to only hire an ethnic Hungarian. He submits that it was an error for the RPD to dismiss this discriminatory treatment just because he did not make a complaint about it, as there is no requirement that an Appellant seek redress if the redress is not reasonably forthcoming. He argues that the documentary evidence supports the lack of redress. [17] The Appellant argues that he suffered in housing due to this ethnicity and that his family was targeted by forced evictions of Roma persons in Miskolc. He submits that the RPD ignored his testimony because he did not provide corroborating documents above the eviction. He argues that the country conditions documentary evidence supports his position that Roma persons in Hungary face severe discrimination in housing and that there is documentary evidence of the forced evictions in Miskolc. [18] The Appellant argues that the RPD did not properly assess the discrimination Roma face in accessing healthcare. He submits that he testified that his grandmother died XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX when she underwent surgery and that she was not properly treated because of her Roma ethnicity. He submits that the RPD's assessment only included the fact that his grandmother was treated, but did not consider the adequacy of the treatment. [19] The Appellant argues that his Basis of Claim (BOC) confirms that he was afraid of skinheads, guardists and the job party and that he was targeted on public transit and in public areas due to his ethnicity. He submits that the documentary evidence corroborates that harassment and various forms of violence are everyday occurrences for many Roma. [20] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred by only assessing each of the areas of discrimination on an individual basis. He submits that he provided credible, unembellished testimony about his experiences in Hungary. He submits was unable to find employment due to his ethnicity and was treated as a second-class citizen in education and healthcare. He argues that he faced racist comments and threats from Hungarians throughout his life. RAD Assessment and Findings [21] The RAD has reviewed the record and has conducted an independent assessment of the evidence. The RAD finds that the Appellant's arguments regarding the cumulative effect of the discrimination he experienced in Hungary have merit. The RAD has listened to the recording of the hearing and finds the Appellant credibly testified about his experiences of discrimination in the areas of housing, education and daily life. The RAD has considered that testimony, as well as the information contained in the Appellant's Basis of Claim form, and finds that for most of his life he has experienced incidents of discrimination which ultimately led him to leave his home country. The RAD is guided by the UNHCR handbook which states that: 54.Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practice his religion, or his access to normally available educational facilities. 55. Where measures of discrimination are, in themselves, not of a serious character, they may nevertheless give rise to a reasonable fear of persecution if they produce, in the mind of the person concerned, a feeling of apprehension and insecurity as regards his future existence. Whether or not such measures of discrimination in themselves amount to persecution must be determined in the light of all the circumstances. A claim to fear of persecution will of course be stronger where a person has been the victim of a number of discriminatory measures of this type and where there is thus a cumulative element involved. [22] The RAD has considered the documentary evidence regarding the situation for Roma in Hungary. The United States Department of State report for 2018 notes that human rights Non-Governmental Organizations continued to report that Roma suffered social and economic exclusion and discrimination in almost all fields of life. 1 It is also noted in this document that Romani woman can suffer multiple forms of discrimination on the basis of gender, ethnicity, class and that they experienced barriers to equal access in education, healthcare, housing, employment and justice. [23] Roma in Hungary face discrimination in all fields of life including education, housing, employment, health care and political participation. The Hungarian Ombudsman has reported that the disadvantageous social situation of Roma is aggravated by discrimination in all of these fields. A report from the Hungarian Central Statistics Office cites that the employment rate for Roma was only 25.9 percent in 2014, as compared to the employment rate for non-Roma, which was 60 percent. This report also states that 67.9 percent of Roma were at risk of poverty. It is noted that 500,000 to 600,000 Roma live in disadvantaged regions of Hungary, mostly in segregated residential zones in poor conditions.2 [24] It is reported that there is an increasing rate of evictions against disadvantaged people and a decreasing amount of social housing. Roma often face discrimination in accessing social housing and municipalities use a variety of techniques to prevent Roma from living in more desirable urban neighbourhoods.3 [25] There is segregation in education. The European Roma Rights Centre notes that there is systemic discrimination and segregation of Romani people in Hungary's schools. Roma children are disproportionately placed in special schools or classes for children with disabilities or special needs, which constitutes a form of segregated education.4 [26] In regard to health care, there is information that Roma women often lack access to medical services due to direct discrimination and degrading treatment at the hospital. It is reported that Roma women are being segregated in certain medical facilities.5 [27] The documentary evidence references efforts to integrate Roma into the general population. However, there is little information on how the government initiatives have benefited Roma specifically.6 The statistics regarding the employment rate for Roma, the risk of poverty, and poor living conditions do not appear to have changed despite government initiatives. Although the RPD mentioned two instances where a hospital and the city of Miskolc were fined because of their treatment of Roma, the fines do not appear to have been extensive and there is no evidence that being fined was an impetus for change. [28] The RAD has considered the documentary evidence concerning the situation for Roma in Hungary relating to discrimination and targeting by right-wing groups, as well as the personal experiences of the Appellant in regard to education, employment, housing and social exclusion. The RAD finds, based on past experiences of the Appellant, and the country conditions evidence, that the Appellant would experience discrimination which would lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature should he return to Hungary. State Protection RPD Findings [29] The RPD found that, while there is evidence that the police are not always helpful to Roma and trust in the places minimal, this did not rebut the presumption of state protection for the Appellant. The RPD noted that the Appellant had not sought out protection and had not provided sufficient evidence that would mitigate his onus to seek state protection. Appellant's Arguments [30] The Appellant argues that there was no adequate state protection for him as a Roma in Hungary. He submits that he was targeted by the police in Hungary and forced to pay fines for petty offensives. He submits that the documentary evidence indicates that the Hungarian state is unwilling and unable to provide adequate protection for Roma in Hungary. He argues that the federal court has ruled that state protection must be reasonably forthcoming and there is no obligation to seek out state protection if it is not reasonably forthcoming. [31] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred by not assessing the operational level of state protection for Roma in Hungary. He submits that the RPD focused the decision on the efforts and policies by the government to address systemic racism faced by Roma from the police and Hungarian society, but did not evaluate whether there was adequate state protection from the police. [32] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred by selectively looking at the country conditions evidence. He submits that the RPD ignored evidence that the Hungarian government continued to discriminate against Roma after receiving the ETA decision referred to by the RPD. He submits that the RPD did not analyse any of the other evidence that contradicted its findings regarding state protection. RAD Assessment and Findings [33] The RAD has reviewed the country conditions documentary evidence, as well as the Appellant's personal circumstances and profile. The RAD finds that Hungary did not provide adequate state protection for the Appellant and would not do so, should he return to that country. [34] The RAD notes that the police have shown prejudice against the Roma community and engage in ethnic profiling. It is noted that police response to hate crimes is not adequate.7 There is evidence that right-wing paramilitary groups organize demonstrations against Roma, Jews and homosexuals.8 Racist violence against Roma remains one of the most pressing issues in the country. Between January 2009 and September 2013 there were more than 100 cases of alleged hate-motivated violence targeting Roma and/or their property. Roma are often subjected to hate speech. These problems have been sustained by the continued hostility Roma face from police forces and officials, reflected in persistent discriminatory practices including ethnic profiling and fines for even the most minor infractions. This also extends to a failure to protect Roma community members from attack.9 The Appellant's evidence regarding his personal experiences in Hungary is consistent with what is stated in the country conditions documentary evidence. [35] The RAD finds, after conducting an independent assessment, that the documentary evidence does not indicate that the police are providing adequate state protection in regard to crimes against Roma and government initiatives have not resulted in any change to the overall situation. The RAD therefore finds that there is not adequate state protection available to the Appellant in Hungary. The RAD further finds that there is no Internal Flight Alternative available to the Appellant in Hungary, as the situation for Roma is consistent throughout the country. CONCLUSION [36] The RAD finds that there is a serious possibility that the Appellant would suffer persecution because of his Roma ethnicity, should he return to Hungary. For this reason, the RAD cannot uphold the decision of the RPD. [37] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes its own determination that the Appellant is a Convention refugee.
