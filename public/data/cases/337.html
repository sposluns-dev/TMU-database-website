Philip Mang is a pharmacist and the principal of the corporate defendants, PM Pharmacy Ltd. ("PMPL") and 1231202 B.C. Ltd. Mr. Mang and PMPL entered into franchise agreements to operate pharmacies in Powell River and Vancouver in Shoppers' name. Those agreements have now come to an end and the parties are in litigation dealing with the aftermath. [2] Shoppers commenced this action by Notice of Civil Claim issued on March 9, 2020. It claims that Mr. Mang and PMPL breached the final franchise agreement and misappropriated its money. It seeks a judgment in debt under the agreement, damages, an accounting, trust remedies, and a remedy under the Fraudulent Conveyance Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 163. Alternatively, it claims on the basis of unjust enrichment. [3] The defendants filed a Response to Civil Claim ("RtCC") and a Counterclaim on April 22, 2020. These pleadings were filed on the defendants' behalf by counsel who has now withdrawn. I attach copies as schedules to these reasons ("Schedule A and Schedule B") [4] Shoppers seeks an order striking the RtCC and Counterclaim in their entirety or, alternatively, striking portions of the defendants' pleadings. It maintains that these pleadings fail to state a reasonable defence, or cause of action in the case of the Counterclaim, and are vexatious, embarrassing, and scandalous. [5] Shoppers' application first came on for hearing on March 11, 2021. The defendants did not have counsel and were not prepared to proceed, and Davies J. ordered that the hearing be adjourned. He ordered that Shoppers was at liberty to reschedule the application for hearing on any date after two weeks had passed "and the hearing date will be pre-emptory on the Defendants". [6] Shoppers rescheduled the hearing on March 31, 2021. The defendants still did not have counsel and were not ready to proceed. Justice Milman again adjourned the application. He granted Shoppers liberty to reschedule the application for hearing on any date after three weeks had passed "and the hearing date will be pre-emptory on the defendants". [7] At the renewed hearing on April 26, 2021, Mr. Mang appeared in person on behalf of the defendants and sought a further adjournment. I refused the request for an adjournment. The application proceeded on the merits and Mr. Mang opposed the application on the defendants' behalf. Unfortunately, he had not filed an application response. [8] It is fundamental that this is an application to strike pleadings. The statements contained in a pleading such as a notice of civil claim, response to civil claim, or a counterclaim are allegations. They concern facts that the party filing the pleading proposes to prove or disprove when the case comes to trial or on an application for summary judgment. The issue on an application to strike a pleading is not whether the allegations are true. That is a question for later in the litigation. The question is whether the allegations are properly advanced in a pleading. [9] Shoppers advances its argument on this application at a high level of generality. It does not address the details of the pleadings in issue. As a self-represented litigant, in oral submissions, Mr. Mang had difficulty differentiating arguments concerning his pleadings--the subject matter of the application--from arguments addressing the underlying facts and the merits of Shoppers' claims against him and his companies. Legal framework [10] The fundamental principle of pleading is that a party must plead the material facts, but not the evidence, it proposes to prove; Supreme Court Civil Rules, R. 3-7(1). A response to civil claim must address each fact alleged in the notice of civil claim and state whether it is admitted, denied, or outside the defendant's knowledge and, if it is denied, state the defendant's version of the fact; R. 3-3(2)(a). The defendant must plead facts or points of law that raise issues of fact not arising out of the preceding pleading; R. 3-7(12). [11] In this Court, a pleading must also set out the relief sought against the opposing party, if any, and contain a concise summary of the legal basis for the relief sought or the party's opposition to the relief sought by its adversary; Rules 3-1(2), 3-3(2), 3-4(6). [12] Rule 9-5 provides the authority for an order striking a pleading. It states, in part: Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. Admissibility of evidence (2) No evidence is admissible on an application under subrule (1)(a). [13] Under subrule (1)(a), a pleading discloses no reasonable claim or defence if the law does not support the pleading party's contention so that it has no reasonable prospect of success; R. v. Imperial Tobacco Ltd., 2011 SCC 42 at para. 17 [Imperial Tobacco]. [14] The question of a reasonable claim or defence is addressed assuming the truth of the factual allegations in the pleading. As set out in subrule (2), no evidence is admissible in addressing this question. However, the court may refer to documents incorporated by reference in the pleading; Bow Valley Resource Services Ltd. v. Kansa General Insurance Co. (1990), 49 C.C.L.I. 230 (B.C.S.C.) at para. 7, leave to appeal refused (1991) 49 C.C.L.I. 247 (C.A. Chambers), aff'd (1991) 56 B.C.L.R. (2d) 337 (C.A.); Pacific Shower Doors (1995) Ltd. v. Osler, Hoskin & Harcourt, LLP, 2011 BCSC 1370 at para. 6; Johnstone v. Gardiner, 2011 BCSC 1843 at para. 14, rev'd but not on this point 2012 BCCA 184 at paras. 10-14; Afifi v. British Columbia (Minister of Jobs, Tourism and Skills Training), 2020 BCSC 1451 at para. 28. [15] Under subrule (1)(b), a pleading is vexatious or frivolous if it is obvious that it cannot succeed or give rise to a good defence; Skender v. Farley, 2007 BCCA 629 at para. 64; Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress, 1999 CanLII 5860 (B.C.S.C.) at para. 47 [Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform]. Under this subrule, the court may have regard to extrinsic evidence of a history or circumstances said to make the proceeding vexatious or frivolous, and this category of attack is often applied in cases of repetitive proceedings in which a party is seeking to re-litigate matters that have already been decided, or where the claim or defence is barred by the doctrine of estoppel. [16] Under subrule (1)(b), a pleading is unnecessary or scandalous if it pleads facts that are legally irrelevant to the substance of the present dispute, so that it threatens to involve the parties in the trouble and expense of addressing collateral issues. Such a pleading may also be characterized as embarrassing or prejudicial to the fair trial of the proceeding under subrule 1(c); Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, para. 47; Simon v Canada (Attorney General), 2017 BCSC 1438 at para. 52, citing Sahyoun (Committee of) v. Ho, 2015 BCSC 392, aff'd 2017 BCCA 96. [17] These various grounds of attack do not occupy watertight silos. They naturally overlap and a particular pleading may be subject to attack on multiple grounds. [18] Whichever category of attack is invoked, a pleading should only be struck where it is plain and obvious that the attack is well founded; Imperial Tobacco, at para. 17; Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083 at para. 20; Nevsun Resources Ltd. v. Araya, 2020 SCC 5 at paras. 64-66. [19] The court must consider the claim or defence as it is pleaded and as it could be pleaded with appropriate amendments; The Owners, Strata Plan LMS2359 v. Sze Hang Holding Inc., 2009 BCSC 473 at paras. 40-41; Strohmaier v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2015 BCSC 1189 at para. 17; Carhoun & Sons Enterprises ltd. v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 BCCA 163 at para. 133. Analysis Application to strike the RtCC in its entirety [20] The RtCC addresses the material facts pleaded in the notice of civil claim over 10 paragraphs in Part 1, Division 1. It admits some of the facts with commentary, denies others, and states that some of the facts are beyond the defendants' knowledge. All this is as contemplated in R. 3-3(2)(a). In Divisions 2 and 3 of Part 1, it goes on to offer the defendants' version of the facts and additional facts, again as contemplated in R. 3-3(2). [21] An example of a paragraph containing admissions is paragraph 1 of Division 1. It addresses paragraphs 1 to 13 of Part 1 of the Notice of Civil Claim, in which Shoppers pleads the identity of the parties, relationships among the defendants, and terms of the various franchise agreements between Shoppers, Mr. Mang, and PMPL. The defendants plead: 1. The facts alleged in paragraphs 1 to 13 of part 1 of the Notice of Civil Claim (the "NOCC) are admitted, save and except that the Defendants Phillip Mang ("Mang") and PM Pharmacy Ltd. ("PM Pharmacy") expected and relied on the Plaintiff and its Officers to conduct its business and affairs with corporate social responsibility and in good faith, and not to act in any manner that would be contrary or detrimental to the operations and success of the Defendants Mang and PM Pharmacy, or for that matter, the Plaintiff itself, and pursuant to the provisions of the Franchises Act, SBC 2015, c. 35 (the "Act") and the law of corporate social responsibility. [22] The admission contained in this paragraph is significant. It is not substantively qualified by the words that follow. The pleading that the defendants expected Shoppers to act in good faith parallels Shoppers' plea, in paragraph 17 of the notice of civil claim, that it "had a reasonable expectation that Mr. Mang and PM Pharmacy would perform their obligations under the Associate Agreement honestly and in good faith". Sections 3(1) and (3) of the Franchises Act, S.B.C. 2015, c. 35 impose on parties to a franchise agreement a duty of fair dealing, including a duty to act in good faith and in accordance with reasonable commercial standards, on the parties to a franchise agreement in the performance and enforcement of the franchise agreement. [23] While the references in paragraph 1 to corporate social responsibility and the law of corporate social responsibility are opaque, the paragraph is otherwise comprehensible and legally cogent. In my view, it would be inappropriate to strike this paragraph from the RtCC. [24] Examples of paragraphs denying allegations in the notice of civil claim are paragraphs 6 and 7. They address allegations in paragraphs 21 to 25 of the notice of civil claim. Shoppers here alleges that, as of December 28, 2019, Mr. Mang and PMPL were deficient in regard to equity obligations under the franchise agreement and owed to Shoppers $418,284 in respect of an "Equity Shortfall". It further alleges that Mr. Mang incurred $70,000 to $80,000 of excessive and unreasonable personal expenses, mostly relating to meals and entertainment, that he misallocated as promotional expenses. The defendants plead as follows in response: 6. The Defendants deny the facts alleged in paragraphs 21, 22 and 23 of Part 1 of the NOCC that PM Pharmacy and Mang were deficient in regard to their equity obligations, and state that these Defendants maintained an equity position of $470,000.00 which they removed on leaving, as such equity position consisted entirely of their own monies or to which they had a color of right. 7. The Defendants deny the facts alleged in paragraphs 24 and 25 of Part 1 of the NOCC alleging improper or misallocated personal expenses and say that all such expenses were promotional or otherwise justifiable expenses incurred in Mang's business discretion and as allowed under the Income Tax Act. [25] If the facts alleged by the defendants in these paragraphs are true, then they raise an arguable defence to Shoppers' claims founded on the allegations in paragraphs 21 to 25 of the notice of civil claim. There is nothing scandalous, vexatious, or otherwise improper about this defence. [26] I conclude that it would be wrong to strike the RtCC in its entirety. The paragraphs I have quoted, among others, advance positions and defences that the defendants should be permitted to maintain at trial or on an application for judgment. Alternative application to strike portions of the RtCC [27] Alternatively, Shoppers applies to strike paragraphs 1, 2, 4, 8, and 10 of Division 1, paragraphs 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, and 12 to 16 of Division 2, paragraphs 1 and 2 of Division 3, and paragraphs 2, 3, 4 and 7 of Part 2 of the RtCC. [28] In broad strokes, the burden of the factual allegations in these paragraphs is as follows: a) Mang had a franchise for one store and wanted a franchise for a second store (Div. 3, paras. 12-13). b) Mang worked hard and effectively for Shoppers, and was assured by a district manager that he would be recommended for a second store (Div. 2, paras. 8, 13; Div. 3, para. 3). c) Mang was refused the second store in an instance of bullying, bad faith, and a breach of corporate social responsibility (Div. 2, para. 13). d) Mang was sometimes overdrawn in his account, but Shoppers' overlooked this. It raised the overdraft in discussion with Mr. Mang in the fall of 2019 and accepted his proposal to eliminate the debt within 12 months (Div. 2, para. 14). Later, they demanded that he repay the indebtedness within 2 weeks or face termination of his contract, despite having sufficient retained earnings to cover any overdrawn amounts, and despite [Shoppers' officer] Kuramoto having agreed in the past that sufficient retained earnings would offset being overdrawn, and despite past Shoppers practice. The Defendants plead estoppel. e) On November 25, 2019, Shoppers threatened Mang with immediate termination if the overdrawn amount was not immediately overpaid. Mang concluded that Shoppers was not acting in good faith and left with $470,000, which was entirely his own money (Div. 2, para. 15). f) Accordingly, the defendants were justified in removing funds from the franchise on November 29, 2019 by Shoppers' bad faith and breach of its obligations to Mang under the Franchises Act. (Div. 1, paras. 3, 4, 8, 10). [29] In my view, it is not plain and obvious that the defence outlined as I have set out lacks any reasonable prospect of success. Conduct including assurances provided and then withdrawn coupled with a threat of immediate termination would arguably constitute bad faith and a failure to adhere to reasonable commercial standards. Such conduct may disentitle Shoppers to the damages claimed in the notice of civil claim. [30] However, some of the paragraphs under attack include scandalous allegations that do not advance the defence I have described. [31] Paragraph 6 alleges, in very general terms, a malign culture among Shoppers' franchisees or "Associates", involving cruel gossip, fierce competition among Associates, and favouritism on the part of Shoppers towards certain Associates. Paragraph 9 alleges illegal conduct on the part of other Associates and elaborates on the allegation of favouritism. Paragraph 10 alleges that the favoured pharmacists were rewarded by Shoppers, to Mr. Mang's detriment. The pleading does not say how benefits provided to others caused Mr. Mang to suffer a loss. [32] Paragraph 12 alleges bullying and defamatory comments by two named Shoppers' Associates directed at Mr. Mang. It alleges that these individuals "had a reputation for being bullies and for harassing others below them in the hierarchy, including Mang, and had had restraining orders issued against them". As with paragraph 6, 9 and 10, these allegations are collateral to the dispute between Shoppers and Mr. Mang in this lawsuit. [33] Paragraph 12 also descends a long way into pleading evidence, as opposed to material facts. [34] Paragraph 16 pleads that Ms. Kuramoto's employment with Shoppers' terminated following the defendants' departure. This is not a material fact. [35] Paragraphs 1 and 2 of Division 3 make general allegations about the conduct of other Associates and employee safety. They are collateral to the arguable defence I have identified. [36] Part 3 (Legal Basis) of the RtCC is summarily pleaded as a series of heads of legal argument. Shoppers seeks to strike the following heads: 2. Law of abandonment or breach of the duty of corporate social responsibility, including the reciprocal obligations and the self-imposed ethics. 3. Breach of fiduciary duty. 4. Breaches of the duty of good faith, both pursuant to the Act and the common law. 7. Bullying and harassment in the workplace. [37] In my view, it is plain and obvious that items 2, 3, and 7 do not identify legally cogent grounds of defence. It is not clear what body of law item 2 seeks to invoke. Item 3, breach of fiduciary duty, is not a defence in this case because the factual pleadings do not lay a foundation for an argument that any fiduciary duties were owed. Item 7, bullying and harassment in the workplace, does not ground a defence to Shoppers' claim on the facts pleaded. [38] On the other hand, item 4 is legally cogent. As indicated above, Shoppers' pleading acknowledges a duty of good faith and s. 3 of the Franchises Act confirms the existence of such a duty as between franchisor and franchisee. In Part 3 of the notice of civil claim, Shoppers itself pleads at para. 41: The duty of good faith contractual performance requires parties to a contract to perform their respective contractual obligations honestly and in good faith. [39] To summarize, I am satisfied that paragraphs 6, 9, 10, and 12 of Division 2 and paragraphs 1 and 2 of Division 3 of Part 1 of the RtCC should be struck. So should paragraphs 2, 3, and 7 of Part 3. It is plain and obvious that much of their content is scandalous and that these paragraphs fail to state a reasonable defence to the action. [40] I am not persuaded that the balance of the RtCC should be struck. While it is far from a model pleading, read generously, it outlines a defence that is not devoid of legal merit. While there may be individual passages in these paragraphs that violate the rules of pleading, such as the prohibition on pleading evidence, Shoppers has not identified them. Application to strike the Counterclaim [41] The statement of facts in Part 1 of the Counterclaim is only 6 paragraphs long. It does not include a statement incorporating the facts affirmatively pleaded in the RtCC. In consequence, the factual basis of the pleading is deficient. [42] One of the claims advanced in the Counterclaim is a claim for "breach of promise". The defendants plead: 3. As the salary earned by each franchisee is limited to $120,000 annually, most franchisees seek more than 1 location to increase their income. The [defendants] sought an additional franchise on 6 consecutive annual occasions but were denied on each occasion based on favoritism and not for any valid or justifiable reasons. Mang performed as a loyal corporate servant and accepted time-consuming jobs such as becoming a PEERS representative on the promise that he would be given a second franchise, and in good faith, but such promises were ignored by Shoppers acting in bad faith. 4. The breach of promise to grant a 2nd franchise to Mang resulted in losses of $120,000 for each of 6 years, and the [defendant] seek [sic] damages in the sum of $720,000.00 for such losses. [Emphasis added] [43] In law, this is a claim for breach of contract, but the defendants have not pleaded material facts sufficient to support a conclusion that a contract to award the defendants (or any of them) a second franchise was entered into. It is plain and obvious that the claim for breach of promise fails to state a reasonable cause of action. [44] The Counterclaim also seeks damages for wrongful termination of the franchise agreement. The only factual pleading in support of this claim is the following: 5. Article 13.00 of the Associate Agreement headed 'Termination' provides the instances where a Franchise Agreement may be terminated by [Shoppers], and none of those provisions apply in the circumstances of this case, no notice of default having been issued or delivered by Shoppers. As a result, the termination was in breach of the Agreement, and the [defendants] seek aggravated damages for the wrongful anticipated termination, done in bad faith. [45] This pleading misstates the terms of the Associate Agreement dated January 28, 2018, between Shoppers, PMPL, and Mr. Mang. Article 13.00 of the Agreement does not require notice of default as a precondition to termination of the Agreement by Shoppers. To the contrary, s. 13.01 permits termination by either party in accordance with s. 4.01(b), and s. 4.01(b) contemplates that a party may terminate without cause on 60 days notice in writing. Pursuant to s. 4.02(a), having given notice of termination, Shoppers may assume "absolute control" of the franchised business during the notice period. [46] It is plain and obvious that a claim for damages for wrongful termination based on a failure to give notice of default is bound to fail. [47] The Counterclaim also seeks "punitive damages for bad faith conduct and poor corporate social responsibility". No material facts are pleaded in the counterclaim that could support either claim. A claim for damages for poor corporate social responsibility is unknown to the law. [48] The relief sought in the Counterclaim does not include a claim for an accounting, but that is evidently an oversight, because para. 6 of Part 1 pleads: 6. Further, Shoppers is obligated to provide an accounting within 90 days of its termination of the Agreement, but has failed to do so, and Mang demands such accounting, and damages for breach. [49] Paragraph 6 may be referring to Shoppers' obligation pursuant to s. 4.02(g) of the Associate Agreement to provide an accounting of all receipts and disbursements made by and in respect of the franchised business during the management period within 90 days following the termination of the Agreement. [50] In oral submissions, Shoppers' counsel advised that Shoppers would accept a counterclaim seeking an accounting and a claim for damages for failure to provide an accounting. [51] As presently drafted, the Counterclaim should be struck because it fails to state a reasonable cause of action. The defendants should have leave to file an Amended Counterclaim that seeks an accounting and damages for failure to provide an accounting as the relief sought in Part 2. If the defendants wish to file an Amended Counterclaim advancing further claims, they should obtain Shoppers' consent or leave of the court. Disposition [52] For these reasons, I order as follows: a) Paragraphs 6, 9, 10 and 12 of Division 2, paragraphs 1 and 2 of Division 3 of Part 1, and paragraphs 2, 3, and 7 of Part 3 of the RtCC are struck; b) The Counterclaim is struck; c) The defendants may file an amended Counterclaim advancing a claim in Part 2 for an accounting and damages for Shoppers' alleged failure to provide an accounting; d) If the defendants seek to file an amended Counterclaim containing further claims, they may do so with Shoppers' consent or leave of the court. [53] Shoppers has been substantially successful on this application and should recover its costs, but not in any event of the cause. "Gomery J." Schedule A Schedule B
