Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXXX XXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of hearing April 16, 2015 Date(s) de l'audience Place of hearing Montréal, Quebec Lieu de l'audience Date of decision and reasons April 29, 2015 Date de la décision et des motifs Panel Marie-Lyne Thibault Tribunal Counsel for the claimant(s) Me Jeanne Larochelle Conseil(s) du (de la/des) demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du (de la) ministre REASONS FOR DECISION INTRODUCTION [1] XXXX XXXX, a citizen of Lebanon and of Israel, is claiming refugee protection under section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (the IRPA). DETERMINATION [2] After analyzing all the evidence, the panel determines that the claimant is neither a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection." ALLEGATIONS [3] The claimant is alleging the following. In XXXX 2000, he left Lebanon to take refuge in Israel with his family. If he returned to Israel, he fears that his life would be at risk at the hands of XXXX and that he would be persecuted by Israel's Arab community by reason of the past activities of his father, XXXX XXXX. [4] The claimant is a Christian originally from XXXX, a small village in Southern Lebanon. During Lebanon's civil war, when Israel controlled Southern Lebanon, the claimant's father was recruited by Israeli military intelligence. [5] In 1988, the claimant's father disappeared after leaving the family home to go on a mission for Israeli military intelligence. In 1989, a representative of the Israeli army informed the claimant's mother that XXXX XXXX was missing and that the Israeli army did not know his whereabouts. [6] When the Israeli army pulled out of Southern Lebanon in 2000, the claimant and his mother, brothers and sister feared that their lives were at risk at the hands of XXXX and obtained asylum in Israel. [7] Before coming to Canada, the claimant lived in XXXX, a city located near the Lebanese border in northern Israel. Israeli Arabs in the region constantly insulted the claimant and his family, calling them traitors and telling them that they would go to hell for having collaborated with Israel instead of the Lebanese government. [8] In 2006 or 2007, on XXXX XXXX, about 10 individuals threatened the claimant and his friend near the church in XXXX when they found out they were Lebanese. [9] In 2006/2007, young men threw rocks at the claimant's house after he put up a XXXX XXXX in his home. That same year, someone threw eggs at the window near where the XXXX XXXX had been put up. [10] In 2008, in a church in XXXX, two individuals threatened the claimant when they found out he was Lebanese, mainly because of his accent. [11] When he was in university, the claimant feared the Arabs who lived in his university residence and who called him a traitor when they heard his accent. [12] The claimant decided to leave Israel when an Arab living in his region began threatening him and his family, telling them that he would give their names to XXXX because of his father's involvement with Israeli military intelligence. Following the kidnapping of two Israeli soldiers by XXXX in 2006 and the kidnapping of XXXX XXXX by XXXX, the claimant concluded that he could suffer the same fate. Given the fact that he is not an Israeli Jew, the claimant concluded that the Israeli authorities would not try to save him if he were kidnapped. [13] The claimant left Israel and arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2009. [14] On September 24, 2009, he claimed refugee protection at an inland office. ANALYSIS Identity [15] Considering the evidence on the record, specifically a copy of the claimant's Israeli passport,1 the panel is satisfied as to the claimant's identity. Assessment of credibility [16] The determinative issue in this case is the claimant's credibility. The panel noted, among other things, an implausibility and significant omissions in the initial version of the Personal Information Form (PIF).2 The panel is of the opinion that when confronted with the issues raised, the claimant was unable to provide a satisfactory explanation. The panel also considered whether the discrimination the claimant would face if he returned to Israel would cumulatively amount to persecution. Risk to life at the hands of XXXX [17] The panel accepts that the claimant's father conducted activities on behalf of Israeli military intelligence and that he went missing in 1988. However, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant failed to demonstrate that he would face reprisals in Israel by XXXX because of his father's activities. [18] It is clear from the allegations made by the claimant in the initial version of his PIF3 that it was his fear of reprisals from XXXX that prompted him to decide to leave Israel. [19] During the hearing, the claimant stated that in addition to the possibility of reprisals he would face for his father's past activities, XXXX would also target him because two of his brothers, XXXX and XXXX, had joined the Israeli army between 2005 and 2006. When asked at the hearing to explain why he had failed to mention in his PIF4 that his brothers' activities also constituted grounds for retaliation against him, the claimant stated that he had been told that he would be given the opportunity to tell his story in detail at his hearing before the Refugee Protection Division. The panel does not accept the claimant's explanation, because this is a de novo hearing and the previous panel5 had criticized the claimant for other omissions in his PIF.6 The panel notes that the claimant failed to mention the possibility of reprisals from XXXX for his brothers' activities, at the hearing held on May 9, 2012,7 and in the additions made to the PIF8 submitted to the panel on April 16, 2015. In the absence of a reasonable explanation, the panel draws a negative inference from the claimant's failure to state in his PIF9 that the possibility of reprisals he would face at the hands of XXXX in Israel would in part be attributable to his brothers' activities. The claimant's credibility is undermined with respect to the reasons why he would be subjected to a risk by XXXX in Israel. [20] When asked whether his brothers XXXX and XXXX had problems in Israel, the claimant replied, [translation] "No, I don't believe so." He then adjusted his testimony, stating that two weeks before leaving Israel, while in the company of his brother XXXX, his brother had a run-in with some Arabs, who asked him, [translation] "What are you doing here?" The claimant also added that since he has been in Canada, he has not asked his family members in Israel whether they have problems when speaking with them. The panel draws a negative inference from the adjusted testimony. Considering the claimant's initial statement that, to his knowledge, his brothers have no problems in Israel, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant's credibility has been undermined with respect to his allegations that he would be targeted by XXXX on Israeli soil because of the fact that his brothers served in the Israeli army. [21] With respect to the possibility of reprisals from XXXX for his father's past activities-in addition to the passage of time and the information contained in the objective documentary evidence, namely, a Response to Information Request10 regarding the treatment of Lebanese who cooperated with Israel during its occupation of Lebanon, indicating that many former members of the South Lebanon Army (SLA) returned to Lebanon and that they benefitted from extenuating circumstances, receiving sentences of only one or two years' imprisonment-the objective documentary evidence also indicates that: Israeli media sources reported that, under the law, former SLA members who returned to Lebanon would be arrested at the border, but their family members would not be prosecuted.11 [22] Although the documentary evidence12 indicates that persons in Lebanon charged with spying for Israel face harsher treatment, even the death penalty, in the panel's opinion the documentary evidence does not support a finding that the family members of persons suspected of spying for Israel would be subjected to reprisals, such as a risk to life, a risk of torture, arrests or arbitrary detentions. When confronted with the objective documentary evidence and when asked to explain why XXXX would kidnap him in Israel if the families of individuals who cooperated with Israel had been able to return to Lebanon without being subjected to a risk to life, the claimant responded that his personal circumstances were unique, because of the fact that two of his brothers had served in the Israeli army and that one of them had fought in the war against Gaza. [23] The panel accepts that XXXX is able to enter Israel. However, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant failed to demonstrate that he has a profile that would give XXXX a reason to target him on Israeli soil. Considering the time that has passed, namely, that the claimant spent nine years in Israel without being targeted by XXXX, and in light of the objective documentary evidence,13 namely, that the family members of persons who cooperated with Israel are not targeted by XXXX, especially when they return to Lebanon, and considering that, according to his testimony, the members of the claimant's family have no problems in Israel, the panel determines that the claimant failed to demonstrate that he would be targeted by XXXX, especially on Israeli soil, and that the claimant is speculating when he states that he would be subjected to such a risk. [24] The panel notes that, although the claimant stated that he has feared being kidnapped by XXXX since at least 2006, he left Israel only in 2009. It is clear from his testimony that he obtained a passport in 2006 while he was a student and that he decided to finish his studies before leaving Israel. It is also clear from his testimony that he came to Canada in spring 2009 as a tourist and that he then returned to Israel. When asked to explain why he returned to Israel in 2009, the claimant testified that he had needed to put his affairs in order, such as collecting his diplomas and other personal documents, and wanted to inform his employer that he was leaving. The panel is of the opinion that the explanations provided by the claimant are not reasonable considering the alleged risk, namely, a risk to life. In the absence of a reasonable explanation, the panel draws a negative inference from the claimant's returning to Israel in 2009. In the panel's opinion, the claimant's behaviour is inconsistent with the alleged risk, namely, a risk to life. His credibility regarding the existence of such a risk is thus undermined. Omissions in the PIF [25] The panel notes that the claimant failed to mention in the initial version of his PIF,14 received on November 18, 2009, the problems he faced as a Christian in Israel, notably the incidents in XXXX and XXXX and those stemming from his putting up a XXXX XXXX in his home. It was only at the hearing held on May 9, 2012,15 that the claimant declared these incidents, specifically that he had been beaten by Christian Arabs on church property. When asked to explain why he had failed to mention these incidents in the initial version of his PIF,16 the claimant stated that the person who had helped him complete his PIF17 had suggested that he not include all the details because he would be given the opportunity to tell his entire story at the hearing and that he had to mention the main facts only. The panel does not accept the claimant's explanations, mainly because he filed his PIF18 in 2009 and was represented by experienced counsel. The panel also takes into account the fact that he did not mention these incidents in his refugee claim forms,19 but limited himself to stating that he feared XXXX and Israeli Arabs who called him a traitor. Considering the panel's finding concerning the claimant's inconsistent behaviour and the speculative nature of the possibility of reprisals from XXXX, the panel would have expected the claimant to mention in his PIF20 the incidents that he himself experienced in Israel because of his profile, especially the fact that he had been beaten, rather than stating the possibility of reprisals from XXXX and limiting himself to the fact that Arabs had called him a traitor. In the absence of a reasonable explanation, the panel draws a negative inference from the claimant's failure to declare, prior to the hearing of May 9, 2012, the incidents in XXXX and XXXX and the ones he experienced when he put up a Christmas tree in his home. His credibility regarding these incidents is thus undermined. Cumulative grounds [26] The panel nevertheless considered whether the treatment the claimant would receive as a Lebanese Christian, if he returned to Israel, would cumulatively amount to persecution. [27] The panel carefully analyzed the objective documentary evidence21 on the treatment of Christians in Israel. According to a recent Response to Information Request (RIR): The Religious Freedom Report for 2011 noted that "in practice, the government generally respected religious freedom" (US 30 July 2012, 2). In his Wall Street Journal opposite editorial, Israel's ambassador to the US stated that Israel is a country "where Christians aren't endangered" (Oren 9 Mar. 2012). The ambassador indicated that Christians have government posts and serve on the country's Supreme Court (ibid.). However, the ambassador noted that it "does not mean that Israeli Christians do not occasionally encounter intolerance" (ibid.).22 [28] Upon reading the RIR23 and all the objective documentary evidence,24 the panel recognizes that acts of vandalism have been committed at Christian sites. However, the panel considers these to be isolated acts. Even if the panel determined that the claimant was credible with respect to the incidents he experienced because he had put up a XXXX XXXX in his home, the panel is of the opinion that, in light of the objective documentary evidence,25 considering that the claimant stated that he allegedly put up a XXXX XXXX in his home only twice and that he does not allege to have been the victim of other incidents perpetrated by non-Christians, in the panel's opinion, these are isolated incidents that do not allow the panel to conclude that the claimant faces a reasonable chance of persecution because of the fact he is Christian. [29] The panel accepts that the claimant was the victim of discrimination, notably insults uttered by Israeli Arabs because he is Lebanese. However, it is clear from the claimant's testimony that he was able to study at university, despite the fact he had been admitted with the help of a professor and a former Lebanese politician, and that he was able to work in his field of study, namely as a XXXX XXXX, for the company XXXX, from 2005 until he resigned in 2009 to come to settle in Canada. The claimant did not demonstrate the he would not be able to obtain housing, study or support himself in Israel. The panel accepts that the claimant does not have the same employment opportunities or opportunities for advancement because of the fact that he did not complete his military service. However, in the panel's opinion, this does not constitute discrimination on a Convention ground. [30] From his testimony, the panel understands that the claimant mainly fears the Arabs in Israel who insulted and threatened him in XXXX and at the church. With respect to the incidents he experienced in XXXX, his city of residence, it is clear from his testimony that XXXX is a city that is almost exclusively Jewish, but that on several occasions, while walking with a friend, Arabs recognized his accent and called him a traitor. The claimant was always able to flee before being physically assaulted. It is clear from the claimant's testimony that these incidents always occurred in the same place, namely, on two main streets in XXXX, where Arabs from a neighbouring village went. It is also clear from his testimony that the claimant stopped going there to avoid problems. Considering that, according to the claimant's testimony, the problems he faced in XXXX were geographically limited to only two streets, considering that the claimant alleges that XXXX is a Jewish city, and considering that he does not allege to have had these types of problems with Jewish people, neighbours or at work, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant failed to demonstrate that he would be subjected to insults and threats throughout XXXX and that he could not avoid the streets on which he was harassed. [31] Considering that the panel found that the claimant's credibility was undermined regarding his alleged assaults in Gesh XXXX XXXX, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant failed to demonstrate that he could not practise his religion if he returned to Israel. [32] With regard to his statements at the hearing that he could not have a family, because young Arab and Jewish girls did not accept him and that the Lebanese population was limited, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant is speculating. The panel also takes into account the fact that the claimant testified that his sister in Israel is married to a Lebanese man. [33] The panel accepts that the claimant was discriminated against because of the fact that he is Lebanese and the son of XXXX XXXX. The panel also accepts that the claimant was treated less favourably in Israel than the Jewish majority. However, considering that the claimant was able to study, obtain housing and work in Israel and considering that, according to his testimony, his family members in Israel work, and other than the fact that Arabs accuse them of being traitors, they have no particular problems-despite the fact they have the same profile as the claimant, namely, that they are Lebanese Christians and family members of XXXX XXXX-the panel is of the opinion that the latter failed to establish that he would be subjected to a reasonable chance of persecution or that the discrimination he may face would cumulatively amount to persecution. He also failed to demonstrate the existence of a risk under subsection 97(1) if he returned to Israel. CONCLUSION [34] After analyzing all the evidence, the panel finds that the claimant failed to discharge his burden of establishing that there is a reasonable chance that he would be persecuted on a Convention ground. He also failed to demonstrate that, on a balance of probabilities, he would personally be subjected to a danger of torture, to a risk to his life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if he were to return to Israel. [35] For these reasons, the panel determines that the claimant, XXXX XXXX, is neither a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection." [36] The claim for refugee protection is therefore rejected.
