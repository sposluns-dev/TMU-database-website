April 21, 2020 INTRODUCTION [1] On July 4, 2016, Justice Hyslop permanently restrained Peter August-Sjodin from entering the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre, located at 1663 Little Shuswap Lake Road, Chase, British Columbia (the "Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre"). The Little Shuswap Lake Indian Band (the "Band") alleges that he failed to comply with this order on two occasions and now seeks to have him found in contempt of court. Legal Principles [25] Contempt proceedings initiated by a party are governed by the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009 [Civil Rules]. Rules 22-8(11) and (12) codify common-law requirements for notice and service. They provide: Proceeding for contempt (11) A party applying for an order for contempt must serve the alleged contemnor with a copy of the filed notice of application and all filed affidavits in support of it at least 7 days before the hearing of the application. Affidavit (12) An application under subrule (11) must be supported by affidavit setting out the conduct alleged to be contempt of court. [26] Strict compliance with Rule 22-8 is necessary. As I indicated at the hearing, I am satisfied the applicant has complied with these notice and service requirements. [27] Civil contempt generally involves private injury or wrong through the disobedience of judgments, court orders or other court processes. Nevertheless, civil contempt is quasi-criminal in nature: Carey v. Laiken, 2015 SCC 17 at para. 42. Persons accused of contempt are presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. [28] Civil contempt has three elements. Each element must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt. They are: 1. the existence of a court order that clearly and unequivocally says what should and should not be done; 2. actual knowledge of the court order by the alleged contemnor; and 3. the intentional committing of an act that the order prohibits or the intentional omission of an act that the order compels Carey at paras. 32-35. [29] The actus reus for civil contempt is an intentional breach of a court order: MacMillan Bloedel Ltd. v. Brown, [1994] B.C.J. No. 268 (CA) at para. 91. The required intention "relates to the act itself, not to the disobedience": TG Industries Ltd. v. Williams, 2001 NSCA 105 at para. 17. [30] The mens rea for civil contempt is simply the intention to do the act which is in fact prohibited by the order. The necessary mens rea for civil contempt resulting from a breach of court order is wilful, rather than accidental, disobedience of the court order. It is not necessary to prove direct intention to disobey the order, though it is necessary to prove the contemnor was aware of the order: North Vancouver (District) v. Sorrenti, 2004 BCCA 316 at paras. 11-12. [31] A person cannot be convicted of contempt for breach of a court order if he did not know of the existence of the order or its terms. Actual knowledge of the order and its terms must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt: Pintea v. Johns, 2017 SCC 23. In this regard, Rule 22-8(14) of the Civil Rules provides that service of an order is not required, as long as there was actual notice. Rule 22-8(14) provides: Service of order not necessary (14) If the court is satisfied that a person has actual notice of the terms of an order of the court, it may find the person guilty of contempt for disobedience of the order, even though the order has not been served on the person. [32] Because these proceedings are quasi-criminal in nature, the rules of strictissimi juris apply. The proceedings require strict adherence to rules of evidence, a precision in pleadings, procedure and evidence: Peel Financial Holdings Ltd. v. Western Delta Lands Partnership, 2003 BCCA 551 at para. 18 and Bassette v. Maggee, 2015 BCCA 422 at para. 35. Decision Liability for Contempt [33] I have no hesitation in finding that the Band has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. August-Sjodin committed civil contempt of court on both December 20 and 22, 2016. [34] The Band has proven the existence of a court order that clearly and unequivocally says what Mr. August-Sjodin should not do. The terms of Justice Hyslop's July 4, 2016 orders could not have been clearer. In particular, the order pertaining to the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre (order 1(d)) was clear and unequivocal in restraining Mr. August-Sjodin from entering the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre, located at 1663 Little Shuswap Lake Road, Chase, British Columbia. [35] The Band has proven that Mr. August-Sjodin had actual knowledge of this court order. Justice Hyslop's written Reasons for Judgment were sent to Mr. August-Sjodin. When Mr. Laug spoke to him after he left the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre on December 22, 2016, Mr. August-Sjodin made it clear he was aware of the above order. His response to Mr. Laug is consistent with his submissions before the court on this application - that he is aware of the order, but simply feels the court has no jurisdiction over him in this matter. [36] The Band has also proven that Mr. August-Sjodin entered the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre on December 20 and 22, 2016. Mr. Laug knows him well and saw him inside and spoke with him right after he exited the front entrance doors on December 22, 2016. He confirmed Mr. August-Sjodin's presence there that day from the video surveillance recordings. Mr. Laug also confirmed Mr. August-Sjodin's presence inside the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre on December 20, 2016 through the video surveillance recordings. Not only did Mr. August-Sjodin enter the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre on both of these days in contravention of the order, he remained inside for extended periods of time and used the pool. [37] Mr. August-Sjodin did not attempt to hide his presence inside the Quaaout Resort and Conference Centre on either occasion. In fact, he signed the pool's waiver form on both occasions, using one of his unique names. There is simply no question that the Band has proven that Mr. August-Sjodin intentionally committed an act, on both December 20 and December 22, 2016, that the order prohibits. He not only wilfully breached the order, he did so openly and brazenly. He offers no lawful excuse for doing so. I find him in contempt of court on both occasions. Costs [38] The applicant seeks special costs of this application, to be assessed. The circumstances here justify such an award. Mr. August-Sjodin wilfully and flagrantly disobeyed a court order on two occasions. He is unapologetic and remains defiant that he is not bound to comply. The Band, having obtained a court order, is entitled to have it obeyed without having to incur any further expense. That this application became necessary represents yet another example of Mr. August-Sjodin's complete lack of concern about the damage he does to the Band and his fellow Band members. His conduct is reprehensible. Penalty [39] As a general rule, civil contempt proceedings are bifurcated into a liability phase and then, if liability is proven, a penalty phase: Carey at para. 18. Having now found Mr. August-Sjodin in contempt of Justice Hyslop's July 4, 2016 order, I order Mr. August-Sjodin to attend court next on Monday, June 1, 2020 at 10 a.m., by telephone, to fix a date for a hearing to determine the penalty to be imposed.
