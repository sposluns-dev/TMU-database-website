Harrison Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Katherine Ramsey Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A ReprÚsentant(e) dÚsignÚ(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] The Appellant, XXXX XXXX, is a citizen of Hungary. He appeals the decision of the Refugee Protection Division ("RPD") dated December 11, 2019, whereby the RPD found that he was neither a Convention refugee, nor a person in need of protection pursuant to the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act1 ("IRPA"). The Appellant has not requested the Refugee Appeal Division ("RAD") to substitute its own decision that the Appellant is a Convention refugee and/or a person in need of protection, but instead, asks the RAD to set aside the RPD decision and to refer the matter back to the RPD for a re-determination by a differently constituted panel. I do note, however, that in addition to the request that the matter be returned to the RPD, the Appellant also requests any other such relief as the RAD "deems just".2 [2] The Appellant has tendered new evidence under subsection 110(4) of the IRPA and has requested an oral hearing pursuant to subsection 110(6) of the IRPA.3 DETERMINATION [3] The appeal is allowed. I substitute my own decision that the Appellant is a Convention refugee pursuant to section 96 of the IPRA and a person in need of protection pursuant to subsection 97(1) of the IRPA. BACKGROUND [4] The Appellant claims to be of Roma ethnicity and to have suffered various forms of systemic discrimination amounting to persecution during his lifetime. He alleges that he has been discriminated against with respect to his education which has resulted in compromised employment opportunities. He also alleges that as a child he was bullied and as an adult he has been physically assaulted on two occasions by right-wing members of the Hungarian citizenry. [5] The Appellant married an ethnic Hungarian woman with whom he had a child. He alleges that the woman's father was racially discriminatory towards Roma and was suspected of being a neo-Nazi. In one of the attacks, the Appellant was threatened with death by his attackers if he did not leave his wife. Following the second attack, he fled Hungary briefly for England but returned a short time later in hopes the situation with his father-in-law might have cooled down. However, the Appellant claims that his father-in-law threatened and assaulted him with a gun resulting in his flight to Canada, purportedly on the advice of a Canadian pastor that he had met years prior in Hungary but with whom he kept in touch. [6] In addition, the Appellant alleges he suffered persecution as a result of his membership in the Faith Church, a Pentecostal Christian Zionist church4 comprised of both Roma and Hungarian congregants who openly support the Jewish faith. Upon his arrival in Canada, he re-connected with a family he had known in Hungary through the Faith Church and formed a relationship with their daughter. This family had, themselves, fled Hungary several years prior and had been accepted as Convention refugees based upon both their Roma ethnicity and their membership in the Faith Church. The Appellant pursued a divorce from his wife through the Ontario court system. He subsequently married this woman and continues to worship with her and her family at Faith Church in Canada. ROLE OF THE RAD [7] My role is to review the RPD decision on a standard of correctness after having conducted an independent examination of the evidence.5 The exception to this rule is where the RPD enjoyed a meaningful advantage in the assessment and weighing of oral testimony heard or documentary evidence tendered at the hearing which may, in certain cases, allow me to defer to the RPD findings. I do not believe that the RPD enjoyed any meaningful advantage in this matter and all evidence from the RPD hearing has been taken into consideration in arriving at my decision. NEW EVIDENCE CONSIDERED [8] Subsection 110(4) of the IRPA provides that new evidence can be admitted on appeal on the following bases: (i) if it arose after the RPD rejection of the claim; or (ii) if it was not reasonably available at the time of the rejection; or (iii) if it could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have been presented to the RPD before the rejection.6 If the evidence meets one or more of the requirements set out in subsection 110(4), I must then decide whether it meets the jurisprudential requirements set out in the Raza7 decision, as modified by the Singh8 decision, that the evidence be relevant, credible and new before I can accept it. Appellant's new evidence submitted in two tranches, therefore subject to two different modes of analysis [9] The Appellant has submitted two tranches of new evidence. The first was submitted with the Appellant's record and consists of the Appellant's affidavit sworn on January 21, 2020, a letter from the Roma Community Centre ("RCC") dated XXXX XXXX, 2020 and a letter from the Appellant's current in-laws dated XXXX XXXX, 2020.9 [10] The second tranche of evidence was submitted to the Immigration and Refugee Board ("IRB") on March 4, 2020, after the Appellant's appeal had been perfected. It consists of a second affidavit sworn by the Appellant on March 2, 2020, and a letter from the Hungarian National Ethnic Minorities Advocacy Association ("EKE") dated XXXX XXXX XXXX 2020.10 As this second tranche of evidence was submitted after the appeal was perfected, not only must it satisfy the provisions of subsection 110(4) of the IRPA and the Singh and Raza jurisprudence, it must also meet the requirements of subrule 29(4) of the RAD Rules, which requires that I consider the document's relevance and probative value, the new evidence it brings to the appeal and whether the person who is the subject of the appeal, with reasonable effort, could have provided the document or written submissions with the appellant's record, respondent's record or reply record.11 General principles regarding new evidence [11] There are certain well-established principles of law regarding new evidence at the RAD that require consideration. Such principles include, for example: (i) the claimant is required to put their best foot forward at their RPD hearing;12 (ii) the RAD is not an opportunity to rectify or supplement a deficient evidentiary record;13 (iii) it is not open to an appellant to wait to forward requisite and relevant evidence until after a negative RPD determination; (iv) the Raza decision stands for the proposition that it is not the date upon which a document is created that determines its date for purpose considering whether or not it is new evidence. It is the date upon which the events in question occurred which is determinative.14 [12] With these general principles in mind, I shall now turn to an examination of the proposed new evidence. First tranche of new evidence - Appellant's affidavit is not admitted [13] The Appellant's first affidavit15 features a mixture of background information, clarification of his testimony at the RPD hearing, additional evidence which expands upon or adds to the testimony and argument taking issue with certain of the RPD findings and the manner in which the Appellant alleges the RPD erred. Specifically, the first two paragraphs provide nothing more than background information, all of which can be found within the RPD record. The third paragraph provides the Appellant's recollection of a question put to him by the panel and paragraph 4 attempts to explain the rationale behind his response and his interpretation of that question. Similarly, paragraph 5 is not evidentiary in nature and provides further explanations and interpretations of the exchange between the Appellant and the panel regarding certain Instagram accounts. Paragraph 6 simply recounts certain evidence provided to the RPD and a declaration of shock that the RPD saw the issue of his ethnicity differently than what he had anticipated. In my view, the sole purpose of these paragraphs are an attempt to argue findings of fact and/or to bolster a deficient evidentiary record that contributed to the negative RPD determination and will not be considered, as they fail to meet the test set out by subsection 110(4) of the IRPA. [14] Paragraphs 7 through 9 are explanatory of the Appellant's efforts regarding the letter from the Roma Community Centre ("RCC"). The Appellant's affidavit troubling for two reasons. First, it attempts to introduce evidence regarding his grandparents which most certainly could have been provided to the RPD. Second, paragraph 8 goes on to quote a statement purportedly made by the RCC representative supposedly providing the basis that this person found him to be Roma. That quotation indeed, the underlying reason for which the RCC representative allegedly drew his conclusion, is noticeably absent in the form letter provided as Exhibit B to his affidavit. As such, although for reasons set out below I find the RCC letter will be admitted, paragraphs 7 to 9 of the Appellant's affidavit are not admissible. [15] Paragraphs 10 and 11 are explanatory of the letter from the Appellant's current in-laws and why the evidence was not presented at his hearing. The Appellant indicates that he felt that he had sufficient evidence to establish his ethnicity and did not think such evidence would be necessary. I am not persuaded by that argument and, based upon the principle that a refugee claimant is to put his or her best foot forward at the RPD, and, based on Raza, I find that despite the date of this letter, this is information in which I consider it difficult to conceive of circumstances that it would not have been available for presentation at the RPD hearing. As such, I find that the letter from his in-laws is not admissible for purpose of this appeal. [16] Paragraph 12 represents yet another example whereby the Appellant is providing further information to bolster the evidentiary deficiencies established at the RPD hearing which may have contributed to the negative result. I note that the Appellant states, "[A]s I explained in my narrative....", and clearly, the narrative formed part of the RPD record but, in what appears to be another attempt to supplement the record, the Appellant goes one step further by suggesting the motivation behind the pastor's comments. As such, it fails to meet the test set out by subsection 110(4) of the IRPA, as it did not arise after the rejection, was reasonably available at the time of the hearing and could reasonably have been expected to have been presented at or by the time of rejection of the claim. As such, it is not admissible for purpose of the appeal and will not be considered. [17] Paragraphs 13 to 16 deals with a certain letter from the EKE that was the subject of a negative credibility finding by the RPD as it provided information that contradicted the Appellant's testimony. Once again, these paragraphs provide argument regarding the RPD finding and justification for the Appellant's testimony. Furthermore, the Appellant argues that he did not realize that he had to provide "minute detail" in his narrative. Once again, the law is clear that claimants must put their best foot forward at their RPD hearing and that the RAD is not the place to attempt to provide more substance to a deficient evidentiary record. I note that the Basis of Claim ("BOC") is quite explicit in informing claimants to include all details that may be relevant to their claim and was prepared with the assistance of counsel.16 Again, while the contents of the affidavit are not admissible, as set out below, I shall admit this letter for purpose of this appeal. [18] As per the preceding discussion, paragraphs 16 to 21 include a wealth of information in the form of testimony, all of which relates to events that preceded the rejection of the claim, all of which was reasonably available and, in my view, might reasonably have been expected to have been presented at the hearing. [19] In my view, the entirety of the Appellant's first affidavit is an attempt to supplement his testimony from the RPD hearing with additional information that clearly could have been provided before the RPD rejection. As such, the Appellant's first affidavit is not admissible as it fails to meet the requirements of subsection 110(4) of the IRPA. I shall now turn my attention to the documents attached as exhibits to the affidavit. Letter from the Roma Community Centre is admissible [20] Subsection 110(4) of the IRPA is quite clear that evidence that arose after the RPD rejection is admissible. While this letter represents a clear attempt to establish the Appellant's Roma ethnicity, an issue that was "live" before the RPD, it clearly did not exist at the time of the hearing and, regardless, I have some discretion in the admission of new evidence. In my view, it is consistent with principles of natural justice and fairness that it be considered. As such, the RCC letter will be admitted and will be weighted accordingly in my analysis of the merits of the appeal. Letter from current in-laws is not admissible [21] I find that this letter is problematic. As mentioned above, the issue of ethnicity was (and remains) fundamental to the Appellant's claim and the authors of this letter have been through the refugee process themselves and were undoubtedly aware of the need to establish ethnicity.17 In my view, what distinguishes this letter from the RCC letter is the fact that the authors are not independent or at arms-length from the Appellant - they are family and, as such, well aware of the process that the Appellant was undergoing. This letter was reasonably available at or prior to the RPD rejection or, in the alternative, it was reasonable to have expected the Appellant to present it at his hearing or before the RPD rejection. Accordingly, I find that the letter from the Appellant's in-laws is not admissible for purpose of this appeal. Second tranche of new evidence is subject to rule 29 Appellant's affidavit partially admissible [22] As with his first affidavit, the Appellant's second affidavit in support of the submission of new evidence which falls within the purview of rule 29 of the RAD Rules features argument and evidence that was given at the RPD (via his testimony). However, one component of admissibility under rule 29 is whether it could have been provided in the Appellant's record and a portion of this affidavit relates his attempts to secure the corrected version of the letter and the delay he encountered. I find that portion of the affidavit to be admissible. Corrected letter from National Advocacy Association of Ethnic Minorities is admissible [23] As indicated, under rule 29 of the RAD Rules, I am required to consider the relevance and probative value of the new evidence which a document might bring to an appeal and whether it could have been provided in the Appellant's record. I find that the corrected version of this letter addresses deficiencies in the evidentiary record which were the subject of comment by the RPD. While it is arguable that a fact check might have prevented the problem, once again, I am of the view that a certain latitude be granted to allow the corrected version to be considered. It would be speculative of me to suggest that the correct version of this letter would have altered the RPD decision but the fact remains that, in its corrected form, the letter contains information that is both relevant and probative and must be considered as part of this appeal. The corrected version of the EKE letter will be admitted and weighed in my analysis of the merits of the appeal. No jurisdiction to hold an oral hearing [24] With respect to the Appellant's request for an oral hearing, I am mandated by subsection 110(3) of the IRPA to proceed without a hearing, except in those specific circumstances set out in subsection 110(6) of the IRPA. I do not find that the new evidence, which I have found to be admissible, justifies allowing or rejecting the claim, is it determinative of the claim or central to my decision. Accordingly, the request for an oral hearing is denied.18 ANALYSIS OF THE MERITS OF THE APPEAL RPD credibility analysis was tainted [25] The RPD found, among other things, that the Appellant was without credibility with respect to the material aspects of his claim19 and that he was not a credible witness regarding his allegations that he was Roma and faced discrimination in Hungary.20 The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in its credibility finding by: (i) setting a "credibility trap" for the Appellant thereby creating an impression of bias resulting in a global credibility finding which impugned the Appellant's testimony; and (ii) engaging in a microscopic review of the Appellant's testimony to dismiss each facet of the Appellant's testimony. For reasons which follow, I find that the RPD credibility analysis was tainted by the Instagram finding which permeated the decision resulting in credibility findings that I cannot sustain. The Instagram account analysis permeates other credibility findings [26] Early in the proceeding, the RPD member asked the Appellant what he (the member) might expect to find if he looked up the Appellant's social media profile.21 Initially responding that he had no profiles, when asked "are you sure about that?" the Appellant revised his answer to indicate that he and his wife had recently downloaded "Snapchat" to post pictures.22 When asked if he had an "Instagram" profile, the Appellant replied in the negative. At this point, the panel instructed counsel to look up the Appellant on Instagram when they took a break and to report her findings back to the RPD. This instruction concluded with the comment that the member liked people to tell him the truth "and maybe you can tell me what that is, ok?"23 [27] Following the break, counsel returned and advised the panel that, in fact, the Appellant had two Instagram accounts but that they were inactive and the Appellant no longer had either the password to access the account or the email address linked to the account. In essence, these accounts, though in existence, were inactive and the Appellant appears to have had no ability to use the accounts. [28] In my independent review of the RPD reasons, I note that the panel wrote extensively on this exchange, ultimately concluding that it demonstrated a willingness to lie both to the panel and to his own counsel. The Appellant argues that by framing the questions in the manner it did, the panel set a "credibility trap" for him. While I agree with the panel that the response was clearly incorrect, I am not persuaded that the questioning was done in any sort of deliberate attempt to induce the Appellant into a trap. However, I am similarly not convinced that the exchange establishes that the Appellant intended to mislead the panel and have attempted to view this issue in a contextual manner. It appears that the accounts in question were inactive and had not been used in some time and, in fact, the Appellant was no longer capable of using the accounts as he did not have either the email account and/or the password with which they were set up. Moreover, I note that the Instagram account had nothing to do with the fundamental issues of the claim, nor did it invite further probing by the panel. Based upon the inactive nature of the accounts, I cannot agree that the incorrect response evidences a deliberate attempt to mislead the panel or lie to the member and, in my view, the adverse credibility inference that followed and which, I might add, appears to pervade the claim, was peripheral to the claim and is not appropriate. Spouse's affidavit is extremely compelling evidence [29] The RPD gave no weight to the affidavit of the Appellant's wife,24 in part because the panel felt it was not objective but particularly due to the panel's concern with the Appellant's credibility.25 I disagree with that finding which is an error; in my view, this is a clear example where the Instagram credibility finding permeates the decision and taints other credibility findings made against the Appellant. [30] With respect to the concern that it was not an objective document, the affidavit is quite upfront stating that although the spouse had been recognized as a Convention refugee herself, her application for permanent resident status has been placed on hold pending an assessment of the Appellant's paperwork.26 There was no attempt to conceal this fact which was clearly set out in the affidavit and it is self-evident that had the Appellant and his wife not disclosed that fact, it may never have come to light factoring into the decision in the manner that it did. While that may be somewhat speculative, I am of the view that it is to the Appellant's credit that the issue was disclosed from the outset, such disclosure displaying an openness that is the hallmark of credibility. Furthermore, the RPD erred in saying that it was in their combined interest for the Appellant and the spouse to "craft a statement" in support of the allegations and that her attempt to regularize her own status in Canada is contingent upon the Appellant's refugee claim.27 In fact, the affidavit states that the rest of her family have received permanent resident status but that her application is outstanding "because XXXX paperwork needs to be assessed."28 [31] There are salient facts contained in this affidavit (and the attached RPD decision29 by which the spouse and her family were found to be Convention refugees) that are relevant to and corroborative of the Appellant's claim. For example, for the spouse's RPD hearing, the panel found as fact that she and her family were members of the Faith Church in Hungary, a Christian Zionist church whose members regularly traveled to spread the word of God in public places and who actively participated in protests with other church members against anti-Roma and anti-Semitic activities of right-wing groups in Hungary at the time.30 [32] While I appreciate that this does not necessarily corroborate the Appellant's allegations that he is a member of the Faith Church, it does set a credible factual foundation of the spouse's affidavit which includes the following information: (i) that she is Roma and fled Hungary for Canada with her parents and siblings due to religious persecution, as well as persecution on the basis of their Roma ethnicity; (ii) their refugee claims in respect of both religious persecution and Roma ethnicity were accepted by the RPD; (iii) she met the Appellant at the Faith Church in Budapest where both he and his mother attended services; (iv) that as a member of the Faith Church, she and her family suffered persecution on the basis of their religious beliefs; (v) like she and her family, the Appellant is Roma, so they share the same culture; (vi) she and the Appellant became re-acquainted through the pastor of the church they attend in Canada (who is the same pastor the Appellant met in Hungary); (vii) as a Roma who has experienced first-hand persecution in Hungary, she is afraid for her husband's safety if returned to Hungary. [33] The spouse has been found by a previous RPD panel to have been credible, and she has provided an affidavit that is highly corroborative of the Appellant's testimony and is consistent with his overall claim. Her evidence overcomes credibility issues raised by the RPD regarding the Appellant's Roma identity such as clothing, language, culture and food. In my opinion, the RPD erred by giving no weight to this affidavit on either the basis of their joint interest in the permanent residency application or on the basis of the Appellant's credibility which was clearly tainted by the Instagram finding. Accordingly, I find that this affidavit is credible and sufficiently corroborates the Appellant's testimony so as to allow me (taking into account the Appellant's testimony and other evidence discussed herein) to conclude, on a balance of probabilities, that he is, in fact, Roma and that he was a member of the Faith Church in Hungary and, along with his spouse, remains an adherent of that church here in Canada. Pastor's letter is also corroborative in nature [34] Similarly, I am of the view that the RPD erred in assigning little weight to the letter provided by the pastor of "Hungarians for Canada Faith Church."31 By way of recap, the pastor had provided this letter of support indicating that he had met the Appellant at the Faith Church in Hungary when the pastor was in that country in 2012. The letter goes on to state that the Appellant had been an official member of the Faith Church to this day. The RPD dismissed the letter of support for several reasons including the fact that the member reasonably expected someone from the actual Faith Church in Hungary could have written the letter, an absence of evidence corroborating the statement that the Appellant is a member of the Faith Church and the absence of linkage between the Faith Church in Hungary and the Faith Church in Canada. With respect to the RPD, I disagree. [35] In terms of linkage, the pastor himself is the very link between Hungarian operation of the Faith Church and operations in Canada. Moreover, I am mindful that there is a unifying thread between the Appellant's spouse's affidavit, the Appellant's testimony and the pastor's letter that is compelling, credible and consistent with the Appellant's claim. Accordingly, I find that the pastor's letter provides corroboration of the Appellant's ethnicity as a Hungarian Roma, as well as his commitment to and continued membership in the Faith Church, both in Hungary and Canada. [36] Although the RCC letter and the corrected version of the EKE letter have been admitted as new evidence, given the above findings, a meaningful analysis or weighting of either letter is not required. This is especially so in the case of the RCC letter, which is directed entirely toward the issue of the Appellant's ethnicity. [37] The Appellant has provided evidence of systemic discrimination that has occurred during much of his life. This discrimination has been pervasive and extends through his educational experience to his employment prospects. He has endured discrimination based upon both his ethnic background and religious practice which I find cumulatively amounts to persecution. In addition, he has provided corroborative evidence that is, in my opinion, both credible and probative of his claim and is consistent with the objective evidence set out in the National Documentation package ("NDP") regarding treatment of Roma in Hungary and whether adequate state protection is available.32 There is a serious possibility that the Appellant will suffer persecution if returned to Hungary [38] The Appellant has a past history of being persecuted in Hungary and has presented evidence that he has (unsuccessfully) attempted to secure police protection. Based on this and the objective NDP evidence regarding treatment of Roma in Hungary, I find that there is a serious possibility he would be persecuted if returned to Hungary. Furthermore, I find that based on the foregoing and all of the particular circumstances of this matter, the Appellant will not receive adequate state protection if returned to Hungary. CONCLUSION [39] For all of the foregoing reasons and pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(b) of the IRPA, I set aside the decision of the RPD and find that the Appellant is a Convention refugee and a person in need of protection. The appeal is allowed. (signed) Christopher G. Harrison Christopher G.
