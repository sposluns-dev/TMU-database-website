The claimant alleges a well-founded fear of persecution by reason of her ethnicity as a Jew. She further alleges that she is a person in need of protection because she would be subjected to a risk to her life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment should she return to her country of origin. SUMMARY OF THE FACTS The Personal Information Form (PIF)1 contains the following facts: In 2005, she allegedly moved in with one XXXXX XXXXX, who knew that she was Jewish from the outset of their relationship. Nevertheless, his attitude toward her apparently started to change when he joined the skinheads. In XXXXX 2005, they allegedly separated at her request, and the claimant moved back in with her father. ANALYSIS Pursuant to section 962 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act, a person may be recognized as a refugee within the meaning of the Geneva Refugee Convention. Pursuant to paragraph 97(1)(b),3 a person may be recognized as a person in need of protection on the ground of risk to life or risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. The panel has heard testimony from the claimant and her uncle and has analyzed all of the evidence. Accordingly, the panel is satisfied as to the claimant's identity. As to the merits of the claim: To be candid, the panel wondered about many things during the lunch break. It is true that when a claimant swears that certain facts are true, there is a presumption that this is the case, unless there are valid reasons to doubt their truthfulness. An important indicator of a witness's credibility is the consistency of his or her narrative.4 Moreover, the credibility of the probative value of testimony must be assessed based on what is generally known about the conditions and laws in the claimant's country of origin, as well as the experiences of people who have been in similar situations in the same country.5 The claimant testified that she and her father had been accused of XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX and that, as a result, they had been detained for four days in a Moscow prison. However, in response to questions 4 a) and b) of Schedule 1 from Immigration Canada,6 the claimant stated that she had never been charged with any criminal offence in her country and that she had never been imprisoned in Moscow. She provided no reasonable explanation for these answers, which contradict her testimony. The claimant told us that her father died XXXXX but that she did not know the specific cause of death, as her father had been healthy before they were detained. However, her uncle, who was called as a witness, told us that the day after the claimant's father died, the claimant told him that he had died following the XXXXX XXXXX, as a result of these XXXXX. It is true that the claimant assumes that her father was killed in XXXXX, but the panel would be very surprised if she had told her uncle that he had died as a result of XXXXX. In the panel's opinion, the claimant would have told her uncle, "my father is dead, he was healthy, and I think it is possible that he was killed in XXXXX." However, the claimant's uncle was clear: he did not say "we assume that he was killed," he said, [translation] "straight out" and spontaneously, [translation] "he was tortured, he was XXXXX, and he died from that XXXXX". This testimony is not consistent with the evidence and, in the panel's opinion, it is implausible. The panel notes that it is also implausible that neither the claimant nor her family-there are, after all, brother and sisters-ever requested an inquiry into their father's death. In addition, during the process to obtain her visa, the claimant stated that her father had died of natural causes. In her written account, the claimant states that she was, and I quote, "Raped in a perverted way". However, when she was asked if she had gone to see a doctor following this rape, she initially answered that she had not and then later stated that she had been too upset to do so. Nevertheless, when the panel wondered about this-specifically, how her father could have taken her to the police without also taking her to see a doctor-she stated that she had not been precisely raped, that there had been no [translation] "penetration", that they had raped her orally only. For reasons that I do not need to go into in this decision, I have a great deal of difficulty believing this. I have read the documentary evidence available to me, and there is no mention anywhere of persecution of Jews in Moscow. It is possible that problems of this type exist in southern Russia, but not in Moscow. The documentary evidence indicates that there is persecution in Moscow, in Russia, of Africans and Roma, if I am not mistaken. I am referring here to the Country Report on Human Rights Practices in Russia.7 No, it is not Roma, but Asians and Africans. Nevertheless, the Country Report states, and I quote: "Muslims and Jews continue to encounter prejudice and social discrimination". In addition, in Exhibit A-2, Tab 2.1, RUS322.53.EFX8 states that anti-Semitism in Moscow is first and foremost a product of poor economic conditions and is not based on religious issues. I am also referring to a paragraph that states that conditions in Moscow are more favourable than elsewhere in Russia and that anti-Semitism is not felt in the street. The document even goes on to state that after the May 1998 bombing of his synagogue-I am referring here to Brevel Lazare-he stated that people from the wider community were very supportive toward the Jewish community. In light of the foregoing, this is the impression I have: The claimant's father passed away. The claimant's mother had died before this. The claimant had therefore lost both her parents and was unemployed in Russia. Her uncle therefore invited her to visit him in Canada. He has been in Canada since 1991, when he was granted Convention refugee status. Once the claimant arrived in Canada, she intended to stay here. This impression can be inferred from the visa notes and from the fact that she did not claim refugee protection upon her arrival in Canada, specifically on XXXXX, 2006, and that she did not leave her country following her father's death. Therefore, it is the panel's impression that the claimant decided to stay in Canada and to use the refugee process to do so. That is unfortunately my impression. Neither the testimony nor the documents on file allow me to conclude otherwise. It is true that there is a psychological report, but it does not corroborate the claimant's allegations concerning what she experienced in her country. CONCLUSION For all of these reasons and based on the above analysis, the panel has no alternative but to reject the claim for refugee protection.
