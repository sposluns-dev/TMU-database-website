DETERMINATION [14] I find the claimants not to be Convention refugees as they do not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Italy. Nor are they persons in need of protection as their removal to Italy would not subject them personally to a risk of life, to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or to a danger of torture. ANALYSIS [15] The claimants' identities are accepted. I have copies of their passports before me.3 [16] I have no reason to doubt the claimants' evidence. [17] The determinative issue in the matter is state protection. Nexus [18] The claimants allege that they are being pursued by the mafia for two reasons-first, because the female claimant is believed to know the whereabouts of XXXX XXXX, and second, because the male claimant is responsible for the arrest of his cousin, XXXX XXXX, who is a member of the XXXX. [19] This situation is akin to a personal vendetta. Victims of vendettas do not constitute a particular social group.4 In making my finding that the claimants are not members of a particular social group, I am guided by the Supreme Court of Canada in Ward.5 [20] The claimants are not members of a particular social group defined by an innate or unchangeable characteristic (such as gender or sexual orientation). Nor are they members of a group, which associates for reasons fundamental to their human dignity (such as human rights activities). Finally, they are not members of a group who are associated by a former voluntary practice, unalterable now due to its historical permanence. The claim fails under section 96 as there is no nexus to a Convention ground (race, religion, nationality, political opinion or particular social group). [21] I must then consider if the claimants are persons in need of protection as described in paragraph 97(1)(b) of the IRPA on a balance of probabilities. The standard for assessing the risk for the claimants is raised from a reasonable chance to a balance of probabilities. Some principles of state protection [22] Countries are presumed to be capable of protecting their citizens unless the state is in a complete state of breakdown. The onus is then on the claimant to provide clear and convincing proof that the authorities of said state are unable or unwilling to protect him or her.6 [23] Countries are not expected to afford perfect protection to all at all times. The fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens does not justify a claim in countries where there are military, police and civilian authorities in place, which are making serious efforts to protect their citizens.7 [24] The more democratic a country, the more difficult it is for the claimant to establish that all avenues of protection have been exhausted.8 The Federal Court has ruled that state protection needs to be adequate rather than effective per se.9 A state, then, requires a legislative and procedural framework to provide protection, and its authorities must be able and willing to implement law and procedure.10 [25] The claimants have the burden of providing clear and convincing evidence that they cannot obtain adequate state protection in their country of citizenship. Italy is a multi-party parliamentary democracy with a bicameral parliament consisting of the Chamber of Deputies and the Senate. The constitution vests executive authority in the Council of Ministers, headed by a prime minister, whose official title is President of the Council. The President of the Republic, who is the head of state, nominates the prime minister after consulting with the leaders of all political parties in parliament. International observers considered the national parliamentary elections February 24-25 free and fair. The law requires the government maintain civilian control over all security forces. Civilian authorities failed at times to maintain effective control over the security forces, and there were some reports security forces committed human rights abuses. Principal human rights problems included substandard living conditions in detention centers for undocumented mixed migrant populations, governmental corruption, societal prejudice, and municipal government policies permitting the mistreatment of Roma, which exacerbated their social exclusion and restricted their access to education, health care, employment, and other social services. Other human rights problems included excessive and abusive use of force by police in some cases, overcrowded prisons, the continued incarceration of pretrial detainees with convicted criminals, an inefficient judicial system that did not always provide speedy justice, violence, and harassment against women, sexual exploitation of children, and anti-Semitic vandalism. Trafficking for sexual and labor exploitation occurred. Observers also reported cases of violence against lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender (LGBT) persons and labor discrimination based on sexual orientation. Child labor and labor exploitation of irregular workers were also problems, especially in the service sector and the southern agricultural region. The government prosecuted and punished officials who committed crimes and abuses.11 [26] Under these circumstances, I find that the claimants' burden is high with respect to the rebuttal of state protection. [27] Claimants have an obligation to approach the state for protection providing that state protection might reasonably be forthcoming.12 Additionally, a claimant cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state13 and doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it.14 [28] In my analysis of state protection, I took a contextual approach when assessing the availability of state protection and whether the claimants have rebutted the presumption of state protection.15 The claimants seek state protection [29] When XXXX XXXX decided to collaborate with the authorities in XXXX 2010, the female claimant did not disown him as his father did. [56] I find that the documentary evidence establishes that the state protection is adequate at the operational level for the principal claimants. [57] There were some reports that security forces in Italy committed arbitrary or unlawful killings. Prosecutors examined these cases and opened investigations when they suspected unlawful killings. The law prohibits torture and other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment. Reports stated that police occasionally used excessive force against individuals, particularly, immigrants detained in connection with common criminal offences and in the course of identity checks. The national police and the Carabinieri maintain internal security. The Carabinieri is the national military police of Italy and one of the four branches of the armed forces but also carries out certain civilian law enforcement duties. The army is responsible for external security but also has specific domestic security responsibilities, such as guarding public buildings. The Ministry of Interior coordinates between the national police and nonmilitary units of the Carabinieri, and the Ministry of Economy maintains control over the financial police (Guardia di Finanza). The government has mechanisms to investigate and punish abuse, and, although there were no reports of impunity involving security forces during the year, long delays by prosecutors and authorities in completing some investigations reduced the effectiveness of mechanisms to investigate and punish police abuses.16 [58] The constitution provides for an independent judiciary. The government generally respected judicial independence, although there were isolated reports of judicial corruption. A number of court cases involved long trial delays.17 [59] In July 2013, a Pescara court convicted former Abruzzo governor Ottaviano Del Turco to nine years and six months in prison for establishing a criminal association, corruption and embezzlement in the public health sector from 2003 until 2008. Another eight individuals were also sentenced to prison terms ranging from two to nine years.18 [60] Judges allegedly colluded with organized crime in some instances. In March 2013, prosecutors in Milan requested the indictment for vote buying by a former councillor of the Lombardy regional administration. The councillor was suspected of having reached an agreement with the 'Ndrangheta crime syndicate, where he purchased 4000 votes in exchange for public contracts, jobs and $270, 000 USD.19 [61] The government has a watchdog for corruption. It is called the National Anticorruption and Transparency Office in the Ministry of Public Administration and Simplification.20 Corruption remains a central issue in Italian politics. In October 2012, a court sentenced former prime minister Silvio Berlusconi to nearly four years in prison for tax fraud, a charge that he was appealing at year's end. This was Berlusconi's fourth lower court conviction and the first since stepping down as prime minister. In February, a court in Milan ended a corruption trial against him for paying a British lawyer, David Mills, to lie for him in court; the court ruled that the statute of limitations had expired. Berlusconi still faces three other trials, including a criminal trial for allegedly paying an underage prostitute for sex. In May, police ordered a series of pre-dawn raids to clamp down on a growing match-fixing scandal that has tarnished the country's national sport, football (soccer). Umberto Bossi, the charismatic founder and leader of the Northern League, resigned in April after an investigation found that he had used party funds to pay for home repairs and purchase cars. Italy was ranked 72 out of 176 countries surveyed in Transparency International's 2012 Corruption Perceptions Index. The judicial system is undermined by long trial delays and the influence of organized crime. Despite legal prohibitions against torture, there have been reports of excessive use of force by police, particularly against illegal immigrants. Some prisons continue to suffer from overcrowding. The country continued to make some gains against organized crime in 2012. In March, police arrested 16 judges accused of taking bribes from a crime syndicate known as the Camorra in the southern city of Naples. However, the mayor of the southern town of Monasterace in Calabria resigned in April after her business was set on fire and her car shot for in what she believed was retaliation by the local mafia for instituting financial reforms. Italy is a destination country for the trafficking of women and children for sexual and labor exploitation. The government has tried to tackle the problem by prosecuting more traffickers; it also finances nongovernmental organizations that work to raise awareness of the problem and support trafficking victims.21 [62] Europol reports that Italy is one of five criminal hubs in the European Union. Italy has developed extensive legislation and administrative measure to prevent, combat and disrupt the activities of criminal networks such as the mafia. In September 2011, the Italian state adopted new legislation to combat organized crime. Harsher penalties apply to perpetrators who use their associations to intimidate and silence as well as to control and manage economic activities and concessions. Penalties are still harsher for armed organizations. Created in 1991, the National Anti-Mafia Office (Direzione Nazionale Antimafia, DNA) is the judicial coordinating body which enforces anti-mafia legislation. It comprises the National Anti-Mafia Prosecutor (Procuratore Nazionale Antimafia) and 20 deputy prosecutors. The DNA works in close coordination with the Anti-Mafia Investigation Office (Direzione Investiga-tiva Antimafia, DIA) which is part of the Department of Public Security [sic] and comprises specialized [sic] personnel tasked with intelligence-gathering and pre-judicial investigations.22 [63] Measures in legislation allow for the suspension and dismissal of local government officials. Laws against corruption and money laundering have been strengthened and complemented by additional laws.23 [64] A claimant's evidentiary burden of proof is directionally proportional to the level of democracy in the state in question-the more democratic the state's institution, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them.24 A claimant from a democratic country (such as Italy) have a heavy burden in showing that they exhausted all recourses available to them in the country they fear persecution.25 [65] The claimants have failed to rebut the presumption of state protection in Italy. The best interests of the minor claimants [66] I am sensitive to the fact that the children have been in Canada for almost a year. [67] The decision before me is not a humanitarian or compassionate one, but whether the minor claimants are persons in need of protection, given their personal experience and the documentary evidence for Italy. [68] I make the same finding for the minor claimants and for the same reasons, as I did for the male claimant and the female claimant. CONCLUSION [69] Having considered the totality of the evidence and for all of the foregoing reasons, the claims of the male claimant, the female claimant and that of the minor claimants are rejected. Furthermore, the claimants have not satisfied the tribunal, on a balance of probabilities, that they would be personally subjected to a danger of torture or face a risk to life, or face a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, if they were to return to Italy.
