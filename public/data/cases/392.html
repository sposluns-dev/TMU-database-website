I have accepted them3 because they are claimant-specific and are relevant to the issues in this case. He fears that he will be denied employment, suffer discrimination, and be subjected to violence because of his Romani ethnicity. The claimant endured discrimination and verbal and physical attacks in school. After school, the claimant tried to find work but was denied the opportunity because of his ethnicity. In XXXXX 2000, the claimant and his common-law spouse, XXXXX, were walking near a river when some people started insulting her. When he tried to defend her, they attacked him and he had to go to the hospital. In 2005, the claimant and his common-law spouse came to Canada for the first time. At that time, the claimant's sister was in the process of applying for refugee status. He did not apply because he thought that if his sister was denied her application, he would be sent home. The claimant and his common-law wife returned to Slovakia but their problems continued. They tried moving to another city, XXXXX, and also in other parts of the country but they still faced discrimination. The claimant went to police for help, but they never protected him, nor did they take his complaints seriously. In XXXXX of 2006, the claimant and his common-law partner returned to Canada. Again they did not make a claim because the claimant's sister still did not have her status. They returned to Slovakia again. The claimant's common-law partner, XXXXX, was kicked by a woman on public transportation. In XXXX of 2008, as the claimant was sitting on a park bench with other couples, they began to insult him. The claimant and his common-law spouse came to Canada for the last time in XXXXX of 2008 at which time they claimed refugee status. DETERMINATION ANALYSIS Applicable Country of Reference [4] I find that the claimant is a citizen of the Slovak Republic and no other country. Counsel for the claimant raised the issue of the claimant's possible citizenship rights or entitlements in the Czech Republic on account of the claimant's mother's birth in the territory of the Czech Republic. A true certified copy of the claimant's Slovakian passport was entered into evidence. I find that this document establishes, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant is a citizen of the Slovak Republic. I find that there are no official government documents originating from the Czech Republic in evidence which establish that the claimant is either a Czech citizen or that he is entitled to automatically obtain citizenship in the Czech Republic. I have also examined the citizenship laws4 of the Czech Republic and all the related provisions. I find that none of provisions grant the claimant automatic citizenship rights in the Czech Republic. Additionally, I note that generally speaking, the citizenship laws of the Czech Republic do not accept or recognize dual nationality, with the exception of certain limited circumstances, such as acquiring the citizenship of another country by birth or by marriage. On that basis, I find that the only country against which the claimant can advance a protection claim is the Slovak Republic. Therefore, I am only going to assess this protection claim against the Slovak Republic. Section 96 - Convention Refugee Grounds Discrimination versus Persecution [5] In his primary school years, the claimant was insulted because of his gypsy ethnicity. He was also physically beaten up by other students in his classes. The claimant's mother complained to school authorities but they told her that if she did not like the school she could send her son elsewhere. During the first few days of his attendance at a secondary school, the claimant was also beaten up by other students. He was also made fun of by girls in his secondary school who made derogatory comments about his gypsy ethnicity. On another occasion, when the claimant was 16-years-old, he was walking in the city centre when he was overheard a woman making disparaging comments about the gypsy culture to her two sons. The woman spit on the claimant. After the claimant completed his secondary school education, he was unable to find work because of his gypsy ethnicity. He had to rely on social assistance from the government and help from his parents in order to survive. When the claimant started dating a Slovakian woman, her family found out and told her that she was not permitted to go out with a gypsy and that they did not want a gypsy in the family. In XXXXX of 2000, when the claimant was walking along a river with his girlfriend, he was confronted by several men who began to make sexist comments about his girlfriend. The claimant tried to defend his girlfriend, and was beaten up. He had to go to a hospital to tend to his injuries. In XXXXX of 2005, when the claimant was travelling on public transportation with his girlfriend, the bus driver got up and told the claimant that he was taking up valuable space and that he should get off the bus so that normal people would have a place to sit. In XXXX of 2008, the claimant was sitting on a park bench when a group of girls and men arrived and sat down next to him. At first, they did not pay much attention to the claimant but afterwards began to making insulting remarks about gypsies directed at the claimant. As the claimant stood up and was about to walk away, he felt a strong punch to the back of his head. [6] The claimant has lived his entire life in the Czech Republic over a period of approximately 27 years. I find that the difficulties that the claimant experienced in the Slovak Republic during that time because of his Roma ethnicity, although they qualify as acts of discrimination and harassment, do not individually or cumulatively, rise to a level of seriousness of harm amounting to persecution. Persecution is defined as the systematic or sustained or repeated violations of one's fundamental human rights. I find that the insults, physical attacks, and denial of employment opportunities do not meet the criteria of this definition. [7] While the test for persecution or a risk to life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment is forward-looking, past experiences are often a good indicator of what might happen in the future. [8] I find that while some of these actions, previously noted, can be viewed as discriminatory, they do not rise to a level of harm amounting to persecution according to the United Nations Human Rights Commission (UNHCR). When discrimination rises to such a level that it prevents a person from accessing health care or education, or the protection of the state, or housing or employment to allow a person to survive, discrimination can become persecution. I find that the claimant has experienced harassment and discrimination in the Slovak Republic. [9] According to the claimant's Personal Information Form (PIF), he resided at the same municipal address in the Slovak Republic for almost 10 years before he came to Canada. Moreover, his PIF indicates that he was able to complete 11 years of education. Although the claimant was denied employment opportunities because of his Roma ethnicity, and was never able to find any work, the claimant was able to access social welfare assistance from the government such that he was able to survive during his adult years after completing school in the Slovak Republic. The claimant was not prevented from going to the authorities to make complaints when he was harassed and physically mistreated by racist Slovakians. According to the claimant's PIF narrative and oral evidence, he was able to approach the Slovak police for assistance when he encountered violence and harassment. [10] I find, based on all of the evidence, including the objective country evidence, that there is no serious possibility that the claimant will face treatment in the Slovak Republic, were he to return there, that would rise to a level of seriousness of harm tantamount to persecution. Therefore, I find that his fear of persecution at the hands of racist Slovakians and skinheads in the Slovak Republic should he return there is not well-founded. Although I agree that ethnic Roma face discrimination in the Slovak Republic, I find that preponderance of the documentary evidence does not establish that ethnic Roma are being persecuted in the Slovak Republic. Subjective fear [11] In 2005 and 2006, the claimant came to Canada. The claimant had relatives living in Canada who had obtained their permanent residence status here after being accepted as refugees. On both occasions, when the claimant came to Canada, his sister, XXXXXXXXXX, was also in Canada and was in the process of making her refugee claim. In 2005, the claimant did not submit a refugee claim. He did not do so because he was afraid that if his sister's claim was rejected it would mean that his claim would also be rejected and he would be sent back home to the Slovak Republic. In 2006, the claimant also failed to submit a refugee claim. He did not do so because his sister, XXXXXXXX, had not yet had her refugee claim heard and he was concerned that since he did not have a work permit there was no way that he could continue to support himself in Canada. The claimant also explained that another reason why he did not make refugee claims in Canada in 2005 or 2006 is that his common-law spouse, XXXXX, who was homesick and no longer wanted to spend any time away from her family in the Slovak Republic convinced him to return to the Slovak Republic. The claimant added that XXXXX's mother had contacted her in Canada begging her to return to the Slovak Republic. The claimant said that his girlfriend had done a lot for him and because of her emotional crisis he decided to go along with her wishes to return to the Slovak Republic. I reject the claimant's explanations for not taking serious steps to apply for refugee status in Canada in either 2005 or 2006, when he clearly had the opportunity to do so. I find that it is clear that the claimant was fleeing from alleged persecution in the Slovak Republic both in 2005 and 2006 and feared returning there. Despite that, on each occasion, the claimant failed to make a refugee claim in Canada, and did not take any other serious steps to try to regularize his status in Canada or get permanent status in Canada. Instead, both in 2005 and 2006, despite fearing alleged persecution in the Slovak Republic, the claimant returned to live in the Slovak Republic. I find that it would be reasonable to expect that a person, in the claimant's circumstances fearing alleged persecution in the Slovak Republic, would at the first available opportunity have advanced a refugee claim in Canada. However, he did not do so. Instead he returned to the very country where he feared he would face persecution in the future. I find that the claimant's conduct in failing to advance refugee claims in either 2005 or 2006 is inconsistent with a subjective fear of persecution in the Slovak Republic. [12] Therefore, I find the claimant is not a Convention refugee and the claim under section 96 of the IRPA fails. The rest of the analysis of this decision will be under section 97 of the IRPA. Section 97 - Persons in Need of Protection State Protection [13] I find that the determinative issue in this case is whether the claimant would, on a balance of probabilities,5 be subjected to a risk to his life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a danger of torture, if he were to return to the Slovak Republic. I find that the claimant is not a person in need of protection because adequate state protection is available to him in the Slovak Republic if he were to return there. [14] In assessing the issue of state protection, I am guided by a number of cases from the Supreme Court of Canada and the Federal Court. In this regard, the courts have established a number of principles relating to state protection and I have applied those principles to the case at hand. [15] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens except in situations where the state is in a state of a complete breakdown.6 To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect, absent an admission by the national's state of its inability to protect that national.7 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration, the test is whether the protection offered is adequate.8 The evidence that state protection is not adequate must be reliable and probative and it must also satisfy me, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.9 The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.10 In the absence of a compelling explanation, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim, at least where the state is a functioning democracy with a willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens.11 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not rebut the presumption of state protection.12 A claimant cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state.13 Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection.14 The fact that state protection is not perfect15 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability to provide protection.16 Indeed, if a claimant believes the actions of some police officers are corrupt, the onus is on them to approach other members of the security forces or authorities.17 There is nothing in the documentation before the Board to suggest that the Czech Republic is in a state of complete breakdown. The burden of proof that rests on the claimant increases with the level of democracy of the state in question. The more democratic a state is, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them to demonstrate state protection was or would not be forthcoming.18 The Czech Republic is a multiparty democracy19 Therefore, the presumption of state protection is a strong one. The claimant must do more than simply show that he or she went to see some members of the police force and that his or her efforts were unsuccessful.20 Even where the protective services of the home state have gaps or deficiencies, a refugee claimant who alleges a subjective fear based on criminality must, in the absence of a compelling justification, take reasonable steps to access those services.21 [16] The Slovak Republic, with a population of approximately 5.4 million, is a multiparty parliamentary democracy led by a prime minister and a 150-member Narodna Rada (National Council). Six political parties, four of which form the governing coalition, participate in the National Council. Security forces reported to civilian authorities.22 [17] Notable human rights problems included some continuing reports of police mistreatment of Romani suspects, concerns about the integrity of the judiciary, corruption in national and local government, and societal discrimination and violence against Roma. Nongovernmental organizations (NGO's) and members of the Romani community cited a continuing trend of mistreatment of Romani suspects by police officers during arrests and while in custody. The CPT also urged the government to investigate all allegations of involuntary sterilization of Romani women promptly and thoroughly. According to reports by NGO's, in July police officers in Senec beat and injured a Roman man who refused to sign what he claimed was a false confession to robbery charges. The district court in Kosice accepted prosecution charges against 10 police officers accused of abusing six Romani boys (ranging in age from 11 to 16) in Kosice following the alleged theft of a purse. The trial started in November, and was pending at year's end. Seven of the 10 policemen have been suspended from duty.23 [18] The national police has sole responsibility for internal and border security and reports to the Ministry of the Interior. Human rights observers believed police were occasionally reluctant to accept the testimony of certain witnesses, particularly Roma, and often failed to investigate cases involving Roma and other minorities promptly and thoroughly. In June of 2009, authorities arrested six police officers following an investigation that indicated they had been working as security guards in a brothel in Senec. The officers involved were removed from the police force. The most common charge authorities brought against police officers was abuse of power; other charges included battery, assault and battery, and illegal intrusion into private homes. In 2009 authorities charged 171 police officers with crimes; 61 of these cases, the crime was abuse of power. Disciplinary action ranged from fines to expulsion from the police force.24 [19] The law provides criminal penalties for official corruption; however, the government did not always implement the law effectively; and some officials engaged in corrupt practices with impunity. Instances of police corruption and misconduct were also reported, primarily the extortion of bribes during traffic stops. Headed by a director who reports directly to the interior minister, the Bureau for Inspection Service of the Police Corps is responsible for investigating police abuses. Cases may be initiated by the inspection service, the police corps, the police department's organized crime unit, and individual citizens, among others.25 [20] Government and societal discrimination against Roma and individuals of non-European ethnicity was a common problem. Racially motivated attacks on minorities (Roma and others) were widely reported throughout the year, but investigation of attacks and law enforcement varied by jurisdiction. During 2009 there were 24 convictions for racially motivated violence. During the first half of the year, there were 14 convictions for racially motivated violence. Roma were particularly singled out for violence, and police detained numerous individuals for attacks against Roma motivated by racial hatred. There were also reports that police mistreated Roma. In March of 2009 police officers in Kosice abused six Romani boys in detention. Skinhead and neo-Nazi violence against Roma and other minorities continued to be a serious problem. The League of Human Rights Activists (LPR) reported that although police were increasingly responsive in their efforts to monitor and control the skinhead movement, the problem persisted. Extreme right, nationalist, and neo-Nazi groups continued to hold events designed to intimidate minority groups. Dressed in uniforms similar to those of the Hlinka Guards (the fascist wartime militia), the groups' members held marches and rallies to commemorate the wartime fascist state and to spread messages of intolerance against ethnic and religious minorities.26 [21] The Slovak National Centre for Human Rights reported receiving 987 complaints of discrimination from January to September; in 2009, the Center received 1, 571 complaints. In most of these cases, the claims involved labor-related discrimination, especially concerning the hiring processes. Other discrimination complaints concerned the provisions of goods and services, social and health care, and education. Widespread discrimination against Roma continued in employment, education, health services, hosting, and loan practices. Activists frequently alleged that employers refused to hire Roma, whose unemployment rate was estimated to be between 80 and 90 percent. There were instances of public officials at every level defaming minorities and making derogatory comments about Roma. Inflammatory speech by government officials continued to increase tensions between ethnic Hungarians and ethnic Slovaks.27 [22] The government made efforts to address violence and discrimination against Roma and other minorities, although some critics worried that judges lacked sufficient training in the relevant laws. The government continued to implement its action plan against xenophobia and intolerance, which included a special police unit to monitor extremist activities. A commission consisting of NGO's, police, and government officials advised police on minority issues. In August the government appointed Miroslav Pollak, a non-Roma with extensive NGO experience in social field work, the plenipotentiary for Romani affairs. The government made only limited progress on its national minority strategy, which incorporated a wide range of education, employment, housing, and social integration policy recommendations from the Romani advocacy community. While the government allocated approximately 200 million Euros (approximately $268 million) of EU structural funds to projects that specifically addressed the needs of the Romani community, NGOs complained that none of the funds had been spent and that the government lacked a comprehensive approach to Romani integration. The plenipotentiary maintained five regional offices to supervise the implementation of governmental policy on Romani issues, support infrastructure development, and cooperate with municipalities and villages to improve interaction between Roma and non-Roma. The Ministry of Labor, Social Affairs, and Family assigned specially trained social workers to Romani settlements to assist with government paperwork and to advocate the importance of education and preventive health care. The Ministry of Health trained Romani-speaking health care assistants to improve the community's access to health services.28 [23] Since the entry into force of the Framework Convention in 1998, the Slovak Republic has pursued its efforts to improve the protection of national minorities. The authorities have continued to show their commitment to the implementation of this convention and have taken steps to complete the legislative framework pertaining to the protection of persons belonging to national minorities. The Slovak Republic has amended the Anti-Discrimination Law, which provides a clear legal basis for protection against discrimination. The law has introduced positive measures aiming to redress social and economic inequalities or disadvantages facing persons belonging to more vulnerable groups. [24] While a climate of tolerance and dialogue generally prevails in the Slovak Republic, negative attitudes and prejudice against persons belonging to certain groups such as the Roma, have continued to be reported. Thus, more substantial measures are needed to enhance inter-ethnic tolerance and inter-cultural dialogue. Hostile discourse by some politicians is a particular concern for the Advisory Committee. Incidents of police misconduct, especially against the Roma, continue to be reported in spite of the authorities' efforts to provide the police with training in a multiethnic environment. According to non-governmental organizations, manifestations of verbal and/or physical intolerance, motivated by hatred towards immigrants and visible minorities, are on the rise. Information received by the Advisory Committee suggests that this phenomenon may partly be related to the economic crisis. In addition, recent legislative initiatives have contributed to an increase in tension within society. The Advisory Committee notes that the Penal Code, amended in June of 2009, provides for a better protection against crime motivated by racial discrimination, by introducing aggravating circumstances for crimes committed on the grounds of national, ethnic or racial hatred or hatred based on skin colour. Moreover, the Slovak Government has adopted the Action Plan on the Prevention of Discrimination, Racism, Xenophobia, anti-Semitism and other forms of Intolerance for the years 2009-2011, containing proposals for improving the legislative and policy frameworks in this field. An Inter-Ministerial Task Force has been set up to monitor and evaluate regularly the plan's implementation. The Advisory Committee notes with satisfaction that a multi-disciplinary group composed of governmental and non-governmental experts was set up to co-ordinate actions for combating racial discrimination. The Advisory Committee regrets, however, that this body doe not meet on a regular basis and has not produced any tangible results so far. There has been a worrying increase in recent years in the number of registered ethnically-motivated crime. According to government sources, 214 such crimes were registered in 2008, representing an increase of 43% compared to 2007. The Advisory Committee welcomes the fact that law-enforcement agencies are more willing to recognize these problems and investigate such cases, which may in part also account for the increase in the number of registered cases. The Advisory Committee is concerned about the reports according to which instances of verbal and physical abuses of Roma by the police and disproportionate numbers of stop-and-search incidents affecting Roma have continued to occur. There is still widespread mistrust amongst Roma towards the police. The Advisory Committee, therefore, finds it commendable that police training on policing in a multiethnic environment, focusing on policing in areas inhabited by the Roma minority, has been carried out in recent years in order to build trust between the police and the Roma. 120 policemen have been trained so far. In addition, the authorities must ensure that misconduct by police officers is effectively investigated, prosecuted, and sanctioned and that effective remedies are available for victims of such abuses. The Advisory Committee urges the authorities to redouble efforts to record, investigate promptly, thoroughly and effectively and prosecute ethnically-motivated crime. [25] The overall situation of the Roma is a matter of deep concern. Many have experienced discrimination in employment, housing and healthcare and segregation in schools. More resolute efforts and additional resources are needed to ensure that the various initiatives undertaken result in tangible and lasting improvement of the situation of the Roma. The Advisory Committee notes with deep concern that persons belonging to the Roma minority continue to face serious problems in most areas of life, such as housing, education, employment, healthcare and social rights. Although a number of measures have been taken in the context of various programs and strategies, the situation of the Roma in terms of equality and discrimination remains a major challenge. In most sectors, there are considerable disparities between Roma and the majority population, as well as other national minorities. The Advisory Committee is concerned by discriminatory practices and attitudes against the Roma in education in spite of the authorities' efforts to improve the situation of these persons. The authorities have strengthened the use of the Slovak language through amendments to the State Language Law adopted in 2009. An adequate balance should be maintained between legitimate promotion and strengthening of the State language and the right to use minority languages in private and public life, as protected by the Framework Convention. The authorities have pursued their efforts to develop textbooks and to provide teachers giving instruction of or in minority languages with training opportunities. Schools with minority language instruction receive increased financial allocations. The Advisory Committee welcomes increased financial allocations to schools with minority language instruction. Regrettably, a decrease in interest in learning minority language has been reported amongst numerically-smaller national minorities, and Roma language teaching has not been sufficiently developed. More efforts are also needed to ensure that the curricula of mainstream schools better reflect the culture and history of national minorities. Notwithstanding the support provided by the authorities to minority organizations for the preservation and promotion of minority cultures and identity, this support does not seem to be sufficient to cover the actual needs of national minorities.29 [26] I find that the documentary evidence referred to above demonstrates that the Slovak Republic has made significant efforts to provide better protection for all its citizens, including Romas and other ethnic minorities. I find that the evidence also demonstrates that the Slovak Republic is serious about curbing crime and corruption. [27] I acknowledge that there is information in the documentation to indicate that Romas face discrimination in the Slovak Republic. However, weighed against this is persuasive evidence that indicates that the Slovak Republic candidly acknowledges this problem and is making serious efforts to rectify the discrimination and problems that exist. [28] I find that the preponderance of the objective evidence regarding current country conditions suggests that, although not perfect, there is adequate state protection in the Slovak Republic for victims of crime, including racially-motivated crimes committed against Romas, that the Slovak Republic is making serious efforts to address the problems of criminality, and that the police are both willing and able to protect victims. I find that police corruption and deficiencies, although existing and noted, are not systemic. I am of the view in canvassing the documentary evidence, that, as a whole, the issues of corruption and deficiencies are being addressed by the Slovak Republic. [29] The claimant was questioned as to what efforts he made to seek state protection before fleeing the Slovak Republic. [30] In the year 2000, the claimant experienced the first major incident. The claimant was physically attacked. It was around 8 or 9 pm and the claimant was walking along the banks of a river with his girlfriend. The claimant was confronted by a group of three or four men. The men began to follow the claimant and his girlfriend. They asked the claimant's girlfriend why she was associating with a gypsy. They made sexist remarks directed at her. The claimant pleaded with the men and asked them to leave his girlfriend out of it. The men then began to brutally beat up the claimant by kicking him in the stomach and his back. The claimant was bleeding on the ground when his girlfriend helped to pick him up and take him to the emergency department of a hospital. After he arrived at the emergency ward of the hospital, a male physician asked the claimant what happened. When the claimant described the attack, the physician did not believe him. He threatened the claimant that he would not treat him unless he told him the truth. The claimant then had to come up with a false story about how he injured himself. He told the doctor that he had fallen down a staircase. The claimant was asked if there were any other people present who witnessed his attack. He said he could not remember and that it was around 9 pm when he was attacked. [31] The day following his attack, the claimant went to a police station and reported what had happened the day before. The claimant had visible signs on him indicating that he had been seriously beaten up. The police told the claimant that they did not believe his story and told him to leave the station. The claimant got scared and left the police station. The claimant reported the beating incident to a police officer working at the front reception desk of the XXXXX police station. The police officer told the claimant to sit down and wait. Around 20-25 minutes later, another police officer came and took the claimant to a different room. After the claimant told his story again, the police officer once again told the claimant that he did not believe his story and warned him that if he was trying to make trouble concerning an incident that was not true, that he would see what kind of trouble he would be getting himself into with the police. The claimant pleaded with the police officer and told him that his story was true. The police officer told the claimant not to speak one more word or else he would get the claimant in trouble. [32] The claimant was asked if he tried to make a complaint about the police officer's conduct and the way in which he was mistreated to any senior police officers at the station. The claimant said he was scared and believed that if he complained to anyone else about the police misconduct that he would be thrown into jail for having taken such action. The claimant was asked if he made any efforts to make a complaint about this police misconduct to any other government agencies. The claimant said that he did not do so because he knew that other government agencies in Slovakia would not help gypsies who were in need. The claimant was asked which government agencies he was talking about where Roma can go to complain about police mistreatment and not get any help. The claimant responded by saying where was he supposed to go to get help, the social welfare office, the Minister of the Interior, which other organization was he expected to approach for help. The claimant said that even the mayor of his town had turned his back on the Roma since the claimant was a child. I find, given the severity of the beating that the claimant sustained, and the disrespectful treatment that he received from police officers at the XXXXX police station who refused to believe his complaint and told him to leave the police station, that the claimant's failure to take any remedial steps whatsoever or to make a complaint about the police officers' misconduct, was objectively unreasonable in the claimant's particular circumstances. I find that the claimant has not provided me with any persuasive evidence to show that there were no avenues of meaningful redress available to him in 2005 for the mistreatment that he received from police officers at the station and for their dereliction of duty in refusing to accept the claimant's complaint about the beating at face value and failing to take the appropriate steps to investigate it. [33] In 2008, the claimant was once again beaten up. The claimant was sitting on a park bench in the downtown area, near a water fountain, when a group of skinheads along with several other girls, decided to sit four or five benches away from him. When the skinheads arrived and sat down on the park bench, the claimant overheard them saying that he was a stupid gypsy who did not belong there. The claimant decided to ignore their comments and avoid any possible confrontation with them by just getting up and leaving. As the claimant was walking away from the park, he felt a strong kick to his back which caused him to end up on his back on the ground. The skinheads then proceeded to give him a brutal beating. They laughed at him and then they left the scene. It was around 1 or 2 pm in the afternoon when the claimant suffered this attack. [34] The claimant was asked if he noticed at the time of the attack if there were any eye witnesses who saw what happened. The claimant said that he was attacked near a water fountain which is in the center square and that there were many people around but nobody said a word. The claimant noticed that there were city police present in the vicinity of the area where he had just been attacked and that they were wearing black uniforms. The claimant noticed that when the skinheads started beating him up the city police, who were around 50-60 meters away, quietly turned around, glanced at him, and started walking away from the scene in the opposite direction and began to laugh. [35] The claimant was asked if he saw the face of the perpetrators at the time of the attack. The claimant responded that he did see some sort of face but was more concerned about protecting his own face and body from the blows he was receiving to his face and back. The claimant was asked if he tried to approach the city police for help as they began to walk away. The claimant responded that he did not try to approach them for help because his entire body was hurting badly and he felt so embarrassed in front of so many people who had witnessed his beating that he just wanted to go home. The claimant said that even though the city police officers had actually seen what had happened to him that they were not going to do anything about it. The claimant was asked why he believed that the police officers were not going to do anything to help. The claimant said that the city police officers had seen him being beaten and if they had been real police officers they would have done what was right by intervening in the attack and breaking it up. [36] The claimant was asked if he reported the attack by skinheads to the police after it happened. The claimant said that he went to the police station in person to make a complaint either the very next day or two days following the incident. The claimant said that they told him to get out of the police station and warned him not to make complaints about decent people unless he wanted to face problems. The claimant entered the police station and approached the reception desk and asked to speak to someone who investigates attacks. The officer on duty at the reception desk was rude to the claimant. He made the claimant wait 20 minutes until another police officer showed up. This police officer asked the claimant what happened but did not take any written notes. When the claimant started to explain to him what had happened in the park, the police officer told the claimant to leave the police station or else he would face problems. The police officer told the claimant that people like him are troublemakers. [37] The claimant was asked why he did not provide any details in his written narrative about his attempt in 2008 to report the attack that he suffered at the hands of skinheads in a park near a fountain. The claimant's explanation for this omission from his PIF narrative was that he had stated in general terms in his narrative that whenever he went to the police to complain they did not help him at all. [38] The claimant was asked if he thought about complaining to any government agency about the police officer's disrespectful conduct and his failure to accept his complaint about the skinheads and follow up with an investigation. The claimant responded that it made no sense for him to complain about the police misconduct because he did not know who to turn to for help. The claimant added that he had only heard of the Roma association during the whole time that he lived in the Slovak Republic. The claimant said that no one ever helped him out and that he was afraid to complain about the police officer because the police officer had warned him that if he did not leave the police station that he would make trouble for the claimant. I find, given the brutality with which he was attacked by a group of skinheads in a public park, that it was objectively unreasonable, in the claimant's particular circumstances, for the claimant not to have taken any steps to hold the police officer accountable for his misconduct and failure to carry out his policing duties by accepting the claimant's complaint about the skinhead attack and launching the appropriate investigation into the incident. I find that the claimant has not furnished any persuasive evidence to establish that in 2008 there were no police oversight mechanisms in place where the claimant could have made a complaint and obtained remedial action or any government agencies that could have assisted him in ensuring that his complaint about the skinhead attack was heard and acted upon by police or law enforcement authorities. I find that the objective evidence before me establishes that the authorities in the Slovak Republic do take complaints about police wrongdoing or misconduct seriously and are willing to arrest, prosecute, and discipline police officers who engage in criminal acts, get involved in corruption, or fail to perform their law enforcement duties. [39] Counsel in his oral submissions argued that I should find the claimant to have a well-founded fear of persecution in the Slovak Republic on the grounds that his sister and her children were determined to be Convention refugees on January 11, 2007. Counsel also pointed out that 17 members of the claimant's family had been accepted as Convention refugees over the past 10 years. Furthermore, counsel argued, taking into account that so many members of his family have been accepted as refugees in Canada, that I should make a determination that it is highly probable that the claimant will face a risk to his life in the Slovak Republic if he were to return there. [40] I find that the prior determinations of the Refugee Protection Division are not binding on me and that each case has to be decided on its own merits, its particular facts and circumstances, and in light of the specific objective evidence filed in the particular case before the Board. The claimant is suggesting that all of these other family members are similarly situated to him and on that basis alone I should find the claimant to be a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. I cannot make such a finding because there is no evidentiary basis to support such a conclusion. I cannot conclude that all the other family members who were accepted as Convention refugees in Canada are similarly situated to the claimant. I have no information about the factual context or legal basis for those refugee claims. Additionally, I have no transcript of the actual evidence that was heard in connection with the refugee claims advanced by the claimant's family members. I have no information about the particular facts and circumstances of the claimant's family members that caused them to come to Canada and make refugee claims. All that I have before me is a notice of decision for the claimant's sister and her children. The claimant has not provided any reasons that explain why the Board made such a determination in January of 2007. [41] I have considered the documentary evidence submitted by counsel. One article30 in counsel's materials is entitled, "Neo-Nazism on the Rise in Eastern Europe". This article states: "...Last November in the eastern Slovak city of Kosice three right-wing extremists beat up a 16-year old dark-skinned girl of Slovak and Cuban origin while yelling Nazi slogans. It also states that racist attacks, mostly against the Roma and foreign students, are not unusual in Slovakia. The police registered 188 racially-motivated crimes, including violent attacks, in 2006, an increase from 121 cases in 2005...:" [42] In another article31 submitted by counsel entitled, "Slovakia getting worse for minorities", it states: "...the Council of Europe has warned in a report that attacks and political racism directed against Jews, Roma and Hungarian minorities have increased since the far-right Slovak National Party joined the government in 2006. Minority rights groups say that the report highlights government reluctance to deal with racism, and the effect of that reluctance on societal tolerance of anti-minority sentiment..." [43] Counsel for the claimant also produced in his supporting materials a report from the European Roma Rights Centre,32 dated July 30, 2004. In this report, it states: "....To date, measures adopted and undertaken by the Slovak government have been insufficient to ensure the effective implementation of the International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination ("Convention"). As to article 2, the government has not fully complied with its obligation to "prohibit" and bring to an end, by all appropriate means, including legislation...racial discrimination"....As to Article 3 of the Convention, the ERRC is concerned that the government of the Slovak Republic has failed to prevent, prohibit, and eradicate the racial segregation of Roma....As to Article 4, anti-Romani hate speech is a regular part of public discourse in Slovakia...Provisions of the criminal code sanctioning hate speech have rarely if ever been applied in cases where Roma are at issue....As to Article 5, Roma have, in recent years, suffered violence at the hands of both law enforcement and non-state actors, in violation of "the right to security of person and protection by the State against violence or bodily harm" protected under the Convention. In several recent cases, Roma have been killed by Slovak police officers while in police custody. Furthermore, authorities continue to fail to provide Roma with adequate protection against racially motivated violence perpetrated by members and sympathizers of national-extremist movements and other vigilante groups....The ERRC and its partner organizations have documented violations of the rights of Roma to be free from discrimination in the exercise of economic and social rights, as protected by Article 5. Roma in the Slovak Republic suffer discrimination in the fields of employment, housing, health care, and access to social assistance and social welfare benefits. ....." [44] Based on my review of the documentary materials on the Slovak Republic, I acknowledge and accept that members of the Roma community continue to face discrimination in employment, housing, education, health care and access to social services. I also acknowledge and accept that there continue to be reported cases of acts of violence carried out against Roma by extremist groups, including skinheads. Furthermore, I acknowledge that there have been instances where the Slovak police have failed to respond with prompt and thorough investigations following racially motivated acts of violence and hatred carried out against members of the Roma community. I also acknowledge that there has recently been an escalation in the number of hate speeches being made against the Roma community by members of extremist groups and politicians which has stoked the fires of racial tensions in the country. [45] However, I find that there is no persuasive evidence to establish a complete breakdown of the state apparatus in the Slovak Republic, nor do I find any persuasive evidence to show that the police in the Slovak Republic systematically or routinely deny Roma protection against threats and violence perpetrated by nationalistic and extremist Slovak groups and skinheads. [46] On the whole, based on my review of the preponderance of the documentary evidence, I find that, despite the magnitude and severity of the problem facing ethnic Roma in the Slovak Republic, in light of the continuing acts of violence perpetrated against Roma, the escalation in the number of hate speeches being made in public against the Roma, the open confrontations and provocations against Roma during demonstrations and marches organized by extremists, the authorities of the Slovak Republic are making serious efforts to protect members of the Roma community who are victims of threats, harassment, or violence at the hands of Slovakian extremists and racists. [47] In summary, I find that there is no persuasive evidence before me to establish a systematic pattern of conduct on the part of the law enforcement agencies and the government of the Slovak Republic that is demonstrative of a lack of state protection for Romani victims of violence and hatred. [48] I find, in the particular circumstances of this case, that the claimant did not make diligent efforts, nor did he take all reasonable steps in seeking out the protection of the authorities when he was attacked by skinheads in the Slovak Republic. I find that the claimant has not produced sufficient reliable, relevant, and probative evidence to convince me that adequate state protection is not available to ethnic Roma who are targeted by racist and neo-Nazi skinheads in the Slovak Republic. I find, therefore, that the claimant, in the particular circumstances of this case, has not rebutted the presumption of adequate state protection in the Slovak Republic with clear and convincing evidence. I am not persuaded that the Slovak Republic would not be reasonably forthcoming with adequate state protection, should the claimant seek it. [49] Therefore, I find the claimant is not a person in need of protection and the claim under section 97 of the IRPA fails. CONCLUSION [50] I determine that the claimant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. Therefore, the Refugee Protection Division rejects his claim.
