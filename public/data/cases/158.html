The Refugee Appeal Division ("RAD") previously dismissed the appeal. The RAD's decision was set aside by the Federal Court on judicial review. According to a Judgment of Mr. Justice K.M. Boswell, dated July 29, 2015, the appeal was referred back to the RAD for redetermination by a differently constituted panel. The Appellant submitted new evidence in his appeal and he did not request an oral hearing under subsection 110(6) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act ("IRPA"). After perfecting his appeal, he filed an application to file new documents. After the Federal Court decision, he filed another application to file new documents. He requests that the appeal be allowed and the decision of the RPD be set aside and the RAD find the Appellant to be a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection or alternatively, that the matter be returned to the RPD for re-determination. DETERMINATION OF THE APPEAL [2] The appeal is dismissed. Pursuant to ss. 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the decision of the RPD that the Appellant is not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. ROLE OF THE RAD [12] The Huruglica1 decision of the Federal Court provides guidance in relation to the appropriate standard that must be applied by a specialized tribunal such as the RAD, to decisions of the first level administrative tribunal. Justice Phelan states in paragraphs 54 and 55 of the foregoing decision: [54] Having concluded that the RAD erred in reviewing the RPD's decision on the standard of reasonableness, I have further concluded that for the reasons above, the RAD is required to conduct a hybrid appeal. It must review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of whether the claimant is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. Where its assessment departs from that of the RPD, the RAD must substitute its own decision. [55] In conducting its assessment, it can recognize and respect the conclusion of the RPD on such issues as credibility and/or where the RPD enjoys a particular advantage in reaching such a conclusion but it is not restricted, as an appellate court is, to intervening on facts only where there is an "overriding and palpable error". [13] Accordingly, the RAD will review all aspects of the RPD's decision and come to an independent assessment of whether the Appellant is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. Deference will be afforded to the credibility findings of the RPD or to other findings where the RPD has a particular advantage in reaching its conclusions. [14] The RPD determined that the Appellant was not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection for the following reason: The Appellant was not credible "...on material aspects of his claim due to material omissions, inconsistencies and implausibilities and failure to make a claim elsewhere." New Evidence [15] The Appellant filed the following document as new evidence with his appeal record: (a) A Freedom of Information/Privacy Act Request, dated March 11, 2014, to USA authorities for the release of documents relating to his applications for a USA visa. [16] After perfecting his appeal, the Appellant filed an Application to File New Documents, pursuant to RAD Rules 29 and 37: (b) A letter from XXXX XXXX, Evangelist, XXXX Blvd., Church of XXXX, dated March 19, 2014. [17] After the Federal Court Decision, the Appellant filed another Application to File New Documents, pursuant to Rules 29 and 37: (c) Ontario Marriage Licence; (d) Undated letter from XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX; (e) Certificate of Baptism and Confirmation for XXXX XXXX XXXX, from XXXX XXXX XXXX Church, XXXX, Ontario, dated XXXX XXXX, 1994; (f) Photographs; and (g) Affidavit of the Appellant, sworn August 25, 2015, and the interpreter's affidavit. [18] Section 110(4) of the IRPA provides that the Appellant may present only evidence that arose after the rejection of his claim or that was not reasonably available, or that he could not reasonably have been expected2 in the circumstances to have presented, at the time of the rejection. [19] It is for the Appellant to make full and detailed submissions in his Memorandum about how any proposed new evidence meets the requirements of section 110(4) and how that evidence relates to the Appellant.3 [20] Section 110(4) establishes a disjunctive test.4 The RAD must first consider whether the proposed new evidence arose after the rejection of the refugee claim. If it did, the evidence meets the requirements of the section. If it did not, the RAD must consider whether the evidence was reasonably available for presentation to the RPD prior to the rejection of the claim. If it was not reasonably available at that time, it meets the requirements of section 110(4). If the evidence did not arise after the rejection, and it was reasonably available, the RAD must consider whether the Appellant could reasonably have been expected, in the circumstances, to have presented the evidence to the RPD prior to the rejection of the claim. If he could not have been expected to do so, the evidence meets the requirements of the section. [21] If the evidence did not arise after the rejection of the claim, was reasonably available, and could reasonably have been expected, in the circumstances, to be presented to the RPD prior to the rejection of the claim, the evidence does not meet the test in section 110(4). The RAD does not have discretion to admit such evidence.5 [22] As the RAD understands it, section 110(4) provides the Appellant with an opportunity to present evidence that could not have been submitted to the RPD-either because the evidence did not exist at the time of the RPD proceeding, or because it did exist but was unavailable, or because it did exist and was available, but the Appellant could not have been expected to present it to the RPD. [23] In considering whether the evidence arose after the rejection of the claim, the RAD will not restrict its analysis to the date on the proposed new evidence. In the RAD's view, a document's "newness" cannot be tested solely by the date of its creation; what is important is the event or circumstance sought to be proved by the evidence.6 Old evidence remains old evidence, even if it is placed in a new document with a recent date. [24] The RAD acknowledges that the Federal Court has stated: ...in order for there to be a 'full fact-based appeal' before the RAD, the criteria for the admissibility of evidence must be sufficiently flexible to ensure it can occur. Often, the evidence at stake will be essential for proving the factual basis of the errors the claimant alleges were made by the RPD. This consideration becomes all the more pertinent in light of the strict timelines a claimant now faces for initially submitting evidence before the RPD. A claimant now has 50 days to present all documents from the date he or she made the claim; the previous legislative scheme required the documents 20 days prior to a hearing, which, on average, took much longer to take place. When the RPD confronts a claimant on the weakness of his evidentiary record, the RAD should, in subsequent review of the decision, have some leeway in order to allow the claimant to respond to the deficiencies raised.7 [25] The Court also held that the "...main issue is whether the evidence 'was not reasonably available, or that the person could not reasonably (or normally according to the French version) have been expected in the circumstances to have presented.'"8 [26] The Freedom of information Request does not provide any evidence refuting the RPD findings about the visa information. It does not give any information about the visa itself or the application for the visa. The results of the Request may have been relevant but they are not provided. The mere fact of the request is not relevant and therefore it will not be admitted in evidence. [27] RAD Rule 29 provides: 29. (1) A person who is the subject of an appeal who does not provide a document or written submissions with the appellant's record, respondent's record or reply record must not use the document or provide the written submissions in the appeal unless allowed to do so by the Division. (2) If a person who is the subject of an appeal wants to use a document or provide written submissions that were not previously provided, the person must make an application to the Division in accordance with rule 37. (3) The person who is the subject of the appeal must include in an application to use a document that was not previously provided an explanation of how the document meets the requirements of subsection 110(4) of the Act and how that evidence relates to the person, unless the document is being presented in response to evidence presented by the Minister. (4) In deciding whether to allow an application, the Division must consider any relevant factors, including (a) the document's relevance and probative value; (b) any new evidence the document brings to the appeal; and (c) whether the person who is the subject of the appeal, with reasonable effort, could have provided the document or written submissions with the appellant's record, respondent's record or reply record. [28] The letter from Church of XXXX, dated March 19, 2014, is almost identical to the letter which the Appellant produced to the RPD.9 However, the RAD will admit the letter in evidence and consider it with the other evidence in the matter. The RAD will also admit into evidence the documents submitted after the Federal Court decision and they will be considered with the other evidence in this matter. Request for Summons [29] The Appellant states that the biometric report showing that the Appellant made two visa applications to the USA is hearsay since what was produced was a computer record. The Appellant denies that the information contained therein is correct. The Appellant requests that the RAD issue a summons to a responsible official from Canada Border Services Agency ("CBSA") and compel that official to provide the original copies of the information provided to Canadian authorities and also to request from the USA counterparts, hard copies of those applications that are reflected in the biometric result. [30] The biometric report was submitted as evidence before the RPD and considered by the RPD. The document was submitted by the Minister under subsection 110(5) of the IRPA. No application for a summons in relation to this report was made before the RPD. The Rules of the RAD only provide for the issue of a summons when the RAD has ordered an oral hearing. Rule 62 provides that in deciding whether to issue a summons, the Division must consider any relevant factors, including the necessity of the testimony to a full and proper hearing, the person's ability to give that testimony, and whether the person has agreed to be summoned as a witness. [31] Section 110(3) of the IRPA provides that the RAD must proceed without a hearing, on the basis of the record of the proceedings of the RPD and may accept documentary evidence and written submissions from the Minister and the person who is the subject of the appeal. Section 110(6) provides that the RAD may hold a hearing if, in its opinion, there is documentary evidence referred to in subsection 110(3) of the IRPA that raises a serious issue with respect to the credibility of the person who is the subject of the appeal; that is central to the decision with respect to the refugee protection claim; and that, if accepted, would justify allowing or rejecting the refugee protection claim. These sections indicate that the documentary evidence must be admitted by the RAD as new evidence and then a hearing can be held to test the credibility of the evidence. In this case, there is no actual document that is being submitted and a summons is requested in order to deal with a document that was produced before the RPD and considered. The request is denied. Submissions [32] The Appellant submits that the RPD erred in finding that there would not be future risk by determining that the Appellant has "...not established that he would face harm rising to the level of persecution should he return to Turkey on account of his ethnicity and or religion."10 The RPD was unclear in applying different standards at different locations in its decision. [33] The Appellant argues that the RPD erred in failing to conduct a separate analysis under s. 97(1)(a) or (b) of the IRPA. [34] The Appellant further argues that the RPD erred by determining that the absence of corroborative evidence was a negative factor. This should not be a negative factor unless there is a valid reason to doubt the Appellant's evidence. There were no, or not sufficient, valid reasons to doubt credibility. [35] The Appellant also submits that the RPD erred in considering his failure to claim in the USA showed that he lacked subjective fear. The Appellant quotes from the book The Law of Refugee Status stating that the relevant subjective fear is said to be one of being persecuted in the country of origin. An applicant's prolonged stay in an intermediate country might, at most, indicate a lack of fear with respect to conditions in that country. A less lengthy stay would not signify even that much. In neither instance was the applicant's failure to claim refugee status in the intermediate state necessarily indicative of his or her level of fear with respect to persecution in the country of origin.11 [36] The RPD erred in finding it implausible that the Appellant would be charged in relation to perceived proselytization on the XXXX. ANALYSIS [37] The RPD made credibility findings and considered documentary evidence about the treatment of Armenian Orthodox Christians in Turkey. The RPD then stated "I find that the claimant has not established that he would face harm rising to the level of persecution should he be returned to Turkey on account of his ethnicity and or religion." The Appellant argues that the RPD applied a higher test than provided by the law.12 [38] The test for whether a refugee claimant has a well-founded fear of persecution was set out by the Federal Court of Appeal.13 The evidence must be established on a balance of probabilities, while the likelihood of persecution is subject to a different test: whether there is a reasonable chance of persecution. This test can also be described as "good grounds", a "reasonable possibility", a "serious possibility", or "no more than a mere possibility" of persecution. The Court elaborated on the standard, holding that "reasonable chance" occupies the field above the lower limit of the minimal or mere possibility and below the upper limit of a 50% chance or probability.14 [39] The Federal Court has held that "more than a mere possibility" is a misstatement of the test in Adjei, which is better phrased as "reasonable chance", "reasonable possibility", "serious possibility", or "good grounds". The Court observed in obiter that the word "reasonable" should be used as a qualifier in the test, and therefore "reasonable chance" and "reasonable possibility" are the most appropriate formulations of the standard of proof.15 [40] The sentence objected to by the Appellant comes at the conclusion of an analysis entitled "Likelihood of Future Persecution". The first sentence of that section sets out the test for persecution and a conclusion based on that test. "The panel then considered whether the claimant would face a serious possibility of persecution in Turkey due to his religion (Orthodox Christian) and ethnicity (Armenian)." This correctly states the test to be applied. The RPD then states "It finds that the claimant would not face such a serious possibility." [41] The RPD then went on to state that the Appellant was asked what would happen should he be returned to Turkey and he replied that he would never be able to find work and he would face systemic discrimination and harassment as an Armenian Christian. The RPD the noted that he did not allege that he proselytized or that he is a Christian missionary or a political activist (despite attending some demonstration in the past). The RPD assumed that because his previous work for many years was working on his family's XXXX farm and also selling clothes in a mall, that he would be able to do this type of work again, even living openly as a Christian. The RPD also noted that he worked on a XXXX that allowed Christian missionaries to come on board regularly, so it did not seem likely that he would be denied employment at such a place on account of his religion. [42] The RPD then went on to consider documentary evidence stating how different faiths are treated in Turkey. It concluded that some of the documents revealed that there is some societal discrimination against Christians but overall they are able to practice their religion freely as the Appellant alleged that he did before he left Turkey, since he stated he attended church every Sunday. It was after this analysis that the RPD stated it found that the claimant had not established that he would face harm rising to the level of persecution should he be returned to Turkey on account of his ethnicity and or religion. Based on the statement at the beginning of the section, the RPD found that he did not face a serious possibility of persecution. Although the final statement does not correctly state the test, the RPD did apply the proper test of serious possibility in reaching its decision. [43] The Appellant alleged that he was persecuted in Turkey because of his political activities. He provided a document that was referred to as a "CHP membership card". The RPD found that it was not a card. It was a magnified photocopy with extremely poor resolution showing his name (in handwriting) and a CHP logo at the top. There is no date of issue. He did not produce the original of this card. The RAD agrees with the RPD that little weight can be assigned to this document. [44] The Appellant stated in his Basis of Claim ("BOC") narrative that he left his hometown in 1998 because of threats he had received due to his "leftist activities". He moved to Istanbul and his family followed. The RPD pointed out to him that in Schedule A with his application for refugee protection, he listed his residence as XXXX, Turkey from XXXX 2003 to XXXX 2010 and that he worked on the family farm during that period of time. When he was asked about this inconsistency he stated that he worked six months on his family's XXXX farm and six months a year he lived in Istanbul. The RPD found that he exaggerated when he alleged that he moved to Istanbul in 1998 due to threats he received for his political activities. The RAD finds that there is no serious possibility that the Appellant will be persecuted for any political activities if he returns to Turkey. [45] The Appellant submits that it was unreasonable for the RPD to find that he was not a Christian based only on documents presented. His own oral evidence should also have been considered, although he does not specify the oral evidence to which he refers. He submits that the RPD implies that it accepted that he is Armenian Orthodox, because it considered his profile within that religious community, which would be unnecessary if there was a finding that he had not established his religious identity. The Appellant further argues that the RPD was being disingenuous in stating that as a "hidden Armenian" he should not fear being perceived as Armenian. In the Appellant's submission, the RPD erred in finding that he was not an activist in the church, as his BOC narrative contradicts this finding. [46] The RPD found that the evidence produced was not credible in certain aspects of his religious affiliation. The RPD found that he had not adequately explained why his Turkish identity card listed his religion as "Islam". The RPD also dealt with the letter from "XXXX", an Armenian Orthodox Church in Istanbul, but it just referred to a number as being a Turkish citizen and a member who has participated in rituals and taken lessons. The letter is an undated, handwritten note. No address is provided and there is no letterhead on the page. No details are provided as to the length of time the member attended this church or the duties, if any, undertaken there. The RAD agrees with the RPD that this does not provide any persuasive evidence that the Appellant practiced as a Christian in Turkey. [47] The Appellant stated that the precipitating factors leading to his coming to Canada and asking for refugee protection began in the winter of 2013 when he was working on XXXX XXXX. Two nationalists found a Turkish Bible in his room. He was attacked, beaten, and threatened to be thrown overboard. When they returned to Turkey in XXXX 2013, the two nationalists reported him to the police and claimed that he was involved in missionary activities. He states that he was arrested by the police where he was questioned and beaten. He was released after two days. He then left the country and returned in XXXX 2013, where he was questioned by the police. He says he was then required to report to the officials whenever he travelled abroad. [48] The Appellant produced a medical report dated XXXX XXXX, 2013, by a doctor from the XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX State Hospital. It is a radiology report interpreting a medical test involving the spinal cord. It does not detail any injuries or prognosis. The RAD agrees with the RPD that this does not corroborate that the Appellant was attacked in XXXX 2013 while working on XXXX XXXX. He did not produce any further documents in regard to his alleged arrest or charges. The RPD stated that it did not find credible the Appellant's central allegations of being arrested due to his Christian activities. The RAD agrees with this finding based on the preceding analysis. [49] The letter from XXXX XXXX dated March 19, 2014, provided as new evidence, contains the same information from the same church that the Appellant provided to the RPD. The letters indicate that the Appellant wanted to become a Christian and that he was subsequently baptized in Canada. There is also two letters from friends who reside in Canada confirming that he is from a Christian family. The letters do not give any other details. The documents produced by the Appellant after the Federal Court decision are intended to show that he married a Canadian woman who is a Christian. There is a letter from his wife stating that she is a Christian and that their household celebrates Christianity. There are also pictures of what appears to be a wedding ceremony. There is also an Ontario marriage license showing that the date of the marriage as XXXX XXXX, 2014, as well as a certificate of baptism and confirmation for his wife dated XXXX XXXX, 1994. The certificate appears to be from a Roman Catholic Church in XXXX, Ontario. The Appellant submits that these documents are relevant and probative as they corroborate that he is a Christian and that he is living in a Christian relationship and living a Christian lifestyle. [50] The RAD finds that these documents corroborate the RPD's finding that the Appellant became a Christian after coming to Canada and that he was not forced to leave Turkey because of his religious beliefs. [51] The RPD then went on to consider whether the Appellant would be persecuted on his return to Turkey as an Armenian Christian. The Appellant indicated that he would never be able to find work and that he would face discrimination and harassment as an Armenian Christian. He did not allege that he proselytized or that he is a Christian missionary. The RPD noted that he worked for many years on his family's XXXX farm and he also sold XXXX in a mall. He would be able to do this type of work again, even living openly as a Christian. He worked on XXXX XXXX that allowed Christian missionaries to XXXX XXXX XXXX regularly so it does not seem likely that he would be denied employment again at such a place on account of his religion. [52] The RAD reviewed the country documentation relating to Armenians and Armenian Christians. Documentary evidence stated that the Constitution provides a single nationality designation for all citizens and does not expressly recognize national, racial, or ethnic minorities. The country's law is interpreted to recognize only three non-Muslim minorities: Armenian Orthodox Christians, Jews, and Greek Orthodox Christians. On December 7, 2012, the Second Criminal Court convicted Okan Bas, an ultranationalist columnist, of hate speech for praising the 2007 murder of prominent Turkish-Armenian journalist Hrant Dink and imposed a five year suspended sentence. Like the previous year, the gatherings on April 24, 2012, to commemorate events relating to the Armenian issue and the tragic events of 1915, were peaceful and received police protection where necessary. On January 17, a court ruled that Yasin Hayal was guilty of masterminding the murder of Hrant Dink. The Court sentenced him to life imprisonment and two other men to 12 years imprisonment each for helping him. Dink's family and human rights associations denounced the verdict, claiming that the court failed to address state officials' alleged involvement. On January 19, at least 40,000 persons marched in Istanbul to commemorate Dink's life and call for justice.16 [53] The Constitution and other laws and policies generally protect religious freedom, although there are some laws, policies, and constitutional provisions that restrict religious freedom. There were reports of abuses of religious freedom, including the imprisonment of at least one conscientious objector for his religious practice. The trend in the government's respect for religious freedom did not change significantly during the year. In general, members of religious groups that had formal recognition during the Ottoman period, including the Greek Orthodox, Armenian Orthodox, Syrian Orthodox, Armenian Protestant and Jewish communities, reported that they had freedom to practice their faiths.17 [54] Although religious speech and conversions are legal, some Muslims, Christians, and Baha'is faced government restrictions, surveillance, and occasional harassment for alleged proselytizing or providing religious instruction to children. In general, members of religious groups formally recognized during the Ottoman period, including the Greek Orthodox, Armenian Orthodox, Syrian Orthodox, Armenian Protestant, and Jewish communities, reported they had freedom to practice their faith. However, the government placed significant restrictions on the administration of their churches and synagogues, such as the requirement that leaders of the Armenian apostolic, Greek Orthodox, and Jewish communities be citizens.18 [55] Only Greek Orthodox, Armenian Orthodox, and Jewish religious community foundations may operate schools under the supervision of the Education Ministry. Although the Constitution stipulates that no one shall be compelled to reveal his or her religious beliefs, there is a space in which to note one's religious affiliation on national identity cards. Applicants must either leave the religious identity section blank or choose from the following: Muslim, Greek Orthodox, Christian, Jew, Hindu, Zoroastrian, Confucian, Taoist, Buddhist, no religion, or other.19 [56] Some of the documents reveal that there is some societal discrimination against Christians, but overall they are able to practice their religion freely. The RAD finds that there is not a serious possibility that the Appellant would be persecuted on account of his Christian religion or his Armenian ethnicity if he returned to Turkey. [57] The Federal Court has consistently held that a separate section 97 analysis is not required if there is no evidence that could go to establishing the person is in need of protection.20 On the basis of the above evidence, the RAD finds that, on a balance of probabilities, the Appellant will not be at risk to his life or at risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment or torture if he returns to Turkey. The RPD did not err in failing to provide a section 97 analysis since there was insufficient credible evidence to establish that the Appellant was a person in need of protection. DISPOSITION [58] The appeal is dismissed. Pursuant to section 111(1)(a) of the IRPA, the RAD confirms the determination of the RPD that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection.
