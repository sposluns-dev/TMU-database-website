XXXXXXXXXXX, (the "claimant"), a 41-year-old citizen of Syria, claims a well-founded fear of persecution at the hands of the Syrian authorities because he is a Christian. His wife, XXXXXXXXXXX, and their two minor children XXXXXXXXX (a.k.a. XXXXXXXXX) and XXXXXXXXX, base their claims on that of the claimant. The panel designated XXXXXXXXXXX as the representative for his two sons. In addition, XXXXXXXXX claims to be a person in danger of being tortured, at risk of losing his life or being subjected to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment in Syria. Allegations The claimant alleges that he is an Armenian Christian and that as a result of his faith the Syrian authorities and the Sunni majority have persecuted him and his family and will continue to persecute him if he were to return to Syria. He claims that Sunni Muslims would threaten and harass his family by calling them "dirty people" because they were Christians. His sons faced harassment by other boys who used to kick and push them because they were not Muslim. The claimant alleges that his wife would be assaulted with garbage and buckets of water because she worked outside the home and refused to be covered. The claimant alleges four specific instances of abuse. In 1999, he claims that Sunni Muslims smashed the church windows with stones and bottles and that he received multiple cuts over his body as a result of being struck by broken glass. In 1995, the claimant alleges that he was physically assaulted and called "foreigner" when he complained about fumes to the owner of a printing factory located in the basement of the claimant's residential building. While working at an auto electronic shop between 1983 and 1992, the claimant alleges that he was cheated by clients who would not pay for work done on their cars or demanded repairs be done for free. Finally, in or about 1985, the claimant states that his car was stolen and that several weeks later he saw it being driven by a policeman. Determination The panel finds that the principal claimant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. Since the principal claimant's wife and two children have relied on his claim, which claim includes alleged incidents relating to his wife and his children, the claims of his wife and his children must also fail. Analysis The determinative issue is whether there is an objective basis for the claimant's fear of persecution on the basis of his Christian faith, should he return to Syria. The panel finds that there is not a valid basis for the claimant's fear. The United Nations Handbook states in paragraph 54: Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn a livelihood, his right to practise his religion, or his access to normally available educational facilities.1 While the claimant's wife took her post-secondary school abroad, the evidence reveals that the claimant successfully completed high school in Aleppo and that his two children were attending elementary school. Neither the claimant nor his wife indicated that their children were subjected to restricted access to normally available educational facilities. Indeed, as opposed to being exclusive of Christians, the documentary evidence indicates that the school system tries to accommodate Christians. Officially, all schools are government run and nonsectarian, although some schools are run in practice by Christian and Jewish minorities. There is mandatory religious instruction in schools with government-appointed teachers and curricula. Religious courses are divided into separate classes for Muslim and Christian students.... Although Arabic is the official language in public schools, the Government permits the teaching of Armenian, Hebrew, Syriac (Aramaic) and Chaldean in some schools on the basis that these are "liturgical languages." Religious groups are subject to their respective religious law on marriage, divorce, child custody, and inheritance.2 There was no evidence to suggest that there were serious restrictions on the claimant's right to earn a livelihood. For the last 8 years, the claimant had been gainfully employed as a technical expert and prior to that as an auto electrician. The claimant's wife had more recently taken up fashion design. As an auto electrician, the claimant indicated that some of his clients refused to pay him or wanted him to work for free simply because he was an Armenian Christian. The panel is of the view, however, that this is a challenge faced by businessmen universally irrespective of nationality or faith. Moreover, even if these events were prompted by the claimant's faith or nationality, there is no evidence suggesting that the claimant was faced with similar experiences as a technical expert during the last 8 years. The claimant alleges that he would be persecuted for his Christian faith if he were to return to Syria; however, the documentary evidence does not support this allegation. The former editor of Arabies and Jeune Afrique magazines stated in July 2000: Christians in Syria enjoy freedom of religion and are well protected by the regime. They maintain very good relations with the authorities and do not face discrimination in employment; many of them hold high-ranking positions in the military and the government.3 The Middle East Council of Churches, a Christian organization based in Cyprus with offices in several countries in the Middle East that conducts studies on human rights, justice, and interreligious dialogue, stated that: Christians in Syria are free to practice their religion openly. As is the case during Muslim feasts, so too during Christian festivals religious services are broadcast on Syrian TV. These include sermons of the three patriarchs and the archbishops resident in the capital, as well as other bishops and leading clergy of the churches throughout the country.4 On the Saturday before Easter Sunday in 1999, when vandals allegedly smashed the windows of the church, the claimant testified that the church was so crowded with worshippers that scores of congregants had to gather outside the church where they listened to the services over a broadcasting system. In the panel's view, these Christians were practising their faith openly. The panel finds that the claimant would not face a serious possibility of persecution for his Christian faith if he were to return to Syria. The claimant asks the panel to look upon the vandalised church in Aleppo as illustrative of the bigotry he would expect if he were to return to Syria. The panel finds, however, that there is a lack of credible evidence to support the occurrence of such an event. Human rights monitors are generally anxious to alert us to this type of violence, however, there is no objective documentation reporting on it. The U.S. Department of State Report on Human Rights Practices in Syria for the year 2000,5 for example, reports on the vandalism of a synagogue in Damascus in October 2000 and then outlines how the Government took immediate steps to ensure that the Jewish community would be protected from further attacks. In the panel's view, the vandalism of a Christian church would be the subject of the same type of coverage by human rights monitors. The panel finds, therefore, that the silence of the documentary evidence on the alleged violation of the church is an indication that the event did not occur. The claimant alleges that he was assaulted by the owner of the print factory located in the basement of his apartment building. The panel notes that this was a single isolated event that occurred 7 years ago and that there was no further abuse even though the claimant and his family continued to reside in the same building until the day they came to Canada. For those reasons, the panel finds that the event has no probative value in determining the well foundedness of the claimant's fear of persecution should he return to Syria. Similarly, the alleged theft of the claimant's car is of no probative value to the claimant's claim. Not only did it occur over 15 years ago, some years before the claimant married his wife, there was no credible evidence presented that linked the theft to the claimant's faith. The claimant alleged that every time his wife went out someone would grab her body and squeeze her breasts because she would not be covered. Once or twice a week, according to the claimant, Muslims would throw garbage at his wife and at other times they would throw buckets of water at her. The panel is mindful of the fact that the PIFs of the claimant and his wife, signed 10 months prior to the hearing, originally alleged in the narrative that the claimant's wife was threatened when she would go out because she was failing to set a good example for Muslim women. The panel is of the view that, on its own, such conduct would be discriminatory and not persecutory in nature. The further allegation that men would grab her on the street was only added to the narrative at the commencement of the hearing. The squeezing and grabbing of the claimant's wife's breasts was a further addition only introduced during the oral testimony of the claimant. In the panel's view, the grabbing of the claimant's wife on the street and the squeezing of her breasts by strangers are events of such significance that they would not have been overlooked by the claimant and his wife when they initially prepared their narrative of significant incidents causing them to seek protection outside of Syria. In that these events were only added to the claim on the day of the hearing leads the panel to conclude that they are not factual events and were embellishments invented by the claimant in an effort to advance his family's claim. There are inconsistencies between the claimant's testimony and that of his wife with respect to the allegations that the claimant's wife had garbage and buckets of water thrown at her by Muslims. The inconsistencies related to how often these events would occur and the source of the garbage and the water. The testimony of both the claimant and his wife regarding these events was also vague and so lacking in detail that the panel does not find them believable. Although he raised the issue, the claimant failed to put forward any evidence on the treatment of failed asylum seekers who are returned to Syria. The claimant indicated that he could not recall any incidents of failed asylum seekers in Syria. The documentary evidence is silent on the issue of returnees to Syria. The panel finds, however, that the silence of the recent reports monitoring human rights abuses with respect to the issue is an indication that there are no serious problems for returnees. It is the finding of the panel, therefore, that the claimant does not have a well-founded fear of persecution if he were returned to Syria, having left without authorization of the Syrian authorities. Conclusion The panel finds that there is insufficient credible and trustworthy evidence that establishes that the claimant has a well-founded fear of persecution by reason of any of the Convention grounds. For the same reasons, the panel also finds that there is no serious possibility of the claimant being subjected to a danger of torture, or a risk to life, or a risk to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment if he returns to Syria.
