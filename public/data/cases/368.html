ANALYSIS [24] For the purpose of the subject application the significant documents are the separation agreement dated April 28, 2015 and the consent divorce order dated March 15, 2016. The latter effected the civil divorce of the parties and that matter is complete. [25] The parties agree that the separation agreement formed the basis of the divorce order; as the respondent puts it, the former was "rolled into" the latter. For this reason, I will primarily refer to the March 2016 consent divorce order. There are no issues about child or spousal support; the former continues until N. is no longer a child of the marriage and the latter continues to 2020. Similarly, as agreed, the respondent paid mortgage payments until April 2017. There is reference in some documents to a car but that also is no longer an issue. [26] Then there is the April 2015 oral agreement dealing with an Iranian divorce. That divorce has not yet taken place as a result of a dispute over the approval of documents related to the Coquitlam property and the respondent's insistence that the Iranian divorce take place before the documents were completed. The documents were not completed and the claimant says she lost a $20,000 deposit on a new apartment because of this. There is also a dispute about the valuation of the 5 kilograms of 24 carat gold described in the mehr. [27] As above, the Coquitlam property was the family home. The claimant and N. lived there after separation and, pursuant to the April 2015 separation agreement, the respondent made mortgage payments for two years. When those payments ended in April 2017 the claimant had to sell the home because she could not afford to make the mortgage payments herself. [28] The Coquitlam property was sold pursuant to the February 2017 court order set out above. At the point of sale, the respondent remained on title. He had agreed to the sale rather than being removed from title but, as will be seen, this nonetheless caused a number of problems. [29] As also described above, one-half of the net proceeds of the sale are being held in the trust account of counsel for the claimant, pending this judgment. The other half has been paid to the claimant. [30] The parties are agreed that the claimant was to be the sole owner of the Coquitlam property and, indeed, the 2015 separation agreement and 2016 consent divorce order are unequivocal on this point. Section 9 of the former expressly states that the title of the property will be transferred to the claimant's name after "approval" of a mortgage. Similarly, s. 4 of the divorce order is as follows (reproduced as written): 4. Claimant #1 [the claimant] and Claimant #2 [the respondent] own a property located at [Coquitlam property]. The parties agree that for the next 2 year starting November 1, 2015 Claimant #2 will pay the amount $2150 the expense of matrimonial home until April 30, 2017. The title will be transferred upon the approval of mortgage of the Claimant #1. [31] Despite these agreements the title of Coquitlam property was not transferred to the claimant and she was not the sole owner on title when the property was sold in 2017. How that came to be is very much in dispute. It has also become more complicated by the respondent's assertion that he is entitled to the one-half of the net proceeds of the sale being held in trust. [32] The respondent points out that the sale of the Coquitlam property was not contemplated by the separation agreement or divorce order. However, he says he intended that any proceeds from the sale of the property after the agreement and order would be divided equally. This means that the March 2016 divorce order is ambiguous and incomplete and, therefore, the separation agreement should be set aside under s. 93(5) of the FLA because of significant unfairness. [33] The claimant says that the divorce order is clear: she demonstrated to the respondent that she had approval of a mortgage and title should have been transferred to her. Instead, the respondent refused to follow the order and that cost her a $20,000 deposit she put down on a new apartment. She now seeks that as damages. [34] Overlaying these issues is the oral agreement of April 2015 which was intended to result in an Iranian divorce. [35] I will proceed by considering the respondent's application to set aside the separation agreement and/or divorce order under s. 93(5) of the FLA, the issue of the "approval" of a mortgage and then the oral agreement and mehr. [36] In addition, there are issues about parenting time but those are essentially new issues rather than being based in the previous orders and agreement. That is, the respondent applies to change the parenting time as recorded in the order and that is currently in place. [37] The parties have not been able to agree on the division of household items. [38] Therefore, the following issues require consideration: (a) Whether the divorce order and/or the separation agreement should be set aside under s. 95(3) of the FLA; (b) The dispute over "approval" of a mortgage; (c) The mehr and the oral agreement; (d) Sharia divorce; (e) Household items, and (f) Parenting time; (a) Section 93(5) of the FLA [39] The respondent seeks to set aside the April 2015 separation agreement and/or the March 2016 divorce order under s. 93(5) of the FLA because they are significantly unfair. The claimant says there was no unfairness because she was to receive the respondent's interest in the Coquitlam property in exchange for giving up her mehr that she values at $270,000. [40] I reproduce all of s. 93 as follows: Setting aside agreements respecting property division 93 (1) This section applies if spouses have a written agreement respecting division of property and debt, with the signature of each spouse witnessed by at least one other person. (2) For the purposes of subsection (1), the same person may witness each signature. (3) On application by a spouse, the Supreme Court may set aside or replace with an order made under this Part all or part of an agreement described in subsection (1) only if satisfied that one or more of the following circumstances existed when the parties entered into the agreement: (a) a spouse failed to disclose significant property or debts, or other information relevant to the negotiation of the agreement; (b) a spouse took improper advantage of the other spouse's vulnerability, including the other spouse's ignorance, need or distress; (c) a spouse did not understand the nature or consequences of the agreement; (d) other circumstances that would, under the common law, cause all or part of a contract to be voidable. (4) The Supreme Court may decline to act under subsection (3) if, on consideration of all of the evidence, the Supreme Court would not replace the agreement with an order that is substantially different from the terms set out in the agreement. (5) Despite subsection (3), the Supreme Court may set aside or replace with an order made under this Part all or part of an agreement if satisfied that none of the circumstances described in that subsection existed when the parties entered into the agreement but that the agreement is significantly unfair on consideration of the following: (a) the length of time that has passed since the agreement was made; (b) the intention of the spouses, in making the agreement, to achieve certainty; (c) the degree to which the spouses relied on the terms of the agreement. (6) Despite subsection (1), the Supreme Court may apply this section to an unwitnessed written agreement if the court is satisfied it would be appropriate to do so in all of the circumstances. [Emphasis added]. [41] As a first consideration, the respondent's submission raises a question as to the relationship between the separation agreement and the divorce order because the submission is not clear whether one or both are challenged. [42] Comparing the two documents, they were drafted by the parties. The divorce order includes the granting of a divorce and has a provision about a car. The separation agreement has some sections not in the divorce order: a provision on debts (as well as "Background" and "Living Separate and Apart") and there is different language for the issues of parenting time and property. The separation agreement sets out that the respondent was to pay mortgage payments on the Coquitlam property for two years. He completed this in April 2017 and that same provision is not in the divorce order. [43] The separation agreement also says that the respondent will be removed from title of the Coquitlam property on the day of a divorce in this court (and "subject lender(s) Bank(s) approval"). The divorce order does not include this statement, presumably because it was made on the date of the divorce (to take effect in 31 days). [44] Other than those differences, as the respondent puts it, the separation agreement was "rolled into" the divorce order. [45] In this context the respondent says that "[t]he court may look at the Separation Agreement to determine the completeness of the divorce order and in doing so may set aside the Separation Agreement as 'substantially unfair' under s. 93(5) of the Family Law Act." [46] It is not clear, but it appears that the respondent's objective is to set aside the divorce order (he consented to) by challenging the separation agreement. Assuming that is the case, it is fully answered by pointing out that, in legal terms, a divorce order can exist independent of a previous separation agreement. Further, I take it as self-evident that a court order can be in addition to or replace a private agreement. Here, as the respondent points out, the divorce order for the most part replaced the separation agreement. Section 214(3) of the FLA is also applicable: Orders respecting agreements 214 (1) If an order is made to set aside part of an agreement, the part is deemed to be severed from the remainder of the agreement. (2) A court may incorporate into an order all or part of a written agreement respecting a family law dispute made by the parties to the proceeding and, unless the court orders otherwise, (a) the order replaces that part of the agreement that is incorporated, and (b) the remainder of the agreement remains effective. (3) Unless the court orders otherwise, if an agreement and an order made after the agreement provide differently for the same subject matter, (a) the order replaces the part of the agreement that provides differently for the same subject matter, and (b) the remainder of the agreement remains effective. [Emphasis added]. [47] If there is a part of the separation agreement that "remains effective" and is not in the divorce order, neither party identifies it or relies on it. [48] The result is that the divorce order is the primary document with respect to the legal relationship between the parties from March 2016 onward. That is, the respondent must set aside the divorce order and not just the separation agreement if he is to succeed. [49] The significance of this conclusion is that, as can be seen above, s. 93 is titled "Setting aside agreements respecting property division." Under s. 93(5), an agreement can be set aside if it is significantly unfair on consideration of specific factors. This provision applies to agreements and not court orders such as the divorce order in this case. I acknowledge that a court order can be set aside on the basis of fraud, duress or other serious grounds: Pond v. Pond, 2017 BCCA 243. However, the respondent does not rely on those grounds and nor is there evidence of them. He relies on s. 93(5) of the FLA but, again, it applies to an agreement and not an order. [50] This alone is sufficient to dismiss the application of the respondent under s. 93(5) of the FLA to set aside the March 2016 consent divorce order. I will briefly address other issues raised in the respondent's submission. [51] The respondent says the divorce order is incomplete because it is "totally silent" about the 2017 proceeds from the sale of the Coquitlam property. That makes the separation agreement and/or the divorce order significantly unfair, according to the respondent. Put another way, if the divorce order (or separation agreement) does not give the respondent a half share in the proceeds of the 2017 sale then it is incomplete and must be set aside as significantly unfair under s. 93(5) of the FLA. [52] It is true that the divorce order is silent about the sale proceeds from the sale of the Coquitlam property. The separation agreement is also silent on this point. This is not at all surprising since neither party intended or considered the sale of the Coquitlam property at the time of the 2015 agreement or 2016 order. Therefore, it is not at all surprising that neither document did or could anticipate the sale. When an agreement does not contemplate future events it can be reopened for those events if the parties agree to do so. There is no such agreement here. In the absence of such an amendment the parties to an agreement must rearrange their affairs to deal with the unanticipated event or events. Here the respondent seeks to rearrange the separation agreement and/or the divorce order after they were executed. [53] The respondent says he intended that the separation agreement and divorce order would apply to the increase in value of the Coquitlam property when it was sold so that he would receive a one-half share of the increase. He says that he never would have entered into the separation agreement and consented to the divorce order if he knew how much the property value would have increased. Further, the divorce order is incomplete because it did not include this protection for him. Put in terms of s. 93(5), it would be significantly unfair not to change the divorce order to reflect the manifest intention of the parties that it would change with future changes in the value of the Coquitlam property and that increase would be shared equally. Since the order is incomplete it should be set aside. [54] However, just as the separation agreement and divorce order are silent about any future sale they are also silent about any entitlement the respondent might have to the proceeds of the sale. There is no evidence these matters were discussed or even contemplated in April 2015 or March 2016. [55] Overall, the respondent's submission is no more than his unilateral wish in 2017 about how the separation agreement and divorce order would operate if he knew in 2015 or 2016 what he knows now. How he could predict the future in that way is not explained. The claimant obviously disagrees with the respondent on his view and, therefore, there is no basis for an agreement between the parties on this issue. I add that, if the respondent's submission was correct, it would also mean that, logically, any reduction of value would result in the re-opening of the divorce order. The respondent obviously does not take that position. I also add that, assuming it is relevant, there is no evidence comparing the value of the Coquitlam property at the time of separation and the time of the 2017 sale so it is not known if the claimant was the beneficiary of a significant windfall. [56] It follows that I do not agree with the respondent on this point. The fact that the separation agreement and/or divorce order did not contemplate the sale of the Coquitlam property at a future time when its value had increased (from the values at the dates of the agreement and order) does not make the agreement or order significantly unfair under s. 93(5). [57] A further difficulty for the respondent is that the issue in dispute under the divorce order relates to the provision about property. Section 215(1) of the FLA provides for a change in an order if there has been a change in circumstances since the order has been made. However, s. 215(2) states that "a court may not change, suspend or terminate an order" made under Part 5 ("Property Division") except as provided in that Part. Section 93(5) is in Part 5 and it is discussed above. [58] Section 93(3) is also in Part 5. It says an order or part of an agreement can be set aside or replaced where there are problems with disclosure of documents, improper advantage by one spouse over the other, a spouse did not understand the nature or consequences of "the agreement" and other common law grounds that would cause all or part of a contract to be voidable. The respondent's application does not rely on s. 93(3) and nor is there evidence of any of the circumstances described there. [59] The jurisprudence is consistent with the statutory restriction on review of orders involving property. Where a final order respecting property has been entered, the court has jurisdiction to vary it only in two narrow circumstances: where there has been a "slip" in drawing the order or where the order fails to express the manifest intention of the parties such that it requires the prevention of an injustice: Ting v. Wong, 2015 BCSC 1808 at paras. 12-21, citing Shackleton v. Shackleton, 1999 BCCA 704, at para. 12, Buschau v. Rogers Communications Inc., 2004 BCCA 142, at paras. 26-27; Sandhu v. Sandhu, 2012 BCSC 1183 at para. 54; and McLachlan v. McLachlan, 2013 BCSC 1733 at para. 114. [60] Here there is no suggestion of a "slip"; the respondent's challenge to the divorce order goes much further than that. He seeks a half-interest in the 2017 proceeds of sale from the Coquitlam property. [61] While it is not specifically addressed, I take from the submissions of the respondent that the March 2016 order must be set aside because it fails to express the manifest intention of the parties and it, therefore, creates an injustice. As above, the focus of the respondent's challenge to the divorce order is that the Coquitlam property has significantly increased in value since the order was made and he should benefit from that increase. [62] I have considered this above and there is no evidence of a manifest intention or any intention that the parties intended that any future increases in the value of the property would be shared equally. The title to the property was supposed to be transferred to the claimant in July 2016 when she confirmed with the respondent that she had mortgage approval. I take from this that the claimant was to have the benefit of any increase in the value of the property, as well as take the loss for any reduction in the value. [63] Other case law does not support the respondent's submission. These were reviewed by Justice Fleming in McLachlan and they involved similar situations where the value of property changed after the issuing of an order: Gilpin v. Gilpin (1990), 1990 CanLII 1576 (BC CA); Akkor v. Roulston, 2009 BCSC 1584; and Hodgkinson v. Hodgkinson, 2004 BCSC 1630, aff'd 2006 BCCA 158. [64] In Gilpin, Justice Lambert pointed out that "[c]ontinuing fairness is impossible to achieve. Fairness at a particular point in time is the legislative goal" (at pg. 259). This was under the former Family Relations Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c 121. The Court noted at that time (i.e. before the FLA) that there were numerous judgments that made it clear that, as a matter of fairness, the valuation date should be the date of trial, unless there is reason for a contrary date (pg. 255). I note that some of these judgments involved situations where the value of family property had decreased since the previous agreement. I can find no principled reason for finding that an increase in value should have a different result. [65] Overall, a final order respecting division of property is not subject to variation (except after a successful appeal). Even if it was reviewable, under s. 87(b), the valuation date is the date of an agreement (in this case, April 28, 2015) or the date of the divorce order (March 15, 2016). And, in any event, the authorities do not support the respondent's submission that an agreement can be set aside as unfair because the subject property has increased in value (or decreased in value). Finally, there is no evidence that the manifest intention of the parties was that the agreement and order would be opened if the value of the Coquitlam property increased or decreased. [66] As a final issue, and assuming there is a legal basis for reviewing the separation agreement and/or the divorce order, I turn to whether the parties' agreement was in fact significantly unfair. This requires consideration of the separation agreement, the divorce order and the oral agreement between the parties. It is clear that they considered all three of a piece. For example, in a heated text message exchange on July 28, 2016 (set out in detail below) the respondent stated, "my decision to giving you the house and the car and paying two years of the mortgage was based upon the Iranian divorce and you signing off your mehr " The claimant accepts this was agreed. In these proceedings the respondent values the mehr at $45,100. [67] It is questionable whether the respondent, a mortgage broker and realtor, would agree to the transfer of his one-half share of the Coquitlam property for $45,100 when it was to be sold in April 2017 for $727,000. Again, there is no evidence the parties discussed the value of the mehr on its own as part of the separation agreement or the divorce order. Nor is there evidence that they compared that value with the value of the Coquitlam property. Obviously, if the mehr is valued at $270,000 the agreement between the parties is more fair than if the mehr is valued at $45,100. The problems with the evidence about the valuation of the mehr are discussed below and it is not possible to make a decision on that issue at this time. And, in any event, there a number of other problems with the respondent's submission that he is entitled to one-half of the proceeds, as discussed above. [68] The respondent's application to set aside the March 2016 divorce order under s. 93(5) of the FLA is dismissed. The result is that the order must be considered as valid, subject to its interpretation which I will address next. [69] Assuming the respondent's submission is also a challenge to the 2015 separation agreement (on its own or as the basis of the 2016 divorce order), I also conclude that it must be dismissed. As with the divorce order it is not an issue of unfairness under s. 93(5) of the FLA for an agreement to not anticipate and address unforeseen future events such as the sale of the Coquitlam property in 2017 in this case. [70] It follows that the submission of the respondent that the separation agreement and divorce order entitles him to a half-interest in the proceeds of the 2017 sale of the Coquitlam property must be dismissed. Those proceeds are to be paid out to the claimant forthwith and such payment is not subject to any condition. [75] The formation of agreements on financial matters on the breakup of a marriage and their interpretation has been discussed in previous judgments. I adopt the following from Reid v. Reid, 2011 BCSC 231 at paras. 25 -28, cited in Stanbridge v. Stanbridge, 2015 BCSC 1468 at para. 31: [25] It is well-established that settlement agreements must meet the requirements of contractual formation: offer, acceptance, consideration and an intention to create legal obligations: S. (O.H.) v. S. (M.J.), 2004 BCSC 600, at para. 22; Jorgensen v. Jorgensen, [1996] B.C.J. No. 1153 (QL) (S.C.) aff'd (1998), 56 B.C.L.R. (3d) 124 (C.A.). [26] Settlement agreements made upon marriage breakdown, and for the purpose of resolving issues such as property division and spousal support, are generally referred to as "separation agreements". While these agreements are subject to the requirements of contract formation, such are not applied with the same rigour in a matrimonial context. In this regard, our courts have recognized the need to achieve fairness as between the parties: Mitchell v. Mitchell, 2000 BCSC 1367, at para. 35. [27] In addition to the foregoing, for a settlement agreement to be enforceable there must be a "meeting of the minds" of the parties on the essential elements of the agreement: Fridman G.H.L., The Law of Contract in Canada, 5th ed. (Toronto: Carswell, 2006) at p. 14. The burden of proving consensus between the parties falls to the party seeking to prove the existence of the agreement, and the standard to be met is proof on a balance of probabilities: Bell v. Bell (1998), 80 A.C.W.S. (3d) 705 (B.C.S.C.). [28] In determining whether the parties were ad idem at the time of contract formation, the manifested intentions of the parties are considered. Generally, the courts apply an objective, rather than a subjective, test and consider whether a reasonable person in the situation of the party would have believed and understood that the other party was consenting to identical terms: Smith v. Hughes (1871), L.R. 6 Q.B. 597 at 607, adopted by the S.C.C. in St. John's Tugboat Co. Ltd. v. Irving Refining Ltd., [1964] S.C.R. 614. The starting point for determining whether the parties were ad idem is the contract, itself; however, where it is unclear whether or not the parties have agreed, the court may resort to the evidence beyond the contractual language, including the circumstances in existence at the relevant time, the genesis and aim of the transaction, and the conduct of the parties during, and subsequent to, the purported making of the contract: Langley Lo-Cost Builders Ltd. v. 474835 B.C. Ltd., 2000 BCCA 365, at 18, and Aerovac Systems Ltd. v. Darwin Construction (Western) Ltd., 2010 BCSC 654, at 26. [Emphasis added]. [76] Returning to the language of the separation agreement and divorce order, I accept that the sentence in the latter that "[t]he title will be transferred upon the approval of mortgage of the Claimant #1" is not clear because it does not specifically say who will approve the mortgage. [Emphasis added.] [77] From this I conclude it is quite clear that the parties' intention was that what was needed was the approval of a lender or bank, not the approval of the respondent. (d) The mehr and the oral agreement [120] In broad terms the parties present the separation agreement and divorce order as reflecting their civil marriage, separation and divorce. There is no reference in the separation agreement or the divorce order to the Iranian marriage of the parties. That is appropriate since this court does not have jurisdiction over substantial issues relating to Sharia law. According to the respondent the elements of this oral agreement were that the parties would obtain a Sharia divorce. The respondent also says that the claimant agreed to forgo her mehr, valued by him at $45,100, in exchange for the respondent paying mortgage payments on the Coquitlam property for two years ($2,150 a month; totalling $38,700); transfer of a car to the claimant (valued at $8,127); and payment by the respondent of spousal support at $263 per month. As he stated in his text message of July 28, 2016, in his mind he transferred his one-half interest in the Coquitlam property to the claimant in return for an Iranian divorce and the claimant forgoing her mehr. [122] As a first comment on the respondent's account of the oral agreement, the numbers do not add up. He says the claimant gave up her mehr, valued by the respondent at $45,100, for three payments: $38,700 for mortgage payments over two years, $8,127 for the car and spousal support of $263 paid over five years (commencing November 1, 2015). The total amount of these items is $62,607. The respondent does not say that he was being generous and, indeed, except for a short period after separation, he has demonstrated a lack of generosity. The result is that I am unable to give much credence to the respondent's evidence about the financial aspects of the oral agreement. [123] For her part, the claimant agrees there was an oral contract and that it was in April 2015. She also agrees that the agreement included a Sharia divorce and the respondent was to pay mortgage payments and spousal support in the amounts and over the time periods as described by the respondent (which he has done). And the claimant agrees that she said she would forgo her mehr. She does not mention the car. She does agree that the oral agreement was to be implemented at the same time as the Coquitlam property was transferred to her. [124] Overall, setting aside the respondent's quite unreliable description of the financial aspects of the 2015 oral agreement, it can be said with some confidence that there was an agreement that the parties would divorce, the respondent would pay spousal support and mortgage payments and the claimant would forgo her mehr. The transfer by the respondent of his one-half interest in the Coquitlam property was part of the separation agreement and divorce order but also related to the Iranian divorce in two respects. First, the Iranian divorce and the transfer of the property were to take place at the same time and, second, the claimant forgoing her mehr was part of the consideration for the respondent entering into the separation agreement and divorce order. The evidence does not support a conclusion that the car was part of the oral agreement (there is a transfer of a car on November 1, 2015 included in the March 2016 divorce order). [125] The transfer of the property never took place because of the respondent's insistence that he had to approve a mortgage on the property in favour of the claimant (then his insistence that he had to approve any sale) and the Iranian divorce had to take place before the property was transferred. I have found above that he was wrong on both counts. [126] I next turn to consideration of the mehr in the Iranian marriage deed. The legal issues of a mehr were usefully discussed by Justice Fisher (as she then was) in Mohammadi v. Mohammadi, 2016 BCSC 1873: Marriage portion (dowry) [45] The parties' Iranian marriage certificate includes a "Marriage Portion" which provides as follows: A volume of Holy Koran, a pane of mirror, a pair of candleholders, which are all submitted to the Wife and she admitted receipt, plus 700 Full Bahar Azadi Gold Coins, being the husband's liability and payable to the Wife upon her demand. Signed by the two spouses. [46] The respondent testified that the value of the 700 gold coins referenced in the marriage portion is approximately $276,000. It appears that the dowry is a contract between the husband and the wife. The respondent described it as both a contract between the families and a contract just between the parties. She said that she has never claimed this amount and did not plan to claim it in Iran, but at the same time, she has never given up her right to claim it. [47] The claimant testified that the marriage portion of the marriage certificate was purely symbolic. He never had the equivalent of 700 gold coins and that number was based on a numeric calculation of the respondent's name. His understanding is that the dowry is security for housewives in Iran who do not work. In any event, he said that the respondent had agreed not to seek her dowry in Iran and submitted that she should not be able to do so here. [48] Similar claims to enforce provisions of these kinds of marriage documents have been accepted in British Columbia where the evidence established that they met the definition of "marriage agreement" in the former Family Relations Act, RSBC 1996, c. 128 (repealed): Delvarani v. Delvarani, 2012 BCSC 162; N.M.M. v. N.S.M., 2004 BCSC 346; Amlani v. Hirani, 2000 BCSC 1653. Given that the Iranian marriage certificate in this case was signed in 1999, the provisions of the Family Relations Act apply to issues involving the enforcement of marriage agreements, per s. 252(2)(a) of the Family Law Act. [49] The Family Relations Act, in s. 61(2) defined a "marriage agreement" as an agreement entered into by 2 people before or during their marriage to each other to take effect on the date of their marriage or on the execution of the agreement, whichever is later, for (b) ownership in, or division of, family assets or other property during marriage, or on the making of an order for dissolution of marriage, judicial separation or a declaration of nullity of marriage. [50] In Delvarani, the court reviewed these cases and concluded that the marriage portion of an analogous religious marriage certificate "has been interpreted to be in the nature of a reverse dowry or sum of money agreed upon by the parties, which is to be paid by the husband to the wife in the event of a separation" (at para. 202). However, it was emphasized that the evidence must support that conclusion in each case. [51] In Amlani, the court was satisfied that it was a term of the marriage contract that the "marriage portion" amount ("maher" or "mehr" in that case) was payable upon dissolution of the marriage. A similar finding was made in N.M.M. In both of these cases, the marriage portion was enforced as a marriage agreement. In Delvarani it was not, as the judge was not satisfied that an agreement had in fact been reached. [52] Even if the dowry is a valid marriage agreement under the Family Relations Act, it is subject to being set aside if the court determines it to be unfair, a less stringent standard than that under the Family Law Act. [143] There are obvious religious sensitivities with this issue as well as some genuine jurisdictional questions and a leading judgment form the Supreme Court of Canada is instructive. [144] In Bruker v. Marcovitz, 2007 SCC 54, the marriage was a Jewish one, the parties appeared before a rabbi for a divorce and this was recorded in a written agreement. However, a get is required before a divorce can be finalized and the wife can only obtain a get from the husband; she remains a wife and unable to marry again without a get. In Bruker the husband refused to grant the get for 15 years even though the agreement was to appear before the rabbinical authorities to obtain a Jewish divorce immediately upon the granting of a civil decree nisi. [145] At trial the agreement to obtain a divorce was found to be a valid and binding civil contract albeit also with religious obligations ([2003] R.J.Q. 1189). Damages were the significant issue and these were awarded to the wife. However, the Court also said this: [19] Defendant claims that he had a religious obligation, not a civil one, and thus this dispute cannot be adjudicated before a civil court. However, once the Defendant signed a civil contract, agreeing to appear immediately before Rabbinical authorities, this obligation moved into the realm of the civil courts, and the religious obligation became embodied in a secular agreement. Consequently, the Defendant had a clear and unequivocal civil law obligation to appear "immediately" before the Rabbinical authorities. He did not appear, however, for 15 years. Once there is a civil contract, even if its object relates to religious obligations, it is justiciable and within the jurisdiction of the civil court. [146] The trial judgment was overturned on appeal on the basis that the obligation in the contract was substantially a religious one ([2005] QCCA 835). A majority in the Supreme Court of Canada allowed an appeal by the wife and restored the trial judgment (Justice Deschamps dissenting). [147] Justice Abella wrote for the majority in Bruker and her reasoning included the following findings and conclusions, summarized by me: (a) The fact that a dispute has a religious aspect does not make it non-justiciable. "The courts will not consider matters that are strictly spiritual or narrowly doctrinal in nature, but will intervene where civil rights or property rights have been invaded" (at para. 41, 42; citing M. G. Ogilvie Religious Institutions and the Law in Canada, (2nd ed. 2003), at pg. 218). (b) That a marriage contract is deeply affected by religious considerations is not determinative; it is "the beginning and not the end of the matter" (at para. 46; citing Re Morris and Morris (1973), 42 D.L.R. (3d) 550 (Man. C.A.), at pgs. 559, 560). (c) The court was not dealing with judicial review of doctrinal religious principles such as whether a particular get is valid (at para. 47). (d) The court disagreed with the husband's submission that requiring attendance before a rabbinical authority to obtain a divorce in accordance with Jewish law to permit remarriage under that law, was against public order because it was a violation of freedom of religion (at paras. 54-93). The court applied the Civil Code of Quebec, S.Q. 1991. (e) The court considered the husband's claim that a civil court taking jurisdiction over his religious divorce would condemn him ex post facto for abiding his religion on the first place (para. 65). It relied on the court's "robust interpretation of freedom of religion" in previous cases and concluded it had to weigh the husband's religious claim with the democratic values, public order and general well-being set out in the Quebec civil code (para. 78). As part of this analysis the Court accepted the right of Canadians to decide for themselves whether their marriage was irretrievably broken down (para. 82). (f) The approach in other jurisdictions was reviewed. The use of damages to compensate someone whose spouse refused to provide a get has been upheld in the European Commission of Human Rights. Similar approaches are taken in France, the United states and Israel. In Bruker the Court concluded that the "international perspective reinforces the view that judicial enforcement of an agreement to provide a Jewish divorce is consistent with public policy values shared by other democracies" (para. 90). [148] As above, the majority in Bruker agreed with the trial judge that the wife was entitled to damages for the husband's violation of a civil contract requiring him to immediately attend the religious authorities to complete a Jewish divorce. In dissent, Justice Deschamps acknowledged the situation of the wife but concluded it was a matter for Hebrew law which is an autonomous source of law in Canada (at para. 183). [149] The issue of the autonomy of Hebrew law was a key difference between the majority and the dissent of Justice Deschamps as can be seen from the following excerpt from the dissent: [183] Although, like the Court of Appeal, I am sensitive to how difficult it is for the Jewish community to modify the rules of religious divorce, the fact remains that the courts are limited to deciding cases that originate in positive law. The instant case is one in which the religious and civil worlds collide. In my opinion, the problem is a matter for Hebrew law. I see no reason to change, for this case, the clear rule that religion is not an autonomous source of law in Canada. [150] The conclusion of the majority also included the following: [184] I will conclude by noting that the reserved approach taken by the Canadian courts to religious precepts is in my view a sound one. Civil rights arise out of positive law, not religious law. If the violation of a religious undertaking corresponds to the violation of a civil obligation, the courts can play their civil role. But they must not be put in a situation in which they have to sanction the violation of religious rights. The courts may not use their secular power to penalize a refusal to consent to a get, failure to pay the Islamic mahr, refusal to raise children in a particular faith, refusal to wear the veil, failure to observe religious holidays, etc. Limiting the courts' role to applying civil rules is the clearest position and the one most consistent with the neutrality of the state in Canadian and Quebec law. Gandhi is credited with saying that each person is responsible for his or her own religion. That responsibility goes hand in hand with the neutrality of the state toward religious precepts and, in the case at bar, favours dismissing the appellant's action. [Emphasis added]. [151] Obviously the Court had in mind the analogous situation of an Iranian divorce and the failure to pay a mehr. [152] Considering the circumstances of the subject application in light of Bruker there are some differences in the facts. For example, in Bruker there was a written agreement while in this case there was an oral agreement. As well, the oral agreement in this case was about two years ago while the agreement in Bruker was in place for 15 years without compliance by the husband. [153] There can be problems of proof with oral contracts. The essential problem is that the parties are usually attempting to setout what was agreed orally in some formal way after the contract was made and after events have been distorted by the passage of time and intervening issues such as personal animosity. However, subject to those problems, there is no reason in law why an oral contract cannot be as enforceable as a written one: Hik v. Redlick, 2012 BCSC 1684 at para. 38. In the subject case these difficulties do not arise because the parties accept that they agreed they would apply for an Iranian divorce, the consideration relating to the mehr and the value of the Coquitlam property. Applying Bruker, this is a civil contract that can be enforced by this Court. [154] Also significant is that, unlike in Bruker, neither party here challenges the jurisdiction of the court to make certain orders with respect to their Iranian divorce. Indeed, each of them bring their own history about why there has not been an Iranian divorce (despite the 2015 oral agreement) but they nonetheless agree there should be one and that this Court should make the appropriate order. [155] I find that the elements for an order as described in Bruker are made out here. There was an oral agreement between the parties in 2015 that there would be an Iranian divorce. The many text messages between the parties attest to this. I have found above that the reason this did not happen is because the respondent insisted on the claimant completing the Iranian divorce before he executed the documents necessary for the sale of the Coquitlam property. The claimant, at least, is entitled to an appropriate remedy from this Court on the issue of the Iranian divorce as a result of the civil oral agreement between the parties and because of the significant negative consequences to her of not being divorced (i.e. not being able to travel to Iran). [156] Unlike in Bruker, there is no need to weigh one party's religious freedom with democratic values such as the parties' right to decide for themselves whether their marriage is irretrievably broken down. There is no doctrinal issue here involving religious principles, only an agreement that the parties would attend for a divorce. I can find no violation of the parties' freedom of religion in ordering them to attend to complete their agreement and the Iranian divorce. The parties are under no illusion that their marriage has broken down and what is required is a civil order to make them complete the divorce as reflected in their civil agreement to do just that. [157] For the above reasons I adopt essentially the approach in Bruker as described in paragraph 19 of the trial judgment in Bruker. A strict application of the evidence would result in an order requiring only the respondent to apply for an Iranian divorce. This is because he was the primary obstacle to a religious divorce in July 2016. However, also in the spirit of bringing closure to this issue, I conclude that the order should apply to both parties.
