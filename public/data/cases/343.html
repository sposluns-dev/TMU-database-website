October 21, 2010 NATURE OF THE APPLICATION Request for disqualification for Apprehension of bias or actual bias 1. Hearing of October 1, 2010 [1] The original application from which this application to disqualify me flows is to set aside an entered order of this Court dismissing a summary conviction appeal as abandoned pursuant to section 825 (b) of the Criminal Code. At the outset of the hearing, the applicant began to question whether I was a Freemason, and later she raised further questions on my personal and professional background. [2] Ms. Sydel is self-represented. I adjourned this matter for one week to enable her to seek legal advice to see whether she was in fact seeking, given the nature of the questions she was posing to me on my personal background, that I be disqualified for apprehension of bias or actual bias. Following that week's adjournment, she was to advise the Court, through the Registry, if she was bringing such an application. We prearranged a date, convenient to her, to have that matter heard should it be required. [3] Nothing in the hearing of October 1, 2010 gave me cause to believe I was in a conflict situation. I did not consider myself under any obligation to make disclosure of anything that could support a plausible argument in favour of disqualification. Accordingly, I did not do so. I was unaware of Ms. Sydel, and had no previous involvement with or knowledge of anything to do with this matter. I do not know Ms. Meneguzzi, counsel for the respondent. Ms. Sydel's prosecution was handled by the Federal Department of Justice. I have never been employed by the Department of Justice. Ms. Sydel's subsequent summary conviction appeal was similarly handled by the Department of Justice and never handled at the Attorney General's office. [4] During the hearing Ms. Sydel continued to express concern and queried me on my past. I explained to the applicant during the hearing that it would be helpful to know if she was bringing an application for bias, and if so, on what basis. In particular, she seemed concerned that prior to my appointment I was employed as Crown Counsel in the office of the Attorney General of British Columbia. She also thought, incorrectly, that I was a provincial court judge prior to my appointment. The matter was accordingly adjourned so she could seek the advice of counsel. [5] The order of the court that she is seeking to set aside is an order of this Court that her summary conviction appeal from conviction on charges on income tax evasion be dismissed as abandoned. That order was pronounced by Garson J. (as she then was) on April 17, 2008 and entered on May 5, 2008 following the Notice of Abandonment of the Appeal signed and filed by Ms. Sydel. I have already heard the complete arguments of both Ms. Sydel and counsel for the Crown respondent from the Department of Justice in respect of the application to set aside the order. 2. Application for disqualification for apprehension of bias/actual bias [6] Ms. Sydel has distilled her argument respecting the application that I disqualify myself for apprehension of bias or actual bias as set out below in written submissions and an affidavit. a. Position of the Applicant [7] Ms. Sydel argues that: 1. There is "a prima facie suspicion" that I am a member of or affiliated to Freemasonry due to the fact that I declined to answer questions in that regard at the hearing. She asserts there was a conspiracy between the trial judge and the lead investigator at the hearing, particularly at the hearing for judicial interim release in her matter in Provincial Court related to their alleged affiliation to Freemasonry. 2. Ms. Sydel submits that her conviction took place in the Provincial Court and as I was an employee of the Ministry of the Attorney General - due to the fact that that is "the organization that administers the Provincial Court of British Columbia and the Supreme Court of British Columbia," I am too close to these matters. 3. Ms. Sydel submitted that I was a Provincial Court Judge prior to my appointment, which I was not, and that I held an administrative position with the Provincial Court. She corrected this written submission at the oral hearing to reflect that I was a prior member of the Judicial Council for British Columbia. She states as well in her affidavit as an insert: "Madam Justice Maisonville was a member of the Judicial Council of the Provincial court, an organization that is completely administered by the Attorney General for British Columbia". I had been a member of the Judicial Council of British Columbia in 2009 in accordance with the Provincial Court Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c. 379 (s. 21(2)(d)), as president of the Canadian Bar Association B.C. Branch. [8] At the hearing of this matter she sought to amend her affidavit and written materials so that where reference was made to my being a Provincial Court Judge, this was instead substituted with "a member of Judicial Council for B.C." [9] Ms. Sydel's position, while failing to articulate whether she relies on the basis of an apprehension of bias or actual bias, was brought as I was "too closely connected to some of the Defendants in [sic] Supreme Court Action which is linked to this appeal..." and prima facie a Freemason. Ms. Sydel had brought an action in the Supreme Court against a number of individuals and entities, including the Ministry of the Attorney General. That action was struck out by Kelleher J. following an application under Rule 19(24). Ms. Sydel advises she is appealing that decision. 3. Position of the Crown respondent [10] The respondent submits the starting point is a strong presumption of impartiality: Wewaykum Indian Band v. Canada, 2003 SCC 45 at paragraphs 59 and 76. [11] The onus is on the applicant to set out the basis on which I should be disqualified. As the unanimous Supreme Court of Canada said in Wewaykum: 59 Viewed in this light, "[i]mpartiality is the fundamental qualification of a judge and the core attribute of the judiciary" (Canadian Judicial Council, Ethical Principles for Judges (1998), at p. 30). It is the key to our judicial process, and must be presumed. As was noted by L'Heureux-Dubé J. and McLachlin J. (as she then was) in S. (R.D.), supra, at para. 32, the presumption of impartiality carries considerable weight, and the law should not carelessly evoke the possibility of bias in a judge, whose authority depends upon that presumption. Thus, while the requirement of judicial impartiality is a stringent one, the burden is on the party arguing for disqualification to establish that the circumstances justify a finding that the judge must be disqualified. [12] The respondent submits that as a Crown Counsel with the Attorney General I had no pecuniary interest in the dispute, no conduct of the litigation relating to the applicant, no role in assigning the judge at the Provincial Court, nor the prosecution, nor the investigation. Respecting the Judicial Council, Crown Counsel submitted that this body does not assign cases. The functions of the Judicial Council are outlined in s. 22 of the Provincial Court Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c. 379 and are chiefly that of considering proposed Lieutenant Governor in Council appointments of Judges and Justices (s. 22(2)(a)), which accordingly does not support the applicant's argument insofar as she asserts the Judicial Council would have assigned or managed this matter. Respecting my employment at the Attorney General, the respondent argued that I was simply a former employee of the Attorney General of British Columbia, a party named in the civil action brought by Ms. Sydel that was dismissed and is now the subject of an appeal to the B.C. Court of Appeal. No credible reason is advanced given that fact by the applicant except that I am "too close". I do not believe that an "informed" person considering the matter "realistically and practically" would consider this a basis to demand my disqualification. [13] The respondent's position was that a reasonably informed person knowing the above information would conclude there was no basis to conclude an apprehension for bias existed and that the applicant has failed to discharge the burden on her in accordance with Wewaykum. [14] The Crown submitted the application ought to be dismissed. [15] I agree with the submissions of the Crown respondent that the application be dismissed for these reasons. 4. The Law [20] The question at issue is: should I be disqualified from deciding this application because of a reasonable apprehension of bias or actual bias? [21] As noted above, there was nothing at the outset of the hearing that led me to be concerned or caused me to believe that I should make any sort of disclosure following the procedure set out by the Canadian Judicial Council's Ethical Principles for Judges (1998), as follows: E. 16 The judge should make disclosure on the record and invite submissions from the parties in two situations. The first arises if the judge has any doubt about whether there are arguable grounds for disqualification. The second is if an unexpected issue arises shortly before or during a proceeding. The judge's request for submissions should emphasize that it is not counsel's consent [to continue without disqualification] that is being sought but assistance on the question of whether arguable grounds exist for disqualification... . [22] Given that she was self-represented, at the applicant's request I adjourned the within matter to permit Ms. Sydel to seek advice. I was unsure what she was seeking. She seemed equally unsure. If she was proceeding to seek my disqualification I needed to know on what basis in an effort to determine whether arguable grounds existed for disqualification, and she indicated she needed an adjournment to obtain advice on that matter. She has now outlined the grounds as above into three arguments which I find do not form grounds for my disqualification. [23] The leading case respecting disqualification is Wewaykum Indian Band v. Canada, [2003] 2 S.C.R. 259, 231 D.L.R. (4th) 1. In it the Court states as follows: 60 In Canadian law, one standard has now emerged as the criterion for disqualification. The criterion, as expressed by de Grandpré J. in Committee for Justice and Liberty v. National Energy Board, [1978] 1 S.C.R. 369, at p. 394, is the reasonable apprehension of bias: ... the apprehension of bias must be a reasonable one, held by reasonable and right minded persons, applying themselves to the question and obtaining thereon the required information. In the words of the Court of Appeal, that test is "what would an informed person, viewing the matter realistically and practically - and having thought the matter through - conclude. Would he think that it is more likely than not that [the decision-maker], whether consciously or unconsciously, would not decide fairly." [24] As discussed above, the Supreme Court further held in Wewaykum, at paragraph 59, that: the presumption of impartiality carries considerable weight, and the law should not carelessly evoke the possibility of bias in a judge, whose authority depends upon that presumption. [25] In Committee for Justice and Liberty v. National Energy Board, [1978] 1 S.C.R. 369, de Grandpré J. writing in dissent, said at p. 395: The grounds for this apprehension must, however, be substantial and I entirely agree with the Federal Court of Appeal which refused to accept the suggestion that the test be related to the "very sensitive or scrupulous conscience". [26] In R. v. Quinn, 2006 BCCA 255, Thackray J.A. confirmed this approach and held for the Court of Appeal at paragraph 55: 55 While the law demands disqualification in some cases where there is no actual bias, that is not the case where there is not even a reasonable apprehension of bias. The perspective to be considered is that of an "informed" person who will consider the matter "realistically and practically" and not that of the "very sensitive or scrupulous conscience". 6. Conclusion [27] Accordingly, the test set out in Wewaykum must be considered with the facts of the matter before me. I find that no arguable grounds exist for my disqualification. I find that a reasonable and informed person, upon due consideration, would know that my involvement with the Judicial Council of British Columbia, and my past employment with the Ministry of the Attorney General as a Crown Counsel have no relationship to the matter now before the Court. Crown Counsel operate entirely independently from the Provincial Court, and vice versa. Anything less would be contrary to the independence of the Bar and the independence of the judiciary and disqualify Crown Counsel from appearing in Provincial Court, and Provincial Court judges from hearing charges brought by the Crown. [28] The applicant has failed to satisfy me that a reasonable and informed person would find my alleged association with the Freemasons of any relevance in this matter. The mere fact that Ms. Sydel alleges that she was a victim of a conspiracy of Freemasons, is insufficient in my view to make that allegation a relevant issue. No credible evidence of a conspiracy of Freemasons was put before me. Given, additionally, the strong presumption of judicial impartiality, there is no merit to this argument. [29] A reasonable and informed person would also be aware that when I became a judge of this Court, I swore an oath and that I have a duty to act in all matters in an impartial manner. I am of the view that such a person would consider that I have no relevant, or indeed any, interest in the subject matter of this case and, further, would not have any reasonable apprehension of bias nor actual bias. I do not, therefore, intend to disqualify myself. "Maisonville J." ___________________________________ The Honourable Madam Justice Maisonville
