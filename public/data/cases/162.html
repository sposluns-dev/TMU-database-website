XXXXX XXXXX, eighty-one year-old male citizen of Israel and his wife XXXXX XXXXX, seventy year-old citizen of Israel, all claim a well-founded fear of persecution based on religion (the wife's conversion to the Jehovah Witness faith), and the husband's membership in a particular social group (Jewish husband with a Christian wife) and their former nationality as immigrants from the former Soviet Union. All claimants are joined. They claim to have a well-founded fear of persecution at the hands of Jewish citizens of Israel and state agents. They claim refugee protection pursuant to Section 96 and 97 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). IDENTITY The panel finds that the joined claimants are citizens of Israel and that the religion of the husband is Jewish and the wife is Christian (Jehovah Witness) based on the claimant's documentary evidence and sworn testimony. SUMMARY OF THE CLAIMANT'S ALLEGATIONS The claimants claim a well-founded fear of persecution at the hands of Jewish citizens and state authorities due to the wife's activities as a Jehovah Witness, their former nationality as former citizens of the former Soviet Union and the husband's membership in a particular social group (Jew married to a Christian). On coming to Israel as retirees, the couple were given an apartment in XXXXX - XXXXX, approximately XXXXX kilometers from Jordan. The local Jewish population was extremely religious and were allegedly hostile to former residents from the former Soviet Union as they considered them to be atheists and people who settled in their land against their wishes. After their son and his family left for Canada, the older couple complained that they were faced with increasing harassment by the general population and that state agents were not successful in addressing their difficulties. In XXXXX 2000, the female claimant alleges that she met XXXXX XXXXX who invited her to meet observers of the Jehovah Witness faith, which provided the female claimant the spiritual nourishment that she needed. Together with XXXXX the female claimant traveled to a church in XXXXX in XXXXX 2000 and on occasion the pair experienced some verbal harassment but "nothing serious ever happened," according to the claimant. When however the female claimant tried to talk to others to explain their religion they were badly treated by the inhabitants and were warned by the police not to try and convert people as they would allegedly be charged and sent to prison. The police allegedly would not let the couple move to another location and the claimants allege that they were harassed and threatened by the general public. On XXXXX, 2001, the couple alleges that they were physically assaulted and pushed down on the street by Orthodox Jewish men, who insulted the claimant's wife's religious activities. Due to this stressful situation, the wife alleges that her husband had a heart attack that required treatment at a local medical facility. The police allegedly did not send someone over immediately to investigate the incident. The elderly couple were told by their son to come to Canada, and so their arrived on XXXXX, 2001 and made claims for refugee protection February 12, 2001. ANALYSIS The issue is whether the claimants are Convention refugees or persons in need of protection within the meanings contained in the IRPA. The standard of proof to be applied in assessing whether good grounds exist for fearing persecution, a risk to life or a fear of torture are set out in Adjei.1 The concept of a well-founded fear, to which this definition refers, includes not only the joined claimants' subjective perception of the fear of persecution to which they testify, but also requires an objective basis for that fear to be found in the facts pertaining to their situation.2 The panel identified the determinative issues of credibility, discrimination versus persecution and state protection. In terms of credibility, the panel identified a number of inconsistencies, omissions, and contradictions in the claimants' testimony. Despite frequent interruptions of the female claimant, and requests for each claimant to have a turn testifying, the panel and counsel had to interject several times to remind the female claimant to wait until it was her turn to testify. In terms of documentary evidence the joined claimants presented a letter to the Police Department of XXXXX XXXXX City, which reported threatening notes and harassment. The note has no address or telephone number, which is a serious omission as it would not be possible for the police to actually contact the XXXXX family to rectify the situation. The three hand written notes are not dated, or signed and are on plain paper. The notes could have been prepared by anyone. The notes encourage the claimants to come to Canada to join their son. Due to the potentially self-serving nature of the letters, lack of supporting evidence and omission of key information for the police to investigate, the credibility of the documentation was questioned by the panel. When asked about the notes the female claimant testified that she only made an oral complaint to the police and did not submit a written complaint. Given the lack of evidence with regard to the incident, the panel finds that there is insufficient credible or trustworthy evidence to support the allegation that the claimants were harassed, by hate notes. The panel believes that if the notes were, in fact, genuine, the claimants would have taken greater steps with the police to obtain state protection. The female claimant testified that she received harassing writing in chalk on her door and that she speculated that the harassment came from the local inhabitants. As a result, she wrote a complaint letter to the police XXXXX, 2000. The complaint letter does not have an address for a follow up police investigation, nor a telephone number for the state agents to contact the claimants. The panel noted the key omissions and asked the claimants regarding the available state protection and the female claimant testified that the state agents had not assisted her. Given the lack of evidence, the panel does not believe that the claimants took serious steps to avail themselves of state protection in the event that the document is bona fide and not created to buttress their refugee claims. The female claimant testified that she went door-to-door prophesizing from XXXXX 2000 to XXXXX, 2001. She alleges that she wrote to the police and they asked her if she was still going out and witnessing harassment on XXXXX, 2001. On further questioning the female claimant stated that it was against the law going door-to-door attempting to convert citizens to Christianity. However, in later testimony it was clarified that the police did not state that it was against the law, but advised her not to do it, given the neighborhood that she lived in. The police had received complaints from the heads of the local Jewish community regarding her activities. The panel noted that the claimant was in an Orthodox neighborhood due to the husband's religion and that the family was not placed in a Christian area due to the wife's and son's religion. Given the availability of neighborhoods with a greater percentage of Christians, the panel finds that the claimants could have applied to live elsewhere in Israel. When asked regarding an internal flight alternative, the female claimant testified that she could not move due to their social subsidized status. When asked about a social subsidized apartment in a Christian area, the claimant contradicted earlier testimony that they would not be granted a subsidized apartment to stating that it was too expensive to move. The panel notes in a letter from XXXXX, that it was possible to move from XXXXX to XXXXX near the Kingdom Hall of Jehovah. There is no evidence that the state prevented either the female claimant or XXXXX from worshiping at the Kingdom Hall of Jehovah. As a result, there is no credible or trustworthy evidence before the panel that the claimants applied to live in another neighborhood nor that state agents refused their request as stated by the female claimant. The panel does not find it credible that the claimants never seriously sought the assistance of state agents to address their alleged harassment. The panel finds that if these events happened, which they do not believe occurred, then these are incidents of discrimination, which cumulatively would not amount to persecution. The female claimant reported to state agents that she was cooking and the gas was not working and it appeared to be filling up her apartment. When asked by the panel if it could be a mal function of her gas stove, she testified "that nothing before had happened like that and that nothing like that happened after the incident". The panel finds that there is insufficient credible or trustworthy evidence before the panel to conclude that the gas problem was not a technical malfunction as opposed to harassment on the part of local Jewish Orthodox members. The panel finds that the female claimant is speculating that Jewish Orthodox members are to blame in the absence of credible evidence. On XXXXX, 2001 the claimants' situation became serious when they were allegedly accosted by a group of Orthodox men near their building. The female claimant fell to the ground after hearing verbal insults "stating that they were goys and that their religion was blasphemous." The female claimant testified that they were told to leave Israel and that the shock had resulted in her husband requiring medical intervention for a heart attack. The female claimant testified that she called the police and they promised to send someone to see them immediately, but nobody came and that she realized that the police were not going to get help for them. In later testimony she testified that no written report was necessary because of her husband's health, but that her friend's son had contacted the police. The claimant in further testimony contradicted the initial testimony and stated that a formal complaint indeed was made one to two days afterwards to police, but they did not wish to respond as in the gas situation at the home. The panel pointed out to the claimants that the gas situation had no witnesses and would be difficult to prove, however, the attack was more serious and there was more evidence regarding the incident. The female claimant testified that she did not report the attack to the police, as she did not "have any hope for assistance." The latter testimony is again in contradiction from earlier testimony that she did, in fact, make a formal complaint. The panel believes that the male claimant had a medical problem that required medical intervention as indicated in the medical report, however, it does not believe the female claimant's testimony regarding obtaining or not obtaining state protection due to the number of contradictions. The panel finds the female claimant's testimony in this regard not credible. The panel notes that the claimants' son is a Canadian citizen and that when asked the claimants testified that no attempt was made on behalf of the son who completed their refugee claims to sponsor his parents. When questioned as to why this was not considered, the female claimant testified that they only considered the refugee option. The panel finds that if the joined claimants had a well-founded subjective fear of persecution in returning to Israel they would have explored with their son every option available to them to remain in Canada. As a result the panel questions the genuine well-found ness of their subjective fear of persecution and finds that their application has more to do with family reunification than persecution. The panel weighed the fact that terrorism is a generalized risk to all inhabitants within Israel and explains in part why a number of inhabitants of Israel chose to immigrate to other countries offering greater peace and economic potential, despite Israel's serious efforts to protect its citizens. The panel finds that there is no credible or trustworthy evidence before it to support the male claimant's allegations that the state is unwilling or unable to protect Christians in Israel. The panel, as a result, finds the claimants' assertion that they could not approach state agents for protection not credible. The panel was not satisfied that what the claimants experienced was harm amounting to the serious harm of persecution. Indeed due to the lack of credibility of the incidents, the panel does not believe that the incidents actually happened, but believes that the incidents were exaggerated to buttress their refugee claims. Although the panel accepts that there is clear discrimination against Christians and former nationals of the Soviet Union in Israel according to the documentary evidence, the panel did not find, even cumulatively, that the ill treatment experienced by Christians and former Soviet Union nationals generally, or the claimant specifically, amounted to the serious harm of persecution. Even if the male claimant had provided evidence that there is a serious possibility that they would be persecuted on return to Israel, which the panel did not find that he did, the onus is on the claimants to prove with clear and convincing evidence that they lacked access to adequate State protection. The documentary evidence before the panel3 states that over a million immigrants from the former Soviet Union have arrived in Israel since the early 1990s of which some 250,000 were considered not to be anarchically Jewish." "The Russian Journal noted in 1999 that a Russian speaker can get by in Israel speaking only Russian because of the number of businesses servicing the immigrant community."4 In the 1990s former Soviet immigrants faced increasing rates of poverty, cultural isolation, a lack of Russian language government social services and a "blanket prejudice against immigrants from the former Soviet Union which has become common in Israeli society. ...Many Israelis reportedly believe that the newcomers are "opportunists" who care "little for Zionist ideology" and that too many are non-Jews who threaten the Jewish character of Israel and that as a group, they provide too much competition on the employment market."5 The documentary evidence would support the fact when weighed cumulatively that the claimants may have faced discrimination, but not persecution in Israel. The panel notes that there is an Ombudsman process in Israel to handle complaints and that the Ombudsman received over 8,000 calls annually.6 In terms of the female claimant's Christian belief, the law in Israel provides for freedom of religion, and the government generally respected this right, however, it imposed some restrictions...three additional communities have been subsequently recognized by the Government - the Druze, the Evangelical Espiscopal Church, and the Baha'i...Missionaries were allowed to proselytize, although the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints voluntarily refrained from doing so under agreement with the Government. The law prohibits anyone from offering or receiving material benefits as an inducement to conversion; however, there have been no reports of enforcement of this law."7 The Government generally respected the human rights of its citizens; however, members of the Israeli security forces committed numerous serious human rights abuses.8 However, Israel has a democratic parliamentary system9... The judiciary is independent.10 ...The law provides for freedom of the press and the Government generally respected this right in practice.11 A wide variety of local and international human rights groups operated without Government restriction. There is a Police Watch unit at the Association for Civil Rights in Israel (ACRI). ACRI assists people in need whether Jewish or non-Jewish. They provide legal counseling for rights violations.12 Almost all the police stations have a service for receiving complaints. Complaints about police behavior are handled by a special Department of the Ministry of Justice, decisions of the investigating department orders may be administratively reviewed or through a petition to the High Court of Justice also to judicial review.13 There is a new police complaints unit.14 There are wide ranges of victim assistance programs.15 Given this information the panel finds, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimants have not provided clear and convincing evidence of a lack of adequate state protection in Israel. A state is presumed to be able to protect its citizens. In circumstances where there is not a complete breakdown of state apparatus and in the absence of an admission by state authorities that they are unable to protect its citizens, the claimants must advance clear and convincing evidence that state protection would not be adequate. According to Villafranca, what should be expected of a state is a "serious effort" to provide protection, not a guarantee of protection. The case law is fairly consistent that there is no guaranteed protection and that the standard of protection that is reasonable is "adequate though not necessarily perfect."16 The panel concluded that the claimants have not met the onus to rebut the presumption of adequate state protection with clear and convincing evidence from their past experience or the country documentation. The panel finds that the claimants were living in a democracy and did not meet the more exacting standard of proof required in such countries. As noted in the Kadenko17 case, the more democratic the state, the more is required of a claimant to exhaust all reasonable remedies to get protection from the state before seeking international protection. The panel finds that the claimants did not provide any evidence of providing the opportunity for the police to provide protection and mount a successful investigation. The police must be given adequate tools in order to investigate a crime. Information is a crucial tool and these claimants failed to pursue protection without adequate justification. In conclusion, having considered the totality of the evidence in the context of the legal tests to be applied and the relevant jurisprudence, the panel does not find there is a serious possibility that the claimants would experience persecution without access to adequate state protection, should they return to Israel at this time. The panel considered also whether the claimants were persons in need of protection under section 97 of the IRPA and determined that the adequacy of state protection is determinative in those cases as well. Therefore, the panel finds that the joined claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. The panel rejects the joined claims of the claimants. They are not Convention refugees or persons in need of protection.
