Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXXXXXXXXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of Hearing March 21, 2012 Date(s) de l'audience Place of Hearing Toronto, Ontario Lieu de l'audience Date of Decision June 5, 2012 Date de la décision Panel Elana Rose Tribunal Counsel for the Claimant(s) El-Farouk Khaki Conseil(s) du / de la / des demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer N/A Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre [1] XXXXXXX, a citizen of Hungary, claims refugee protection pursuant to Sections 96 and 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act1 (IRPA). ALLEGATIONS [2] The specifics of the claim are set out in the narrative to the claimant's Personal Information Form2 (PIF); however, the following is a summary. The claimant had different allegations contained in his Point of Entry (POE) notes, first narrative, and amended narrative. The allegations set out below are advanced in oral testimony and his amended narrative. [3] The claimant states that he was assaulted in 2003 due to his Roma ethnicity, and did not report this to the police. He states that he had problems in the workplace due to his ethnicity, but kept his job until his departure to Canada. He alleges that he has on many occasions been denied access to nightclubs, malls and restaurants. He feels that generally, Hungarian society looks down upon him. DETERMINATION [4] I find that the claimant is not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. ANALYSIS [5] The determinative issues in this claim are the failure to establish a claim and state protection. Identity [6] The claimant's identity as a Hungarian citizen is established by his testimony and by the personal identity documents filed, including his Hungarian passport.3 Credibility [7] When a claimant swears that certain facts are true, this creates a presumption that they are indeed true unless there is valid reason to doubt their veracity4; and the determination as to whether a claimant's evidence is credible is made on a balance of probabilities.5 [8] I did not find that the claimant established past persecution with credible evidence. Inconsistencies [9] In oral testimony, the claimant alleges that in 2003 he was assaulted by a group of men who asked him for a cigarette, and then asked him if he was Roma. He also alleged that in 2006 he was stopped about three or four times while walking in the city, and asked by police for identification. He considers this harassment. [10] The claimant was asked if he had reported his assault in 2003 to the police, and he indicated that he had not. When asked to explain why he would not report an assault to the police, he indicated that he did not report it as he did not trust the police as they had stopped him three to four times for identification purposes. When it was put to the claimant that his evidence was that he was stopped in 2006, three years after the alleged assault, he then changed his testimony and indicated that he does not know why he did not file a report. Further confusion is raised by the claimant's sworn POE evidence,6 where he indicates that his reason for claiming protection in Canada is that his ethnicity is not accepted, that they only see negatives in him, and that he cannot assimilate, or get into entertainment places. There is no mention of any violent events, or fear of any violence. [11] The claimant was asked if he sustained injuries in the 2003 assault, as there is also no medical report confirming the event. He indicated that he had not. Given the failure to mention this event in previous sworn statements, the claimant's own confusion on the timing of this event and his alleged police interactions, and the failure to report, I find that the claimant embellished the event to bolster his claim. I do not find that he suffered persecution in the way of an ethnically motivated assault in 2003. Subjective Fear [12] By definition, a Convention refugee is someone who has a well-founded fear of persecution. Fear is, by its very nature, subjective, while the well-foundedness of that fear is an objective matter. [13] Both subjective and objective components are required to meet the definition,7 and a lack of evidence going to the subjective element of the claim is in itself sufficient for the claim to fail.8 Given the claimant's evidence that he was assaulted in 2003, harassed by the police in 2006, and been discriminated by his local population at nightclubs and in the workplace, the claimant was asked why he had failed to relocate from the same location throughout that period. [14] He was asked why he would not relocate to a big city, like Budapest, where no one knows him and he could remain more anonymous. It is also noted that while he resided at the same location until departure, there is also a significant delay in departure from these alleged events. [15] The claimant's response was that he was too poor to relocate. I do not accept this as reasonable, as the claimant also gave evidence that he was working up until his departure to Canada to save money to come to Canada. It is also noted that the claimant provided evidence at the hearing that he had in fact at one point lived and worked in Budapest, for a period of time. The claimant's conduct of continued residence at the same location, and his evidence that he could not relocate within Hungary as he was too poor, but could relocate to Canada, is conduct and evidence inconsistent with a genuine subjective fear. Given the lengthy delay in departure from pivotal events, I find that the claimant's conduct is more representative of a longer-term plan of relocation, due to being disheartened with life in Hungary, than of a genuine refugee in need of international protection. [16] The purpose of Canada's refugee determination system is to provide safe haven to those who genuinely need it, not to give a quick and convenient route to landed status for immigrants who cannot or will not obtain it in the usual way.9 [17] Similarly the claimant indicates that it is the events at work in XXXX 2009 which caused him to come to Canada in 2010. He alleges that he had problems with his co workers. He felt that they were threatening him, with their looks. He reported these problems to his employer, who then spoke with the co workers and moved the claimant to another area, in order to separate them all. The claimant continued to work at the same place of employment until departure, with no evidence of further problem. [18] I do not find that the conduct of the claimant's continued work, nor his evidence on the treatment by management of his complaints, establishes that any discrimination or persecution occurred in the workplace. When asked if there was something else that management should have done, the claimant could not think of anything. GENERAL FINDING [19] I do not find that the claimant has established, with credible evidence, past persecution or discrimination amounting to persecution. [20] To be considered persecution under the Convention, the mistreatment suffered or anticipated must meet two criteria. First, it must be serious, which is determined by examining the interest at risk of harm-in this case, the claimant's ethnicity, and the extent to which the exercise of that interest may be compromised. Second, the mistreatment or inflicting of harm should generally occur with repetition or persistence, or in a systematic way. Neither threshold has been established with credible evidence. [21] According to the Handbook on Procedures and Criteria for Determining Refugee Status, 10 while discrimination may amount to a violation of human rights, it will not necessarily amount to persecution. Paragraph 54 of the Handbook states:11 Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practise [sic] his religion, or his access to normally available educational facilities.12 [22] The incidents of discrimination in this case, as outlined by the claimant, do not, in the panel's view, constitute a serious constraint on any of the claimant's ability to work, pursue educational opportunities, or exercise freedom of religion. Failure to rebut the presumption of state protection [23] Even though I disbelieve many of the events that the claimant recounted orally and in the PIF, I turn now to the general situation for Roma people in Hungary. While I do note that problems exist for people of Roma ethnicity, including some instances of violence,13 I find that, even based on the general profile of the claimant as Roma, the claims fail as he has failed to rebut the presumption of state protection. [24] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens. A claimant may rebut this presumption by providing "clear and convincing" proof of lack of state protection. The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.14 [25] Evidence that the protection being offered is not necessarily perfect 15 is not clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, as no government can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. However, where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police, and civil authorities in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not be enough to justify that a claimant is unable to avail oneself of protection.16 [26] When the state in question is a democratic state, the claimant must do more than simply show that they approached a member of a police force and that their efforts were unsuccessful. The burden for the claimant is to prove an absence of state protection that is directly proportional to the level of democracy of that state.17 The more democratic the state's institutions, the greater the onus is on the claimant to show that they have exhausted all courses of action available.18 The claimant never approached the police or any other institution for any alleged problems throughout his life in Hungary, and he provided no reasonable explanation for his failure to do so. In fact his evidence initially was that he had failed to report in 2003 due to events with the police in 2006; however, that was recanted. [27] Even had he established that there was a local failure by the police, local failures by the authorities to provide protection, or that they had harassed him by asking him for identification three or four times in 2006, does not mean that the state as a whole fails to protect its citizens, unless the failures form part of a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection.19 There is no persuasive evidence of that. [28] The documentary evidence shows that Hungary is a country of 10 million people with a functioning, multi-party parliamentary democracy with free and fair elections.20 I find that Hungary is in effective control of its territory and has in place a functioning security force to uphold the laws and constitution of the country. There is nothing in the documentation before the Board21 to suggest that Hungary is in a state of complete breakdown. [29] There is no question that racism exists in Hungary and while efforts to combat racism have achieved mixed results, serious efforts are being made. This is not state sponsored racism. [30] Although progress has been made, discrimination against Roma in education, housing, employment, and access to social services continues. Violent attacks against Roma did take place, and this caused a wave of fear amongst Roma communities, understandably, and triggered an exodus from Hungary; however, these attacks caused intense public concern and, while non-governmental organizations (NGOs) alleged that the various authorities were reluctant to note a racial basis for some attacks, there was an intense public debate with respect to the actual existence and scale of racially motivated crimes.22 As addressed below, there was solid action taken to restore calm to the communities being targeted, and prosecution followed. Police [31] Despite the fact that there are reports that the police sometimes use excessive force against Roma, the objective documentary evidence states that if complaints are made, action is taken. This is important as protection must not only be adequate; it must have some operational effectiveness also. [32] The Parliamentary Commissioner for National and Ethnic Minority Rights (Minority Ombudsman) takes complaints based on a government agency's decision, proceedings or negligence, based on a violation that has taken place of a complainant's national or ethnic minority rights, or when the direct threat of such a violation is imminent. [33] The Protective Service of Enforcement Agencies (Protective Service) of Hungary's Ministry of Interior has a mandate to prevent and investigate crimes committed by authorities such as the police, border guard and civil defence, and national and local fire stations. The Protective Service investigates such crimes as abuse of office and crimes committed by those acting in an official capacity.23 An Independent Police Complaints Board was created in 2008.24 However, there are concerns about the scope of its mandate.25 When problems are reported to the relevant Ombudsman, the Ombudsman's recommendations were usually accepted.26 [34] Operational success is confirmed by the statistics. Between 2006 and 2009, the authorities conducted 203 criminal investigations of police misconduct with respect to large antigovernment demonstrations and riots in Budapest and other major cities in 2006. Prosecutors terminated their investigations in 171 cases, mainly because they could not identify individual perpetrators; courts rejected the charges in eight cases; and the prosecutor pressed charges in 20 cases. Courts found the defendants guilty of causing bodily harm in ten of these cases; the verdicts were not subject to appeal. Of the other cases, three remained under investigation, and three were transferred to the military prosecutor's office for action. A subcommittee was formed in the National Assembly and a recent report was received detailing apparent incidents of misconduct. The investigations are ongoing.27 [35] In the first 10 months of the year, authorities found 2,914 police officers responsible for breaches of discipline, 868 guilty of petty offences, 310 guilty of criminal offences, and eight unfit for duty. In the same period, courts sentenced seven police officers to prison terms, gave suspended sentences to 25, fined 315, demoted four, and dismissed eleven. In the same period, courts convicted 32 officers of corruption. No information was available on the number placed on probation.28 There were also instances were higher courts have intervened sometimes to decrease sentences, sometimes to increase sentences and even to overturn a previous acquittal.29 This is reflective of a functioning state mechanism, and an example of effective check and balance on state authorities. [36] Taken as a whole, I find that, while the objective documentary evidence indicates that some police do still commit abuses against people, including the Roma, there is no evidence that it is the majority; and it is reasonable to expect authorities to take action in response to cases of police abuse, as evidenced above, as the police cannot act with impunity. The claimant failed to report any problems to the police at any time, and to discharge his onus of seeking domestic protection prior to protection internationally. Programs for Inclusion [37] The claimant alleges that due to his Roma ethnicity, he experienced discrimination; however, nothing was ever reported. The Hungarian government generally does not condone racism or discrimination by the authorities or the public. [38] The state has undertaken a serious and a substantial effort to ensure the future existence of the Roma, and other minorities, is protected. There are a number of programs to help Roma people find work, get education, and find housing. Hungary has laws to combat discrimination wherever it is faced by Roma. The 1993 Law for the Protection of Minorities was reinforced in 2003 with the Law on Equal Treatment and Promoting Equal Opportunity. Then in January 2005, the Equal Treatment Authority was established to supervise proper implementation of the law and is empowered to put a stop to contraventions and to impose fines and penalties under other legislation as applicable.30 [39] In 2006, a state secretary responsible for equal opportunities in the Ministry of Social Affairs and Labour was established to supervise the execution of the tasks of Roma integration. The Ministry established a Department for Roma Integration to coordinate governmental tasks. The Ministry pays attention to the enhancement of the labour market opportunities of the Roma population, operates a scholarship scheme for young Roma, and cooperates with organizations for the protection of the rights and interests of Roma people. Further, in 2009 the Prime Minister's Office had a state secretary responsible for Roma affairs and the ministries of education, culture and economy had ministerial commissioners to coordinate the tasks which served the promotion of the social integration of the Roma and the improvement of their living conditions.31 [40] In April 2007, Hungary adopted the Strategic Plan of the Decade of Roma Inclusion Programme. The overall objectives of the program include four priority areas (among others) including education, employment, housing and health care, each with regard to the enforcement of equal treatment.32 Most ministries and county labour affairs centres had special officers for Romani affairs focused on the needs of the Romani community. The Ministry of Education and Culture continued to offer financial incentives to encourage schools to integrate Romani and non-Romani children in the same classrooms and to reintegrate Roma inappropriately placed in remedial programs. The Ministry of Social Affairs and Labour operated a program to finance infrastructure development in Romani communities. The Ministry of Justice and Law Enforcement operated an anti-discrimination legal service network that provided free legal aid to Roma in cases where they encountered discrimination based on their ethnicity. Roma, like the other 12 official minorities, are entitled to elect their own minority self-governments (MSGs), which organize minority activities and handle cultural and educational affairs. The president of each MSG also has the right to attend and speak at local government assemblies.33 [41] There is no doubt that under-employment of Roma people is a serious problem that has serious social implications. To address this, Hungary has implemented a number of programs. Since January 2009, in order to apply for EU and government funds for urban rehabilitation and public education projects, every city must attach to its proposal a desegregation plan outlining planned actions to eradicate segregation in housing and public education. The government opened 200 positions in public administration for Romani college graduates. By year's end 165 applicants had passed the mandatory civil servant entry exam and 66 were placed in various national and county government offices.34 [42] Since 2003, billions of forints have been dedicated to employment and opportunity programs to promote employment and training to unemployed Roma.35 In 2006, the Regional Training Centre in Pecs carried out a complex program in employment with assistance from the National Council for Adult Education to improve the chances of Roma people. Since 2003, there have been 55 complex programs under the auspices of labour centres to help those in long-term unemployment situations, including Roma people, and programs continued in 2008. The government runs a number of programs to improve the employment prospects of Roma people that assist with finding employment, offer training sessions to help them develop marketable skills, provide financial incentives to employers who hire minorities, and provide Roma desk officers in every labour exchange centre and employment office. Employers are fined for acting in contravention to the principle of equal treatment which disqualifies them from state aid for two years. For example, in 2008 a company was fined for refusing to hire Roma people as cleaners on account of their ethnicity.36 Under these kinds of programs tens of thousands of Roma people have found employment.37 [43] While there is criticism that some of these measures have been sometimes short-term and can only help a limited number of people at one time,38 when looked at as a whole, it cannot be denied that the state is making serious efforts and that progress has been made. These are positive steps to address the decades of marginalization of the Roma community. Education [44] Segregation of Roma students has definitely been a serious problem as it lowers education standards and rates for Roma students and contributes to under-employment and poverty. However, wide-ranging programs have been set-up to address segregation through facilitating access to kindergarten, introducing stricter requirements how local authorities draw the boundaries between catchment areas, organizing the composition of classes within schools, and the drawing up of new cognitive tests designed to take better account of cultural differences and socioeconomic disadvantage in testing children's development.39 However, the efforts of the central authorities are frequently hampered by the manner in which local authorities interpret the measures and translate them into practice. Flaws in the assessment system allow for further segregation and parents of Roma and non-Roma students contribute by moving their children to preferred schools. [45] According to the European Roma Rights Centre (ERRC), desegregation efforts by the national government in Hungary have been amongst the strongest in the Central European region, including efforts to subsidize schools that integrate Roma and non-Roma students in the same classroom, reintegrate Roma students inappropriately placed in remedial programs, getting schools to accept disadvantaged children in order to prevent non-Roma parents from withdrawing their children from certain schools, and a scholarship program for young Roma which in 2007-2008 had 11,352 recipients.40 Also, on 19 November 2008, the Supreme Court upheld a ruling in a school segregation lawsuit against primary schools in Hajdúhadháza, according to which two primary schools of the municipality violated the law in an act of illegal segregation, and the Ministry of Education and Culture issued a statement agreeing with the judgment.41 Housing [46] The Roma population is concentrated in economically disadvantaged rural areas and urban slums. Access to public housing can be contingent upon financial resources which many Roma people do not have, and Roma people are generally not aware of their rights or judicial recourse available to them.42 The government has stated their aim is to reduce segregation in housing by eliminating Roma ghettoes. A program was introduced to refurbish social housing and encourage Roma living in segregated settlements outside towns and villages to move into the refurbished social housing inside the town or village. Further, to ensure that the principle of desegregation is taken into account in the award of state or European Union funding for various projects, the authorities are introducing an equal opportunities subsidy policy. To receive funding for urban development projects, whether or not these are directly related to desegregation efforts, a town will be required to submit a desegregation plan aimed at the elimination of segregated living in the town.43 I am mindful of reports of occasional resistance from local authorities and individuals when a Roma family has sought to move into a new neighbourhood, sometimes resulting in damage. Attitudinal change is not quick or universal; however, the work of the authorities is ongoing and persistent. While progress is slow, progress is being made. [47] In 2007, the United Nations reported that the Hungarian government was financing housing renovations in nine Romani localities, "dramatically improving living conditions for some families."44 The Ministry of Social Affairs and Labour earmarked a total of 2.875 billion forints for the implementation of the Housing and Social Integration Programme for Residents of Roma Colonies in 30 municipalities of the country. Eleven municipalities were awarded support under the call for applications announced at the end of 2007 for the third time.45 The NGO Roma Civil Rights Foundation (RCRF) accused some municipalities of using a variety of methods to prevent Roma from moving to more desirable neighbourhoods, such as auctioning off social housing units to the highest bidder, or evicting Roma from housing under renovation and providing inadequate compensation. However, the authorities will take action against such tactics. For example, in 2009, the Budapest Court fined the 2nd district of Budapest about 400 Euros per capita for having evicted 40 Roma, half of whom were children, from housing that they were occupying without entitlement.46 Recourse [48] There is no question that the world-wide economic downturn has had an effect on Hungary. Far-right extremists have used this opportunity to blame minorities, including the Roma, for recent events. Violence and violent rhetoric increased as did societal discrimination as is reflected by misinformed comments of some politicians. Families have been deprived of access to social housing by discriminatory rules and practices of local authorities and Roma children are still sometimes confronted with segregation in schools, which has a devastating impact on education outcomes for these children, and leaves them with limited future life choices and employment prospects. There has been a sharp rise in racism in public discourse and anti-Roma and anti-Semitic discourse appears to be becoming increasingly virulent and wide-spread.47 [49] Some criticisms were levelled against the authorities for not preventing demonstrations made by groups that some believed would promote violence. I appreciate how the events of 2008 and 2009 created widespread fear among the Roma communities. There is criticism in the documentary evidence48 of the police inaction during the marches of the Hungarian Guard. However, it must be noted that it is not police inaction, but in fact the Hungarian freedom of assembly/speech laws that forbid the prevention of lawful demonstrations. The police did ban some demonstrations where there was a clear risk of violence; otherwise they were just monitored.49 [50] On August 9, 2010, the National Bureau of Investigation announced the completion of its investigation into a series of physical attacks against Roma in 2008 and 2009. As a result of the investigation, the Pest County Prosecutor charged four suspects with the murder of six persons, the serious injury of five others, and threats to the safety of an additional 55. The case was scheduled to go to trial in 2011.50 While such crimes are terrible, the offenders are being dealt with by the criminal justice system. This confirms that the criminal system is functional and takes these crimes seriously. [51] There has been effort to bolster public relations efforts to garner support for tolerance such as efforts to encourage public personalities to speak out against racism. Numerous programs have been funded in an effort to provide Roma better access to various programs. The Ministry of Justice and Law Enforcement operated the Roma Anti-discrimination Customer Service Network, a legal service network that provides free legal aid to Roma in cases where they encountered discrimination based on their ethnicity. With the expansion of the Roma Anti-Discrimination Customer Service Network in 2008, there were 30 lawyers at 47 municipalities in the country and the coordination of the program in the Ministry is ensured by a desk officer for Roma affairs. Clients can obtain information on the customer service contact details of Network lawyers 24 hours a day. There was a nation-wide campaign promoting the network and toll-free number. Since 2004, the Network has handled more than 7,200 cases.51 [52] There is also recourse to the Equal Treatment Authority. Since 2005, it has provided individuals with a direct avenue of redress for violations of the prohibition of discrimination in a variety of public- and private-law relationships. In the first year, there were nearly 500 complaints lodged and the number has risen steadily every since. There are also other remedies such as seeking compensation through the courts, or turning to one of the Parliamentary Commissioners where public authorities are concerned.52 There is also the Roma Police Officers' Association in Hungary and Roma people are able to file a complaint to the association. The complaints mostly concern discrimination in employment, discriminatory treatment, and discrimination by law enforcement authorities or police officers.53 [53] Taking all of this into account, while not perfect, I find that there is recourse for Roma people who face discrimination in Hungary. It is reasonable to expect the state would be reasonably forthcoming with serious efforts at protection should they be sought. [54] Unfortunately, as noted by the Council of Europe's European Commission against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI), it is a failure of the local authorities in implementing centrally enacted legislation that is at the heart of much of the discrimination experienced by Roma in daily life.54 However, others note that Hungary has one of the most advanced systems for minority protection in the region. Despite these efforts, funding sometimes fails to reach groups with the greatest needs; no system is perfect.55 [55] Finally, as noted above, Hungary is a democracy. Freedom House notes in its 2009 report that democratic institutions are robust and likely to hold despite reckless party politics, illiberal rhetoric, high-profile corruption, and radicalization on the political Right aimed at the minority populations in Hungary.56 Counsel for the claimants' documents [56] In addition to the documentation on file from the Board, I have also reviewed the documents and submissions57 presented by counsel for the claimant. [57] Much of the documentary evidence confirms the ongoing struggle to assist the historically marginalized Roma communities in Hungary. There is also a psychological report58 which confirms the claimant's difficult upbringing after his mother died, his anxiety over returning to Hungary, and his desire to remain in Canada. The report is based on facts that have been relayed to the psychologist in contemplation of the claimant's refugee claim, and does not predate the claimant's arrival in Canada. The report does not serve to establish events that were not deemed credible in the claimant's testimony at the hearing. [58] It is accepted that the claimant may have experienced persecution due to perceived Roma ethnicity, though with a fair complexion and blue eyes, he acknowledges that he does not always know how anyone knows he is Roma. [59] The documentary evidence further highlights the need of continued focussed programming and implementation, to ensure that the help is reaching the right communities; however, I find that overall, the evidence from the many independent sources before the panel who have no vested interest in their assessment of the situation in Hungary for minorities indicates that there has been success associated with every program implemented. There is empirical evidence of the treatment of racist violence under the criminal system, with successful investigation and prosecution of violent attacks as set out above. CONCLUSION [60] The burden is on the claimant to provide clear and convincing evidence that the state protection available would not be adequate. Evidence shows that state's efforts are serious, that progress is slow, but that there are positive signs and there are concrete results, and I find that adequate protection is available. [61] As a result, the claims pursuant to section 96 of the IRPA fail. There being no other evidence that would indicate that the claimants would be subject to the risks delineated in section 97 of the IRPA, the claims pursuant to that section fail as well. [62] I find that the claimant, XXXXXXX, is not a Convention refugee, nor a person in need of protection.
