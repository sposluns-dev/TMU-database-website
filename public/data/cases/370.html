The respondent, Ms. Fariba Nasser, seeks a declaration that the Loan is family property. Before considering this issue, I will provide a brief background on the procedural history of the dispute between the parties and their appearances before me. [2] The parties first appeared before me in 2018 and I pronounced reasons indexed at 2018 BCSC 695 (the "First Reasons"). At that time the main issue before me was Ms. Nasser's request that Mr. Kariminia complete certain forms so that an Islamic Iranian divorce could be obtained. Ms. Nasser also sought a compensation order for family assets and reapportionment in her favour. She claimed that two properties in Iran, a house and an apartment, were family assets (the "House" and the "Apartment"): see First Reasons at para. 56. I found that Mr. Kariminia owned the House at the time of separation in September 2012 and it was family property: see First Reasons at para. 78. I was unable to find that either party owned the Apartment at the time of separation and, as such, it did not qualify as family property: see First Reasons at para. 79. [3] There was some evidence respecting the Loan, made by Mr. Kariminia to an individual in Iran, which I will address further in these reasons. [4] The parties returned before me in the fall of 2018 on a contempt application against Mr. Kariminia for his failure to comply with orders I made. I dealt with the contempt application in unpublished reasons indexed at 2019 BCSC 614 (the "Second Reasons"). There were no findings or orders made in these reasons respecting the Loan. [5] The parties returned before me in November 2019 to resolve outstanding issues relating to arrears in child support, the claim for spousal support, the valuation of the House and costs. I addressed the further relief in unpublished reasons indexed at 2020 BCSC 636 (the "Third Reasons"). I ordered that an appraiser be jointly retained to provide a fair market value of the House. There were no findings made in these reasons respecting the Loan. [6] The parties returned before me again in August 2021 on a number of issues, including a request that Mr. Kariminia pay 50% of the value of the House to Ms. Nasser. In unpublished reasons, indexed at 2021 BCSC 1969 (the "Fourth Reasons"), I ordered that the value of the House as of 2014 was $70,000. Ms. Nasser's share of the House was $35,000: see Fourth Reasons at para. 58. [7] The issue of a different loan, one that Mr. Kariminia received from the Ministry of Science, Research, and Technology (the "Ministry"), was canvassed in the Fourth Reasons. I found that the loan was a family debt and ordered Ms. Nasser be responsible for paying 50 percent of the amount actually paid to the Ministry in satisfaction of this debt: see Fourth Reasons at paras. 91-96. [8] The Loan was not addressed in the Fourth Reasons. [9] On November 4, 2021, Ms. Nasser filed a notice of appeal of the Fourth Reasons. In her factum, she seeks to challenge the findings respecting the debt to the Ministry and the valuation of the House. She further seeks to adduce fresh evidence respecting Mr. Kariminia's ability to pay various outstanding debts. I will further address the issues raised in the appeal later in these reasons. Finally, I will provide my findings on the Loan Application and costs. Relevant Background [19] The parties were married on November 24, 1994 in Iran. They separated on September 23, 2012 in British Columbia. They were divorced in British Columbia on September 24, 2015. They have a daughter, born in November 2003, and a son, born in July 2006. [20] The parties' financial situation leading up to Loan and thereafter is of particular relevance. The Parties' Financial History [21] At the time of the marriage, Mr. Kariminia was attending university in Tehran, studying for a master's degree in soil science. He received a scholarship for his master's degree. He says he went from high school directly into a bachelor's program and did not have a history of work or income. His family paid his expenses while he completed the bachelor's program. [22] After completing his master's degree, he began working as a university professor. His income was approximately 1,500,000 Rials per month. He estimates that as of November 1994 this would be the equivalent of approximately $130 per month (or an annual income of $1,560). In 2004, he quit his job and came to Canada. At this time, his monthly income was approximately 3,000,000 Rials or approximately $375 per month (or an annual income of $4,500). [23] According to Mr. Kariminia, at the time of the marriage he did not have any significant savings, assets, inheritance, income or history of income. [24] In 2004, Mr. Kariminia began attending the University of British Columbia in a PhD program ("UBC"). He had a scholarship from the University of Gilan in Iran, which included a monthly living allowance of some $2,000, which was increased to $3,500 after the first years of his studies. He was enrolled at UBC for approximately 8 years. He did not complete the program for a number of personal reasons. [25] The terms of the scholarship required that Mr. Kariminia return to Iran and resume his teaching job at the University of Gilan. His family members are named as guarantors and a mortgage was registered against his mother's residence. He estimates he is indebted to the University of Gilan for approximately $150,000 CAD. [26] He stopped receiving the living allowance in approximately 2008, four years into his program. To replace the income, Mr. Kariminia began working as a teacher's assistant at UBC and received approximately $1,000 to $1,500 per month. Ms. Nasser also began working on part-time basis earning approximately $700 per month. [27] Mr. Kariminia says that the parties' living expenses were approximately $3,500 per month and that university fees were approximately $1,000 per month. Their incomes were not enough to cover these expenses. To supplement their incomes, Mr. Kariminia took out two students loans totalling $90,000. In 2006, Mr. Kariminia took out a line of credit of approximately $60,000 to $65,000, which was ultimately increased to approximately $75,000. He claims that his mother also contributed to their living expenses. [28] Mr. Kariminia says that over a period of four years their annual expenses dwarfed their annual income by approximately $35,000 each year. [29] Mr. Kariminia says that in 2010, approximately $20,000 (or about 290,000,000 Rials) was transferred to help Ms. Nasser's mother purchase a property. He says that the funds were wired to Mr. Kariminia's brother on May 1, 2010 and, on the same day, the funds were used to purchase a property in the name of Mahvash Karimi Kalimani, Ms. Nasser's mother, for the amount of 433,000,000 Rials. [30] Ms. Nasser does not dispute that a house was purchased for her mother. [31] In November 2012, Mr. Kariminia declared bankruptcy. In September 2015, Ms. Nasser declared bankruptcy. The Loan [32] The full details of the Loan and the interaction between the various parties to it remains unclear to me. There are some documents produced in Farsi, translated into English, from court proceedings in Iran. It is clear to me that the entirety of the court file has not been produced. The following is my best attempt at understanding the Loan transaction and the various court proceedings in Iran, without the consideration of the evidence in the New Affidavits. [33] Mr. Kariminia does not dispute that a loan was made to Askar Rezaei in Iran for the amount of 3,600,000,000 Rials. Mr. Rezaei was a childhood friend of his. The Loan was guaranteed by Mr. Rezaei's father-in-law, Karim Ahadzadeh Ghanad ("Mr. Ahadzadeh"). Mr. Kariminia says that the Loan was an investment and was made to take advantage of the high interest rates in Iran. [34] On May 1, 2010, Mr. Kariminia, as creditor, executed a mortgage and security documents in Iran for 3,600,000,000 Rials owed to him by Mr. Ahadzadeh, as borrower and mortgagor. The term was for 51 months, from April 21, 2010 to July 22, 2014 with various repayment dates. The mortgage secured a piece of property in Shalkou village, Iran (the "Property") owned by Mr. Ahadzadeh. Although the mortgage and security documents were executed in May 2010 it is unclear when or how the 3,600,000,000 Rials were actually transferred to Mr. Rezaei. [35] Mr. Kariminia argues that even though his name appears on all of the loan documents, only a minor percentage of the loan amount came from him. He says that the majority of the funds came from his brothers, Ramin Kariminia and Mohammed Amin Kariminia ("Ramin" and "Amin"). Mr. Kariminia provided conflicting evidence on the exact amount he contributed. I address this issue later in these reasons. [36] Mr. Kariminia explains that he knew Mr. Rezaei much better than his brothers did. He says that the documents were drafted in his name with the belief that Mr. Rezaei would be more likely to repay to someone he felt closer to. Ms. Nasser disputes that Mr. Kariminia had a closer relationship to Mr. Rezaei than his brothers did. Repayment of the Loan [37] There is conflicting evidence regarding how much of the Loan, if any, was repaid to Mr. Kariminia. Mr. Kariminia asserts that he never received any funds back in relation to the Loan. This is disputed by Ms. Nasser. [38] Ms. Nasser provided a series of translated documents which she argues show payments made to Mr. Kariminia towards satisfaction of the Loan (the "Documents"). The translated documents show payments from a number of individuals made from May 2, 2002 to August 31, 2010. On the translations provided by Ms. Nasser, the recipient account holder for each payment is identified as Mr. Kariminia. Ms. Nasser has totaled the payments to 928,500,000 Rials, or the equivalent of $92,850 CAD. It appears that the remittances were all made to bank accounts in Iran. [39] Mr. Kariminia asserts that five of the Documents were mistranslated and wrongly reflect Mr. Kariminia as the recipient, when the payments were in fact made to his brother, Amin. Mr. Kariminia provided corrected translations of those documents. Mr. Kariminia says that the documents only show that the equivalent of $1,000 to $2,000 CAD was deposited to him after the mortgage and security documents were executed in May 2010. [40] It appears that at some point in time Mr. Kariminia commenced proceedings against Mr. Ahadzadeh in Iran. He obtained a default judgment and Mr. Ahadzadeh was ordered to sell the property. [41] The Property devalued since 2010 and was sold on May 8, 2012 for 2,710,000,000 Rials. Mr. Kariminia says that he did not receive any funds from the sale of the Property. He claims that the funds were used to pay his brothers since they had contributed the majority of principal amount loaned to Mr. Rezaei. Ms. Nasser asserts that all of the money from the sale of the Property was received by Mr. Kariminia. [42] The sale of the Property occurred before the parties separated in September 2012. [43] Following the sale of the Property, attempts were made in Iran to collect the balance of the funds owing under the Loan of approximately 800,000,000 Rials. Mr. Kariminia says they were not successful. He says that the actions were taken under his name, since the original documents were in his name, but the legal efforts were directed by his brothers. He was advised by his brother that, to date, his brothers have spent 360,000,000 Rials on legal fees. He says neither he nor Ms. Nasser have contributed to these legal fees. [44] On July 21, 2013 the Division 1 Rasht Public (Legal) Court issued a default judgment ordering Mr. Ahadzadeh to pay the difference between the proceeds of the sale of the Property and the principal amount of the Loan. [45] On March 12, 2014, the Iranian Court issued a writ of execution. It ordered Mr. Ahadzadeh to provide a list of assets available to satisfy the outstanding amount owed. [46] On August 23, 2014, the Iranian court issued an arrest warrant for Mr. Ahadzadeh for the financial debt he owed Mr. Kariminia. The arrest warrant puts the debt at 799,993,314.1 Rials. [47] On November 11, 2014, the Iranian court issued a letter stating that the arrest order of Mr. Ahadzadeh was cancelled. [48] Mr. Ahadzadeh appealed the judgment regarding the debt owed to the plaintiff. As part of the appeal, on January 12, 2017, a report was prepared by Karim Karimpour, an "Official Expert to the Judiciary-Accounting and Auditing" (the "Karimpour Report"). The report was to examine the existing financial documents and calculate the real claim of the creditor, how much had been paid to the creditor, whether any installments had been paid, and the amount outstanding. [49] The relevant findings in the Karimpour Report are: · The financial transactions by and between Mr. Rezaei and Mr. Kariminia existed long before May 1, 2010; however, when their financial relations began and the amount of their primary debt was unclear; · Upon the execution of the mortgage, through which the property belonging to Mr. Ahadzadeh was mortgaged, the remaining claim of the creditor from Mr. Rezaei was 3,600,000,000 Rials on May 1, 2010; · The debtor, Mr. Rezaei, paid three consecutive installments to the Registration Department of Anzali each for 30,000,000 Rials, for a total of 90,000,000 Rials. The payments were dated April 21, 2010, May 22, 2010, and June 22, 2010; · Due to the non-payment of subsequent installments by the main debtor, Mr. Rezaei, the Property was sold for 2,710,000,000 Rials; · The remaining claim of Mr. Kariminia was 800,000,000 Rials; · Mr. Kariminia has not received any amount of money, including the installments related to the mortgage deed, directly from the appellant, Mr. Ahadzadeh; and · Mr. Rezaei and other individuals made various payments to Mr. Kariminia leaving a debt balance outstanding of 204,500,000 Rials. [50] The Karimpour Report refers to various attachments; however, the copy of the report provided in evidence does not include the attachments. Mr. Kariminia says that the Karimpour Report was later rejected by the Court of Appeal and the appeal was dismissed as the Court found that Mr. Ahadzadeh and Mr. Rezaei had not repaid Mr. Kariminia. [51] On January 29, 2017, the 8th Division of Guilan Province Courts of Appeal issued a written judgment. The salient parts of the court ruling are: · This was an appeal of a written judgment dated December 20, 2014 of the 1st Division of Rasht Legal Court; · The Court found that the remaining claim was 700,000,000 Rials; and · The appeal was rejected. [52] On November 30, 2017, a warrant for the arrest of Mr. Ahadzadeh was issued. On January 13, 2018, a further warrant of arrest was issued. It is not clear to me what happened in regards to these arrest warrants. Discussion of the Loan in the Prior Reasons of this Court [53] The issue of the Loan was addressed in the First Reasons. As Mr. Kariminia was not present at the hearing, his evidence on this issue was only obtained from an affidavit sworn in September 2014. The First Reasons state: [69] The respondent further claims that Mr. Kariminia made a loan in Iran for IRR 3,600,000,000, approximately $360,000 CAD, to Asgar Rezaei on May 1, 2010. The loan was secured by Karim Ahadzadeh, Mr. Rezaei's father-in-law. The loan had to be paid back in installments. She claims that the loan was not back and Mr. Kariminia took legal action against Mr. Ahadzadeh to obtain repayment. As a result of the legal action, the respondent claims that Mr. Kariminia was able to secure the repayment of the loan with interest. [70] Mr. Kariminia's evidence in his September 2014 Affidavit at para. 51 is: I agree that in 2009 the respondent and I co-signed a student line of credit in the amount of $75,000. However, I did not trick the respondent in any way with respect to the loan. We made the decision together to take out the loan in order to give the money to Mr. Askar Rezaei in Iran, who said that he could invest the money in Iran and provide us with a high rate of return. Mr. Rezaei did not pay us back the full amount of the money we gave him and we therefore were unable to pay back the student line of credit. [71] The respondent has produced some documents summarizing principal and interest paid on the loan. She has summarized the various payments, which total IRR 928,500,000, amounting to $92,850 CAD. It is not clear from the documents when the last payments were made. It appears that the last receipt is dated August 2010. [72] The respondent sought reapportionment of the assets in her favour, although the basis for a reapportionment was not clear. [54] There was insufficient evidence adduced regarding the Loan. As such, I made no findings nor orders related to the Loan in the First Reasons. The First Reasons were not appealed. [55] The Fourth Reasons are subject to an appeal. While the Fourth Reasons did not concern the issue of the Loan, the issue of the Loan re-emerged in Ms. Nasser's factum. In her factum, Ms. Nasser seeks to adduce fresh evidence of "business proceeds" of approximately $350,550 that Mr. Kariminia allegedly received, which she asserted should be considered family assets. She claimed that she should receive $165,275 of these proceeds. It is my understanding that these "business proceeds" refer to alleged repayments made in satisfaction of the Loan. In his factum, Mr. Kariminia argued that Ms. Nasser was asking the Court of Appeal to make findings of facts concerning the determination and valuation of family property at the first instance, when this was not the subject of the decision appealed from and where the Supreme Court had already made a decision concerning the issue in dispute in a previous order that was not appealed. Issues [56] I will address the following issues: 1. Is Ms. Nasser entitled to have the issue of the Loan addressed further? 2. Is Mr. Kariminia entitled to rely on the New Affidavits? 3. Was there a loan made by Mr. Kariminia to Mr. Rezaei in Iran during the relationship? 4. What costs should be awarded? Issue 1: Is Ms. Nasser entitled to have the issue of the Loan addressed further? Position of the Parties Position of Mr. Kariminia [57] Mr. Kariminia argues that Ms. Nasser brought a summary trial application on January 2, 2018 seeking final judgment on the issue of division of property. He argues that the First Reasons extinguished the family law claim regarding the Loan and that this judgment was not appealed. He points out, however, that Ms. Nasser sought to appeal the issue of the Loan in the appeal of the Fourth Reasons. He argues that this supports that it was an issue that was determined; if it was still outstanding there would be no need to file an appeal of that issue. He relies on the doctrines of abuse of process and cause of action estoppel, a branch of res judicata, to support his position. Position of Ms. Nasser [58] Ms. Nasser submits that when she was before the Court in 2018, there was little evidence about the Loan available and the Court did not rule on whether the Loan was family property. She has now obtained more evidence. [59] Ms. Nasser argues that Mr. Kariminia is attempting to use the doctrines of res judicata and abuse of process to bar her from a fair and equitable result that is in the interest of justice. Further, Mr. Kariminia is attempting to use the cause of action estoppel as a sword instead of a shield which should not be permitted. Analysis [60] I reject Mr. Kariminia's arguments that the doctrines of res judicata or abuse of process apply in the circumstances before me and, in any event, I find that it is in the interest of justice to allow for further consideration of the Loan, including the circumstances under which it was made and whether any repayments of it have been made. This is the only way to achieve a just outcome and to decide this issue on the merits: Wozny v. Gelber, 2021 BCSC 314 at para. 46. [61] I disagree with Mr. Kariminia's submission that the First Reasons extinguished the family law claim in this action. [62] Mr. Kariminia asserts that the application filed by Ms. Nasser on January 3, 2018 was a summary trial. It is not clear to me that the application was brought as a summary trial and the notice of application does not, on its face, seek a summary trial. [63] In the First Reasons, I addressed the Loan under the heading, Division of Assets in Iran. I did not make a finding that Mr. Kariminia had advanced the full sum of approximately $360,000 nor on what amounts were allegedly repaid. I made no order respecting the Loan, whether it was a family property or the amount of monies repaid on the Loan. The only asset in Iran that I determined was family property was the House. I further note that Mr. Kariminia was not in attendance at this hearing. [64] I am not persuaded that Ms. Nasser should be barred from making further submissions on the issue of the Loan in light of the further court documents she obtained from the courts in Iran in November 2020. Issue 2: Is Mr. Kariminia entitled to rely on the New Affidavits? [65] Mr. Kariminia filed a notice of application on July 6, 2023 seeking leave to make further submissions and rely on the New Affidavits. [66] This application for new evidence was made while the matter was adjourned to permit the parties to make further written submissions. A decision had not yet been rendered. Legal Principles [67] The test for the admission of fresh evidence is set out in Palmer v. the Queen, [1980] 1 S.C.R. 759 at 775, 1979 CanLII 8, as cited in First Majestic Silver Corp. v. Santos, 2013 BCCA 458 at para. 25: 1. The evidence should generally not be admitted if, by due diligence, it could have been adduced at trial provided that this general principle will not be applied as strictly in a criminal case as in civil cases; 2. The evidence must be relevant in the sense that it bears upon a decisive or potentially decisive issue in the trial; 3. The evidence must be credible in the sense that it is reasonably capable of belief; and 4. It must be such that if believed it could reasonably, when taken with the other evidence adduced at the trial, be expected to have the affected the result. [68] The Palmer test is described by the Supreme Court of Canada as "purposive, fact-driven, and driven by an overarching concern for the interests of justice": Barendregt v. Grebliunas, 2022 SCC 22 at para. 31 [Barendregt]. [69] Before considering the position of the parties, I will provide a summary of the evidence in the New Affidavits. Summary of the New Evidence [70] The individuals referenced in the New Affidavits are: · Mr. Kariminia and his two brothers, Ramin and Amin; · The brothers' uncle: Kiyormarsh Karimi (the "Uncle"); and · The Uncle's sister: Aunt to the brothers, and mother of Ms. Nasser: Mahvash Karimi (the "respondent's mother"). [71] The salient portions of the Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia respecting the Loan are: · Ramin was the primary person involved in giving the loan to Mr. Rezaei and trying to collect from him; · There were three individuals who contributed the bulk of the Loan: Mr. Kariminia, Ramin, and their other brother, Amin; · In early 2010, Mr. Kariminia sent $60,000, equivalent to 613,200,000 Rials, from Canada to Iran. Of that sum, approximately $29,000, equivalent to 290,000,000 Rials, was used to purchase a house for the respondent's mother. The remaining $31,000 was Mr. Kariminia's contribution to the Loan; · The contributions to the Loan consisted of: § $31,000, equivalent to 323,200,000 Rials from Mr. Kariminia, being approximately 9% of the total Loan made; § $10,500, equivalent to 110,000,000 Rials, from the sale of a rural property owned by the brothers' father; § $39,000, equivalent to 400,000,000 Rials, from Amin; and § $270,000, equivalent to 2,766,800,000 Rials, from Ramin. · The brothers agreed that each would receive the money back in the order of who contributed the most, with Ramin receiving his share first, followed by Amin, and then Mr. Kariminia; · Mr. Rezaei paid Ramin three consecutive instalments of 30,000,000 Rials for a total of 90,000,000. After Mr. Rezaei stopped payments, the Property was sold and the total sum of 2,800,000,000 Rials was received by Ramin. The outstanding amount owed for the Loan was 800,000,000 Rials. By that time, Ramin incurred a further sum of 360,000,000 Rials for lawyer's fees and disbursements; · There have been no further payments made on the Loan since the sale of the Property. Mr. Kariminia and Amin have not received anything back on the contributions they made; and · Ramin has restarted the court process to see if any further money can be collected on the Loan. [72] Ramin further provides the following explanation respecting why he was previously not interested in assisting Mr. Kariminia. He states: 15. For a very long time I have been upset with Armin over this loan. For many years the family had lost a lot of money on this venture. Even after the sale of Ahadzadeh's house I still didn't have all my money back and my brother Mohammad-Amin hadn't received any of his money back. To add to that I had to spend many hours, days and weeks in hiring/instructing lawyers or going to court over this loan. This has been very stressful on us here in Iran while Armin has continued to live in Canada. Because of this for a long time I did not have much interest in helping him with his divorce case in Canada. Ultimately, I agreed to help by providing this affidavit because I did not think it is fair for him to have to pay for money, he himself hasn't received. [73] The evidence of Amin is that he contributed 400,000,000 Rials to the Loan and he has not received any money back. [74] The Uncle's evidence is that in 2009 and 2010, they were looking to buy a house for Ms. Nasser's mother, his sister. In 2010, the Uncle found a good place in the city of Banhar Anzali. He confirmed the price of the house was 433,000,000 Rials, with Mr. Kariminia and the respondent agreeing to contribute 290,000,000 Rials to the purchase price. On May 1, 2010, Ramin brought the 290,000,000 Rials to the real estate office where the purchase was initiated and the house bought. Position of the Parties Position of Mr. Kariminia [75] Mr. Kariminia argues that the test in Palmer is met and the New Affidavits should be admitted to clarify the position taken by Ms. Nasser in her submissions. He gives the example that Ms. Nasser relies on the Karimpour Report to assert that all of the monies that were repaid were repaid personally to Mr. Kariminia. He argues that this submission is contrary to the Karimpour Report which found that the three installments totalling 90,000,000 Rials were paid to the Registration Department of Anzali and not personally to Mr. Kariminia. The New Affidavits explain that Mr. Kariminia did not receive this money, but his brother Ramin did. [76] The respondent also submits that Mr. Kariminia personally received the funds from the sale of the Property. The Karimpour Report says these funds were paid to the benefit of the creditor. The New Affidavits confirm that Ramin received these funds. [77] Mr. Kariminia argues that the first branch of the test is met as he has provided an explanation on why he did not obtain the affidavits from his brother earlier. The evidence is that his brother, Ramin, was unwilling to help until he was advised that Ms. Nasser was seeking an order that Mr. Kariminia pay her money that he never received. [78] Mr. Kariminia submits that the second branch of the test has been met since the evidence is highly relevant as it goes to the heart of the issues of how much money was loaned and how much monies were received by Mr. Kariminia. The Affidavit of the Uncle provides a further explanation respecting the loan made to purchase Ms. Nasser's mother a home. [79] Mr. Kariminia argues the third branch of the test is met since the evidence comes from the individuals directly involved in the making of the Loan and in the court proceedings in Iran. [80] Mr. Kariminia submits that the final branch is met since the evidence, if believed, would have a significant impact on the decision of the court in respect to the Loan. Position of Ms. Nasser [81] Ms. Nasser argues that Mr. Kariminia did not take all reasonable steps to present their best case as required by Barendregt at para. 36. She argues that Mr. Kariminia took no steps to obtain the evidence in the New Affidavits prior to 2018 or before the commencement of the last hearing. She argues that the New Affidavits are not practically conclusive of any results. She submits that the New Affidavits merely reiterate Mr. Kariminia's own version of events and the evidence in them is "confusing and conflat[es]" facts. Analysis [82] On the application of the test in Palmer, I find that all branches of the tests have been met with respect to Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia. On the due diligence branch, the evidence supports that the brother, Ramin, was unwilling to participate in this divorce proceeding. It is understandable why he had no interest in doing so. I accept that when he was advised that Ms. Nasser was seeking to have Mr. Kariminia pay monies which he understood Mr. Kariminia had not received he was prepared to swear an affidavit explaining the circumstances. In my view, some additional leeway should be provided when dealing with obtaining evidence from Iran. It is not as straight-forward as obtaining evidence from someone locally resident. [83] There can be no dispute that the evidence in Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia is relevant to the issues before me. The evidence directly addresses the two main issues that I need to decide being who funded the Loan and whether any repayments have been made to Mr. Kariminia. [84] I have considered whether there are any reasons to reject Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia on the basis that the evidence is not credible. When looking holistically at all of the evidence, the information provided makes sense and I am satisfied as to its credibility. [85] Finally, I am persuaded that the evidence in Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia plays a role in deciding the key issues. In my view, to refuse to consider this evidence would be unfair and result in an unjust decision. I exercise my discretion and admit Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia into the record. [86] With respect to the affidavits of the Uncle and the other brother, Amin, I find the first branch of the Palmer test is not met. Mr. Kariminia has not provided an adequate explanation as to why this evidence could not, by due diligence, have been adduced earlier. Further, I am not satisfied that the evidence provided in the two affidavits could reasonably be expected to affect the result of the Loan Application given that it does not provide any new information not already provided by Mr. Kariminia or in Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia. I find that Mr. Kariminia is not entitled to rely on the affidavit evidence of his Uncle nor his brother, Mohammed Amin. Issue 3: Was there a loan made by Mr. Kariminia to Mr. Rezaei in Iran during the relationship? [87] The parties agree that if monies from the line of credit or other family money was used to make a loan while the parties were living together that loan is family property. They further agree that any repayment on any such loan should be divided equally subject to any claim for reapportionment. The two disputed issues are: 1. What was the amount of money that was loaned by Mr. Kariminia? 2. What amount, if any, was repaid to Mr. Kariminia? What was the amount of money that was loaned by Mr. Kariminia? [88] I agree with the submissions of Ms. Nasser that Mr. Kariminia has given inconsistent evidence on the amount that he advanced. A summary of Mr. Kariminia's evidence is: Affidavit #2 sworn September 29, 2014 · He co-signed a student line of credit of $75,000 in 2009. The parties made the decision to take out the line of credit in order to give the money to Mr. Rezaei in Iran, who said he could invest the money in Iran and provide them with a high rate of return: at para. 51. Affidavit #4 sworn November 14, 2018 · Only 10-20% of the Loan came from Mr. Kariminia and the rest from his brothers: at para. 38. Affidavit #10 sworn November 9, 2022 · Some funds were used from the RBC Student Line of Credit in 2010 towards the Loan: at para. 2; · Only about 10-20% of the Loan came from him, the rest from his brothers: at para. 3. · Most of the line of credit was used for family expenses and a portion was sent to Iran for the Loan: at para. 3. · It was impossible for him to grant the full amount of the Loan due to his income and expenses and only a small amount was sent to Iran: at para. 3. The Claimant's Reply Submissions, dated March 15, 2023 · The parties took out student loans and a line of credit totaling approximately $165,000. Of this total amount, only approximately $40,000 could have been used for anything other than the family's expenses: at para. 40. · He contributed 5-10% of the Loan: at para 12. · His recollection is that he contributed between $20,000 to $30,000 to the Loan: at para. 42. [89] Based on the evidence before me, and in particular, the financial situation that the parties had leading up to May 2010, I am not persuaded that Mr. Kariminia made a loan of $360,000 to Mr. Rezaei in Iran. The evidence does not support that he had any available funds to make such a payment. Ms. Nasser has not provided any explanation of how, on the income that she and Mr. Kariminia had, combined with the line of credit and student loans, that amount of money could have been saved. [90] Ms. Nasser submits that it makes no legal or logical sense to draft the documents exclusively and solely in the name of Mr. Kariminia. She further asserts Mr. Kariminia's statement that Mr. Rezaei knew him better than his brothers so all the documents were drafted in his name with the belief that Mr. Rezaei would be more likely to repay is "unfounded". She points out that Mr. Kariminia did not even live in Iran. [91] In assessing the conduct of individuals from a different culture I must be mindful not to make assumptions based on what may seem improbable based on Canadian culture and values. The words of Justice Griffin, as she then was, in Fu v. Zhu, 2018 BCSC 9 are apt: [41] As well, I have kept in mind that motives and conduct that might seem improbable to a person raised in a Canadian culture might not be improbable in another cultural context. The very structure of the transactions at issue in this case was unusual in the Canadian context, as it involved large sums of money changing hands over several years, without any written agreements in place or any common accounting practices. I have been mindful that different cultural contexts can affect the court's perspective as to inherent probabilities or improbabilities. [92] I accept that in Iran transactions may well be carried out differently and the importance of the relationship with an individual may well be of specific importance. I cannot find that the rationale provided by Mr. Kariminia does not make sense in light of the cultural context. [93] Ramin's evidence supports that the amount that was transferred to Iran by Mr. Kariminia was $60,000 CAD. From that amount approximately $29,000 CAD was used to assist in the purchase of a home for Ms. Nasser's mother. That left approximately $31,000 CAD that was used to contribute to the Loan. I accept this evidence. [94] I make the declaration that $31,000 of the Loan is family property. What amount, if any, of money was repaid to Mr. Kariminia? [95] This is another challenging issue to decide on affidavit evidence since the evidence of Mr. Kariminia is inconsistent and conflicting. A summary of his evidence is: Affidavit #2 sworn September 29, 2014 · Mr. Rezaei did not pay the parties back the full amount of the money they gave him and therefore they were unable to pay back the student line of credit: at para. 51. Affidavit #4 sworn November 14, 2018 · He did not receive any funds back in relation to the Loan: at para. 42. Affidavit #10 sworn November 9, 2022 · He did not receive proceeds from any "business dealings" from Mr. Rezaei: at para. 8. · The proceeds from the sale of the Property were not paid to him, but to his brothers: at para. 8. · He cannot say for certain what funds, if any, were received in 2010 by him from Mr. Rezaei in repayment of the loan since this was 12 years ago, however he does not believe any such funds were received: at para. 16.b. · He did not receive any funds from his brothers, Seyed Mohammad reza Emman, or Shahram Sanadiani in regard to the Loan: at para. 16.c. · None of the funds received pursuant to the Loan were exchanged into Canadian dollars or sent to Canada and he has not received any funds or any benefits of the Loan: at para. 17. In addition, one of the amounts in the summary of the Documents records the sum paid on September 17, 2009 as 170,000,000 Rials, although the receipt produced shows a payment of 17,000,000 Rials for the date. Issue 4: What costs should be awarded? [105] Ms. Nasser seeks an award of special costs against Mr. Kariminia on the basis that Mr. Kariminia has concealed the proceeds of the Loan from her and the Court. [106] The applicable legal principles were summarized by Justice Milman in Yusufi v. Yusufi, 2022 BCSC 900 at para. 60: [60] The circumstances in which this Court has in the past awarded special costs in matrimonial litigation were conveniently summarized by Warren J. in Hu v. Dickson, 2015 BCSC 218, as follows: [46] Special costs are awarded where a party's litigation conduct can be characterized as "reprehensible". In this context the word reprehensible encompasses both scandalous and outrageous conduct and also milder forms of misconduct deserving of reproof or rebuke This does not mean that all forms of misconduct justify a special costs order - rather, the misconduct must be such as to be deserving of reproof or rebuke [47] The purpose for this high level of costs is punitive and intended to express the court's disapproval of the party's conduct. It is not necessary that all aspects of a party's conduct in the litigation be reprehensible in order to make an award of special costs that applies to the entire action However, pursuant to Rule 16‑1(14) of the Supreme Court Family Rules, the court has the discretion to award costs that relate to only certain aspects of a proceeding and may do so where it would be disproportionate to award special costs of the entire proceeding [48] The court must exercise restraint in awarding special costs and as such the party seeking special costs must demonstrate exceptional circumstances to justify a special costs order [49] In Kim v. Hong Justice Griffin quoted extensively from the judgment of Justice Williams in Schwabe v. Dr. Lisinski where he summarized a number of cases in an effort to discern the kinds of conduct that had been characterized as reprehensible and thus warranting an award of special costs. Justice Griffin also categorized the conduct that had been found to justify an award of special costs in a number of family cases. From her reasons, it is apparent that the kinds of conduct that warrant an award of special costs include the following: · acting with an improper motive, such as to intimidate, exhaust or financially drain the other party in the hopes that they will give up or soften their position in the litigation; · dissipating and/or not disclosing assets; · abusing the court's process by, among other things, failing to disclose documents, delaying in disclosing documents, failing to respond to reasonable requests, causing unnecessary interlocutory applications, and breaching the Rules of Court in a manner that prejudices the other party; · misleading the court, through outright fabrications or through evasive and/or equivocal responses; and · disobeying a court order. [Citations omitted.] [107] While I do find that Mr. Karaminia's conduct throughout this litigation has been problematic, in my view, it would not be appropriate to characterize it as reprehensible. I am not persuaded that a special costs award should be made against Mr. Kariminia. However, I have taken the implications of Mr. Karaminia's conduct into account in my costs award set out below. [108] Pursuant to R. 16-1(7) of the Family Rules, costs should be awarded to the successful party unless the court orders otherwise. It is accepted that "success" in this context means "substantial success": D.N. v. M.R., 2020 BCSC 475 at para. 12. [109] As noted in D.N., the following cost principles apply in family law cases: [12] A succinct summary of the legal principles respecting costs in family law cases was provided by Justice Bernard in S.A.M. v. J.A.M., 2017 BCSC 2348 at para. 7: (a) The costs of a family law case must be awarded to the successful party, unless the court otherwise orders (Rule 16-1(7)) of the Supreme Court Family Rules [Rules]; (b) The "successful party" is the party who has achieved substantial success at the trial/hearing (Fotheringham v. Fotheringham, 2001 BCSC 1321; Marquez v. Zapiola, 2014 BCCA 35); (c) The court's discretion to "order otherwise" must be exercised judicially, bearing in mind a non-exhaustive list of factors that includes hardship, earning capacity, the purpose of the particular award, the conduct of the parties in relation to the litigation, and the importance of not upsetting the balance achieved by the court in relation to the substantive issues (Gold v. Gold (1993), 106 D.L.R. (4th) 452 (B.C.C.A.)); (d) The party who asks the court to "order otherwise" bears the onus to persuade the court why it should do so (S.D.W. v. C.W.W., 2006 BCSC 162); (e) Financial hardship, without more, is not a sufficient basis for the court to exercise its discretion to depart from the usual rule that successful parties are entitled to their costs (S.D.W. v. C.W.W., supra.; K.D.P. v. A.R.K aka R.K., 2012 BCSC 1025; Brown v. Kucher, 2016 BCSC 194); (f) Where an offer to settle a family case has been made, the court may award double costs for all or some of the steps taken after the date of delivery or service of the offer (Rule 11-1(5) of the Rules); (g) As a general rule, an award of double costs should be made against a party who did not accept an offer to settle that should, in all the circumstances, have been accepted (Hartshorne v. Hartshorne, 2011 BCCA 29); and, (h) In determining whether a double costs order should be made, the court may consider any factor the court considers appropriate, including whether the offer was one that ought reasonably to have been accepted upon receipt or at any later date, the relationship between the offer and the judgment, and the relative financial circumstances of the parties (Rule 11-1(6) of the Rules). [110] In my view, Mr. Kariminia achieved substantial success in opposing Ms. Nasser's application and some success in having one of the New Affidavits admitted into the record. [111] However, in the circumstances of this case, it is my view that the successful party, being Mr. Kariminia, ought to be deprived of his costs for the following reasons: · He failed to make full document disclosure during the litigation with respect to the documents respecting the Loan and the court proceedings in Iran; · His evidence in his affidavits on the Loan and repayments was misleading, confusing, and inaccurate; and · His actions resulted in a waste of court time and added expense to Ms. Nasser. [112] Despite Mr. Kariminia being successful, I exercise my discretion to depart from the normal rule of costs and deprive him of the costs of both applications. The parties will be responsible for their own costs except for the disbursements incurred by Ms. Nasser in obtaining court records in Iran and translating them. Mr. Kariminia ought to have produced those documents. He clearly had the authority to do so since he was a party to the proceedings. He chose not to do so but to place that burden on Ms. Nasser. Ms. Nasser will be entitled to be fully reimbursed for the disbursements she incurred to obtain and translate those documents. Conclusion [113] The following orders are made: 1. A declaration that the loan made by Mr. Kariminia of $31,000 is family property; 2. If Mr. Kariminia receives any payments on this loan he is to advise Ms. Nasser and share the proceeds equally with her; 3. The application of Mr. Kariminia to admit Affidavit #1 of Ramin Kariminia is permitted; 4. The application of Mr. Kariminia to admit Affidavit #1 of Kiyomarsh Karimi and Affidavit #1 of Mohammad Amin Kariminia is refused; 5. The application of Ms. Nasser for special costs against Mr. Kariminia is dismissed; 6. The parties will each bear their own costs of the applications except that Ms. Nasser is entitled to be fully reimbursed for the disbursements she incurred to obtain and translate the court documents from Iran; and 7. I am not seized with any aspect of this family law proceeding. "The Honourable Justice C. Forth"
