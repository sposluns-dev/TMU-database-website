Buttigieg Tribunal Counsel for the Claimant(s) Robin Morch Conseil(s) du / de la / des demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Tribunal Officer N/A Agent(e) de tribunal Designated Representative(s) N/A Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX, the claimant, is a citizen of Russia. He claims refugee protection pursuant to section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). ALLEGATIONS [2] The details of the claimant's allegations are documented in his Personal Information Form (PIF) narrative1 and testimony. The claimant faced many problems in Russia because of his Jewish faith/ethnicity. He described how he was beaten by fellow students in 2006. When the hospital contacted the police, the police arrived and took his statement. The police did not take any further action. The claimant was beaten by the police in XXXX 2010 after he was stopped and his identity documents were checked. He reported this incident to the Prosecutor's Office. A case was opened, but the claimant did not pursue his complaint. He was beaten again by anti-Semitic skinheads in XXXX 2010. When the police were called to the hospital, the police took no action, telling him to move elsewhere. He decided to leave Russia. He traveled to Canada on XXXX XXXX 2010 and made a claim for refugee protection on XXXX XXXX 2010. DETERMINATION [3] The panel finds that the claimant is not a Convention refugee as he does not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Russia. The panel also finds that the claimant is not a person in need of protection in that his removal to Russia would not subject him personally to a risk to life or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, and in that there are no substantial grounds to believe that his removal to Russia will subject him personally to a danger of torture. ANALYSIS [4] The claimant established his personal identity and his identity as a national of Russia through his testimony and the documents he presented.2 [5] The determinative issue is whether the claimant has rebutted the presumption of state protection. The panel finds that he has not for the following reasons. [6] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens except in situations where the state is in a state of complete breakdown.3 To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect absent an admission by the national's state of its inability to protect that national.4 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration, the test is whether the protection offered is adequate.5 The evidence that state protection is not adequate must be reliable and probative and it must also satisfy the panel, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.6 The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.7 [7] Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not be enough to justify a claim that the victims are unable to avail themselves of protection.8 The fact that state protection is not perfect9 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability to provide protection.10 The burden of proof that rests on the claimant increases with the level of democracy of the state in question. The more democratic a state is, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them to demonstrate state protection was or would not be forthcoming.11 [8] In this case, the claimant experienced mistreatment and feels he was let down by the authorities in Russia when he needed protection. When the police did not take action after he was beaten, the claimant did not report the incident to a higher authority. He received a document12 from the police stating that, due to lack of evidence, a criminal case will not be commenced. This document also informed him as follows: "The decision can be appealed in the Office of the District Prosecutor in the next ten days." The claimant, however, did not appeal this decision despite his dissatisfaction with the police's inaction because he did not think he would get any help. [9] The panel notes that doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection. [10] When the claimant was hit by the police in XXXX 2010, he reported the incident to the Prosecutor's Office. He disclosed a letter from the Prosecutor's Office13 stating that his complaint was sent for further consideration to the State Department of Police in the city of XXXX. The claimant says that he did not pursue this because he was frustrated that the case was referred to the same district where the police who hit him worked. It is not unreasonable for the same department to be charged with investigating an allegation of inappropriate behaviour of one of its subordinates. It shows that the authorities were taking a reasonable first step to have that officer investigated. [11] The claimant says that he subsequently received a telephone call from the police threatening to cause him trouble if he pursued the complaint. The fact that the police officer in question contacted the claimant indicates that steps were, as a minimum, commenced to investigate the claimant's complaint. His decision not to pursue the allegation results in speculation as to what the outcome would have been should he have pursued the case. The fact that the officer threatened him because he filed a complaint shows that the officer was concerned about some repercussion or consequence and that the process has some value. [12] While the officer's attitude is unfortunate, and must have been difficult to endure, the authorities appeared willing to assist. This is not clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, state would not be forthcoming with adequate protection. With respect to the other instances where the claimant did not report the police officer's behaviour, the panel notes that it was open to the claimant to approach a higher authority, such as the prosecutor's office to make a report or complaint. [13] While documentary evidence supports that police are sometimes slow to recognize the racial elements of crimes, they do prosecute under ordinary criminal offences which are easy to prosecute, and there is growing evidence of increased law enforcement efforts that has been responsible for reducing racially motivated attacks as is discussed below.14 According to the NGO Moscow Bureau for Human Rights, law enforcement officials are investigating vandalism in Voronezh, where 20 Jewish gravestones in a local Jewish cemetery were knocked down on 27 July 2010. On 07 October 2010, anti-Semitic inscriptions appeared on a Jewish synagogue in Barnaul. The local police are investigating. Documentary evidence indicates that the state is making serious efforts to combat anti-Semitic rhetoric, publications, crimes, and to change society attitudes. While progress is slow in some areas, it is clear the state is committed and there has been progress. [14] Estimates of the country's Jewish population range from between 250,000 and one million, with the majority living in Moscow, where the claimant lived, and Saint Petersburg. Jews living in Russia, but some believe this number to be underestimated because of the hesitation of some Jews to publicly identify their background. Jewish leaders reported improvements in official attitudes towards Jews, but anti-Semitism remained a problem at the societal level. Even so, violent attacks against Jews were infrequent with only a few episodes occurring during 2010. The SOVA Center, a non-government organization (NGO) that seeks to combat extremism and nationalism, monitored acts of anti-Semitism in 2010. According to SOVA, in 2009 approximately 71 persons were killed and 33 injured in attacks based on race or religious affiliation. There were 42 documented violent attacks motivated by religious hatred on individuals from January 2009 to April 2010 of which 20 were on Jehovah's Witnesses, 17 on Muslims, two on Protestants, and two on Russian Orthodox priests. In addition from January 2009 until April 2010, there were 65 documented acts of vandalism against the property of religious communities of which 27 were against Russian Orthodox property; 23 against Jehovah's Witnesses' property; seven against Jewish property; three against Muslim property; and one each for Protestants, Catholics, Armenian Orthodox, Mormons, and pagans.15 This indicates that the state's effort over the years has dramatically improved the circumstances for Jews in Russia. Obviously, not perfect, but clearly positive signs of progress. [15] The government has publicly criticized nationalist ideology and expressed support for legal action in response to anti-Semitic acts. While incidents still occurred and sometime authorities did not consider the racial motive, authorities continued to investigate and prosecute. In September 2009, skinheads in Khabarovsk threw Molotov cocktails into a synagogue and into the house of a policeman who had been investigating cases of extremism. Khabarovsk Anti-extremist Department police detained the group, and criminal proceedings were opened against two of the suspects. They faced up to five years' imprisonment for the synagogue attack and up to life imprisonment for the attack on the police officer.16 This indicates authorities do take action in crimes against Jews. It also indicates that authorities are sacrificing their own lives to stem extremism. [16] Other examples where authorities have acted include an incident in May 2010 when drunken soccer fans from Saint Petersburg beat a Jewish man and shouted anti-Semitic epithets including "Heil" and "Kill the Jews." Authorities began an investigation and promised to bring those responsible to justice. In December 2009, unknown assailants violently attacked two Jews outside a Moscow yeshiva, and a young Jewish man was assaulted by a neo-Nazi who shouted "Heil!" in the Moscow subway. In the latter incident, police detained the alleged attacker but charged him only with "minor hooliganism." While only charged with a minor offence, the man was detained and charged for simply shouting an offensive phrase; this is still indication that authorities will act even on what they perceive to be the most insignificant offences. In October 2009, the Tyumen Region Prosecutor's Office announced that a professor of sociology in Tyumen and member of the missionary department of the Tobolsk-Tyumen Diocese of the ROC, had been charged with incitement to religious hatred and denigrating the worth of a person or group because of their religion after some of her students complained about the content of her lectures in 2008. The professor spoke about blood libel, the spurious charge that Jews use the blood of Christian children to make matza.17 The authorities are clearly willing to take action regardless of who the perpetrator is. [17] There were several instances in which the government successfully prosecuted individuals for anti-Semitic statements or publications.18 There were many reports of anti-Semitic publications during 2010. There was also evidence of a concerted government campaign to limit the sale and distribution of anti-Semitic literature.19 [18] Federal authorities, and in many cases regional and local authorities, facilitated the establishment of new Jewish institutions. The Federation of Jewish Communities reported that federal officials and some regional officials had been cooperative in the community's efforts to seek restitution of former synagogues, although some Jewish groups asserted that the government had returned only a small portion of the total properties confiscated during the Soviet period. In April 2010 a pilot program based on a presidential order from July 2009 began for religious education in schools in 19 regions of the country (with 15,445 teachers trained for the purpose), including the Muslim-majority North Caucasus, Russian Orthodox-majority areas of the country, and the Jewish Autonomous Region in the Far East.20 [19] Despite these efforts to combat extremism including anti-Semitism and the resultant improvements, it cannot be denied that anti-Semitism remains a serious societal problem. However, according to SOVA, counteraction to anti-Semitism in Russia has substantially improved in Russia over the past few years, and promoting anti-Semitic propaganda was hardly prosecuted from 2004 to 2006, but from 2007 to 2008, 22 of 70 hate-speech convictions were for inciting anti-Semitism, although in 9 of the 22 cases, offenders received only probation sentences, and many high-profile ultra-nationalists who promote anti-Semitism were not prosecuted. This continues throughout 2010. [20] The state is making serious efforts to combat anti-Semitism and extremism, and there are positive signs of progress. The claimant experienced local failure in 2006 and in 2010, but the preponderance of evidence indicates that they could reasonably expect state protection would be forthcoming if he were to require it, particularly in the larger centres such as Moscow where he lived prior to coming to Canada and St. Petersburg, where the majority of Russia's Jews reside.21 [21] The claimant did not raise any allegation that he was unable to obtain medical assistance or housing on account of his Jewish faith. He has XXXX years of formal education obtained in Russia, and he worked for XXXX years. [22] The claimant has not satisfied the panel with clear and convincing evidence that, on a balance of probabilities, the state would not be forthcoming with adequate protection. Changing societal attitudes is not an easy function, but there is clear evidence of the state taking action against anti-Semitic and extremist criminal activity. CONCLUSION [23] The panel has considered all three protection grounds under s. 96 and ss. 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act and, on the basis of the totality of all the evidence made available to the panel, finds that there is not a serious possibility that the claimant would be persecuted in Russia, nor that he would be subjected personally to a risk to his life, cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or to a danger of torture. [24] For these reasons, the claim is rejected. (signed) "C. Buttigieg" C.
