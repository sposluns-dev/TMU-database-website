Owimar v. Warnett

Warnett, 2018 BCSC 2310 Date: 20181231 Docket: S180226 Registry: Vancouver Between: Bassam Owimar Plaintiff And Paul Warnett, Robert McGraw Defendants Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Murray Reasons for Judgment The Plaintiff: No Appearance Counsel for the Defendant, Paul Warnett: L. Bumstead Counsel for the Defendant, Robert McGraw: D. Pilley Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. December 14, 2018 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. December 31, 2018 Introduction [1] The defendants seek to have the claims filed against them by the plaintiff dismissed. [2] The plaintiff's claims against the defendants arise from their involvement in civil suits brought by the plaintiff for injuries he claims to have suffered in several motor vehicle accidents. Background [3] The plaintiff was involved in eight motor vehicle accidents ("MVA") between November 1996 and mid-March 2016. He initiated civil claims in relation to each. [4] The plaintiff's claim regarding his first MVA settled out of court in 1999. [5] In about 2002 the defendant Mr. Warnett, a lawyer, was retained by the Insurance Company of British Columbia ("ICBC") to defend actions of the plaintiff. The first three MVAs were dealt with in one action ("the action") which went to trial in 2005. Mr. Warnett was counsel for all of the defendants. [6] Dr. McGraw was retained by the defendants in the action. He provided a number of medical legal reports and testified at the 2005 trial. [7] At the conclusion of the 2005 trial the trial judge dismissed the plaintiff's action. The plaintiff successfully appealed the verdict and the Court of Appeal ordered a new trial. [8] In February 2007 Mr. Warnett ceased to act for the defendants in the actions concerning the plaintiff. [9] After the filing of the first claim and during the course of the litigation regarding the action, the plaintiff continued to file civil claims relating to his further MVAs. [10] The plaintiff's outstanding actions relating to the remaining MVAs were settled out of court in 2013. [11] A central issue in the last seven civil actions was whether the plaintiff's lumbar spondylolisthesis was a developmental condition or was caused by the MVAs. [12] In total Dr. McGraw examined the plaintiff three times, provided five medical reports from 2003 to 2013 and testified in court. It was his opinion after examining the plaintiff and reviewing his November 1996 x-ray which predated the MVAs that the condition was a developmental condition and was unrelated to any of the MVAs. [13] The plaintiff brought the current action against the defendants in January 2018 ("the claim"). [14] Against both Mr. Warnett and Dr. McGraw the plaintiff alleges various kinds of fraud and negligence in their respective capacities as defence counsel and expert witness and claims that they substituted his lumbar spine x-ray taken in November 1996 with an x-ray that would disprove his claims of being injured in the MVAs. [15] Mr. Warnett seeks to have the plaintiff's claim dismissed, without leave to amend, pursuant to Rule 9-5(1) arguing that it violates all of the subsections therein and under Rule 9-6 as it is limitation barred. [16] Dr. McGraw seeks to have the claim dismissed pursuant to Rule 9-5(1)(a) arguing that it advances no reasonable claim and pursuant to Rule 9-6 contending that there is no genuine issue for trial. In making this argument Dr. McGraw relies on the doctrine of witness immunity. [17] The application before me was scheduled in consultation with the plaintiff. Although scheduled on a date of his choice, the plaintiff failed to appear for this hearing. The Issue [18] Whether the claim should be struck/dismissed against the defendants. The Law [19] Rule 9-5 of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009 governs applications to strike. It reads as follows: (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [20] Although the subsections address different concerns, there is overlap between them. For example a pleading may disclose no cause of action contrary to Rule 9-5(1)(a) and also be unnecessary and vexatious contrary to Rule 9-5(1)(b): Sahyoun v. Ho, 2015 BCSC 392 at para. 92. [21] The test for striking a claim under Rule 9-5(1)(a) is whether it is plain and obvious, assuming the facts plead are true, that the claim discloses no reasonable cause of action and has no reasonable prospect of success: R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42 at para. 17. [22] The rule that material facts in a notice of civil claim must be assumed to be true does not mean that allegations based on assumptions and speculation must be taken as true. Rather, such allegations must be subjected to a skeptical analysis in order to determine their true character: Young v. Borzoni et al, 2007 BCCA 16 at paras. 25-32. [23] The test for striking a pleading under Rule 9-5(1)(b) was summarised by Fisher, J. as she then was in Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083: Under Rule 9-5(1)(b), a pleading is unnecessary or vexatious if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action, if it does not advance any claim known in law, where it is obvious that an action cannot succeed, or where it would serve no useful purpose and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v Canadian Jewish Congress, [1999] BCJ No. 2160 (SC); Skender v Farley, 2007 BCCA 629. If a pleading is so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is pleaded, it may also be unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious. An application under this sub-rule may be supported by evidence. [24] Vexatious actions also include those brought for an improper purpose including the harassment and oppression of other parties by multifarious proceedings brought for purposes other than the assertion of legitimate rights: Re Lang Michener et al. v. Fabian et al., [1987] O.J. No. 355. [25] Abuse of process under Rule 9-5(1)(d) is a flexible doctrine that allows the court to prevent a claim from proceeding where to do so would violate principles of judicial economy, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice: Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E. Local 79, 2003 SCC 63 at paras. 35-37. [26] Rule 9-6 which governs summary trials reads in part as follows: . (3) An answering party may respond to an application for judgment under subrule (2) as follows: (a) the answering party may allege that the claiming party's originating pleading does not raise a cause of action against the answering party; (b) if the answering party wishes to make any other response to the application, the answering party may not rest on the mere allegations or denials in his or her pleadings but must set out, in affidavit material or other evidence, specific facts showing that there is a genuine issue for trial. (4) In an action, an answering party may, after serving a responding pleading on a claiming party, apply under this rule for judgment dismissing all or part of a claim in the claiming party's originating pleading. (5) On hearing an application under subrule (2) or (4), the court, (a) if satisfied that there is no genuine issue for trial with respect to a claim or defence, must pronounce judgment or dismiss the claim accordingly, . (d) may make any other order it considers will further the object of these Supreme Court Civil Rules. [27] The test under Rule 9-6 is whether it is manifestly clear on the relevant facts and law that there is no genuine matter to be tried. [28] Rule 9-6(5)(a) is mandatory, accordingly if there is no genuine issue to be tried this Court must dismiss the claim: Litynsky v. Litynsky, 2012 BCSC 1160 at paras. 43-46. The burden of showing that there is no genuine issue of material fact is on the party seeking summary dismissal: 4 Corners Properties v. Buffo Developments (Smithe) Ltd., 2013 BCSC 1926 at paras. 20-24. [29] Summary judgment may be granted on limitation defences under Rule 9-6. Where a claim is statute barred, there is no genuine issue to be tried: Brown v. Canada (Attorney General), 2014 BCSC 357 at para. 16, affirmed 2015 BCCA 102. Analysis [30] Under Rule 9-5(1)(a), the allegations advanced by the plaintiff are nothing more than wild speculation. Even if all of the facts advanced by the plaintiff are taken to be true, there are no material facts that make out a cause of action against either defendant. As such, I am satisfied that the plaintiff has no reasonable prospect of success. [31] I am further satisfied that the pleadings are so fundamentally flawed that they can not be saved by amendment. [32] Having made that finding it is not necessary to consider the other grounds for dismissal in Rule 9-5. I will state however that with regard to claims made against Mr. Warnett that in my view the claim violates all of the subsections in Rule 9-5(1). [33] In addition I find that the action against Mr. Warnett is statute barred by operation of either the Limitation Act, S.B.C. 2012, c. 13 and/or the Limitation Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 266 repealed. Limitation periods under both of those acts have long expired. As stated above, where an action is statute barred, there is no triable issue and the claim should be dismissed summarily under Rule 9-6(5)(a). [34] With regard to Dr. McGraw I am satisfied that the doctrine of witness immunity applies. Under that doctrine witnesses are immune from civil liability. In addition as for expert witnesses the doctrine applies to anything they say in court as well as pre-trial activities including assessments and reports: P.(J.) v. Eirikson, 2015 BCSC 847 at paras. 21 and 25. [35] Our Court of Appeal recently confirmed that a professional witness who gives evidence in court is protected from civil action in 311165 BC Ltd v. Canada (A.G.), 2017 BCCA 196: [50] It must be kept in mind that the immunities from suit that prevent claims based on evidence given in court and on bringing litigation are broad in order to protect the justice system. Witnesses should not be dissuaded from giving evidence or fettered in what they tell a court by the fear that an aggrieved person will sue them. Prosecutorial decisions must be allowed to be made in an atmosphere that is free from the chilling effects of potential civil liability. Access to the courts must not be impeded by leaving litigants in fear of being open to lawsuits brought in retaliation. [36] As a result of the witness immunity defence I am satisfied that the plaintiff's allegations against Dr. McGraw will fail. Accordingly there is no genuine issue to be tried and the claim must be dismissed under Rule 9-6(5)(a). [37] In conclusion, having considered all of the evidence and all of the submissions I am satisfied that the action against the defendants must be dismissed as it offends Rule 9-5(1). In the alternative I am satisfied for the reasons above that the action against the defendants must be dismissed under Rule 9-6. [38] Regarding costs, the defendants, in an effort to bring closure and finality to these proceedings, seek fixed costs of $1,000. Conclusion [39] The plaintiff's claims against both defendants as set out in the notice of civil claim file no. 180226 Vancouver Registry is struck without leave to amend, and the plaintiff's claims against both defendants are dismissed pursuant to Rule 9-5(1). [40] In the alternative all claims are dismissed pursuant to Rule 9-6. [41] Costs to each defendant in the amount of $1,000 is payable forthwith. "The Honourable Madam Justice Murray"
