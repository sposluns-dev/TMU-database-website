Zargarian-Tala v. Bayat-Mokhtari

2018 BCSC 1106 Zargarian-Tala v. Bayat-Mokhtari IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Zargarian-Tala v. Bayat-Mokhtari, 2018 BCSC 1106 Date: 20180619 Docket: E173279 Registry: Vancouver Between: Farshid Bayat-Mokhtari Petitioner And Leila Zarif Zargarian-Tala Respondent - and - Docket: E180285 Registry: Vancouver Between: Leila Zarif Zargarian-Tala Claimant And Farshid Bayat-Mokhtari Respondent Before: The Honourable Mr. Justice Macintosh Oral Reasons for Judgment Counsel for Farshid Bayat-Mokhtari: Bahareh Danaei Counsel for Leila Zargarian-Tala: M. Sodagar Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. June 15, 2018 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. June 19, 2018 [1] Because each party has filed a petition, I will refer to the parties only by their names, and I will take the liberty of referring to them by their first names, given my poor pronunciation. [2] The husband, Farshid, applies in this matrimonial dispute to have set aside the order granted ex parte, and marked interim and without prejudice, dated January 24, 2014, made by the Court of Queen's Bench of Alberta. That order, among its other provisions, granted "sole custody and decision-making powers for the children" to Leila, and ordered Farshid to pay $594 per month in child support, and $275 per month for s. 7 expenses. The parties agree that "sole custody and decision-making powers" given in that order equate to sole guardianship in this province. [3] If that order is set aside, Farshid asks for interim joint guardianship of the couple's eight-year-old son, Ryan, and a revised interim child support order. [4] Farshid also applies to have set aside the order dated April 10, 2017, made by the Court of Queen's Bench of Alberta. Although that order was not stated to be interim, it granted liberty to apply to vary it, and was interim in nature. It directed Farshid to have no contact with Ryan until further order or agreement. The Court noted that Farshid intended to later apply to obtain access to Ryan, and noted Farshid's recognition that his reinstated access with Ryan should only occur with a period of reintegration. [5] If the April 10, 2017 order is set aside, Farshid seeks to have parenting time, or at least contact, with Ryan. [6] Leila, in her petition, seeks orders that Farshid, within seven days, do all that is necessary to allow her to obtain an Iranian divorce and apply for an Iranian passport for herself. She also asks the Court to order Farshid, within seven days, to lift the Iranian travel ban against her. [7] My jurisdiction to address the Alberta orders derives from s. 6 and s. 16 of the Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1995, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), because Leila and Ryan now live in North Vancouver. Both parties want their matrimonial proceedings transferred to this province from Alberta. [8] With respect to the interim nature of the Alberta orders under review, Farshid cites authority that there need not be a material change in circumstances in order for an interim custody order to be varied: see Javid v. Kurytnik, 2006 BCCA 565. [9] Leila and Farshid are from Iran. He is 44 and she is 41. They married in Iran in February 1995. Soon after, they moved to Canada, first to British Columbia, and then, in 2007, to Alberta. In addition to young Ryan, they have a 21-year-old son attending Queen's University. Farshid and Leila separated in either 2012 or 2013. The separation date is contested. They were divorced by the Alberta Court on July 3, 2014. They have not been divorced in Iran, under the law of that country, which explains part of the relief requested in Leila's petition. In Alberta in 2010-2102, the couple ran a small business unsuccessfully. They both filed for bankruptcy when that business failed. [10] Today, Farshid lives in Iran. He returned there in 2013. From approximately 2012, he suffered from depression. In 2013, he earned only $7,922. He deposes that he was fully recovered by February 2015, and that he is now employed in a good position, earning approximately $20,000 Canadian annually, based on current exchange rates. I am told by counsel that such income permits a person a reasonable standard of living in Iran. [11] Farshid has had little contact with either son since he and Leila separated. He has had perhaps a single phone call and no other contact, with Ryan. He alleges, or at least suggests, alienation by Leila. She, on the other hand, blames him for not initiating or maintaining proper contact with the boys. Leila has made accusations of family violence against Farshid. She presented those accusations in this proceeding, I believe for the first time. They were not mentioned to the Alberta judges, from a review of the transcripts of the proceedings in that province. Farshid denies those accusations. [12] Any order I make today inconsistent with the Alberta orders will mean that the Alberta orders are set aside or varied to that extent only. Otherwise the Alberta orders will stand as orders of this Court until they are considered further by this Court in subsequent applications. [13] My first order, I believe by consent, is that all further proceedings in this country will be in the courts of this province. The Divorce Act and the Family Law Act, S.B.C. 2011, c. 25, will have application. If I have jurisdiction to exclude the Divorce Act from having application to this case in British Columbia, I decline to exercise that jurisdiction. I am mindful that it was only through the provisions of the Divorce Act that this matter was transferred to this province. [14] With respect to the orders Farshid requests, I am not satisfied from the evidence that I should make significant changes to the Alberta orders at this stage. [15] Ryan, who is only eight, has not seen his father since his parents separated, when he was only two or three. He has perhaps spoken to his father once. No matter how the evidence is viewed, Farshid has taken very few steps before now to reunite with Ryan. It may be that Farshid's history of depression, his bankruptcy and his relocation to Iran are the explanations for that lack of initiative. Whatever the reasons for his father's absence, it is not in Ryan's best interests to reunite with Farshid in any way until the reunification is better planned than it is at present. My concern is that Ryan might be reintroduced to his father briefly, never to see him again after that, with resulting confusion and insecurity. Ryan was born prematurely, and was described to me as being a delicate and sensitive boy. [16] In the result, in accordance with the April 10, 2017 Alberta order, Farshid is now at liberty to apply in this Court to obtain access to Ryan, probably by way of a supervised contact order to begin with, but only after filing evidence of a detailed plan. The plan would include the proposed times, locations and durations for contact, the names of those who could supervise the contacts, including evidence of such peoples' backgrounds and willingness to act in the supervisory capacity, and evidence of Farshid's financial capacity to arrange his travel here from Iran, and to pay for his accommodation and other expenses here. [17] The evidence at present provides almost no particulars for any of those elements, and Farshid's filed financial evidence does not demonstrate how he could afford regular trips here, and the other costs of arranging and maintaining contact. I repeat my concern that, without such evidence, there is a high risk that Farshid might see his son only briefly and only once or twice, with resulting instability and anxiety for Ryan. [18] Farshid has not filed evidence to demonstrate why at this stage it is in Ryan's interest that his mother's sole guardianship of him be changed to joint guardianship. Perhaps it will be different if Farshid successfully re‑establishes regular contact with Ryan. Similarly, in my view, it is premature to relieve Farshid at this stage of his existing retroactive and ongoing child support obligations. Farshid has paid nothing toward child support. Before he applies for renewed contact with Ryan, it would be in his interest to start honouring his current support obligations. [19] Leila's applications in her petition summarized earlier in these reasons cannot succeed either. The requested orders would require this Court to order a dual Iranian-Canadian citizen, who resides in Iran, to take steps in Iran to facilitate an Iranian divorce for the couple, and for Leila to obtain an Iranian passport and travel rights in that country. [20] Leila cited two cases in support of her applications, Bakhshi v. Hosseinzadeh, 2015 ONSC 7407; and Etemad v. Hasanzadeh, 2014 ONSC 6737. I do not read either of those cases as giving this Court jurisdiction to order Farshid, who resides in Iran, to take steps in Iran to facilitate either an Iranian divorce or travel rights for Leila in that country. I am told that each of the parties has a lawyer in Iran. In my view, absent any expert evidence to guide me as to Iranian law, Leila will need to proceed in Iran for the relief sought in her petition. [21] Neither party has succeeded in their petition, and with that divided success, I am making no costs ordered. "MACINTOSH J."
