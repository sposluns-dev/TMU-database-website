He claims to have a well-founded fear of persecution on the basis of sections 961 and 97(1) a) and b)2 of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). More specifically, the claimant alleges persecution on the grounds of his religious belief and ethnicity as a Russian Jew. FACTS ALLEGED The claimant, who is 47 years of age, was born in the city of XXXXX on XXXXX, 1966. His common-law wife and 14 year old son are presently living in XXXXX. His mother and sister live in XXXXX. The claimant did most of his schooling in XXXXX from 1975 on, and obtained an XXXXX degree at the XXXXX in XXXXX XXXXX in 1992. The claimant alleges he fled his country in 1994 because all his life he had faced anti-Semitism because of his Jewish ethnicity and religion. His problems became worse in XXXXX, 1992, when a group of nationalists wearing "semi-military" uniforms with heavy boots and shaven heads, in their twenties, managed to make their way through the security guards in the restricted factory district where the claimant had his office, and started to demolish his equipment and the premises. They spray-painted the walls with anti-Semitic slogans. They also assaulted the claimant. When they left, the claimant called the guards and the police, however, no one came to register the assault since the damages were minor and there were no severe injuries or deaths. In XXXXX 1994, he was attacked on the street by the same group that had destroyed his office in 1992. He was beaten-up and cursed at by the attackers who painted his car with three swastikas. When the police arrived, they said it was a car accident and laughed at the paint on his car. The claimant then decided to seek protection outside Russia and went to the US in XXXXX of that year (he worked and lived in New York for five years). He returned to Russia in XXXXX 1999, after an unsuccessful attempt at making a refugee claim. Upon his return to Russia, the claimant changed his family name from XXXXX to XXXXX. His father, years before, had changed his Jewish name XXXXX in order to be able to register with the XXXXX XXXXX XXXXX as a XXXXX. The claimant went to live in the city of XXXXX but was again the victim of a vicious attack by a group of men at his house in XXXXX 1999. He then decided to return to the United States. In XXXXX 2000, the claimant alleges he entered the US illegally through the Mexican border and tried to claim asylum a second time, unsuccessfully. He decided to leave on his own in XXXXX 2002 when he got news of an attack against his father and sister by skinheads who assaulted them as they left a Jewish restaurant in XXXXX. Back in Russia, the claimant continued to work in XXXXX XXXXX in his previous business "XXXXX XXXXX". On XXXXX, 2003, he passed by a demonstration to support the independence of the Chechen Republic. As he was looking at posters and photos, he was assaulted by a group of skinheads shouting slogans against non-Russians and black people. They were shouting at the demonstrators and hitting those present. The claimant, who was injured, was picked up by police and detained for 24 hours at the police station. He was accused of disturbing the peace and told he would be charged with a criminal offence next time. The claimant filed a complaint for police brutality, but was told the police had done nothing wrong. In XXXXX 2004, the claimant was again the victim of an attack by a group of youngsters dressed in black uniforms and shouting nationalist slogans. He was beaten-up and had to go to the hospital for treatment. Police told him there was no one against whom to press charges, so a complaint was useless. Later on, his apartment door was broken and anti-Semitic slogans were painted on it. Again, the police were of no help. In XXXXX 2004, the claimant was attacked and beaten by a group of skinheads at his apartment building. He alleges the group knew him and had targeted him specifically. Two tenants entered the building and together, they managed to catch two of the attackers. The police took them to the station, but later released them without charges. In XXXXX 2005, the claimant alleges a group of skinheads attacked his office again and threatened they would return to kill him. There was no protection and the police did not answer the call made by the secretary. The claimant then decided he would look for ways to leave his country. In XXXXX 2006, he got an invitation to go to Canada and obtained a visa. On XXXXX, 2006, the claimant alleges a group of skinheads attacked his 13 year old son as he was coming home from school. His son died in hospital as a result of the beating. His wife was a witness to the attack. She was hospitalised for shock for two weeks following the event. Three weeks later, on April 17, 2006, the claimant left his country and arrived in Canada the same day. He claimed refugee status at the airport. If he returns to Russia, he fears he would continuously be the target of groups like the skinheads because he is Jewish. DECISION The tribunal reviewed the claimant's testimony in light of his written narrative as presented in his Personal Information Form (PIF), as well as in light of his other declarations made to Canadian immigration authorities when he first arrived in the country. The tribunal also examined the various documents, personal and other, which he adduced in support of his claim. In coming to its decision, the tribunal also took into consideration the documentary evidence on the political and social situation in Russia, in particular, that of Russian Jews. The tribunal has come to the conclusion that the claimant is not a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection" for the following reasons. ANALYSIS Identity As pointed out by the Hearing Officer, the claimant did not submit any of the usual and current Russian identity documents such as the birth certificate and the internal displacement passport required for travelling within the country. These documents would have clearly confirmed the claimant's Jewish ethnicity. The tribunal, however, is willing to grant the claimant the benefit of doubt as regards his Jewish background. The claimant's passport, issued to him on XXXXX, 2002, by the authorities of his country confirms his Russian citizenship3. Credibility The tribunal, on the other hand, found several credibility issues both with the claimant's testimony and several of the personal documents he adduced in support of his claim. On the whole, the tribunal did not find the claimant to be a credible witness after he delivered a vague, evasive and altogether not very convincing testimony. The tribunal first enquired as to the whereabouts of his wife and whether she had problems in Russia. The claimant said she was living with her family in the city of XXXXX, some 600 kilometres from Moscow. He indicated that she was not working and remained in her home because she never recovered from her son's death in XXXXX 2006. The claimant said she did not have any other problems. Asked if he could live there as well, he answered he was not sure. His response was not specific and lacked conviction. The tribunal then questioned him concerning his name change. The tribunal noted that the claimant first left his country in 1994 and spent 5 years in the United States. It also underlined the fact that he fled Russia because he alleged he was persecuted because he was Jewish. The claimant declared he travelled under the name XXXXX which his father had changed to in the 50's in order to register at the military academy, and changed back to the old Jewish family name XXXXX (German) when he returned from the US in XXXXX 19994. Asked why he would choose to use a Jewish sounding name if it was precisely for reasons of his Jewish ethnicity that he had fled the country in the first place. He had never used any other name but XXXXX since his birth. The claimant here again gave little detail for this change, simply saying he wanted to go back to his original family name. He admitted, however, that neither his father nor his sister had done so. The claimant gave very little information to explain his behaviour. His name change suggests a lack of subjective fear as regards his allegation of persecution based on his Jewish ethnicity. The tribunal then questioned the claimant about his forced departure from the US following a refusal of his first Refugee claim and asked him why he had not submitted any evidence concerning this claim given these documents could have been readily available. The claimant did not give any satisfactory answer. Asked again why he did not submit any documents concerning his second claim in the US, when he returned in XXXXX 2000, he did not have any clear explanation here either. The tribunal believes that the claimant's name change from XXXXX, used in his first trip to the US, to XXXXX, used for his second trip to the United States, is not simply a wish to return to his old family name. The claimant did not deny that, hardly one year later, he re-entered the United States illegally through the Mexican border, under a different name. Although he states that he filed a second refugee claim at that time, the claimant did not submit any evidence to that effect. The tribunal considers that the claimant's behaviour on these issues lacks transparency in the same manner as when he filed for a Canadian visa in Russia, in XXXXX 2006, submitting false information and thus misleading Canadian immigration authorities5. The tribunal does not believe that the claimant's change of name is simply coincidental. Furthermore, changing his name to a supposedly more Jewish-sounding name when returning to Russia undermines his subjective fear, both in 1999 and in 2002. The tribunal has serious doubts that the claimant was truly the victim of persecution in his country, as he alleges. Moreover, after his second return to Russia in XXXXX 2002, the claimant travelled to Italy, Finland, and Costa Rica in 2003, and returned to Russia despite continued allegations he was constantly targeted by skinheads who harassed him and threatened to kill him on several occasions. The claimant never took those opportunities to seek protection. Also, the fact he acquired a new passport in XXXXX 2006, prior to coming to Canada, indicates he was re-availing himself of his country's protection. His behaviour further undermines his subjective fear and his allegations of persecution. The claimant alleges in his narrative that he was the victim of repeated harassment and discrimination on the part of ultranationalist groups in XXXXX. Twice, he indicates, his offices were vandalised and the equipment inside destroyed, in XXXXX 1992 and in XXXXX 2005. He also says he was attacked by skinheads on the streets and severely beaten-up on five other occasions, which he describes in his PIF. He adduces certain personal documents to support some of these events. Finally, the claimant alleges his son was killed by a group of skinheads in front of his home and in the presence of his wife. For this most tragic event, however, he submits no evidence which could have been obtained from hospital authorities as well as from the police authorities. The absence of such information sheds further doubt on these events. The tribunal is aware that there still exists in Russia, as in other Eastern European Countries, ultra-nationalist and fanatic groups, such as skinheads, who frequently demonstrate in public showing anti-Jewish and anti-Semitic posters and are often the authors of violence and criminal activities. Documentary evidence on the subject also indicates that these activities are no longer condoned by the state6. Although far from being perfect, state protection is not unavailable as suggested by the claimant. Although the claimant submitted several personal documents to support the events he describes in his PIF narrative and to show that he was systematically refused help and protection from the authorities, upon closer examination of these documents, the tribunal found several discrepancies that shed serious doubt on their authenticity. For example, the claimant alleges he returned to Russia from the United States in XXXXX 2002, following an attack against his father and sister on XXXXX. He alleges his father died on XXXXX, 2002, of the consequences of this attack. However, he submits as evidence a letter addressed to Mr. and Mrs. XXXXX, from the Public Ministry, defending the actions taken by the police the day of the aggression7. At the hearing, the claimant explained that this letter was not addressed to him but to his father and his sister. The claimant, however, could not explain how the letter, dated XXXXX, 2002, could be addressed to his father, given he had already died several weeks earlier. On XXXXX, 2003, the claimant alleges he was attacked by a group of skinheads and beaten in front of police officers. He happened to pass by a demonstration in support of the Republic of Chechnya and was looking at posters and photo displays. He alleges he was arrested by the police and detained at the police station for 24 hours. In support of this event, the claimant submitted a medical certificate from the municipal hospital in XXXXX dated XXXXX, 2003, indicating he was hospitalised on that date with internal cerebro-cranial trauma, cerebral contusions and haematomas, among other injuries8. The tribunal finds it difficult to understand that the claimant in such a critical physical condition could have been detained for 24 hours at a police station without medical attention and then just released the next day. In his PIF narrative, the claimant simply says he "got help at a medical station for his injuries". Notwithstanding the plausibility of this situation, the tribunal finds that the claimant's description of his physical condition is not compatible with the information contained in the medical report he adduced. The tribunal cannot grant this document any probative value. Again, in XXXXX of 2004, the claimant indicates he was attacked by skinheads in his building, on the 3rd floor. With the help of two other tenants, they fought the aggressors and managed to capture two of them and turn them over to the police. The claimant gives no details concerning his physical condition. However, he adduces, in support of this event, another medical report from the same hospital which describes his injuries as serious internal cerebro-cranial trauma with contusions and haemorrhaging9. The tribunal noted that the claimant gives no specific date and makes no mention in his narrative about this serious medical condition. Moreover, it finds it difficult to see how the claimant, in such conditions, could have managed to fight-off and subdue two of the attackers, albeit with some help, and surrender them to the authorities. Again, despite the omissions in the claimant's story, the tribunal finds the medical certificate to be incompatible with the event as described by the claimant in his PIF. The police report for the complaint he made concerning the same event fails to give any pertinent details as to where the events took place and the address. Also, the police report makes erroneous reference to the gravity of the claimant's injuries as compared to the description in the medical report, which the claimant surely filed with his police complaint. Given these discrepancies, the tribunal does not give these documents any probative value. Finally, as regards the death of the claimant's son, the tribunal found the claimant's testimony particularly void of pertinent details and clear explanations given the seriousness of his allegations that his son was beaten to death by skinheads in front of his house and in view of his wife. The certificate he adduced in support his son's death does not indicate any address, the names of the parents of the deceased, where he died specifically, or what the cause of death was10. This event is surely the most important one submitted by the claimant, but for which he did not adduce any corroborating evidence. However, the claimant said his son died at the hospital and that his wife accompanied him. Yet, there is no hospital document to support this and no police report as to what would be described as a serious criminal act and a murder. Also, the claimant could not explain why his son, born as XXXXX XXXXX in 1993, would be registered as XXXXX on the death certificate. When questioned, the claimant was nervous and particularly vague and evasive. He could not explain why he did not obtain any documents concerning his son's murder, just as he had done for many other lesser events. He did not say why there was no police enquiry or why his wife, a witness to the fatal beating by a group of skinheads, had not been questioned by the police. It may be that the claimant's son did indeed die. However, the tribunal does not believe that he died in the manner described in the claimant's story and does not find him credible on this issue. His lack of credibility on this point undermines his story as a whole. The tribunal does not find him credible on a general basis. CONCLUSION Given the above, given the many inconsistencies and the vague and often evasive testimony, as well as the untrustworthiness of several documents adduced, the tribunal does not believe the claimant's story and concludes that he is not a "Convention refugee" nor a "person in need of protection". Should he return to his country of origin, the tribunal does not believe that his life or his safety would be at risk. Also, given the fact that the tribunal does not believe his story, it does not believe he would be submitted to any cruel and unusual treatment should he return to Russia. The tribunal rejects his claim. Jeannine Beaubien-Duque Jeannine Beaubien-Duque December 21st, 2007 Date /jpp/rc 1 " 96.
