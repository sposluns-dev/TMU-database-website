Collins v. Abrams et al.

2001BCCA0022 Citation: Collins v. Abrams et al. Date: 20010111 2001 BCCA 22 Docket: V03571 Registry: Victoria COURT OF APPEAL FOR BRITISH COLUMBIA ORAL REASONS FOR JUDGMENT Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Rowles January 11, 2001 The Honourable Madam Justice Prowse The Honourable Mr. Justice Finch Victoria, B.C. BETWEEN: DOUG COLLINS PETITIONER (APPELLANT) AND: HARRY ABRAMS, BRITISH COLUMBIA HUMAN RIGHTS TRIBUNAL, DEPUTY CHIEF COMMISSIONER OF THE BRITISH COLUMBIA HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISSION, and ATTORNEY GENERAL OF BRITISH COLUMBIA RESPONDENTS (RESPONDENTS) Douglas H. Christie No one Katherine Hardie Deborah K. Lovett, Q.C. Lisa J. Mrozinski Appearing for the Appellant Doug Collins Appearing for the Respondent Harry Abrams Counsel for the Respondent BC Human Rights Tribunal Counsel for the Respondent Deputy Chief Commissioner of the British Columbia Human Rights Commission Counsel for the Respondent Attorney General of British Columbia [1] FINCH J.A.: The petitioner appeals the order of the Supreme Court of British Columbia pronounced 2 December 1999, remitting to the B.C. Human Rights Tribunal issues as to the constitutional validity of s.7(1)(b) of the Human Rights Code, and questions of institutional bias and lack of independence in the Tribunal contrary to s.11(d) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. The petitioner contends that the Tribunal has no jurisdiction to adjudicate these issues, that it is unnecessary and inefficient to remit them to the Tribunal, and that the issues should have been decided by the Court. [2] These proceedings commenced with a complaint of discrimination against the petitioner, and the North Shore News, for publication of a series of articles alleged to expose Jewish persons to hatred and contempt. The petitioner applied to the Tribunal for severance of issues relating to the constitutionality of s.7(1)(b) from the question of whether that section had been violated. The Tribunal agreed to a severance and proceeded to hear the issue of whether s.7(1)(b) had been violated. It found that both the petitioner and the North Shore News were in breach of that section, and ordered compensation of $2,000 to be paid to the complainant, and publication of a summary of the Tribunal's decision. The North Shore News complied with the orders against it. [3] The petitioner, however, applied for a stay of the order pending determination of the constitutional issues. The Tribunal ordered a stay of the monetary part of the order so as to give the parties 30 days to consider their position. The petitioner took no further steps before the Tribunal. [4] In April 1999 the petitioner applied in the British Columbia Supreme Court for judicial review of the Tribunal's decision. He sought to have it quashed, and he sought declarations that s.7(1)(b) and ancillary provisions of the Human Rights Code infringe the Charter, and further that s.7(1)(b) be held ultra vires the Province. [5] The learned Chambers judge held that the petitioner's application for judicial review was premature, that the issues raised had not been addressed by the Tribunal, and that it should be given the opportunity to complete its task. [6] In support of his position that the legal issues should not be remitted to the Tribunal, the petitioner's counsel relied upon Berscheid v. Westbank Indian Band, [1998] B.C.J. No. 355 (Q.L. (C.A.) and Cooper v. Canada (Human Rights Commission), [1996] 3 S.C.R. 854 (S.C.C.). The learned Chambers judge held that both cases were distinguishable on their facts from the case at bar. Counsel for the petitioner contends that in this she erred. [7] In Berscheid the proceedings commenced as a petition for judicial review. The Chambers judge adjourned the judicial review application and remitted the case to the Tribunal for determination of constitutional and other issues. On appeal, Berscheid abandoned his argument that the Tribunal did not have jurisdiction to hear the complaint against him. The Court of Appeal converted the petition to a civil action, and directed the case be transferred to the trial list. The procedural question ceased to be whether, as a matter of a Chambers judge's discretion, Berscheid's application should proceed or be remitted to the Tribunal. The only remaining procedural issue was whether he had sufficient standing to pursue his civil action. [8] The Berscheid decision does not support the petitioner's position that the Human Rights Tribunal does not have jurisdiction to decide constitutional issues. Nor does it hold that the Court must always hear and adjudicate applications for declaratory relief. [9] In Cooper, at issue was s.15(c) of the Canadian Human Rights Act, which provided that it was not discriminatory for an individual's employment to be terminated because he had reached the normal retirement age for persons employed in similar positions. The issue was whether the Canadian Human Rights Commission could determine the constitutionality of that provision in its enabling statute. The Supreme Court of Canada held that the Commission could not because the section was a limit on the Commission's power to consider questions of age discrimination. [10] The Court held that the Commission could not hold invalid a limiting provision in the enabling legislation because to do so would be to assume a jurisdiction which Parliament had not conferred upon it. [11] In my view, the Cooper case does not support the petitioner's position that questions of constitutionality are generally beyond the competence of statutory tribunals. Indeed, in my view, Cooper is authority to the contrary. In every case it is a question of statutory interpretation to determine the jurisdiction which Parliament, or the Legislature, intended to confer either by express words, or by implication. [12] At paragraph 15 of her reasons, the learned Chambers judge said: I have had regard to the substantial number of authorities to which I was referred and am satisfied that an administrative tribunal that has been given the power to decide questions of law, as the British Columbia Human Rights Tribunal, also has the power to decide the validity, under the Charter, of the particular law which it is to apply. To put it another way, provided that the Tribunal is discharging a function assigned to it by its legislation it may, while doing so, consider and decide Charter issues. In this regard see Douglas/Kwantlen Faculty Association v. Douglas College, [1990] 3 S.C.R. 570, Terteault-Gadoury v. Canada (Employment and Immigration Commission), [1991] 2 S.C.R. 22 and Cuddy Chicks Ltd. v. Ontario (Labour Relations Board), [1991] 2 S.C.R. 5. I respectfully agree. [13] In my view, the learned Chambers judge did not err in holding that the B.C. Human Rights Tribunal had jurisdiction to hear and decide the constitutional issues raised in this case. [14] The remaining question is whether the learned Chambers judge erred in the exercise of her discretion to grant relief sought on the application for judicial review. That the granting of such relief is within the discretion of the Court is clear from the express language of subsection 2(2) of the Judicial Review Procedure Act. [15] As with all such discretionary powers, the discretion under the Act must be exercised judicially. That is, the judge should not consider irrelevant matters, and must consider all relevant factors. In addition, the Court's conclusion must be reasonable. [16] The reasoning of the learned Chambers judge as to the exercise of her discretion appears from the following passages in her reasons: [19] On behalf of Mr. Collins it is argued that this matter ought to come straight to the Supreme Court for a determination to avoid duplication and to best achieve judicial efficiency. [20] On behalf of the Attorney General it is argued that, taking into consideration the numerous issues relevant to this kind of an inquiry, including the questions of judicial efficiency and of fragmentation, this matter ought to be remitted to the Tribunal. In particular it is argued that because there will have to be a full hearing of the evidence relating to the issues of the constitutional validity of the section, the jurisdiction of the Tribunal and the question of bias, the Tribunal is in a better position to conduct that hearing than is the court and the resulting record will then available to the Court should either of the parties wish to bring the matter forward for review. ... [24] In the present case Mr. Collins continues to seek a determination that the provisions of section 7 of the Code violates the Charter and are not justified under section 1 of the Charter. In addition he raises claims of bias and lack of independence and he seeks relief under section 24 of the Charter. The Tribunal has not completed its work in the sense that it has not yet conducted a hearing with respect to the questions raised by section 1 of the Charter nor with respect to the claims of bias and lack of independence of the Tribunal contrary to section 11(d) of the Charter. [25] In the circumstances of this case, distinguishable as they are from the circumstances in Berscheid, I have concluded that the Tribunal must be given the opportunity to complete its task. [17] In my respectful view, the learned Chambers judge did not err in the exercise of her discretion. The decision to refer the case back to the Human Rights Tribunal was reasonable in the circumstances for the reasons she gave. [18] I would dismiss the appeal. ROWLES, J.A.: I agree. PROWSE, J.A.: I agree. The appeal is dismissed. "The Honourable Mr. Justice Finch"
