ANALYSIS Background: Situation of the Roma in Hungary [9] It was submitted that state protection in Hungary for members of the Roma ethnic group is ineffective. Documents submitted by the Refugee Protection Division (the "RPD")7 and the claimants8 indicate that Hungary has had a history of discrimination against Roma people. [10] Counsel submitted that adequate state protection would not be forthcoming in the particular circumstances of this claimant, because the documentary evidence indicates that the police in Hungary systemically fail to provide protection to the Roma and that the Roma faced discrimination by the police. Counsel submitted that the Roma are in the disadvantage situations and that they are a marginalized group. Counsel submitted that the Roma are victims of widespread discrimination. Furthermore, counsel submits that organizations such as Amnesty International indicate that the police must do more in Hungary to protect the Roma. Mr Aladar Horvath testified on behalf of 39 claimants all related to the main file of TB1-14713, and all represented by the same counsel. [11] Counsel presented a witness who came from Hungary to testify at this hearing. The witness, Aladar Horvath,9 was questioned by Counsel regarding the situation in Hungary and events involving the XXXX family. Mr. Horvath testified that he was the first Roma Member of Parliament in Hungary and has many experiences working in the area of Roma rights. Mr. Horvath testified that he is the Coordinator of the Roma Civil Rights Institute with the XXXX XXXX XXXX College in XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXXHungary. In his documentary evidence he mentions speaking recently with the IRB's Documentation Centre in Toronto, however given that the Centre closed to the public some time ago, I am somewhat confused that he was able to do that. The Panel believes that he may have spoken to the IRB's research staff in Ottawa, as he testified that he was in Ottawa to meet with a Human Rights Committee. While he may have been confused about with whom he had spoken, more concerning is what he says in this document. [12] Throughout the document the author criticizes numerous things as being detrimental to Roma people, using rather strong language and does not really mention anything that might be good in Hungary. He describes the State of Hungary as "defunct" and "despotic", but does not explain how this is so, other than to reference Jobbik, a minority far-right party. While he criticizes the introduction of a work for welfare type of program (seek and maintain employment or have welfare benefits cut off) I note that other jurisdictions around the world have introduced similar measures, even right here in the Province of Ontario by former Premier Mike Harris. I also read in this document this type of program is not limited to Roma but applies to all Hungarians, so the program does not appear to be disturbing as the author tries to imply. It is further noted that the Jobbik Party has proposed lowering the age of criminal culpability from 14 years of age to 12 years of age. The author goes on to state that this will lead to children being imprisoned. However, apart from Jobbik being a minority party and this just being a proposal, it should also be noted that the age of culpability in Canada is already 12 years of age, so I do not see how this is a troubling development for the Roma people. [13] The author goes on to make a rather bold statement that the government is ending policies of segregation of students in school, but does not say what this actually means, and provides no evidence to corroborate this. Mr. Horvath gave viva voce testimony that the government has only changed the name, by calling the program "catching up school." Mr. Horvath did not provide any corroborating evidence in regard to the government policies of segregation. The author also notes that the governing party has appointed people aligned with its viewpoint to various positions. However, this is true of most governments around the world; therefore I fail to see how this can be viewed as being nefarious. While the author quotes the Constitutional Court as stating that the Constitution should not be continually amended to deal with short-term goals, there is no mention of any amendments being unconstitutional. The author also states that a 16% tax negatively affects low-income Roma, but unfortunately he doesn't explain the impact on the Roma or non-Roma for that matter. The author also states that there is a media chill, but uses the example of one of his press conferences that generated little attention as the prime example. If there was a severe media chill I would have expected the author to use a more serious example. Regrettably, the author does not state the basis for the refusal or the reasoning or the circumstances behind the decision of the state not to prosecute. The Panel is not aware upon what evidence those decisions were based, so I give these documents little weight. Among the most important human rights problems during the year were societal discrimination and exclusion of the Romani population and violent right-wing extremism. Discrimination against Roma exacerbated their already limited access to education, employment, health care, and social services. Right-wing extremism, including public campaigns by paramilitaries to intimidate and incite hatred against Roma and other minorities, increased. Also the government began implementing a new law that restricts media freedom by increasing government influence over the media in general. The government also adopted a new Fundamental Law to replace the 1949 constitution, as well as more than 20 cardinal laws. New laws concerning the judicial system, religious organizations, and media freedom gave rise to concerns that the new legislation could undermine the country's democratic institutions by removing key checks and balances. The Fundamental Law and most cardinal laws were to come in to force on January 1, 2012.11 [23] Based on the Immigration and Refugee Board (the "Board's") latest documents, conditions for the Roma have deteriorated.12 Even with the foregoing statements, in a functioning democracy, a claimant will have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status.13 The documentary evidence14 before the Board indicates that Hungary is a democracy and that there are free and fair elections; there is a relatively independent and impartial judiciary. Even though Hungary's state protection mechanisms related to the Roma are criticized as falling short of the EU's expectations,15 still the claimant must do more than merely show that he or she went to see members of the police force and that those efforts were unsuccessful. A claimant, even with respect to the above-mentioned remarks, must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, the steps taken and the claimant's interactions with the authorities.16 [24] I have canvassed the documentary evidence, and I have determined that the documentary evidence indicates that the attitudes toward the Roma by some Hungarian people, including people who are in authority, are discriminatory and prejudicial. It is clear from the documentary evidence that the effect has been to marginalize the Roma people. Roma are generally under-employed, under-educated, frequently live in subsistence housing, and are now subject to violence from radical elements who are gaining support from the general public to some extent. Roma in Hungary are "consciously despised by the majority population and pushed to the edge of society," while previously "hidden anti-Roma attitudes are becoming more open."17 The documentary evidence indicates that persecutory acts are often promoted and carried out by right-wing extremist groups, such as members of the disbanded Hungarian Guard, whose members have continued their activities under different names and in newly formed different organizations.18 Additionally, the right-wing Jobbik Party feeds on sentiments from its constituency, which contributes to the discrimination and persecution of the Roma. According to sources, Jobbik is an "extreme right-wing" political party with nationalistic roots and a strong anti-Roma and anti-Semitic agenda. Sources indicate that the Hungarian Guard's popularity, as well as the party's campaign against so-called 'Gypsy crime', significantly contributed to Jobbik's growth."19 On the other hand, while there seemed to have been some surprise at the Jobbik Party's moderate success in the last national election, in which they became the third-place party with nearly 17% of the vote, the documentary evidence also indicates that the Jobbik support dropped in April 2011 to 13 percent among likely voters and in August 2011, it was 15 percent among decided voters. To gain back its support, the Jobbik Party has renewed its campaign against Roma with rallies in villages across the country.20 [25] It is against this background and in taking into consideration the particular circumstances relating to this claim that I must determine whether or not adequate state protection exists for this particular claimant in Hungary. State Protection [26] I considered the issue of state protection in Hungary. In this particular case I find that the claimants have to some extent rebutted the presumption of state protection to a certain level based on their personal experiences. [27] In assessing the issue, I am guided by a number of cases in which the principles were applied to this case. [28] There is a presumption, except in situations where the state is in a complete breakdown, that it is capable of protecting its citizens. A claimant who alleges that state protection is inadequate must persuade the Board that, on a balance of probabilities, the evidence establishes that the state protection is inadequate.21 The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) is not obliged to prove that the state can offer the claimant effective protection, rather, the claimant bears the legal burden of rebutting the presumption that state protection exists by adducing clear and convincing evidence which satisfies the RPD on a balance of probabilities.22 For example, a claimant might advance testimony of similarly situated individuals let down by the state protection arrangement or the claimant's testimony of past personal incidents in which state protection did not materialize. [29] The onus is on the claimant to approach the state for protection in situations where state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.23 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration,24 the preponderance of recent Federal Court decisions have held that the test for a finding of state protection is whether the protection is adequate, rather than the effectiveness per se.25 The protection need not be perfect.26 In determining whether protection is adequate, it is important to consider whether a legislative and procedural framework for protection exists and also whether the state, through the police or other authorities, is able and willing to effectively implement that framework.27 A state must engage in serious efforts to protect its citizens at the operational level.28 Recently, a number of federal court decisions have seemingly shifted the emphasis from adequacy of state protection to the effectiveness of state protection at the operational level, especially with respect to Hungarian Roma, which appears to be a change from established jurisprudence. The onus seems to have shifted to the Board to demonstrate that adequate state protection is available in Hungary, rather than the onus being on the claimant to rebut the presumption that adequate state protection exists. [30] To my knowledge in reading all the cases, the courts have yet to define what is adequacy or effectiveness, some have gone so far as to say there is no state protection without stating why. In Buri,29 Mr. Justice Campbell essentially states that there is no evidence that state protection is available. [31] In one case, serious efforts plus evidence of arrests still doesn't equate to adequate state protection. In Muntyan,30 the court states the Board's assessment of the documentary evidence is flawed because it equates the measures being taken and the arrests being made by the Hungarian government, regardless of the circumstances, with adequate state protection. [32] For example, in Majoros,31 the court states that although the Board reviewed the documentary evidence separately, its analysis is problematic in that it fails to recognize that the corollary of the principle that protection need not be perfect is not the principle that there is adequate protection if it is found on occasion. [33] As I have stated above, no government is expected to guarantee perfect protection to all of its citizens at all times, and the fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens is not enough to justify a claim, especially where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authorities in place and is making serious efforts to protect its citizens.32 Less than perfect protection is not a basis to determine that a state is either unwilling or unable to offer reasonable protection.33 [34] Furthermore, the law states that the burden to prove an absence of state protection is directly proportional to the level of democracy of that state.34 Hungary is a democracy, therefore, the presumption of state protection is a strong one. The claimant must do more than merely show that he or she went to see members of the police force and that those efforts were unsuccessful. The more democratic the state's institutions, the greater the onus on the claimant to show he or she has exhausted all courses of action available.35 Moreover, local failures by the authorities to provide protection do not mean that the state, as a whole, fails to protect its citizens, unless the failures form part of a broader pattern of state inability or refusal to provide protection.36 Indeed, if a claimant believes the actions of some police officers are corrupt, the onus is on them to approach other members of the security forces or authorities.37 [35] The documentary evidence before the Board38 indicates that Hungary is a democracy and that there are free and fair elections.39 There is a relatively independent and impartial judiciary. 40 Hungary is in effective control of its territory and has in place a functioning security force to uphold the laws and constitution of the country. There is nothing in the documentation before the Board to suggest that Hungary is in a state of complete breakdown. The government adopted a new Fundamental Law to replace the 1949 Constitution, as well as more than 20 Cardinal laws in 2011. New laws concerning the judicial system, religious organizations, and media freedom gave rise to concerns that the new legislation could undermine the country's democratic institutions by removing key checks and balances. The Fundamental Law and most cardinal laws were to come into force on January 1, 2012.41 There is no persuasive evidence to show that Hungary is not a democracy. Any parts of the law that do not comply with the basic tenants of the European Union will be challenged. [36] In this case, the claimants were assaulted on numerous occasions because of their ethnicity. They had some difficulties with the education system. I find that the claimants were discriminated to a certain degree in Hungary on the basis of the cumulative acts of discrimination directed at them. They have also somewhat rebutted the presumption of state protection in their personal circumstances. CONCLUSION [37] Having considered all of the evidence and counsel's submissions, I find that the claimants are Convention refugees. The Refugee Protection Division, therefore, accepts the claims for refugee protection. (signed) ' Luis F. Agostinho ' Luis F.
