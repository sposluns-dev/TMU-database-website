Background Facts [7] The genesis of this action arises from a demand that Safari Express made of Mr. Salinger of approximately $16,800 for arrears of rent for space in an airplane hanger at the Airport used by Mr. Salinger to store his Cessna aircraft shell together with its log books, engine, and miscellaneous parts (collectively the "Cessna"), and related transportation and towing services. In approximately 2014, Mr. Salinger entered into an oral sublease with Safari Express to park his Cessna outside Safari Express' hangar at the Airport at a cost of $200 per month. Safari Express terminated the sublease when Mr. Salinger failed to pay rent, and had his aircraft towed to another location at the Airport. [8] The plaintiffs commenced this action against Safari Express in June 2015. In their notice of civil claim, they alleged that Aero Attack as opposed to Mr. Salinger had rented the space from Safari Express. Until December 2021, when the plaintiffs amended their notice of civil claim, they also advanced a claim against Safari Express for $54,327, which they alleged was owing for airplane repair services Safari Express had requested of the plaintiffs (according to the plaintiffs, that sum was the net of an original amount owing of $86,703.75 less a credit note of $32,377). I refer to this as the "Repair Services Claim". [9] Mr. Salinger was not allowed to attend at the Airport as a consequence of a restraining order ("Restraining Order") issued against him in another action involving different defendants (VA S192835). KiTov and Mr. Salinger are the plaintiffs in that action. They sued Alpha Aviation Inc. ("Alpha") and the City of Delta in respect of the same Cessna shell that is the subject of the instant action. I will refer to it as the "Alpha Action". [10] This action remained dormant until September 2017, when a settlement agreement ("Settlement Agreement") was reached between the parties. The parties agreed that the plaintiffs would discontinue the Repair Services Claim against Safari Express and Mr. Salinger would pay Safari Express' claim in the reduced amount of $10,000. [11] Mr. Salinger paid $2,000 of that sum but no more. The plaintiffs also did not withdraw the Repair Services Claim. [12] Safari Express issued a counterclaim against Mr. Salinger in January 2020 seeking specific performance of the Settlement Agreement because the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger had still not performed their outstanding obligations under the Settlement Agreement. [13] On December 31, 2020, the plaintiffs filed their amended their notice of civil claim to withdraw the Repair Services Claim, leaving their claim for performance of the Settlement Agreement and costs extant. However, they did not pay the $8,000 owing under the Settlement Agreement. [14] Safari Express eventually filed a summary trial application predicated on the Settlement Agreement and scheduled it for hearing in April 2021. The application was adjourned because Mr. Salinger maintained he had not been served. The summary trial application was rescheduled for hearing on July 5, 2021, peremptory on the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger, by the order of Master Cameron issued April 28, 2021. [15] I presided over the hearing of the summary trial application on the newly scheduled date. The plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger initially opposed the application, premised in part on unfiled and unsworn affidavit materials. However, through the course of submissions, Mr. Salinger confirmed that he would pay the remaining amount owing under the Settlement Agreement so long as he could inspect the Cessna before it was returned to him. The difficulty with the inspection was that Mr. Salinger could not attend at the Airport since the Restraining Order was still in effect. [16] The inspection issue was resolved by the parties during the hearing, and the parties agreed to an order I issued with their consent dismissing, inter alia, all claims except costs ("Consent Order"). Both sides advised they would seek special costs from the other (the terms of the Consent Order are discussed in a subsequent section of these reasons). [17] The judgment amount was paid, the Cessna was inspected and returned to the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger, and the Consent Order was entered with the registry. In the result, all claims except those concerning costs were dismissed by consent. [18] Only Safari Express filed application materials seeking an order for special costs. During his oral submissions on Safari Express' instant application for special costs, Mr. Salinger said that he should be entitled to special costs, although he did not file any application materials (by a court ordered deadline) in that respect in spite of ample opportunity for him to do so. Overview of Safari Express' Application [19] Safari Express contends that Mr. Salinger, acting for himself and on behalf of the plaintiffs, engaged in reprehensible conduct throughout this litigation. Safari Express advances the following five overarching categories of reprehensible conduct to support its claim for an award of special costs: (a) Mr. Salinger commenced and pursued a meritless claim (i.e., the Repair Services Claim) on behalf of the plaintiffs against Safari Express; (b) After letting this action sit dormant, Mr. Salinger reactivated the action with an improper motive in response to what he viewed to be a hostile affidavit sworn by Mr. Mohamed and filed on behalf of the defendants in the Alpha Action; (c) Mr. Salinger obstructed service of court documents in this action, improperly used service issues to delay, and in some instances avoid altogether, interlocutory applications, and attempted to delay or thwart certain interlocutory processes, all of which put Safari Express to unwarranted expense; (d) Mr. Salinger made resolution of this action far more difficult than it ought to have been; and (e) Mr. Salinger engaged in reprehensible correspondence with the law firm representing Safari Express. [20] Mr. Salinger and the plaintiffs vehemently deny that any of their conduct was reprehensible and deny any grounds exist to award special costs. Mr. Salinger, for himself and the plaintiffs, advances the following answers to the claim. [21] Mr. Salinger denies that there is any basis to suggest that the Repair Services Claim lacked merit. He points out that the plaintiffs' ex parte application for an interim order to enjoin Safari Express from selling or dealing with or moving the Cessna predicated on that claim was successful. Justice Watchuk granted an initial order on June 8, 2015, valid for 48 hours expiring at 4 pm on June 10, 2015. On June 10, 2015, the order was extended "until further order of this Court" by Justice Bruce. Mr. Salinger also points out that neither order was appealed and no application was brought to set either aside. [22] Although he concedes that Mr. Mohamed's affidavit was hostile to his interests in the Alpha Action (and the evidence shows he accused Safari Express of breaching the Settlement Agreement when the affidavit was served in the Alpha Action), Mr. Salinger argues that it had no bearing on his conduct and that of the plaintiffs in this action. Moreover, he says the plaintiffs' decision to abandon the Repair Services Claim was based on considerations of costs and upon a promise from counsel for Safari Express that if the claim was withdrawn, the case would be remitted to the jurisdiction of the Provincial Court, Small Claims Division, where costs awards are generally not available. [23] Mr. Salinger contests Safari Express' assertions that he has impeded service and used service issues as a delaying tactic. He acknowledges that Safari Express' applications for orders requiring the plaintiffs to post security for costs (Aero Attack does not own any land or real property in this province; according to Safari Express, it is not aware of KiTov owning any land or real property in British Columbia as well, and KiTov did not adduce any evidence to establish that it did at any time material to this action) and for specific performance of the Settlement Agreement did not proceed to hearing due to issues with service on the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger. However, Mr. Salinger contends that he and the plaintiffs were entitled to rely on their right under the Supreme Court Civil Rules [Rules] to insist on personal service. [24] Mr. Salinger also denies that he and the plaintiffs made resolution of this action more difficult. To the opposite, he says that he sought to resolve the case on the terms agreed upon in the Settlement Agreement with the proviso that before they paid the remaining funds owing, he should have the right to inspect the Cessna to ensure it had not been damaged (which was the position that he and the plaintiffs took at the hearing of Safari Express' summary trial application). [25] Mr. Salinger also points out that in January 2020, Safari Express offered to waive its costs, and consequently, it should not now be allowed to seek an award of costs. [26] Lastly, Mr. Salinger contends that it is Safari Express and its law firm who should be rebuked for their conduct in the action, and in particular, for their anti-Semitic remarks and behaviour towards Mr. Salinger. When Special Costs May Be Ordered [50] Special costs may be ordered against a party who engages in reprehensible conduct in the proceeding. They are punitive as opposed to compensatory in order to rebuke and deter such conduct and are awarded when a court seeks to dissociate itself from such conduct in a proceeding: Rules, R. 14-1(1)(b); Sandhu v. Mangat, 2019 BCCA 238 at paras. 4-5; Garcia v. Crestbrook Forest Industries Ltd. (1994), 119 D.L.R. (4th) 740 (B.C.C.A.) at para. 17; Smithies Holdings Inc. v. RCV Holdings Ltd., 2017 BCCA 177 at paras. 57, 133-134. [51] In Sandhu, citing from Garcia, the Court of Appeal said at para. 4 that there is a "continuum of circumstances in which special costs could be awarded, ranging from 'milder forms of misconduct deserving of reproof or rebuke' to 'scandalous or outrageous conduct.'" Reprehensible conduct can include conduct that is neither scandalous nor outrageous: Garcia at para. 5. Reprehensible conduct, Justice Lambert wrote in Garcia at para. 17, "is a word of wide meaning. It encompasses scandalous or outrageous conduct but it also encompasses milder form of misconduct deserving of reproof or rebuke." [52] From the case authorities, it is clear that special costs may be ordered in the following circumstances: (a) where a party pursues a meritless claim and is reckless with regard to the truth; (b) where a party makes improper allegations of fraud, conspiracy, fraudulent misrepresentation, or breach of fiduciary duty; (c) where a party has displayed "reckless indifference" by not recognizing early on that its claim was manifestly deficient; (d) where a party made the resolution of an issue far more difficult than it should have been; (e) where a party who is in a financially superior position to the other brings proceedings, not with the reasonable expectation of a favourable outcome, but in the absence of merit in order to impose a financial burden on the opposing party; (f) where a party presents a case so weak that it is bound to fail, and continues to pursue its meritless claim after it is drawn to its attention that the claim is without merit; (g) where a party brings a proceeding for an improper motive; (h) where a party maintains unfounded allegations of fraud or dishonesty; and (i) where a party pursues claims frivolously or without foundation. [53] See, e.g., Garcia at paras. 23-25; International Hi-Tech Industries Inc. v. FANUC Robotics Canada Ltd., 2007 BCSC 1724 at paras. 7-13; Webber v. Singh, 2005 BCSC 224 at para. 28; McLean v. Gonzalez-Calvo, 2007 BCSC 648 at paras. 26, 29; Buchan v. Moss Management Inc., 2008 BCSC 1286 at paras. 11-12; and Edwards v. Bell, 2004 BCSC 399 at paras. 12, 43-45; Mayer v. Osborne Contracting Ltd., 2011 BCSC 914 at para. 11. Meritless Claim and Response to Hostile Affidavit [54] In terms of the merits issue, the plaintiffs persuaded Watchuk J. to grant a temporary injunction based on the prima facie merits of their application materials. Safari Express has not demonstrated that Watchuk or Bruce JJ. were misled on the ex parte applications. Despite the temporal proximity of service of Mr. Mohamed's affidavit in the Alpha Action and the filing of the plaintiffs' notice of intention to proceed in this action, Safari Express has not established that Mr. Salinger specifically reactivated this action in response to Mr. Mohamed's affidavit. Safari Express has also not established that Mr. Salinger's other explanation for the plaintiffs dropping their claim, i.e., that he did not believe the costs to pursue it were not warranted, lacks credibility. I therefore agree with Mr. Salinger that Safari Express has not established reprehensible conduct in respect of initiating and/or pursuing a meritless claim and of Mr. Mohamed's affidavit. That said, I reject Mr. Salinger's submission that Safari Express breached the Settlement Agreement when Mr. Mohamed swore his affidavit and allowed it to be used by parties in the Alpha Action. Impeding Service and Delay [55] While I agree with Safari Express that some of the reasons that Mr. Salinger proffered in the past for objecting to service by email (e.g., he told Safari Express that he is prohibited by the Defence Production Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. D-1 from downloading documents) are not supported by the evidence or of dubious veracity and that he was often late in filing application materials, the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger were entitled as a matter of right under the Rules to insist on personal service. No application for an alternate means of service was brought by Safari Express. [56] I disagree with Safari Express that the two incidents it highlighted as overt examples of service issues used by Mr. Salinger to cause delay constitute reprehensible conduct. [57] The first incident involved Safari Express' attempt to personally serve application documents by courier on Mr. Salinger at his home in November 2020 during the early stages of the COVID-19 pandemic lockdown. Mr. Salinger says he was concerned about the risk of transmission of the COVID virus into his home where his elderly mother resided with him at the time. [58] There is a conflict in the evidence as to whether the courier actually gave the documents to Mr. Salinger or left them at the front door. Mr. Salinger contends they were soiled as a result of him leaving the documents on his front door step until the documents were picked up for return to the law firm. The courier's evidence is that he handed the documents to an individual who took them inside Mr. Salinger's residence. Regardless, Mr. Salinger caused them to be returned to counsel for Safari Express, and told the law firm that he did not wish documents to be brought into his home in the midst of the pandemic at a time when there were widespread reports of easy transmission of the virus by touching objects that were not subject to quarantine. The documents were returned to Safari Express soiled by water and some debris. [59] While I agree with Safari Express that it acted properly in serving the documents at Mr. Salinger's residence since it was Mr. Salinger who had insisted on personal service at that address, I do not agree that his reaction in the context of the information concerning transmission of the virus at that time constitutes reprehensible conduct. Moreover, once the documents were returned, Mr. Salinger readily offered up an alternate means of service. He arranged for documents to be served on him at the office of Flash Couriers in Vancouver on an ongoing basis and filed a notice of address for service dated December 4, 2020, with the registry on December 10, 2020, to that effect (according to Safari Express, Master Cameron rejected the appropriateness of service by way of delivery to a courier company and said service should occur by way of registered mail, but there is no evidence Safari Express took any further steps to challenge the change in method of service). [60] The other incident pertains to the scheduling of the hearing of Safari Express' special costs application. The hearing was originally scheduled for January 18, 2022. It was adjourned when Mr. Salinger advised that he had not been notified by Flash Couriers that he had been served with the application documents until shortly before the hearing because Flash Couriers had misplaced them. [61] Mr. Salinger produced an email dated January 19, 2022 (through his affidavit no. 7, sworn February 4, 2022), he said he received from Flash Couriers advising that it could not confirm if it had in fact notified Mr. Salinger of service, and I adjourned the application to February 14, 2022. [62] The email put into evidence by Mr. Salinger confirms his advice that he was not notified of service but does not state that it was because Flash Courier had misplaced the documents. The contents of that email are set out below: From: Shipping Sent: January 18, 2022 10:20 AM To: Rod Salinger Cc: Shipping Subject: RE: File delivered. Dear Rod After checking our records I confirm that Flash Couriers received a shipment from Kornfelds on about 23 November 2021 but for some unknown reason we overlooked sending you advice that it had arrived and on forwarding it to you. We are very sorry for causing you any inconvenience. Yours sincerely, Flash Couriers, 1213 Frances St Vancouver BC [Emphasis added] [63] During the hearing of its special costs application, Safari Express produced an email from the president of Flash Couriers sent on January 19, 2022. Its contents, Safari Express says, contradict the document produced by Mr. Salinger. Allocation [94] I have considered whether special costs should be awarded following the Settlement Agreement since that is the inception point for Mr. Salinger's reprehensible conduct. Generally, when special costs are ordered, it is for the entire action, absent special circumstances: The Owners, Strata Plan LMS 3259 v. Sze Hang Holding Inc., 2017 BCCA 346 at para. 81. Departure from the rule should be invoked sparingly. The goal is to avoid a "minute dissection of the success or failure of litigants on the completion of a trial" (Gotaverken Energy Systems Ltd. v. Cariboo Pulp & Paper Co. (1995), 9 B.C.L.R. (3d) 340 at 343 (S.C.)) and a "meticulous costs post mortem which would add yet further expense and be contrary to the interests of justice" (Laichkwiltach Enterprises Ltd. v. F/V Pacific Faith (Ship), 2008 BCSC 282 at para. 33). [95] However, R. 14-1(15)(b) provides discretion to award a party costs that relate to some particular application, step or matter in or related to the proceeding": Youyi Group Holdings (Canada) Ltd. v. Brentwood Lanes Canada Ltd., 2021 BCSC 2144 at paras. 88-91. [96] In Sutherland v. Canada (Attorney General), 2008 BCCA 27, the Court of Appeal set out the test for apportionment as follows: [31] The test for the apportionment of costs under Rule 57(15) can be set out as follows: (1) the party seeking apportionment must establish that there are separate and discrete issues upon which the ultimately unsuccessful party succeeded at trial; (2) there must be a basis on which the trial judge can identify the time attributable to the trial of these separate issues; (3) it must be shown that apportionment would effect a just result. [97] The overarching consideration is whether it would be unfair to award the successful party all of its costs: Youyi at para. 92. [98] In Sze Hang Holding, Justice Goepel said at para. 81 that: [81] While special costs are usually awarded for the whole of a proceeding, it is open to a judge to make a partial award of special costs if he or she is of the view that it would be disproportionate to award special costs for the entire proceeding [99] I do not consider it an impediment to consider apportionment, even though Mr. Salinger did not argue it in the alternative. I must be satisfied that the scope of the award effects a just result. Justice Sinclair Prowse pointed out in Webber v. Canadian Aviation Insurance Managers Ltd., 2003 BCSC 274 at para. 26 that "the circumstances of the case must establish that the failure to apportion costs would result in basic unfairness." I raised the issue with the parties and it was only Safari Express who responded by providing me with the decision of Justice Mayer in Youyi. [100] As I said above, the evidence points directly to a basis on which I can identify the inception point of reprehensible conduct in this action, i.e., following the Settlement Agreement. As Safari Express has not established that the plaintiffs commenced or prosecuted a meritless claim prior to that point, and in circumstances where no other reprehensible conduct has been identified prior to the Settlement Agreement, an award of special costs for the whole of the action would be unjust, disproportionate, and result in basic unfairness. I have determined that allocation is appropriate on the facts of this case and order that special costs be awarded from the date of the Settlement Agreement. Costs incurred prior to that date are awarded as ordinary Rules costs. Joint and Several Award [101] I agree with Safari Express that the award should be against Mr. Salinger personally for the plaintiffs' reprehensible conduct that occurred prior to him being added as a party by counterclaim on January 17, 2020. The court has inherent jurisdiction to award costs against non-parties; however, such discretion must be exercised sparingly and only in special circumstances: Anchorage Management Services Ltd. v. 465404 B.C. Ltd. Inc., 1999 BCCA 771 at para. 21. Special circumstances include situations where the non-party has engaged in fraudulent conduct, an abuse of process, gross misconduct in the commencement and/or conduct of the litigation, or when the non-party is the "real litigant": Perez v. Galambos, 2008 BCCA 382 at para. 18. [102] In International Hi-Tech, Justice Ballance applied the holdings from Anchorage Management and awarded special costs against the corporation and its chief operating officer (who was not a named party). She also discussed whether the elements of abuse of process in the context of special costs were the same as those required to establish the tort of abuse of process, and concluded they were not: [54] The issue was revived some years later in Anchorage. After surveying the case authorities, including the Oasis decision, Hall J.A. indicated that there appeared to exist four bases for making an award of costs against a non-party: 1. fraudulent conduct; 2. abuse of process; 3. gross misconduct in the commencement and/or conduct of the litigation; and 4. where the real litigant stays out of the action and offers up a "man of straw" to prosecute the claim. [55] I do not take Mr. Justice Hall as purporting to enumerate an exhaustive list of the circumstances that could legitimately found the liability for costs of a non-party. Moreover, these categories are not airtight compartments conceptually and the likelihood of overlap among one or more of them is readily apparent. [66] However, in my mind, it remains an open question as to whether the nature of the abuse of process contemplated in Anchorage must have all the elements of the tort of abuse of process. It is trite to observe that this court has an inherent jurisdiction to control its own process and protect against the misuse of its forum and procedure. In that sense, the concept of an abuse of process is far broader in its scope than that which is encompassed by the tort of abuse of process. In my opinion, it is that expanded notion of an abuse of process to which the courts refer in the context of a solicitor's liability in Billows and Cunningham. It also finds expression in Rule 19(24) of the Rules of Court. There can be no doubt that Abi-Rached abused this court's process, in this broader sense. That may be sufficient to fix him with costs personally. However, I decline to use that reasoning as the footing to do so because this line of argument was not addressed or developed by counsel and, for the reasons that follow, I find him accountable in costs based on his commission of a fraud upon the court. [Bold in original; underline emphasis added] [103] In OZ Merchandising Inc. v. Canadian Professional Soccer League, 2020 ONSC 119 at para. 53, aff'd 2021 ONCA 520, leave to appeal ref'd 2022 CanLII 16723 (S.C.C.), Justice Ryan Bell held that an abuse of process, in the context of non-party costs, encompasses "situations of gross misconduct, vexatious conduct, or conduct that undermines the fair administration of justice." Conduct that causes extra expense or delay was viewed to be deleterious to the fair administration of justice. The reasons refer to 1318847 Ontario Ltd. v. Laval Tool & Mould Ltd., 2017 ONCA 184 where Chief Justice Strathy held at para. 87: [87] Safeguarding public confidence in the fair administration of justice depends on preserving the availability of court facilities for justifiable proceedings and not permitting the costs of proceedings to be needlessly inflated, particularly at a time when delays and costs of litigation are so concerning from the perspective of access to justice In my mind, those factors fall within the ambit of the types of misconduct spoken of in Anchorage Management and Perez. [104] Accordingly, in view of my findings concerning Mr. Salinger's misconduct since the Settlement Agreement was entered into, I order special costs on a joint and several basis against the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger. Summary [105] Special costs are awarded to Safari Express following the parties entering into the Settlement Agreement in September 2017. Costs prior to that date are awarded to Safari Express as ordinary Rules costs. [106] Special costs (but not ordinary Rules costs) are awarded on a joint and several basis against the plaintiffs and Mr. Salinger, such that Mr. Salinger is obliged to pay them personally.
