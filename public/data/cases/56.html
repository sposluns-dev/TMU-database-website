The Appellant has provided new evidence in support of her appeal. She has indicated that she is not asking for an oral hearing. She further requests that the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) set aside the determination of the RPD and find that she is a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection. Alternatively, she requests that the matter be remitted to the RPD for reconsideration by a differently constituted panel. DETERMINATION [2] Pursuant to subsection 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and finds that the Appellant is a person in need of protection. The appeal is allowed. Background [3] The Appellant alleged in her Basis of Claim (BOC) narrative that she fears persecution on the basis of her Jewish religion. She described incidents in this regard in her home town of Donetsk, beginning in XXXX 2013. She was treated in a hospital, and she reported the incident to the police, but they did not offer help. [4] In XXXX 2013, she received threatening anti-Semitic notes. In XXXX 2013, she stayed with a friend in XXXX and remained until XXXX when her friend moved to Kiev. In XXXX, she and her friend were physically attacked. The police refused to open a case because there were no injuries. They were also chased from a synagogue. The next day, the Rabbi told her to leave XXXX. [5] The Appellant returned to Donetsk. She did not return to her apartment since her neighbours were responsible for the incidents described above. She was unable to exchange her apartment because too many people were leaving the Donetsk area. [6] In XXXX 2014, the Appellant was attacked by two masked men in her apartment building. A neighbour helped her and called the police. The Appellant was hospitalized for 12 days. [7] Her Rabbi told her it was unsafe to remain in Ukraine, and she decided to come to Canada to join her daughter. She left Ukraine on XXXX XXXX, 2014. THE ROLE OF THE RAD [8] The RAD notes that the Federal Court of Appeal's decision, in Huruglica,1 provides the RAD with guidance in relation to the appropriate standard that must be applied by a specialized tribunal, such as the RAD, to decisions of a first-level administrative tribunal. [9] The RAD concludes from its statutory analysis, with respect to findings of fact and mixed fact and law, that the RAD is to review RPD decisions applying the correctness standard, and that there may be deference to the RPD where the findings of fact involve the assessment of oral evidence. [10] The RAD must carefully consider the RPD's decision and, after doing so, must carry out its own analysis of the record to determine whether, as submitted by the Appellant, the RPD erred. The Issues [11] The panel indicated that the determinative issue in regard to this claim is internal flight alternative (IFA). [12] The Appellant submits that the issues in this appeal are the following: (a) Is there a serious possibility that the Appellant would be persecuted in Kiev? (b) Did the panel apply the wrong standard in assessing the proposed IFA? (c) Could the Appellant move to Kiev without undue hardship? ADMISSIBILITY OF EVIDENCE PRESENTED ON APPEAL [13] Subsection 110(4) of the IRPA provides that the Appellant may present only evidence that arose after the rejection of his/her claim or that was not reasonably available, or that he/she could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented, at the time of the rejection of the claim. [14] The Appellant has provided internet documentation, dated May 2015, concerning the cost of living in Kiev. The Appellant argues that this documentation was not available prior to the RPD hearing. The Appellant further argues that the Appellant was unaware that Kiev would be identified as a possible IFA, and she was aware only after the hearing that this documentation would be relevant. [15] The RAD has reviewed this documentation, and it has considered the concerns noted in an earlier RAD decision concerning this case.2 The RAD finds, however, given the arguments of the Appellant noted above, and acknowledging the possible shortcomings of the evidence as to the identity of the compilers of the data, that this evidence was not reasonably available to the Appellant before the RPD's decision on May 5, 2015, and therefore it is admitted as new evidence. THE PANEL'S FINDINGS AND ANALYSIS Internal Flight Alternative (IFA) [16] The panel acknowledged that the Appellant had experienced assaults in Donetsk and XXXX. [17] The panel reviewed the two-prong test with respect to an IFA as set out in Rasaratnam. The RPD notes that it must be satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that there is no serious possibility of the Appellant being persecuted in the proposed IFA, and that it is reasonable, in all the circumstances, for the Appellant to relocate there.3 [18] The panel acknowledged the current problems associated with the separatist movement in the Donetsk region and the Russian action in Crimea. The panel cited country documentation indicating that minority groups, including Jews, are targeted for violence in the Donetsk area.4 The First Prong of the IFA Test [19] The Appellant was questioned as to whether she could move to Kiev. She said no and explained that she had no place to stay in Kiev; she is elderly and fearful that nationalists would target her in Kiev. Counsel stated that she would be targeted as a Jew. [20] The panel cited country documentation indicating that, although separatists controlled the east, Kiev was relatively safe. The panel considered counsel's submission that a Rabbi asked Kiev's Jews to leave the city due to fears of being targeted by "Brown Shirts".5 The panel found, however, that the preponderance of evidence suggests that acts of anti-Semitism had not increased in Kiev.6 [21] The panel found that Kiev is a safe place for Jews and cited country documentary evidence indicating that only isolated anti-Semitic incidents were reported in February 2014, after a recent period of unrest. Incidents had been reported in Zaporizhzhya in central Ukraine and two attacks in the Podol neighbourhood of Kiev. This documentation further states that representatives of these Jewish communities indicate that they do not feel threatened, and this is confirmed by the Association of Jewish Organizations and Communities of Ukraine in a March 5, 2014 letter.7 [22] The panel cited further documentation indicating that neo-Nazi graffiti had been painted on houses and tomb stones in Kiev. It is further indicated that leaders of the Right Sector and the Ukrainian National Assembly met the Rabbi, assured him that these organizations had not participated in these acts, and joined the municipal service and pro-unity activists in washing off the graffiti. On April 8, the police opened a criminal investigation into the case. [23] The panel noted that the head of the Association of the Jewish Organizations and Communities of Ukraine stated there was no increase in anti-Semitic incidents in Ukraine and that incidents had been declining since 2007.8 [24] Country documentation indicates that there were acts of vandalism and reports of anti-Semitic graffiti on Jewish monuments in Odessa; however, representatives of the Jewish community indicated that they are well integrated into society throughout Ukraine, enjoy their rights as a religious minority and that incidents are rare.9 [25] The panel found, given the evidence in the record, that the Appellant had failed to establish that she would be targeted in Kiev, a city of over two million people. The panel further found, on a balance of probabilities, that the Appellant would not be subjected to a risk to her life or the harms pursuant to subsection 97(1) of the IRPA, in Kiev. The Second Prong of the IFA Test [26] The panel found it was reasonable in all circumstances of the Appellant's situation, that she could return to Ukraine and live in Kiev. [27] The panel noted that the Appellant was a widow whose children live elsewhere. She was educated in Ukraine and worked there for 46 years. The panel further noted that the Appellant would face no linguistic or cultural barriers in Kiev. [28] The panel found that such difficulties of not having a home in Kiev are inherent in relocating and not in itself an unreasonable burden. The panel further found that the Appellant could go to Kiev and register there, as Ukraine has allowed freedom of movement for its citizens since 2004.10 [29] The panel acknowledged the Appellant's age and lack of family in Ukraine. The panel noted, however, that the Appellant has lived her entire life in Ukraine, and her children left the country some time ago. The panel acknowledged the difficulties in relocating but noted the Appellant has friends, a pension and medical coverage. [30] The panel noted the difficulties that Internal Displaced Persons (IDPs) may encounter, as well as the efforts of local agencies, the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) and non-government organizations (NGOs) to assist them. [31] The panel found, on a balance of probabilities, that there are no serious social, economic or other barriers to the Appellant relocating in Kiev, and it would not be unreasonable, in all the circumstances, including those particular to the Appellant, for her to seek refuge there. [32] The panel acknowledged that the Appellant is elderly and suffers from depression and that she wished to be with her family in Canada. While sympathizing with the Appellant, the panel noted that it was outside its authority to consider this when determining a refugee claim. The panel further noted that such issues were more properly considered under Canada's principles of family reunification and humanitarian and compassionate consideration. The Appellant's Submissions and Argument Anti-Semitism [33] The Appellant cites country documentary evidence concerning the risk for Jews in Kiev and Ukraine, in general. The Appellant further cites country documentation concerning three main nationalist groups which are anti-Semitic and who operate throughout Ukraine including Kiev. [34] The Appellant submits that the panel's finding that she could live in Kiev without risk of persecution and harm is unreasonable. The Appellant further submits that, if she was attacked in two cities in Ukraine, there was more than a mere possibility that she would be persecuted in Kiev. The panel submits as well that, whether the level of anti-Semitism has increased or not is irrelevant. What is at issue is whether there is a serious possibility of persecution based on current levels of anti-Semitism. The Appellant cites 2013 and 2014 evidence from the Jewish community that there was a rise in nationalism and attacks on Jews. [35] The Appellant submits that there are political parties and nationalist groups like Right Sector in Ukraine that threaten Jews, and there is a serious possibility that she would be at risk if she were living in Kiev. Internal Flight Alternative (IFA) [36] The Appellant submits that her current pension is 1900 Gryvni a month, and she lacks the means of buying or renting an apartment in Kiev. The Appellant further submits that finding a place to live is increasingly difficult because of the influx of displaced people moving from Eastern Ukraine to Kiev. [37] The Appellant submits as well that she is elderly and suffers from depression and other health issues. The Appellant further submits that her neighbour was able to move to Kiev because she could live with her son. The Appellant has no one she could live with in Kiev. [38] The Appellant submits that she could not access her pension or other services without registration, and in order to obtain registration she would need a place to live. The Appellant further submits that many renters cannot register because rental agreements are unofficial. [39] The Appellant argues that the panel has applied too high a standard in determining that she could safely relocate to Kiev. The Appellant submits that the panel need only be satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that there is a serious possibility that she would be persecuted in Kiev. The Appellant argues by incorrectly stating that the test is a "balance of probabilities", without reference to the lower standard of a "serious possibility" of persecution, the panel has raised the standard to more likely than not she would be persecuted. [40] The Appellant argues that, given the speculative nature of the concept of IFA, as she has never resided in Kiev, the standard should not be a higher one than in parts of the country where she has resided. [41] The Appellant further argues that the panel applied a "balance of probabilities" standard without reference to a "serious possibility" and that is an error so fundamental that the decision should be overturned. [42] The Appellant submits that the panel acknowledged that she suffered from depression, and argues that verbal threats, insults, physical and emotional intimidation would be extremely serious. As to her subjective circumstances, the Appellant submits that she is an elderly woman with health issues and depression that would worsen if exposed to such negative non-physical treatment. The Appellant argues that the panel applied too high a standard by focusing on physical attacks in determining whether she would suffer persecution, and it disregarded her personal circumstances. The Appellant further argues that, if the panel had applied the correct standard, it might have reached a different decision. [43] The Appellant argues that the panel failed to conduct a fulsome analysis on the issue of obtaining registration in Kiev. [44] The Appellant argues that the panel only looked at what the law states and not its application. The Appellant further argues, given that access to her pension and health care depend on registration, that the panel was obligated to assess her ability to obtain registration in Kiev, and the lack of this analysis makes the panel's decision seriously flawed. [45] The Appellant submits that her evidence indicates that she could not move to Kiev because she could not buy or rent accommodation given her pension. The Appellant further submits that the situation in this regard in Kiev has only gotten worse given the influx of people from the East. [46] The Appellant cites country documentary evidence concerning anti-Semitism and anti-Semitic attacks in Ukraine, the large numbers of internally displaced persons, problems with registering in another city and the high cost of living in Kiev.11 [47] The Appellant argues that the panel made its decision without regard to her credible evidence that she cannot live in Kiev or the country documentary evidence that supported her evidence. [48] The Appellant submits that the panel acknowledged that she is elderly and suffers from depression and likely other maladies. The Appellant argues that the panel failed to assess the significance of depression and failed to adequately consider her depression-particularly in an elderly woman-would impact the reasonableness of the expectation that she resettle to a city without family or personal support network. The Appellant further argues that the panel's failure in this regard is significant and invalidates its decision. THE RAD'S ANALYSIS AND DECISION [49] The RAD has reviewed and assessed all of the evidence in the record concerning the availability of an internal flight alternative (IFA) to the Appellant. The RAD notes that the panel has acknowledged that the Appellant experienced physical attacks in her home city Donetsk and also in XXXX, because she is Jewish. The RAD further notes that the panel acknowledged that Donetsk is at the centre of a separatist movement in eastern Ukraine, and the attacks on the Appellant and on members of other minority communities are related to the enhanced nationalist identity of the separatists and their actions in this area. [50] The RAD notes that the panel reviewed the country documentation concerning anti-Semitism and anti-Semitic attacks in Ukraine. The RAD further notes that the Appellant has provided additional documentation that records anti-Semitic attacks and the views and actions of various nationalist groups concerning minority communities in Ukraine, including Jews. [51] The RAD has reviewed all of this documentation and finds that anti-Semitic views are widespread in Ukraine, including among political parties in Parliament and members of the government. These views have been reflected over time in discrimination, vandalism, media stories and physical attacks.12 [52] The RAD notes that the evidence is mixed as to the situation for Jews in Ukraine. While anti-Semitic acts, including physical attacks continue, they are episodic and some sources indicate that there is a "trend of improvement" in the level of anti-Semitism.13 The RAD further notes evidence cited by the panel that acts of anti-Semitism have not increased in Kiev. While there were isolated anti-Semitic incidents, representatives of Jewish communities indicate that they do not feel threatened.14 [53] The Appellant argues that whether the level of anti-Semitism has increased or not is irrelevant, and she further argues that there is a serious possibility of persecution based on current levels of anti-Semitism, including in Kiev. [54] The Appellant submits that the panel applied too high a standard in determining that she could safely relocate in Kiev, by incorrectly stating the test as "a balance of probabilities", without reference to the lower standard of "a serious possibility" of persecution. [55] The RAD notes that, while the panel stated the correct test at the beginning of its IFA analysis (para. 17), it did not repeat a reference to "serious possibility" in its concluding finding (para. 25). The RAD finds that the panel's awareness of and intention to apply the correct test is clear, and its failure to repeat the words reflecting the intentions of the test in its concluding finding is a writing error and not a reflection of its failure to apply the proper test. [56] The RAD has, however, reviewed the panel's decision in this regard and finds, in the context of its review of all of the evidence concerning this issue, that the panel did not err in its finding. The RAD finds that, while anti-Semitism remains widespread in Ukraine and likely in Kiev, the incidents cited in the documentary evidence, including physical attacks do not support a finding that, on a balance of probabilities, there is a serious possibility that the Appellant would be at risk of persecution, if she were to relocate to Kiev. [57] While the RAD finds the panel's conclusion reflects a correct application of the first prong of the IFA test; it has concerns about the second prong as to whether it would be reasonable in all the circumstances, including those particular to the Appellant, for her to relocate to Kiev. [58] The RAD notes that the Appellant is an 85-year-old woman who suffers from depression and has experienced physical attacks because she is Jewish. The RAD further notes that the attacks took place not only in Donetsk, where separatism and heightened nationalism have enhanced the jeopardy of minority communities, including Jews, but also in XXXX XXXX more than 1,200 kilometres to the west. [59] The RAD finds that, while it has found there is not a serious possibility that the Appellant would be persecuted in Kiev, it further finds, given the Appellant's particular circumstances, that it is likely she would have an honest belief that she would be in jeopardy of persecution, and this would have an impact on her mental health as a person who already suffers from depression. [60] The RAD notes that the Appellant's particular circumstances, in addition to her age and her experience of physical attack, include as well her lack of family support, as she is a widow, and her children live out of the country. The RAD has reviewed the evidence concerning internal displaced persons in Ukraine and the difficulty in finding another place to live. [61] The RAD cites a UNHCR report on protection concerns related to developments in Ukraine.15 While acknowledging that an IFA is likely to be available for many individuals in areas of the country not affected by recent events, it is noted that personal circumstances should be considered on a case-by-case basis. It is further noted that these circumstances include access to accommodation and essential services, administrative difficulties for IDPs, lack of family support, discrimination and fear of persecution because of ethnicity or religion, the availability of specialized medical and psychological support and the likelihood that assistance programs are insufficient to meet the need. [62] The RAD has reviewed the country documentation concerning registration of residence in Ukraine. It is noted that, while Ukrainians have freedom of choice concerning their place of residence, there are shortcomings in the system. Citizens cannot always register if they do not own their residences. Many renters cannot register because most rental agreements are unofficial. In addition, without a place of residence people may face difficulties accessing medical care and social services.16 [63] Another document indicates that the authorities require confirmation of the registration for obtaining medical care and getting pension and social benefits.17 The RAD notes that the panel found that the Appellant could resettle in Kiev and register there, as the law allows freedom of movement in Ukraine. The RAD further notes that the Appellant has raised a doubt as to the application of this law, and based on documentation cited above, the RAD shares that doubt. [64] The RAD finds that it is not unlikely that the Appellant would encounter difficulties in finding and registering a residence in Kiev based on her need to rent an apartment, the fact that most rentals are unofficial, her limited resources and prejudice as to her Jewish identity. [65] The RAD finds, in the context of the analysis above, that it would be unreasonable in all the circumstances, including those particular to the Appellant, for her to seek refuge in Kiev. The RAD, therefore, finds that the requirements of the second prong of the test have not been met and that the Appellant does not have an IFA in Kiev. DISPOSITION [66] Pursuant to subsection 111(1)(b) of the IRPA, the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and finds that the Appellant is a person in need of protection. The appeal is allowed.
