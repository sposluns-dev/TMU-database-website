Motifs et décision Claimant(s) XXXX XXXX XXXX Demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Date(s) of hearing April 3, 2014 May 16, 2014 May 22, 2014 Date(s) de l'audience Place of hearing Montréal, Quebec Lieu de l'audience Date of decision and reasons September 22, 2014 Date de la décision et des motifs Panel Dany Dubé Tribunal Counsel for the claimant(s) Me Nicolas Ranger Conseil(s) du (de la/des) demandeur(e)(s) d'asile Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister Eric Theriault [Filing of documents] Conseil du (de la) ministre Reasons for Decision [1] XXXX XXXX XXXX claims that he is a citizen of Mexico. He is alleging that he is a "Convention refugee" and a "person in need of protection" as defined in section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). ALLEGATIONS [2] The claimant's complete allegations can be found in the response to question number 2 of the Basis of Claim Form (BOC Form) and do not need to be repeated here in detail. The following is a brief summary. [3] When the claimant was about 45 years old, his father told him the truth about his birth. He told him that he was not born in Mexico, but, rather, in Israel. He told him that he had had an affair with a woman when he was working in Israel and that they never married. He told him that he was born at home and that his mother died in child birth. When he was about nine months, his father took him from Israel to Mexico, where he registered him with the Mexican authorities as born in Mexico. [4] His father told him few things about his biological mother. In particular, he told him that she was a Holocaust survivor who had left Poland for Israel and that her name was XXXX XXXX. His father had no documents to confirm his mother's identity, her death or the birth of the claimant in Israel. His father subsequently died. [5] The claimant had been raised Christian, but when he learned the truth about his heritage he converted to Judaism. He chose Messianic Judaism. [6] He experienced several problems in Mexico because of his Jewish faith. Specifically, he had problems with a neighbour, the electricity company, and his employers and colleagues in the field of time sharing. Moreover, the latter had ties to cartels, including the Zetas and Los Pelones. [7] In 2011, he started his own XXXX XXXX business with his children, in Cancun. [8] In XXXX 2012, he was approached by two male members of the criminal group Los Pelones who demanded that he pay them 10% of his daily income, in order to protect him and his family. They came every day to get the money, always made anti-Semitic remarks to him and insinuated that he was wealthy. [9] Thereafter, the 10% he had to pay them increased to 50% of his daily income, and despite paying them every day, he started receiving text messages informing him that he owed them money. [10] He then decided to shut down his business, and one day in XXXX 2012, he did not go to work. As a result, three representatives of Los Pelones went to his home and beat him, again making reference to the fact that he is a Jewish person and insinuating that he was wealthy. They then took his computer and told him that if he went to work the following day and paid the money he owed, they would return it. [11] He was injured, but he nonetheless went to work on the following day. He then asked them permission to have someone else take care of his business for a few days, and they accepted. [12] He subsequently travelled to Acapulco and applied to obtain a passport and a tourist visa to come to Canada. Then, he returned to Cancun. [13] He continued to work until late XXXX, sent his children to live with their mother and left the country on XXXX XXXX, 2012. [14] The claimant arrived in Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2012, and travelled to St. Catharines, in Ontario. At the time, he wanted to sell his house in Mexico from within Canada and make an investment in Canada with the proceeds from the sale in order to acquire a status and remain here. However, he was unable to sell his house because it did not have electricity. [15] He began to attend the XXXX XXXX XXXX Church because it is the only Christian church that celebrates Sabbath on Saturday as Jewish people do. [16] In XXXX 2013, he attended a seminar at the XXXX XXXX XXXX Church in Buffalo, in the United States. He travelled there by bus with a group from the St. Catharines church. By doing this, he also hoped to obtain a status upon returning to Canada. He stayed in the United States for a few days and returned to Canada by bus with his group. However, when he returned to Canada, no one examined his passport. [17] When he was in Ottawa, he spoke about his situation to a member of his synagogue who advised him to claim refugee status. He then went to the Catholic immigration centre, explained his situation to them, and the staff also advised him to file a claim for refugee protection. [18] Subsequently, he obtained legal aid and the services of a lawyer, with whom he prepared the documents to claim refugee protection in Canada. [19] He filed his claim for refugee protection on March 5, 2014. [20] At the start of the hearing, the claimant stated that, were he to return to Mexico, he fears being killed by the cartels (Los Pelones and the Zetas), XXXX XXXX XXXX, the CFE electricity company and the Catholic Church and/or the tourism workers' unions because of his religious beliefs and/or damages that he caused them. DETERMINATION [21] The panel determines that the claimant is not a "Convention refugee" or a "person in need of protection," for the reasons that follow. ANALYSIS Identity [22] To establish his Mexican identity, the claimant notably filed his Mexican passport. The panel concludes that the claimant provided acceptable identity documents and is satisfied with them. Hearing postponements, Minister's intervention and amendments to the Basis of Claim Form (BOC Form) [23] On March 26, 2014, the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) received a notice of intervention from the Minister of Citizenship and Immigration Canada (the Minister). [24] In his notice, the Minister claims that because the claimant is a Jewish person he may be granted a status in Israel and, therefore, his claim must be excluded from the application of the IRPA. He is requesting that the panel conclude that the exclusion under Article 1E of the Convention on the protection of refugees applies to the claimant, that it exclude his claim from the application of the Convention and that it determine that he is not "a Convention refugee" or a "person in need of protection." In the notice, the Minister also informed the RPD that his representative would not be attending the hearing. [25] The hearing regarding the claimant's refugee protection claim was initially to be held on April 3, 2014. However, the claimant then requested a postponement of his hearing because he was refused legal aid and no longer had a lawyer as a result. He then informed the panel that he had appealed against this decision and that he needed a postponement pending the result of this appeal. The hearing was therefore postponed to May 5, 2014. [26] On April 29, 2014, the claimant sent the RPD an initial amended BOC Form. [27] On April 30, 2014, Nicolas Ranger appeared as the claimant's new lawyer and requested another postponement of the hearing. That request was granted and the hearing was rescheduled for May 16, 2014. [28] On May 6, 2014, the RPD received additional documents from the Minister in support of his notice of intervention. [29] On May 6, 2014, the RPD also received a second amendment to the claimant's BOC Form. Credibility [30] The claimant was not credible with respect to the essential elements of his claim for refugee protection. The main reasons are as follows. Amendments to the BOC Form [31] From the start, the claimant's initial BOC Form, as received at the IRB on March 10, 2014, contained major anachronisms with respect to the events set out in that same document. The claimant notably stated in the document that he learned the truth about his birth when he was about 45 years old and that he subsequently converted to Judaism. It states as follows: 2. When I was approximately 45 years old, my father told me the truth of my birth. ... 5. I had been a Christian. When I learned to [sic] truth about my heritage I became a practicing Jew. [32] The fact that the claimant was born on XXXX XXXX, 1958, implies that he would have learned the truth about his heritage around 2003-2004, and that he subsequently would have converted to Judaism. However, in the same written account, he states that he had problems because of his Jewish religious beliefs well before 2003. In fact, he alleged that he had problems when he worked for XXXX, in Acapulco, before he went to work in Cancun, in 1991. He also alleged that he had this kind of problem in 1993, 1994 and 2002. [33] In his amendment of April 29, 2014, the claimant notably changed the age at which his father allegedly told him the truth about his heritage. In this first amendment, he rather stated that he was about 33 when he learned the truth about his heritage. In doing the math, this implies that he learned the truth about his heritage more or less between the month of November 1991 and October 1992, that is, when he was 33. Considering that he testified at the hearing that he left to work in Cancun in early 1991, the fact of the matter is that this amendment did not correct all the anachronisms with respect to his Jewish beliefs, as noted above. [34] When asked by the panel why he did not write the correct age from the start, the claimant answered that it was his lawyer's error. [35] In his second amendment, that of May 6, 2014, the claimant notably added an essential event to his claim for refugee protection. He stated that he was witness to a conversation that the former governor of the state of Quintana Roo, XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, had with other people and in which he directly threatened one of his acquaintances. Because Mr. XXXX XXXX was a fugitive at the time, the claimant alleged that he then anonymously called the Office of the Mexican Attorney General to provide information in order to help find him. Mr. XXXX XXXX was allegedly arrested that night and has remained in detention since. The claimant believes that his appeal had an impact on this arrest and that Mr. XXXX XXXX and his accomplices were informed of that. [36] In that same amendment, the claimant added that Mr. XXXX XXXX had ties to his former bosses, which made the latter even more motivated to persecute and/or mistreat him. He also added that he was subsequently kidnapped and threatened by the Zetas and that he suspected that this was due to the problems that he had at work and to the fact that his boss knew that he had informed the authorities about Mr. XXXX XXXX whereabouts. [37] The panel then asked the claimant why this amendment had not been made earlier. He answered, as he had written in his second amendment, that it was because he had not told his previous lawyer about this, fearing that Canada could exchange information with the Mexican government and that the information could become public. He added at the hearing that his previous lawyer had not told him that RPD hearings were held in the absence of the public, contrary to his new lawyer who allegedly did so and assured him that the information would not become public. [38] All these explanations are not satisfactory in the panel's opinion. The claimant did not require the assistance of an interpreter during the preparation of his BOC Form or during the hearing. Throughout the hearing, he showed a very good command of the English language. Therefore, the panel is of the opinion that the claimant was capable of fully understanding the statement that he signed in his BOC Form, found at question 11 of the BOC Form, which reads as follows: I declare that the information I have provided in this form is complete, true and correct. I declare that I am able to read English and that I have fully read and fully understood the entire content of this form and all attached documents. My declaration has the same force and effect as if made under oath. [emphasis added] [39] The amendments made by the claimant are not minor amendments in the eyes of the panel. [40] The date of his conversion to Judaism is a very important date in the claimant's account and constitutes a "basis" of his refugee protection claim. In fact, his written account suggests that all of his problems began after his conversion to Judaism. Further, claiming that there was an error between numbers as different as 33 and 45 is asking a lot of the panel in terms of credulity. [41] As for the addition of the event involving Mr. XXXX XXXX and the fact that he was assaulted by the Zetas in XXXX 2007, the panel again finds that these are major amendments to the claimant's account. These are not amendments that could be considered aesthetic or cosmetic, but, rather, allegations of direct persecution. The fact that the claimant was afraid that the Canadian authorities may transfer this information to the Mexican authorities and/or that his lawyer at the time allegedly failed to inform him that hearings are held in the absence of the public does not warrant the claimant's lengthy delay before making these amendments in his BOC Form. [42] The claimant has been in Canada since XXXX XXXX, 2012. He signed his initial BOC Form on February 25, 2014, but he waited for two postponements of the hearing and a second amendment to finally add these important facts to his account. Considering that the claimant had been in Canada for nearly 18 months; that he was represented by a lawyer when he signed his initial BOC Form; that he is a university-educated person; that he expresses himself very well in the English language; and that the Claimant's Guide is clear to the effect that refugee protection claim hearings are held in the absence of the public, the panel finds that the claimant was in a position to inquire about the procedures and about the confidentiality of the information disclosed before the RPD. Therefore, he should have been able to establish the true account of events in his initial BOC Form. [43] For all these reasons, the panel finds that the claimant's initial amendments to his BOC Form were made opportunistically to correct shortcomings in his account, not to establish the true account of events, as appropriate. [44] The panel is entitled to examine the claimant's BOC Form both before and after the amendments1 and finds that his failure to mention in his initial BOC Form the facts surrounding the denunciation and arrest of Mr. XXXX XXXX, as well as the assault he experienced at the hands of the Zetas in XXXX 2007, are major omissions between the various versions of the claimant's BOC Form. The panel draws a negative inference with respect to the claimant's overall credibility. Return to Mexico [45] During the hearing, the claimant testified that the only way to escape his problems in 2008 was to flee Mexico. However, the fact of the matter is that the claimant did not claim refugee protection once he arrived in Canada in 2008 and that, on the contrary, he returned to Mexico a few weeks later. The panel then asked the claimant why he had returned to Mexico, when he had just stated that the only way to escape these problems was to flee Mexico. He then spontaneously answered that he returned there because his former employers owed him money and that he needed it, in particular to feed his family. [46] Later on during the hearing, the claimant's lawyer also asked him questions about his return to Mexico. This time the claimant somewhat qualified his previous answer by stating that he had come to Canada only to refresh his mind and let things cool down. He stated that he had to return to Mexico to take care of his children. [47] In the panel's view, the claimant was adjusting his testimony depending on the questions put to him. The panel is of the opinion that returning to a country where persecution and a risk to life is alleged is inconsistent with the behaviour that might be reasonably expected of a person in similar circumstances. This is especially so because in his second amendment the claimant alleges that at the time he left Mexico to travel to Canada in 2008, he was also being pursued with respect to his involvement in the arrest of Mr. XXXX XXXX. It is reasonable to expect a person in similar circumstances to claim refugee protection in the first safe country where he arrives. Once again, this undermined the claimant's credibility. Failure to claim upon arrival in Canada and in the United States and delay in claiming [48] The claimant arrived in Canada with a visitor's visa on XXXX XXXX, 2012, and did not claim refugee protection on arrival. At the hearing, he repeated what he had indicated in his written account, namely, that his objective was to sell his house in Mexico and be able to immigrate to Canada as an investor with the proceeds of the sale. [49] However, the claimant's visa expired in XXXX 2013, and he then lost his legal status in Canada.2 According to his written account and his testimony at the hearing, it was only in XXXX 2013 that the claimant attempted to regularize his status. He stated that he travelled by bus to Buffalo, in the United States, with a group of 20 to 25 people from his church, to attend a seminar. He stated that he thought he would obtain a status upon returning to Canada. He stayed in the United States for a few days and returned to Canada, but neither customs officers from the United States or Canada allegedly requested or examined his passport. [50] The panel notes that the claimant did not apply for asylum in the United States, nor upon his return to Canada, despite claiming that he feared mistreatment or persecution if he returned to Mexico at the time. The claimant explained that he had not applied for asylum in the United States because he thought he would obtain a valid status in Canada upon returning and that he did not know that people like him could claim refugee protection. [51] In the panel's view, that is implausible. The fact that U.S. customs allowed a bus with 20 to 25 people to pass without verifying their documents is one matter, but the fact that the documents of these same people were not verified by Canadian customs upon returning from that same trip is another. It is asking a lot of the panel in terms of credulity to believe that such unlikely events occurred in these circumstances. In addition, the fact is that the claimant did not approach the customs officers in an attempt to regularize his status, even though he claimed that he wanted to regularize it. Therefore, the panel does not believe that the claimant attempted to regularize his status by travelling to the United States and returning to Canada in XXXX 2013. [52] The claimant finally submitted his claim for refugee protection on March 5, 2014, more than a year and a half after arriving in Canada and more than a year after his visitor status expired. The claimant explained at the hearing that it was only in February 2014 that he heard about the possibility of filing a refugee protection claim. [53] The panel does not find this explanation to be satisfactory. It has been established that the claimant has a university education,3 is highly proficient in the English language and that he had a group of people to turn to in Canada through the religious groups he met with. In the panel's opinion, not knowing something is not an obstacle to inquiring about it, which the claimant failed to demonstrate he did diligently. Therefore, considering the claimant's specific circumstances, and particularly the fact that he had been without status for over a year before filing his claim for refugee protection, the panel finds the claimant's behaviour inconsistent with that which could be reasonably expected of a person that fears being persecuted or killed should he return to his country of origin. Once again, the panel draws a negative inference regarding the claimant's credibility. [54] Considering all of the above, the panel does not believe any part of the claimant's account of persecution, torture, threats and mistreatment. [55] With respect to documents C-30 and C-32 to C-41, filed in evidence by the claimant, the panel can believe that the claimant has a home in Mexico, that he had a problem with the electricity company, that he worked for the companies he alleged and had legal troubles with them, but this does not demonstrate or corroborate that these problems amount to persecution, that they are related to his Jewish faith or that they put him at risk of mistreatment. [56] The panel also finds that even if the police report filed as Exhibit C-31 were real, it does not establish that the alleged assault is related to the jobs held by the claimant or the fact that he is Jewish. The panel is of the opinion that if this assault actually occurred, it was because the claimant was at the wrong place at the wrong time, that this risk is not different from that faced by other Mexican citizens, and that this makes it a generalized risk. [57] With respect to the photographs submitted by the claimant in Exhibit C-38, considering the claimant's non-credible testimony, the panel is of the opinion that they in no way show the cause or time of the injury. Risk for Jewish people in Mexico [58] The panel analyzed whether there was a serious possibility that the claimant would be persecuted merely because of his Jewish faith, were he to return to Mexico. [59] Considering the objective evidence on file4 establishing relevant incidents and anti-Semite demonstrations, the panel finds-although it abhors these types of attitudes and demonstrations-that they do not constitute persecution under the Convention, but rather harassment and discrimination. [60] Therefore, the panel is of the opinion that there is no serious possibility that the claimant would face persecution merely because of his Jewish faith, were he to return to Mexico. Exclusion and possibility of Israeli and/or Polish citizenship [61] With respect to the Minister's application, the panel is of the opinion that the exclusion set out in Article 1E of the Convention on the protection of refugees does not apply to the claimant because he never [translation] "established his residence" in Israel under the terms of this article. [62] Lastly, at the hearing, the claimant was asked whether he could obtain Israeli and/or Polish citizenship, particularly since he alleges he was born in Israel and that his mother was Jewish, Israeli and Polish. [63] However, as explained above, the panel does not believe the claimant's account of persecution and/or risk in Mexico. In these circumstances, the panel is of the opinion that it is not necessary to consider the claimant's possible Israeli and/or Polish citizenship. CONCLUSION [64] Having analyzed all of the evidence, the panel concludes that the claimant has not discharged his burden of establishing that there is a serious possibility that he would be persecuted on a Convention ground. [65] He also failed to establish, on a balance of probabilities, should he return to Mexico, that he would be personally subjected to a danger of torture, to a risk to his life, or to a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. [66] For all these reasons, the panel determines that the claimant is not a "Convention refugee" or a "person in need of protection" and rejects his claim for refugee protection.
