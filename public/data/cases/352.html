Dixon Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia February 16, 2017 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, British Columbia February 16, 2017 Summary: The appellants, Dr. Sahyoun and his wife, and Dr. Sahyoun appearing as litigation guardian for his son Antonios, appeal an order that dismissed the underlying action and dismissed an application by Dr. Sahyoun to revisit an earlier unsuccessful application he made to have a state-funded lawyer appointed to act for Antonios. The underlying action advanced multiple claims against 49 defendants relating to events that allegedly occurred over 20 years ago when Antonios was a young child. Antonious is now 30 and is a person with a legal disability. The action had been case managed for over seven years with little progress achieved. The order under appeal dismissed Antonios' claims for want of prosecution and dismissed the Sahyouns' claims as disclosing no reasonable cause of action, being unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious, and otherwise an abuse of process. One week before the scheduled hearing date of his appeal, Dr. Sahyoun unsuccessfully applied before a chambers judge of this Court to have a new litigation guardian and state-funded lawyer appointed to act on behalf of Antonios but was given liberty to renew his application before the division hearing the appeal. On the day of the appeal, Dr. Sahyoun requested an adjournment so that he could prepare for a s. 9(6) review of his application a week earlier. He also conceded that his and his wife's claims would fall if the dismissal of Antonios' claims was upheld. The Court dismissed his application to adjourn the appeal, but granted him liberty to re-argue his application for the appointment of state-funded counsel for Antonios, without the necessity of having to meet the more onerous legal test under s. 9(6) of the Court of Appeal Act. Dr. Sahyoun refused to proceed as directed, withdrew as Antonios' litigation guardian and left the courtroom. Held: Appeal dismissed as abandoned. [1] D. SMITH J.A.: The appellants, Dr. Sahyoun and his wife, Mrs. Sahyoun (the "Sahyouns") are the parents of Antonios Sahyoun ("Antonios"). Antonios was born in 1987. He is now 30 years old and is a person with a legal disability. In December 2007, he was found to be incapable of managing his affairs. His father, Dr. Sahyoun, was appointed his committee. Dr. Sahyoun was also Antonios' litigation guardian in the underlying action. [2] The appeal is from an order made by Mr. Justice Voith on March 12, 2015 (the "Order"). Mr. Justice Voith was appointed the case management judge for the action in December 2009. [3] The action was commenced in January 31, 2008. The Sahyouns and Dr. Sahyoun, as Antonios' litigation guardian, advanced multiple claims against 49 defendants. In an attempt to bring some structure to the claims, Voith J. categorized the defendants as follows: (a) ten different physicians (the "Defendant Physicians"); (b) a collection of individuals, health-care facilities and authorities, the Vancouver Board of Education (formerly the Vancouver School Board) and the Gateway Society: Services for Persons with Autism (the "Health and School Defendants"); (c) the University of British Columbia ("UBC"); (d) various individuals employed by the Province of British Columbia and the Province of British Columbia (the "Provincial Defendants"); (e) six police officers and the City of Vancouver (the "City and Police Defendants"); and (f) David Duncan and the B.C. Legal Services Society (the "LSS Defendants"). [4] At the heart of the action is the Sahyouns' belief that when Antonios was a child he was misdiagnosed with autism when he actually suffered from prelingual hearing loss due to a serious ear infection that went untreated. They allege that this misdiagnosis caused improper and delayed treatment and services for Antonios, his inability to acquire language skills, exposure to illnesses that caused him to suffer brain damage, and ultimately caused the apprehension of Antonios and his two siblings. (In the action, Dr. Sahyoun also advanced claims on behalf of his two other children but when they reached the age of majority they each signed a consent dismissal of those claims.) [5] Many of the claims involve events that occurred over 20 years ago. Broadly categorized (as best as I am able to understand), they include allegations of professional malpractice, negligence, perjury, fabricating evidence, refusing to provide legal assistance, and breaches of common law and statutory duties, including Charter and Criminal Code breaches. [6] The Order granted the respondents' application: (1) to dismiss Antonios' claims for want of prosecution, pursuant to R. 22-7(7) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009 (SCCR); (2) to dismiss the Sahyouns' claims, which the Sahyouns characterized as "derivative" from Antonios' claims, in that, what has affected him has affected them, for disclosing no reasonable cause of action and/or unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious and/or otherwise an abuse of process, pursuant to R. 9-5(1) of the SCCR; and (3) ordered the Sahyouns to pay lump sum costs of $1,000 to each of the various groups of defendants in the action (except the LSS defendants who did not ask for costs). Mr. Justice Voith also dismissed Dr. Sahyoun's cross-application to have a state-funded counsel appointed to act for Antonios, noting that the appellants had made the same application four years earlier, which he had dismissed for reasons I shall review below and from which no appeal was taken. II. Background [7] The appellants have been self-represented throughout this litigation. [8] On March 11, 2011, the Defendant Physicians applied for directions on whether Dr. Sahyoun, as Antonios' litigation guardian, could represent Antonios in the action. On May 2, 2011, Voith J. concluded that he could not as to do so contravened R. 20-2(4) of the SCCR. That provision requires a lawyer to act for a litigation guardian of a person under a legal disability. The judge issued a stay of proceedings until such time as the Sahyouns were able to retain counsel to act for Antonios (the "Stay Order"). See Sahyoun v. Ho, 2011 BCSC 567. No appeal was taken from the Stay Order. [9] In his reasons for granting the Stay Order, Voith J. also dismissed Dr. Sahyoun's cross-application for interim or advance costs to retain a state-funded lawyer to act on behalf of Antonios. Dr. Sahyoun advised the judge that he could not find a lawyer who would act on behalf of Antonios. He also refused to have the Public Guardian and Trustee (the "PGT"). The PGT confirmed with the judge that in those circumstances it would not be able to act for Antonios and in any event it was of the view that the action lacked merit and was not in Antonios' best interests. [10] Mr. Justice Voith found the appellants had not met the legal test for granting an order for interim or advance costs as set out in British Columbia (Minister of Forests) v. Okanagan Indian Band, 2003 SCC 71, and Little Sisters Book and Art Emporium v. Canada (Commissioner of Customs and Revenue), 2007 SCC 2. In particular, he found that the issues raised in the action did not transcend the interests of the parties, the legal issues were well established and the public interest was not engaged in any meaningful way. He also held that he could not compel the PGT to act for Antonios in these circumstances, stating: [20] Dr. Sahyoun and his wife are adamant that they are not willing to have the Public Guardian and Trustee act as litigation guardian. They say that it is only they, or more specifically, Dr. Sahyoun, who properly understands the history of this matter and who can properly advance the interests of Antonios. Counsel for the Public Guardian and Trustee appeared at the hearing before me and confirmed that in the present circumstances the Public Guardian was unable to act. [11] The next developments in the litigation concerned the appellants' pleadings. The initial Writ of Summons and Statement of Claim were filed in 2008. At the time the former Supreme Court Rules, B.C. Reg. 221/90 governed the pleadings. On February 7, 2008, the appellants amended the Writ of Summons and Statement of Claim. On April 14, 2009, they filed a second amended Writ of Summons and Statement of Claim. Neither set of amended pleadings complied with the Supreme Court Rules. [12] In 2010 the SCCR replaced the Supreme Court Rules. Thereafter the respondents requested the appellants to amend their pleadings so that they complied with the SCCR. In response, on April 2, 2012, the appellants filed a Notice of Civil Claim. The new pleading was 49 pages in length. It did not comply with the SCCR. [13] On April 25 and 26, 2013, the Defendant Physicians, the Health and School Defendants, UBC and the Provincial Defendants, applied for an order that the appellants amend their amended Notice of Civil Claim to comply with the SCCR. [14] On June 26, 2013, Voith J. ordered the appellants to file within 60 days an amended Notice of Civil Claim that complied with the SCCR (the "Pleadings Order"). In his reasons for judgment, the judge identified a number of "deficiencies" with the appellants' pleading, which he described as "severely deficient', "extremely prolix", written like a "running narrative", failing "to identify which plaintiff asserts what cause of action against which defendant", failing to plead conduct that would ground a cause of action, and generally incomprehensible. See Sahyoun v. Ho, 2013 BCSC 1143. [15] On August 26, 2013, the appellants filed an amended Notice of Civil Claim. Again, it did not comply with the SCCR. This time the pleading had increased to 86 pages. [16] Between June and November 2014, all the respondents collectively filed applications that gave rise to the Order under appeal. III. THE CHAMBERS JUDGMENT [17] Mr. Justice Voith provided lengthy and comprehensive reasons for lifting the Stay Order, dismissing the underlying action, and dismissing Dr. Sahyoun's application to revisit his earlier application to have a state-funded lawyer appointed to act for Antonios. See Sahyoun v. Ho, 2015 BCSC 392. [18] The respondents had applied to dismiss Antonios' claims for want of prosecution pursuant to R. 22-7(7). Mr. Justice Voith set out the requisite criteria for meeting the legal test under R. 22-7(7), as outlined in Irving, v. Irving, (1982), 38 B.C.L.R. 318, and Busse v. Robinson, Morelli, Chertkow, 1999 BCCA 313: (1) inordinate delay; (2) inexcusable delay; (3) serious prejudice to the defendants, a rebuttable presumption if the first two elements are met that then shifts the burden to the plaintiffs to establish an absence of prejudice on a balance of probabilities; and (4) the broader consideration of whether, on balance, justice requires the dismissal of the action. The judge reviewed the evidence and made findings of fact that established each of the criteria for the legal test. [19] The judge also considered Antonios' special circumstances: that he was a legally disabled adult and that his father acting on his behalf in the action did not comply with R. 20-2. He noted the dismissal of Dr. Sahyoun's earlier application to obtain state-funded counsel for Antonios in the Stay Order. He restated the test for advance costs against the Crown and repeated his reasons for why Dr. Sahyoun's application did not satisfy that test. He also noted that the action had been stayed since 2011 for Dr. Sahyoun to retain counsel to act on Antonios' behalf and that the Sahyouns' opposed the appointment of the PGT as counsel for Antonios. He considered the PGT's unwillingness to act for Antonios in these circumstances, which included an assessment that the claims lacked merit and that pursing them would not be in Antonios' best interests, and noted that the PGT could not be compelled to do so. After carefully weighing all of these competing factors, the judge concluded that Antonios' special circumstances were unlikely to change in the future. Therefore, any further adjournment of the proceedings would serve no useful purpose as the dismissal of the appellants' claims was inevitable and the ongoing delay was prejudicial to the respondents. [20] In the result, the judge held that the test for dismissing Antonios' claims for want of prosecution was satisfied and that the interests of justice, in these circumstances, required that they be dismissed. He stated: [55] The foregoing assessment engages the numerous considerations that I have identified. It recognizes that one imperative of R. 1-3(1) [that the object of the SCCR is to secure the just, speedy and inexpensive determination of every proceeding on its merits] is that claims be advanced, if not always with despatch, at least in some reasonable way. The progress of an action cannot be frozen indefinitely when there is no prospect for any change in that status. Furthermore, all such assessments require that the interests of all parties be fairly addressed and weighed. I am satisfied, having regard to the assessment that I have described that, on balance, justice does require that Antonios' action be dismissed. [21] The judge then considered the respondents' application to dismiss the Sahyouns' claims pursuant to R. 9-5(1). He set out the correct legal tests to be met under each of the subsections (a), (b) (c) and (d) including Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959 for R. 9-5(1)(a); the summary of the jurisprudence in Willow v. Chong, 2013 1083 for R. 9-5(1)(b); Keddie v. Dumas Hotels Ltd. (1985), 62 B.C.L.R. 145 for R. 9-5(1)(c); and Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E., Local 79, 2003 SCC 63 for R. 9-5(1)(d). He relied on the deficiencies in the pleadings that he had identified and addressed in his reasons for the Pleadings Order (2013 BCSC 1143), which he had reviewed in order to provide some guidance for the Sahyouns. He concluded that the appellants' August 26, 2013 amended Notice of Civil Claim had still not rectified those deficiencies and continued to offend Rule 9-5(1), stating: [64] In this case, the Amended Pleading is so overwhelmed with difficulty that it is simply not possible to fully identify all of the specific inadequacies that exist, or to categorize those difficulties into the specific subparagraphs of R. 9-5(1). It is also not possible to deal with the deficiencies in the Amended Pleading that relate to individual defendants, as often multiple causes of action are advanced against a single defendant. I am, however, satisfied that most of the Amended Pleading does not comply with R. 9-5(1), and that very significant portions of the Amended Pleading offend one or more of the subparagraphs in the rule. [22] Finding that it was impossible to delineate every flaw in each of the claims as it related to each defendant, the judge instead identified patterns of deficiencies. Those included: (1) the failure to be clear and concise in pleadings that are "impenetrable and often incomprehensible"; (2) the inability to identify which plaintiff advances what cause of action against which defendant; (3) pleading evidence in a "running narrative" rather than material facts, contrary to R. 3-7(1); and (4) the failure to advance recognized causes of action against many defendants, specifically in circumstances where (a) there is no duty owed, (b) there is no recognized cause of action, (c) the claim is an impermissible collateral attack, and (d) the defendants are protected by statutory or other immunities. The judge also noted that many or possibly all of the claims were statutorily barred by the expiry of the applicable limitation period. In the result, he concluded the amended pleading should be struck for the following reasons: [144] Striking a pleading under R. 9-5(1) is a draconian remedy that is only granted in egregious circumstances. With that said, it plays a central role in ensuring that parties comply with the Rules, and it provides the court with a necessary mechanism to ensure such compliance. Absent access to R. 9-5(1), the court's ability to control the content and form of pleadings is diminished, the broader objects of the Rules such as efficiency, timeliness, and cost-effectiveness are undermined, and considerable unfairness may be imposed on opposing litigants. [145] In this case, all of these considerations are engaged. This action was commenced more than six years ago. The Amended Pleading [the August 26, 2013 pleading] is the fifth pleading that Dr. and Mrs. Sahyoun have drafted and delivered to the defendants. The earlier hearing that gave rise to the Pleading Reasons, together with this hearing, has occupied seven days of court time. Dr. and Mrs. Sahyoun have been provided with as much guidance, through the correspondence and submissions of counsel, and through the Pleadings Reasons, as is possible. In the Pleading Reasons, I declined to strike a pleading that was then before me and decided, instead, to provide Dr. and Mrs. Sahyoun with a further opportunity to rectify the numerous deficiencies that existed in that earlier pleading. [146] Such efforts and such opportunities have effectively been wasted. The Amended Pleading remains a thoroughly unworkable and deficient document. It continues to offend each subparagraph of R. 9-5(1). This is so in the many and varied ways that I have described. [147] Furthermore, I consider that the defendants have lived under a cloud of allegations, related to distant wrongs, that in many cases could scarcely be more severe in nature, for long enough. [148] I am satisfied that providing Dr. and Mrs. Sahyoun with a further opportunity to address the inadequacies of the Amended Pleading will not yield a different result. I am also satisfied there are no lesser measures available to me that would properly balance the various interests that I have described. [23] Lastly, the judge considered the respondents' application for lump-sum costs. The LSS defendant did not seek any order for costs and UBC requested party-and-party costs. After considering the Sahyouns' indigent status (now no fees status), their limited financial means, the principled basis for making a "hollow" costs award (citing Keremelevski v. V.W.R. Capital Corporation, 2013 BCSC 612 at para, 71, aff'd [2013] B.C.J. No. 3055 (C.A.)); and, the purposes of costs orders generally (citing Giles v. Westminster Savings and Credit Union, 2010 BCCA 282 at paras. 74 and 94; and, Hartshorne v. Hartshorne, 2011 BCCA 29 at para. 5), the judge concluded it was appropriate to grant lump sum costs against the Sahyouns in the amount of $1,000 for each group of defendants, stating: [167] The cost orders that most of the defendants seek aligns with these twin objectives. A lump sum cost award of $1,000, payable to each group of defendants, is appropriate. It does not begin to recognize the expenses they have incurred. It does attach some appropriate consequences to Dr. and Mrs. Sahyoun's conduct within the legal framework and considerations that I have described. [168] I do not consider that the usual cost order that UBC seeks would be appropriate for the reasons I have described. [24] The Sahyouns appealed the Order and applied in this court for indigent status. Mr. Justice Goepel, sitting in Chambers, dismissed the Sahyouns' application concluding that with respect to Antonios' claims, Voith J. had found that Dr. Sahyoun could not represent Antonios as he was not a lawyer, Dr. Sahyoun had not appealed that order, and therefore Dr. Sahyoun had no status to bring the appeal on behalf of Antonios. Mr. Justice Goepel also dismissed the Sahyouns' application for no-fee status in their appeal of the dismissal of their claims because in his view the "impenetrable and often incomprehensible" pleadings were bound to fail. See Sahyoun v. Ho, 2015 BCCA 235. [25] The appeal was scheduled for hearing on February 16-17, 2017. [26] On January 10, 2017, Dr. Sahyoun applied in this Court to have a new litigation guardian and state-funded counsel appointed. Mr. Justice Savage, in Chambers, dismissed his application but granted him with liberty to renew his application before the division of the Court hearing the appeal. Instead, Dr. Sahyoun applied to vary or discharge that order pursuant to s. 9(6) of the Court of Appeal Rules. IV. ON APPEAL [27] On February 14, 2017, Dr. Sahyoun applied to adjourn the appeal. Mr. Justice Donald, in Chambers, heard the application. Dr. Sahyoun advised Donald J.A. that unless his application was granted he would resign as Antonios' litigation guardian on the first day of the appeal hearing. Mr. Justice Donald dismissed the application. On February 16, 2017, the first day of the appeal, Dr. Sahyoun withdrew as Antonios' litigation guardian. [28] Before the division, he also applied again to adjourn the appeal so that that he could prepare for his s. 9(6) review of Savage J.A.'s January 10, 2017 order. We advised Dr. Sahyoun that we were granting him liberty pursuant to that order to re-argue his application for the appointment of state-funded counsel without the need of having to meet the more onerous test under s. 9(6). We advised him that we would proceed with that application first as Antonios' appeal could only proceed if there was counsel to act on his behalf. Dr. Sahyoun insisted that it was his "right" to pursue the s. 9(6) review and to have 30 days to prepare his Book of Authorities, which he did not have with him, for that application. We offered to provide him with copies of the cases that he intended to rely, and to give him some time to review them before hearing the application that day, but he refused. He further advised that if his application was dismissed, he and his wife would not pursue their appeal of the order dismissing their claims as they were intricately connected to Antonios' claims. [29] We saw no merit in an adjournment of Dr. Sahyoun's application as, a month earlier, he had been granted leave to re-apply for state-funded counsel before this division, which gave him a month to prepare for that application, and we were ready to proceed. At that point, the Sahyouns withdrew from the appeal and left the courtroom. [30] Counsel for the respondents were present at the hearing and ready to proceed with the appeal. They took no position with respect to Dr. Sahyoun's applications. [31] In my view, Voith J. thoroughly canvassed any prejudice to Antonios, as a legally disabled adult, if the action was dismissed (as outlined in para. 22 above). He concluded that it was unlikely the circumstances that had resulted in the inordinate and inexcusable delay of the action would change. The Sahyouns could not retain counsel to act for Antonios, they opposed the appointment of the PGT and, in any event, the PGT was of the view the action lacked merit, was not in Antonios' best interests, and could not be compelled to act. [32] The Sahyouns have failed to comply with the Court's rules governing the appeal process by refusing to participate in the hearing of the appeal that has been scheduled for some time. Dr. Sahyoun refused to make submissions on a renewed application for state-funded counsel to act on behalf of Antonios, although he was granted leave to do so by the division. In a final attempt to delay this matter, Dr. Sahyoun withdrew as Antonios' litigation guardian and abandoned his appeal. [33] In all of these circumstances, and pursuant to R. 28 (a) of the Court of Appeal Rules, I would dismiss the appeal as abandoned. [34] FENLON J.A.: I agree. [35] DICKSON J.A.: I agree. [36] D. SMITH J.A.: The appeal is dismissed as abandoned. "The Honourable Madam Justice Smith"
