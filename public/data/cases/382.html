Bobkin Tribunal Counsel for the persons who are the subject of the appeal Bahar Karbakhsh-Ravari Conseil des personnes en cause Designated representative XXXX XXXX ReprÚsentant(e) dÚsignÚ(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] I dismiss the appeal brought by XXXX XXXX (the Principal Appellant), XXXX XXXX XXXX (the Associate Appellant), and XXXX XXXX (the Minor Appellant.) The Appellants are citizens of Iran, and the Principal Appellant and Minor Appellant are also citizens of Greece. [2] The adult Appellants were born in Iran and are of the Turkish-Azari ethnic group. They allege that they face persecution there on the basis of their ethnicity. The Principal Appellant fled Iran for Turkey and then moved to Greece in 2001. He obtained citizenship in 2017. In 2012, the adult Appellants married, and in 2014, the Associate Appellant moved to Greece where she obtained a residence permit. The Minor Appellant was born in Greece in 2015 and is a citizen of Greece. The Appellants allege that they experienced discrimination in Greece including difficulty renting housing, anti-foreigner sentiment from Greeks and discrimination from schoolchildren at the Minor Appellant's school who refused to play with the Minor Appellant. The Appellants came to Canada in XXXX 2018 and alleged that, while here, they decided to stay and make refugee claims. [3] The Refugee Protection Division (RPD) held that the Appellants were generally credible but that they had not established a claim for protection. The RPD determined that the Associate Appellant was excluded from protection under article 1E of the refugee Convention based upon her status in Greece. The RPD also held that the Appellants had not established an objectively well-founded risk in Greece and that they had not rebutted the presumption of state protection. [4] The Appellants argue that the RPD erred. They argue that they do not have state protection in Greece because the state brutalizes non-Greek people. They argue that they do face a risk of persecution in Greece and that they cannot re-enter Greece and therefore will have to return to Iran where they face a risk. They also argue that they face a risk due to the COVID-19 pandemic. The Appellants have submitted new evidence on appeal but have not requested a hearing. [5] I find that the determinative issue is the Appellants' status in Greece and whether they face a risk there. I find that the RPD correctly determined that the Appellants have status in Greece and that they have not established a claim for protection against Greece. DECISION [6] I dismiss the appeal. The RPD was correct in finding that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. NEW EVIDENCE [7] According to the law,1 I can only accept evidence that: (i) arose after the RPD's decision; or (ii) was not reasonably available at the time of the decision; or (iii) that the Appellants could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to bring to the RPD before the decision. [8] If the evidence meets one or more of these requirements, I must decide if the evidence is new, credible and relevant before I can accept it.2 The Appellant submitted twelve news articles as new evidence. I will address them as follows: (a) News about Greece: Four news articles about the treatment of immigrants in Greece (two post-date the RPD's rejection, and two pre-date the RPD's rejection) and one news article about COVID-19 in Greece; and (b) News about Iran: Six news articles about the current situation in Iran and one news article about COVID-19 in Iran. [9] I find that the two news articles in category (a) which post-date the rejection of the Appellants' claim and the one article in category (a) about COVID-19 in Greece are admissible. I find that the two news articles in category (a) which pre-date the rejection of the claim are inadmissible. I find that none of the news articles in category (b) are admissible. I find that the test for an oral hearing is not met. News about Greece [10] The Appellants submitted five news articles on Greece. Four relate to migrants and Islamophobia in Greece, and one relates to the COVID-19 pandemic in Greece. Of these five articles, three post-date the rejection of the claim, and two pre-date the rejection of the claim. I find that the three articles which post-date the rejection of the claim by the RPD are admissible, and the two articles that pre-date the rejection of the claim are inadmissible. [11] Counsel argues that: . . . most of the articles we are presenting now are new in the sense that they post-date the hearing. The ones that do not post-date the hearing, though available at the time of the hearing, were not produced because the prior representative did not advise the claimants to collect evidence to that effect and did not provide any such evidence herself.3 [12] I do not agree with this argument. The two articles which pre-date the rejection of the claim relate to the treatment of Greek police of marginalized people4 and Islamaphobia in Greece.5 Previous Counsel submitted evidence about racism and police mistreatment to the RPD,6 and, therefore, I do not accept that she did not advise the Appellants to collect such evidence or provide such evidence. Moreover, the Appellants have not alleged that prior Counsel was negligent, and, therefore, they have not established that prior Counsel failed to advise them about their obligation to provide corroborating documents. Therefore, the two articles which pre-date the rejection of the claim are not evidence which the Appellants could not reasonably have been expected to provide prior to the rejection of their claims. The Appellants concede that these articles were reasonably available to them and do not post-date the rejection of the claim. For these reasons, the two articles are inadmissible under subsection 110(4) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). [13] The three articles about Greece which post-date the rejection of the claim are admissible. These articles relate to issues at Greece's borders,7 treatment of migrants by police,8 as well as closures of Greek schools due to COVID-19.9 All of these articles post-date the rejection of the claim by the RPD. They, therefore, meet the requirements of subsection 110(4) of the IRPA and are also new under the case law. The articles come from objective third-party sources and are credible for admission. They relate to country conditions in Greece and are broadly relevant to the appeal. Therefore, they are admissible. [14] The articles are general country conditions evidence and do not raise any credibility concerns with regard to the Appellants. Therefore, they do not meet the test for an oral hearing. News about Iran [15] The determinative issues in this appeal all relate to Greece. The articles about Iran are not relevant to the appeal. They are not capable of proving or disproving any fact relevant to the claim for protection, as I am only determining the claims in relation to Greece. Therefore, they do not meet the test for relevance required under the case law. Even if some of the articles meet subsection 110(4) of the IRPA, they do not meet the requirements of the case law and, therefore, are inadmissible. THE ROLE OF THE REFUGEE APPEAL DIVISION (RAD) [16] My role is to look at all the evidence and decide if the RPD made the correct decision.10 There is an exception to this, where the RAD defers to the RPD if the RPD had a meaningful advantage in assessing the credibility of oral evidence. I do not find this case falls into the exception. Therefore, I will be looking at whether the RPD's decision was correct. ANALYSIS [17] The Appellants' arguments are not clearly set out in their memorandum. However, after a close review, I understand them as follows: (a) The RPD erred in finding the Appellants had access to state protection because they are at risk from the state; (b) The Appellants face a risk of persecution in Greece; (c) The Appellants may not be able to re-enter Greece; and (d) The Appellants are at risk in Iran. [18] After reviewing the record, I find the RPD's decision is correct. I find that the Appellants have not established that they cannot re-enter Greece, on a balance of probabilities. I find that they have not established that they face a serious possibility of persecution or a section 97 risk, on a balance of probabilities, in Greece. I do not find it necessary to assess state protection, as this finding was made in the alternative by the RPD. I will address the Appellants' submissions on this point only as they relate to an allegation of risk to the Appellants in Greece. Additionally, I will not address the Appellants' arguments that they face a risk in Iran because this is not determinative, as I find that they have not established that they cannot re-enter Greece, on a balance of probabilities, and as I find they have not established a claim against Greece. The Appellants have not established that they cannot re-enter Greece, on a balance of probabilities [19] The RPD found that the Associate Appellant was excluded from protection under article 1E of the refugee Convention because it concluded that the resident status, which the Associate Appellant had, entitled her to rights which are substantially similar to that of a Greek national including access to health care, education, employment and mobility.11 The RPD also concluded that the Associate Appellant had not established, on a balance of probabilities, that she could not re-enter Greece. In the alternative, the RPD also found that the Associate Appellant failed to acquire status that she was entitled to in Greece. [20] The Appellants have not clearly challenged the finding that the Associate Appellant is excluded. However, they argue that: . . . it is not clear that the claimants would be allowed to re-enter Greece. As the claimants testified, since they have been outside of Greece for more than a year now, it is unclear whether the Greek authorities will allow them back into Greece. It is especially unclear what will come of the female claimant's ability to re-enter Greece as she was not a citizen of that country when she left.12 [21] The Appellants make this statement in relation to a request that the RAD assess their claim against Iran. [22] With regard to the Principal Appellant and Minor Appellant who are citizens of Greece, the Principal Appellant's Basis of Claim (BOC) states that: "Greece has precedent Article 19 of the Greek Citizenship Code, which allowed the government to revoke the citizenship of non-ethnic Greeks who left the country."13 I reviewed the Greek Citizenship Code. Article 19 discusses the loss of Greek citizenship for children of foreign nationals who acquired it by declaration or naturalization of their parents.14 The Article talks about renunciation of citizenship by the children of foreign nationals who became Greek citizens before the age of majority. This does not apply to the Principal Appellant who became a Greek citizen as an adult. If it applies to the Minor Appellant, it does not indicate that citizenship is lost but, rather, that citizenship can be renounced in certain circumstances.15 The Appellants with Greek citizenship have not provided any credible evidence that their citizenship has been or would be revoked, on a balance of probabilities. [23] With regard to the Associate Appellant, the RPD considered whether her status entitled her to the right of re-entry which is part of a 1E analysis. The RPD concluded that the Appellant had the status of "resident" as opposed to "permanent resident" but that this status had the rights and obligations which were substantially similar to that of a national of Greece. In considering the issue of re-entry, the RPD noted that the Associate Appellant had travelled between Greece and Iran while on her resident status. The RPD also noted that there was some evidence that this status would not be affected by temporary absences that do not exceed six months per year. However, the RPD noted that the National Documentation Package (NDP) evidence did not establish that status would "necessarily be lost automatically after six months" and that circumstances in which the six-month absence would be extended included birth of a child, which applied to the Associate Appellant who gave birth in Canada.16 Therefore, the RPD rejected the Appellants' allegation that the Associate Appellant's status would be revoked or invalid. [24] The Appellants have not provided any evidence to show the RPD erred in this finding but have merely asserted that it is "unclear" what would happen to her. I find this argument speculative. The Federal Court has noted that, once a prima facie case of exclusion is raised, the burden shifts to the claimant to "establish they do not have such status in the third country."17 The Appellants cannot merely assert that it is "unclear" but must actually discharge their burden. [25] I have reviewed the legislation governing the Associate Appellant's status. The legislation explicitly states that: "Third-country nationals legally residing in Greece who temporarily depart from Greek territory shall be entitled to reenter, provided that their residence permits are valid at the time of reentry."18 The Associate Appellant's resident permit is valid until XXXX 2023.19 With regard to temporary absences, the legislation notes: "[t]he validity of the Residence Card and the continuous residence shall not be affected by temporary absences that do not exceed six months per year . . . or by absence of up to twelve consecutive months for serious reasons, mainly pregnancy and motherhood, . . ."20 [26] As noted by the RPD, absence due to pregnancy and motherhood is explicitly considered, and there is no statement that loss of status automatically occurs after the six or twelve months have passed. At the date of the hearing, which is the relevant date,21 the Associate Appellant had been out of Greece for approximately 13 months. The Appellants have not provided evidence which establishes, on a balance of probabilities, that she would not be permitted to re-enter Greece on her valid Residence Card. Moreover, the Greek legislation states that, as the spouse of a Greek citizen, the Associate Appellant is entitled to a residence card subject to public policy or national security concerns so long as she has a valid passport and a familial relationship with a Greek citizen.22 As of the date of the hearing, the Associate Appellant met these requirements. [27] In a similar case, where there was some evidence that absence from the 1E country for a period of time might lead to loss of status, the Federal Court held that the applicants had to provide evidence to meet their burden to demonstrate loss of status.23 The Federal Court held that the Board did not err in concluding that, where evidence did not show that loss of status was automatic, the applicants had not discharged their burden.24 I find the Appellants have similarly failed to discharge their burden here. Looking at the evidence on the whole, I find that given the Associate Appellant's residence card remains valid and she had a child during her absence from Greece, it is more likely than not that she could re-enter Greece. In any event, she has not established, on a balance of probabilities, that she could not re-enter Greece. [28] This is the only error the Appellants have alleged with regard to the 1E assessment. I do not find it persuasive. I will not address the RPD's reasons further. Reviewing the record, while I find the RPD's reasons could have been clearer, I concur with the result. I find that the RPD correctly concluded that the Associate Appellant is excluded from protection under article 1E of the refugee Convention because her status as a resident family member of a Greek citizen entitles her to substantially the same rights as a national of Greece. The RPD correctly concluded that the Appellants have not established an objectively well-founded risk in Greece [29] The RPD found that the Appellants had not established an objectively well-founded risk in Greece because the harm they feared amounted to discrimination and not persecution.25 The Appellants argue this was in error. For the following reasons, I concur with the RPD. [30] The RPD considered the Appellants' evidence that they had difficulty finding housing and that the Minor Appellant faced rejection from her peers at school and from children in public who did not want to play with her. The RPD considered that the Appellants felt Greek people preferred those with Greek heritage and blamed immigrants for economic problems. The RPD considered that, despite difficulties, the Appellants secured a home, and the Principal Appellant secured employment. Based upon this, the RPD concluded that the discrimination did not amount to persecution. The Appellants argue that the RPD erred in several ways. I do not find their arguments persuasive. [31] First, the Appellants argue that the RPD erred by comparing the Appellants' rights in Greece to those of people with Greek ancestry as opposed to those of a particular social group of a religious and ethnic minority and migrant group which faces violations of their rights.26 The Appellants have not explained where in the decision the RPD made such an error, which they are obligated to do. Reviewing the record, I do not find such an error occurred. The RPD states that the Appellants allege that they face persecution based upon their status as ethnic minorities.27 The RPD considered the testimony that the Appellants had problems based on being immigrants (as opposed to being of Greek ancestry).28 The Appellants did not testify to any problems based on their religion, and they have not explained this on appeal. Therefore, I do not find this argument has merit. [32] The Appellants also argue that the RPD erred by failing to understand the impact of xenophobic and Islamophobic rhetoric and policy which impacts their safety.29 Again, the Appellants have not explained where in the decision this error was made or what evidence shows this risk. I find it unsubstantiated. [33] The Appellants argue that the RPD did not consider items 13.4 and 13.1 in the NDP.30 I have reviewed these items. I find that they are largely irrelevant, but where they are relevant, the RPD considered them appropriately. The document at item 13.4 of the NDP, included in the Appellants' record, relates specifically to the group called the "Turkish minority of Western Thrace."31 The Appellants were not residing in the area of Western Thrace, which is in the northeast of the country. The Appellants lived in Athens.32 Item 13.1 discusses the popularity of the Golden Dawn party in 2015 in parts of Greece and notes that the party is anti-migrant and anti-Semitic.33 While the RPD did not mention this article, the RPD mentioned the issue and evidence that the government is prosecuting the illegal activities of the Golden Dawn party.34 I note that the Appellants are not from the region which is reported to be the primary area of issues from Golden Dawn (Lesbos and Kos), and I, therefore, find the RPD appropriately considered this issue. [34] The Appellants argue that the RPD erred by failing to consider the evidence of police brutality toward marginalized people and migrants and how this impacts the Appellants despite their status in Greece. I do not find this argument persuasive. [35] The RPD considered the evidence in the NDP and put forward by the Appellants. The RPD found that accounts of mistreatment of migrants in refugee camps or crossing the border into Greece did not apply to the Appellants. I agree. The evidence shows that there have been incidents of violence against migrants crossing the border, and in refugee camps, but this does not necessarily indicate that authorities would be violent toward the Appellants who live in Athens. The Appellants have failed to establish a link between this evidence and their circumstances, on a balance of probabilities. In fact, as noted by the RPD, the evidence showed that the authorities were cracking down on racist violence, including from far-right political groups. 35 [36] The new evidence submitted on appeal is similar to that rejected by the RPD. It discusses the fact that migrants crossing into Greece from Turkey have been met with violence from border guards36 and that there have been reports of Greek police abusing migrants crossing the border including beating them.37 As the Appellants are citizens and residents of Greece, and not migrants, I do not find this evidence is relevant to their situation for the same reasons as given by the RPD and explained above. [37] The evidence the Appellants submitted about police brutality towards marginalized people is inadmissible (as discussed above) and, in any event, is not relevant to the Appellants, as it discusses homeless people, drug users and sex workers, which are not the Appellants' circumstances.38 The Appellants did not provide any evidence that they had faced abuse or violence from any state actors. [38] The Appellants also argue that the RPD erred in assessing their difficulties finding housing by mischaracterizing their evidence and omitting details which support their argument of persecution.39 I disagree. The RPD considered the allegations that the Appellants had faced discrimination in accessing housing. The RPD held that the evidence showed they, ultimately, could find an apartment and never faced homelessness.40 The Appellants argue that the Principal Appellant had to live with "6-7 other people in one house" and the Appellants had to share a three-bedroom house with another family and that the Principal Appellant would be asked about his ethnicity and denied housing despite his status.41 [39] The RPD acknowledged this evidence and agreed that this was discriminatory. I do not dispute this. The RPD similarly considered that the Minor Appellant was discriminated against in school and in parks because children did not want to play with her. The Appellants are re-stating the evidence and their position that this amounts to persecution but have not provided any legal argument to support their belief. [40] The question of whether discrimination amounts to persecution is whether the Appellants face systemic or persistent harm which lead them to facing an insecure existence.42 Given that the Appellants had access to housing, employment, healthcare, education and had not been subject to violence, I find that the Appellants have not met the relevant threshold. I concur with the RPD's conclusion. I find the Appellants have not established a serious possibility of persecution or a section 97 risk, on a balance of probabilities, based on their ethnicity, religion or membership in the particular social group of persons not of Greek ancestry. The Principal Appellant has not established a risk based upon military service [41] The Appellants argue that the Principal Appellant stated in his BOC that he cannot return to Greece because he must serve in the military, and he did not wish to do so based on his bad experience of serving in the military in Iran.43 At the hearing, the Principal Appellant testified that he could not go to the military because he could not leave his wife alone.44 He also testified that he believes there is an exemption from service for individuals who have two children.45 [42] The RPD did not address this allegation in the decision. I do not find this to be a material error, as I have the jurisdiction to consider this on appeal. I do not find the Principal Appellant has established a risk on this basis. [43] First, I would note that not wanting to leave one's family for military service does not establish that there is a risk of persecution based on conscription. The law is clear that mere conscription as a result of a law of general application is not enough to constitute persecution.46 Additionally, there is evidence on the record that the Appellant could serve in reserve duty as opposed to regular duty, which would alleviate the need for prolonged absence from his family.47 [44] Second, the Principal Appellant's past experience in the military in Iran does not establish that he would face discrimination or persecution in the military in Greece. It is his obligation to bring evidence to show that he would face mistreatment in the Greek army, and he has not done so. Reviewing the record, I find insufficient evidence to establish this, on a balance of probabilities. The Principal Appellant's evidence is that the Greek military is "generally regarded as a trustworthy institution", and the article submitted makes reference to gender discrimination but not discrimination based on religion, ethnicity or national origin.48 [45] Finally, I note that the Appellant testified that individuals with two children can be exempt from military service,49 and the Principal Appellant now has two children (as he had a second child in Canada).50 Therefore, according to the Principal Appellant's own testimony, he would now be exempt from serving. [46] For these reasons, I find that the Appellants have not established that the mandatory military service in Greece exposes the Principal Appellant to a serious possibility of persecution or a section 97 risk, on a balance of probabilities. The Appellants have not established a risk due to COVID-19 [47] The Appellants argue that they are "very worried about the situation with regard to the virus [COVID-19] in both countries and are fearful that their life would be in danger in this regard should they be forced to return to Greece or Iran."51 I do not find that the Appellants have established that the situation of COVID-19 in Greece exposes them to a risk under section 96 or 97 of the IRPA. [48] The Appellants have submitted one article explaining that, in March, 2020, Greece shut schools due to local transmission of COVID-19.52 The Appellants have not explained how this poses a risk to them and, notably, schools in Ontario, where the claimants live now, shut down at the same time. The Appellants have not submitted any evidence to establish that they have particular risks due to COVID-19 or that they would be discriminated against in receiving care for COVID-19 in Greece should they require it. They have not submitted any evidence that restrictions in Greece due to COVID-19 would be discriminatory to them or would expose them to any particular risk. [49] For these reasons, I find that the Appellants have not established that the situation of COVID-19 in Greece exposes them to a risk under section 96 or 97 of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [50] For the reasons above, I find that the RPD's decision is correct. The Appellants have faced some discrimination in Greece but have not established that it rises to the level of persecution. They have family in Canada and now have a Canadian-born child. While these factors may be relevant to an application for humanitarian and compassionate consideration, they do not amount to a successful refugee claim. [51] I dismiss the appeal and confirm the decision of the RPD that the Appellants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. (signed) Erin Bobkin E.
