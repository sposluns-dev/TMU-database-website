Consistent with its policy, the City of Coquitlam Council (the "Council") granted that Kingdom Hall, in common with the other places of worship, an exemption from municipal taxation. In the 1990s, the petitioners were forced to find a new site for their Kingdom Hall because of an expropriation by the Province. A new Kingdom Hall was built and occupied by June 2, 2004. [2] However, Council refused to grant an exemption from taxation for the new Kingdom Hall. Coincident with the construction of the new Kingdom Hall Council had adopted a policy that it would grant no new exemptions from taxation. The petitioners made submissions for an exemption but to no avail. In the result, the petitioner's Kingdom Hall is the only place of worship in Coquitlam that is not exempt from taxation. This litigation stems from that result. [3] In this litigation, the petitioners argue that Council's refusal was ultra vires and that Council's decisions infringed the petitioners' freedom of religion and right to equality. In particular the issues to be determined are: (a) what discretion is granted to the City of Coquitlam (the "City") with respect to permissive exemptions in relation to places of worship; (b) did the City's bylaws unlawfully discriminate; (c) did Council provide the Westwood Congregation of Jehovah's Witnesses (the "Congregation") with the appropriate procedural fairness; (d) did Council in considering the Congregation's request for an exemption improperly fetter its discretion; and (e) did Council's refusals to grant permissive exemptions from taxation infringe the Congregation's freedom of religion? (f) did Council's refusals to grant permissive exemptions from taxation infringe the Congregation's right to equality? The Attorney General appeared with respect to the constitutional issues. FACTS [4] The Trustees of the Westwood Congregation of Jehovah's Witnesses (the "Trustees") hold beneficial title on behalf of the members of the Congregation, a federally registered religious charity, to a place of worship known as Kingdom Hall located at 1360 Pipeline Road in Coquitlam, British Columbia. The members of the Congregation, as well as the Burke Mountain, Eagle Ridge, and Mary Hill Congregations (the "Congregations") of Jehovah's Witnesses, use the Pipeline Road Kingdom Hall as a place for prayer, Christian worship, Bible study and guidance in Christian educational work within the community. DISCUSSION What Discretion is Granted to the City With Respect to Permissive Exemptions in Relation to Places of Worship? [41] The Trustees submit that the Annual Taxation Exemption Bylaws at issue are ultra vires because the governing legislation, the Community Charter, S.B.C. 2003 c. 26, does not grant to the City the discretion to determine that there will be no exemptions in the case of places of worship. In the Trustee's submission, the discretion extends only to the determination of how much land will be exempt. The City's position is that the governing legislation grants it a wide discretion to determine both whether there will be any exemption and if so, how much. [42] Exemptions from taxation are provided for in the Community Charter, ss. 220 and 224 as follows: General statutory exemptions 220(1) Unless otherwise provided in this Act or the Local Government Act, the following property is exempt from taxation to the extent indicated: ... (h) a building set apart for public worship, and the land on which the building stands, if title to the land is registered in the name of (i) the religious organization using the building, (ii) trustees for the use of that organization, or (iii) a religious organization granting a lease of the building and land to be used solely for public worship; ... Division 7 -- Permissive Exemptions General authority for permissive exemptions 224(1) A council may, by bylaw in accordance with this section, exempt land or improvements, or both, referred to in subsection (2) ... from taxation under section 197(1)(a) [municipal property taxes], to the extent, for the period and subject to the conditions provided in the bylaw. (2) Tax exemptions may be provided under this section for the following: ... (f) in relation to property that is exempt under section 220(1)(h) [buildings for public worship], (i) an area of land surrounding the exempt building, (ii) a hall that the council considers is necessary to the exempt building and the land on which the hall stands, and (iii) an area of land surrounding a hall that is exempt under subparagraph (ii); (g) land or improvements used or occupied by a religious organization, as tenant or licensee, for the purpose of public worship or for the purposes of a hall that the council considers is necessary to land or improvements so used or occupied; ... [43] The Trustees submit that the bylaws in issue are ultra vires. The standard for review of the vires of a municipal bylaw is correctness; see Nanaimo (City of) v. Rascal Trucking Ltd., [2000] 1 S.C.R. 342, 2000 SCC 13 [Nanaimo]. [44] As noted in Nanaimo, the process of delineating municipal jurisdiction is an exercise in statutory construction. Mr. Justice Major, speaking for the court, described the approach to be taken at para. 20: This conclusion follows recent authorities dictating that statutes be construed purposively in their entire context and in light of the scheme of the Act as a whole with a view to ascertaining the legislature's true intent. See Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes Ltd. (Re), [1998] 1 S.C.R. 27, at paras. 21-23, M & D Farm Ltd. v. Manitoba Agricultural Credit Corp., [1999] 2 S.C.R. 961, at para. 25, and the B.C. Interpretation Act, s. 8. [45] The Trustees submit the intention of the legislature is that places of worship and the land they occupy will be exempt from taxation. Under the legislative scheme, the Trustees submit, the province determines whether the facility is a place of worship. The municipality is then given flexibility to determine how much of the surrounding area will be granted an exemption. The legislature did not intend the municipality's discretion to extend to determining that there will be no exemption. [46] In United Taxi Drivers' Fellowship of Southern Alberta v. Calgary (City), [2004] 1 S.C.R. 485, 2004 SCC 19, Mr. Justice Bastarache, speaking for the court, noted at para. 11: It is well established that the legislature is presumed not to alter the law by implication: Sullivan and Driedger on the Construction of Statutes (4th ed. 2002), at p. 395. Rather, where it intends to depart from prevailing law, the legislature will do so expressly. [47] The Trustees submit that since 1881, places of worship have received exemptions from taxation in this province. The intention of the legislature, unchanged to the present, is as expressed in Bishop of Vancouver Island v. Victoria (City), [1920] 3 W.W.R. 493 at 504, 507 (B.C.C.A.) [Bishop], per McPhillips J.A.: The apparent policy which the language at once indicates is exemption in favour of all buildings used for the public worship of God and of course churches used for the public worship of God come within this terminology... [T]hroughout there has been a plain statutory policy of exemption of all buildings in which the public worship of God takes place. ... The plain intention of the Legislature is that "Every building set apart and in use for the public worship of God" shall be withdrawn from the operation of the Municipal Act, and coming specifically to the consideration of the exemption section and subsections, (R.S.B.C. 1911, ch. 170, sec. 228; 1912, ch. 25, sec. 34; 1914, ch. 52, sec. 197[1], [3]) it will be seen how anomalous the situation becomes if the words "building" and "hospital" do not include the land upon which the structures are built. [48] The legislative purpose for granting the exemption was, as stated by Mr. Justice Martin in Bishop at p. 503, "to encourage, as their statute has it, the public worship of God"". The Trustees submit that there has been, through the successive versions of the legislation, no departure from this intention. [49] It follows, the Trustees submit, that the City, being subordinate to the legislature, cannot use its power to frustrate the purpose of the legislation: see Reference Re Bill 30, an Act to Amend the Education Act (Ont.), [1987] 1 S.C.R. 1148 at 1193-94 [Reference Re Bill 30]. Accordingly, in the case of land that is the subject of the religious worship exemption, the Trustees submit that a municipality can determine how much land will be exempt, but cannot decide that, as the City has done, that there will be no exemption. [50] In support of this proposition, the Trustees rely upon Corporation of the District of Burnaby v. New Vista Society, [1986] B.C.J. No. 2425 (S.C.) [New Vista]. In that case, dealing with a companion exemption, Oppal J. determined that: In my view, in addition to the rules concerning statutory construction, the purpose of s. 398 is significant. Counsel on behalf of Vista makes a convincing argument when he submits that this provision addresses the situation where a home for the elderly is located on a large lot only partially in use for the benefit of the building and its occupants. Because of the difficulties of drafting an exemption formula of general application for lands surrounding the building, the Legislature has delegated this determination of exemption to council. The purpose of s. 398 is not to enable the municipality to impose taxes per se; it is to require the municipality to determine the extent of the exemption in a particular case. This determination is essentially a question of "how much" - it is not a question of "whether or not" there should be any exemption at all for surrounding land. I agree with this construction and interpretation of s. 398(i). [51] The decision was reversed on appeal, 1990 Carswell BC 1418 (C.A.), but not on this issue. [52] The City submits in response that the intention of the legislature was to grant to a municipality the discretion to determine both whether to grant the exemption, and if granted, the scope. This intention, it is submitted, is manifest in the plain meaning of the language of the section, as well from the fact that the legislature has created separate permissive exemptions. In so doing, the legislature has granted to the municipality an entirely discretionary power to create and exercise its own policy. [53] This construction, the City submits, is also consistent with the purpose of the Community Charter as manifested in ss 3 and 4(1), which provide: 3 The purposes of this Act are to provide municipalities and their councils with (a) a legal framework for the powers, duties and functions that are necessary to fulfill their purposes, (b) the authority and discretion to address existing and future community needs, and (c) the flexibility to determine the public interest of their communities and to respond to the different needs and changing circumstances of their communities. 4(1) The powers conferred on municipalities and their councils by or under this Act or the Local Government Act must be interpreted broadly in accordance with the purposes of those Acts and in accordance with municipal purposes. ... [54] This, it submits, underscores the legislative intention to grant a broad discretion as the municipal council is best situated to make judgments about a municipality's financial needs and the effect of the group's activities on the public good within that municipality. [55] The City notes that the provisions of the Municipal Act at issue in New Vista were very different. Section 398 of the Municipal Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c. 290, provided at the time: Unless otherwise provided in this Act, the following property is exempt from taxation to the extent indicated: ... (i) a building and the land on which it stands constructed or reconstructed with the assistance of aid granted by the Province after January 1, 1947, and owned and used exclusively without profit by a corporation to provide homes for elderly citizens, and an area of the land surrounding the building determined by council; but this paragraph does not apply to a building or land where the grant by the Province was made after March 31, 1974; [56] In its analysis, a court must first consider the language of the section. In Yeung (Guardian ad litem of) v. Au (2006), 51 B.C.L.R. (4th) 258, BCCA 217, Madam Justice Newberry, speaking for the court, articulated the approach to be taken as follows at para. 32: [T]he approach to statutory interpretation endorsed by the Supreme Court of Canada in Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes Ltd., Re, [1998] 1 S.C.R. 27 (S.C.C.), adopted from Elmer Driedger, Construction of Statutes (2nd ed., 1983): Today there is only one principle or approach, namely, the words of an Act are to be read in their entire context and in their grammatical and ordinary sense harmoniously with the scheme of the Act, the object of the Act, and the intention of Parliament. [At 87.] The Court in Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes Ltd. also emphasized the principle, encapsulated at s. 8 of the Interpretation Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 238, that every act "must be construed as being remedial" and must receive "such fair, large and liberal construction and interpretation as best assures the attainment of its object." [57] In addition, it is important to adopt a construction that gives the appropriate level of deference to a municipality and avoids a narrow, overly technical approach. As Newbury J.A., speaking for the majority, stated in R. v. Reid, 2006 BCCA 251 at para. 24: In answering this question, it is noteworthy that the Supreme Court of Canada has rejected a narrow approach to the interpretation of municipal enabling statutes in favour of a more liberal approach, which generally calls for greater deference to municipal authorities carrying out their mandate. (See, for example, Shell Canada Products Ltd. v. Vancouver (City), [1994] 1 S.C.R. 231, (per McLachlin J., as she then was, speaking for the minority, but later affirmed in majority judgments), Nanaimo (City) v. Rascal Trucking Ltd., [2000] 1 S.C.R. 342, 2000 SCC 13, 114957 Canada Ltée (Spraytech, Société d'arrosage) v. Hudson (Town), [2001] 2 S.C.R. 241, 2001 SCC 40, and United Taxi Drivers' Fellowship of Southern Alberta v. Calgary (City), [2004] 1 S.C.R. 485, 2004 SCC 19.) [58] In my view, the language of the section does not bear the construction proposed by the Trustees. Rather the language of the section confers a broad discretion upon a municipality with respect to permissive exemptions that is not limited to determining the scope of such exemptions. Such a construction is consistent as well with the purposes of the Community Charter as set out in s. 3. It is clear that the policy of the legislature as manifested in s. 220(1)(h) of the Community Charter is that buildings set aside for public worship, together with the land on which the buildings stand, will be exempt from taxation. This policy remains consistent with the policy disclosed by the Court of Appeal in Bishop. However, in my view, the legislature has chosen to treat the land surrounding the exempt building in a different fashion. This difference is reflected first in the fact that the question of exemption for the surrounding land is dealt with in a separate section, headed "permissive exemptions". The language in this section is permissive "a council may ... exempt" in contrast with the mandatory language of the general exemption section "the following property is exempt". As noted by the City, this is a very different language from that of the provisions considered by Oppal J. [59] Thus, I conclude that, pursuant to s. 224 of the Community Charter, the City was granted the discretion to grant no permissive exemption to land or improvements surrounding a place of worship. Did the Bylaws Unlawfully Discriminate? [60] The Trustees submit that the City's bylaws passed in 2004 and 2005, and the decisions to prohibit future municipal tax exemptions that found them, are discriminatory. They draw a distinction between religious organizations in otherwise identical circumstances based solely on whether a particular organization's place of worship was built, occupied, and approved for exemption before October of 2004. The Trustees argue that a double standard exists in that the municipality's decisions accord places of worship in the same category different kinds of treatment. As such, the Trustees submit, the bylaws are ultra vires: see Wedman v. Victoria (City) (1979), 15 B.C.L.R. 303 (S.C.). [61] The Trustees cite Shell Canada Products Ltd. v. Vancouver (City), [1994] 1 S.C.R. 231 at 251, per McLachlin J., as she then was, as authority for the proposition that the power to pass municipal bylaws does not entail that of enacting discriminatory provisions. For example, provisions that draw a distinction, unless the enabling legislation authorizes such discriminatory treatment, or the discrimination is a necessary incident to the exercise of the power delegated by the province, are not lawful uses of a municipality's power. [62] In Fredericton (City) v. McInroy (1989), 101 N.B.R. (2d) 221 (C.A.), the New Brunswick Court of Appeal held that the same rule applies to policies, stating at paras. 9-10: In Rogers, The Law of Canadian Municipal Corporations 2nd Ed.), vol. 1 at p. 400, para. 65.46, the author refers to a principle of fairness which we think applies to policies as well as to bylaws: "It is a fundamental principle of municipal law that bylaws must affect equally all those who come within the ambit of the enabling enactment. Municipal legislation must be impartial in its operation and must not discriminate so as to show favouritism to one or more classes of citizens. Any bylaw violating this principle so that all the inhabitants are not placed in the same position regarding matters affected by it is illegal, unless the enabling statute clearly specifies that certain persons or things may be excepted from its operation or expressly authorizes some form of discrimination." Thus, in cases of policies, the city cannot prefer one person over the other. The city cannot endorse a policy which cannot be applied fairly to all concerned and certainly not if the policy has the effect of modifying a subdivision plan. [63] The Trustees submit that the Community Charter does not authorize the discriminatory treatment in the case at bar. [64] The authority of a municipality to establish variations is found in s. 12 of the Community Charter which provides: 12(1) A municipal bylaw under this Act may do one or more of the following: (a) make different provisions for different areas, times, conditions or circumstances as described by bylaw; (b) establish different classes of persons, places, activities, property or things; (c) make different provisions, including exceptions, for different classes established under paragraph (b). [65] However, ss. 192 and 193 of the Community Charter provide: 192 Municipalities have the following revenue sources: (a) fees under Division 2 [Fees]; (b) taxes under Division 3 [Property Value Taxes]; (c) taxes under Division 4 [Parcel Taxes]; (d) taxes under Division 5 [Local Service Taxes]; (e) taxes under section 353 [taxation of utility company property] of the Local Government Act; (f) fines and other penalties referred to in section 261 [payment of fines and other penalties to municipality]; (g) revenues raised by other means authorized by or under this or another Act; (h) revenues received by way of agreement, enterprise, gift, grant or otherwise. 193(1) A municipality may not impose fees or taxes except as expressly authorized by or under this or another Act. (2) Section 12(1) [authority to establish variations] does not apply in relation to bylaws imposing taxes referred to in section 192(b), (c), (d) and (e). (3) A council may only provide an exemption from property taxes if expressly authorized by this Part or another Act. (4) For the purposes of assessment, taxation, recovery of taxes and tax sale, parcels combined under the Assessment Act to form one parcel are deemed to constitute one parcel. [66] Finally the Trustees submit that it is well settled that in matters of taxation, all taxation must fall equally on those within the same class: see Poole v. Victoria (City) (1892), 2 B.C.R. 271 (S.C.). [67] The City submits that the Community Charter does authorize Council to discriminate in that s. 224 states that a council may exempt land or improvements from taxation pursuant to that section. Therefore, it submits, the section clearly authorizes Council to exercise discrimination in the sense of drawing a distinction in granting tax exemptions. [68] The City relies upon the decision of Madam Justice Smith in 511784 B.C. Ltd. v. Salmon Arm (District), 2001 BCSC 245 at para. 44, as authority for the proposition that: All discretion is inherently discriminatory in that decisions are made which result in a choice or distinction being made. The exercise of discretionary power is only unlawful if the discretion is exercised in an improper discriminatory manner, that is for some improper or on some irrelevant basis. (See Bignell Enterprises Ltd. v. Campbell River (District) (1966), 34 M.P.L.R. (2d) 193 (B.C.S.C.). [69] Thus, given that the legislature has conferred discretion on Council, the City submits that the Policy and bylaws could only be found to improperly discriminate if they operated unfairly and were partial and unequal in their operation between different classes, or if the discretion were exercised for some improper purpose or upon some irrelevant basis. [70] The City submits that in the case at hand, the Policy is intended to apply fairly and equally to all applicants for permissive tax exemptions, from the date the Policy was implemented until it is amended or repealed. The Congregation can only show that Council improperly discriminated if they can demonstrate that a similarly-situated group applied for a tax exemption and was considered under the same Policy, and yet was granted an exemption where there were no compelling considerations dictating otherwise. [71] In the case at bar, as I have concluded, the governing legislation grants the City the discretion to exempt certain land from taxation. Therefore, the legislation provides a basis for drawing a distinction, assuming that such a distinction is consistent with the Charter. [72] In the case at bar, there is no evidence, as there was in 356226 British Columbia Ltd. v. Vancouver (City) (1998), 54 B.C.L.R. (3d) 253 (C.A.), of an underlying purpose to discriminate or other impropriety. Accordingly, I conclude that the bylaws at issue are not ultra vires on the basis that they discriminate. Did Council Provide the Congregation the Appropriate Procedural Fairness? [73] The Trustees submit that the City's decision to deny the Congregation's applications for tax exemption adversely affected their rights and interests and as such, the City was bound by a duty of procedural fairness: see Congrégation des témoins de Jéhovah de St-Jérôme-Lafontaine v. Lafontaine (Village), [2004] 2 S.C.R. 650, 2004 SCC 48 [Congrégation Lafontaine]. [74] As Congrégation Lafontaine establishes, the content of the duty of fairness varies according to five factors: (1) the nature of the decision and the decision-making process employed by the public body; (2) the nature of the statutory scheme and the precise statutory provisions pursuant to which the public body operates; (3) the importance of the decision to the individuals affected; (4) the legitimate expectations of the party challenging the decision; and (5) the nature of the deference accorded to the body. [75] The Trustees submit that analysing these five factors suggests the City had a heightened duty of fairness to carefully consider the Congregation's applications and to articulate cogent reasons for refusing them. The Trustees argue, based on the five factors, that: (1) where a municipal council "acts in an arbitrary fashion in the discharge of its public function, 'good and sufficient reason' exists to warrant intervention from a reviewing court in order to remedy the proven misconduct." (Congrégation Lafontaine at para. 7); the absence of an appeal provision in the Community Charter demands greater municipal solicitude for fairness; Council's decision imposes a substantial financial burden on the Congregation's place of worship in the amount of $19,300 per year; Council had exempted the Congregation's previous place of worship for 35 years, and during the three-year construction approval process never said their new place of worship would not benefit from this same exemption; in so doing, this gave rise to a legitimate expectation that their applications for tax exemptions would be thoroughly vetted and carefully considered; and rather than turn its mind to the merits of the Congregation's requests, Council blindly adhered to an arbitrary decision, entailing no expertise at all. [76] Municipal councillors, the Trustees submit, must always explain and be prepared to defend their decisions. If a municipal council has a duty to give reasons and refuses to articulate reasons for exercising its discretionary authority in a particular fashion, the council may be deemed to have acted arbitrarily and violated its duty of procedural fairness. Giving reasons for refusing to grant a tax exemption serves the values of fair and transparent decision-making, reduces the chance of arbitrary or capricious decisions and cultivates the confidence of citizens in public officials. [77] However, the Trustees submit that Council repeatedly disregarded its duty to articulate reasons in this case. In September of 2004, the presiding minister of Westwood Congregation asked the City Clerk why the Congregation was not notified of Council's refusal for the 2005 exemption and the reasons for its decision. The response was that they were not required to give notice or reasons. Like the municipality in Congrégation Lafontaine, the City asserted the discretion conferred on it by the legislature did not require it to offer any justification for refusing the Congregation's application. The City wrote to the Congregation on November 12, 2004, informing them of its decision and providing no reasons. [78] In 2005, the Congregation requested written reasons both for the City's refusal of its request for an exemption for 2006 and for Council's rejection of the Congregation's request to grandfather their place of worship under its tax exemption policy. The only reason the City provided was its decision to refuse all new requests for exemptions. The reasons did not address the merits of the Congregation's requests. [79] The Trustees submit that the City's failure to articulate reasons for refusing the July 18, 2004, and June 30, 2005, requests for tax exemptions, beyond simply citing its decision to prohibit all new requests, can only lead to the conclusion that Council adopted its decision in an arbitrary and capricious manner. [80] The Trustees submit further that Council breached its duty of procedural fairness when it made decisions detrimental to the Congregation at closed meetings contrary to the Community Charter. The general rule is that meetings of a municipal council are to be open to the public subject to certain exceptions stipulated in the Community Charter. Sections 89 and 90 of the Community Charter provide: 89(1) A meeting of a council must be open to the public, except as provided in this Division. (2) A council must not vote on the reading or adoption of a bylaw when its meeting is closed to the public. 90(1) A part of a council meeting may be closed to the public if the subject matter being considered relates to or is one or more of the following: (a) personal information about an identifiable individual who holds or is being considered for a position as an officer, employee or agent of the municipality or another position appointed by the municipality; (b) personal information about an identifiable individual who is being considered for a municipal award or honour, or who has offered to provide a gift to the municipality on condition of anonymity; (c) labour relations or other employee relations; (d) the security of the property of the municipality; (e) the acquisition, disposition or expropriation of land or improvements, if the council considers that disclosure could reasonably be expected to harm the interests of the municipality; (f) law enforcement, if the council considers that disclosure could reasonably be expected to harm the conduct of an investigation under or enforcement of an enactment; (g) litigation or potential litigation affecting the municipality; (h) an administrative tribunal hearing or potential administrative tribunal hearing affecting the municipality, other than a hearing to be conducted by the council or a delegate of council; (i) the receipt of advice that is subject to solicitor-client privilege, including communications necessary for that purpose; (j) information that is prohibited, or information that if it were presented in a document would be prohibited, from disclosure under section 21 of the Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act; (k) negotiations and related discussions respecting the proposed provision of a municipal service that are at their preliminary stages and that, in the view of the council, could reasonably be expected to harm the interests of the municipality if they were held in public; (l) discussions with municipal officers and employees respecting municipal objectives, measures and progress reports for the purposes of preparing an annual report under section 98 [annual municipal report]; (m) a matter that, under another enactment, is such that the public may be excluded from the meeting; (n) the consideration of whether a council meeting should be closed under a provision of this subsection or subsection (2); (o) the consideration of whether the authority under section 91 [other persons attending closed meetings] should be exercised in relation to a council meeting. (2) A part of a council meeting must be closed to the public if the subject matter being considered relates to one or more the following: (a) a request under the Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act, if the council is designated as head of the local public body for the purposes of that Act in relation to the matter; (b) the consideration of information received and held in confidence relating to negotiations between the municipality and a provincial government or the federal government or both and a third party; (c) a matter that is being investigated under the Ombudsman Act of which the municipality has been notified under section 14 [ombudsman to notify authority] of that Act; (d) a matter that, under another enactment, is such that the public must be excluded from the meeting. (3) If the only subject matter being considered at a council meeting is one or more matters referred to in subsection (1) or (2), the applicable subsection applies to the entire meeting. [81] In the present case, Council addressed the issue of the exemptions at the following closed meeting, none of which, in the Trustee's submission, fall within the circumstances enumerated in s. 90 of the Community Charter: June 7, 2004 - Closed Council Meeting - General Manager Corporate Services provided overview of staff report and recommendation on Permissive Tax Exemption Policy, including implications of narrowing scope of permitted exemptions and not granting exemptions. July 5, 2004 - Closed Council Meeting - Council approved Policy to grant no new tax exemptions except those included in bylaw for 2004. November 15, 2004 - Closed Council Meeting - Council directed staff to bring back Permissive Tax Exemption Policy for Council to review. May 30, 2005 - Closed Council Meeting - Council reviewed its 2004 Permissive Property Taxation Exemption Policy and resolved to maintain it for 2005. October 3, 2005 - Closed Council Meeting - Discussion regarding Westwood Congregation's request for permissive tax exemption. Council directed staff to write letter to Congregation. [82] The City agrees that a public body is bound by a duty of procedural fairness when it makes administrative decisions affecting individual rights, privileges and interests. However, the City submits first, that the adoption of the Policy was a legislative as opposed to an administrative decision and as such, no duty of procedural fairness arises. The indicia of a legislative decision were all present; namely, the decision was general and not directed to a particular person, it was based on broad considerations of public policy rather than facts pertaining to an individual and it created a policy rather than applying a policy to an individual situation; see Berg v. Saskatchewan (2004). 254 Sask. R. 177, 2004 SKCA 136. [83] The City submits that the Congregation was accorded procedural fairness in relation to the decisions to pass the bylaws and to refuse its requests for exemptions. While the City concedes that the Congregation had the right to be heard in relation to Council's decision to pass the bylaws, it submits that the Congregation was afforded this right. The Congregation made submissions to Council. While it did not have the opportunity to be heard in the first instance by Council, the City submits that the Congregation did not have the right to an oral hearing. [84] The City submits further that later steps that accord full procedural fairness can cure any procedural defects in an earlier step: see Do Rav Right Coalition v. Richmond/Airport/Vancouver Rapid Transit Line Project and RAV Project Management Ltd., 2005 BCSC 991. In the case at bar, the City submits, the Congregation was ultimately granted the opportunity to make full submissions to Council in advancing its appeal for a permissive tax exemption, thereby remedying any earlier deficiencies with respect to fair process. [85] I will deal first with the issue of a right to a hearing, considering first the situation in 2004. By letter dated July 18, 2004, the Congregation requested the opportunity to make submissions to Council. The City neither granted nor responded to this request. Instead, the July 18, 2004 letters were attached to the report dated September 1, 2004, dealing with the proposed Annual Taxation Exemption Bylaw No. 3663, 2004. This material was then placed before Council when it deliberated in October of 2004 with respect to the Annual Taxation Exemption Bylaw No. 3663, 2004. [86] The City contends that placing the July 18 letters before Council satisfied the City's duty to give the Congregation a hearing. In my view, that it not the case. I agree that there is not necessarily a right to an oral hearing and that, in an appropriate case, an opportunity to make written submissions will suffice to discharge council's duty. If the City had responded to the Congregation's letters inviting the Congregation to state its case in writing, the Congregation may well have no cause for complaint. However, no such invitation was extended. The Congregation was never given the opportunity to make a submission in writing. [87] Moreover, it is abundantly clear that the July 18 letters were not intended by their author to be submissions; rather, they contained a request to make a submission. It is not for city officials to determine the content of the Congregation's submissions and to decide that there is nothing more to be said. It follows that the Congregation was not given a right to be heard with respect to the 2004 application for a permissive exemption in 2005. [88] The Congregation was given the opportunity to make a submission in writing and to appear before the Committee in connection with its efforts to obtain a permissive exemption in the following year in relation to Annual Taxation Exemption Bylaw No. 3718, 2005. It was therefore afforded with procedural fairness in the sense of a right to be heard in relation to that year. [89] Council's deliberations were with respect to the Annual Taxation Exemption Bylaw for that particular year. These are separate deliberations concerning separate matters - namely taxation for the year in question. Accordingly, in those circumstances, in my view, it is not the case that providing the Congregation with a hearing with respect to Bylaw No. 3718, 2005, provided a remedy for the absence of a hearing in relation to Bylaw No. 3663, 2004. [90] I turn then to the question of reasons. The circumstances in the Congrégation Lafontaine decision pertained to a rezoning application brought by a congregation of Jehovah's Witnesses to permit them to build a place of worship on a parcel of land outside the zone designated by the municipality for places of worship. Chief Justice McLachlin reached the following conclusion with respect to the requirements of procedural fairness in the circumstances at paras. 12 - 13: The five Baker factors suggest that the Municipality's duty of procedural fairness to the Congregation required the Municipality to carefully evaluate the applications for a zoning variance and to give reasons for refusing them. This conclusion is consistent with the Court's recent decision in Prud'homme v. Prud'homme, [2002] 4 S.C.R. 663, 2002 SCC 85, at para. 23, holding that municipal councillors must always explain and be prepared to defend their decisions. It is also consistent with Baker, where it was held, at para. 43 dealing with a ministerial decision, that if an organ of the state has a duty to give reasons and refuses to articulate reasons for exercising its discretionary authority in a particular fashion, the public body may be deemed to have acted arbitrarily and violated its duty of procedural fairness. Giving reasons for refusing to rezone in a case such as this serves the values of fair and transparent decision making, reduces the chance of arbitrary or capricious decisions, and cultivates the confidence of citizens in public officials. Sustained by both law and policy, I conclude that the Municipality was bound to give reasons for refusing the Congregation's second and third applications for rezoning. This duty applied to the first application, and was complied with. If anything, the duty was stronger on the Congregation's second and third applications, where legitimate expectations of fair process had been established by the Municipality itself. [91] In the case at bar, the same considerations apply to Council's deliberations with respect to the permissive exemptions from taxation. As in Congrégation Lafontaine, Council's decision affected members of the Congregation's practice of their religion. The special circumstances of having had an exemption in the past, and having been forced to move because of the expropriation created a legitimate expectation that if Council refused to grant an exemption, it would provide reasons for its decision. [92] I conclude that Council was required to give reasons to the Congregation for its refusals to include the Congregation in the permissive exemptions. Such reasons needed to include Council's reasons for refusing the Congregation's requests for exemption. The reasons needed to go beyond a mere recitation of the Policy. [93] Council did not provide reasons for its refusal that addressed the merits of the Congregation's applications. Accordingly, I find that Council breached the duty of procedural fairness that it owed to the Congregation in its deliberations with respect to Bylaws No. 3663, 2004 and No. 3718, 2005. Did Council Improperly Fetter its Discretion? [94] The Trustees submit that Council's decision to adopt and implement its Policy to refuse all future requests for permissive tax exemptions effectively transformed its discretion to grant exemptions into a prohibition. This amounted to a misuse of power that frustrated the very legislative scheme under which the power had been conferred: see Reference Re Bill 30 at pp. 1193-94. [95] The Trustees rely upon the decision of Mr. Justice Macdonald in Bubas v. Saanich (Dist.) (1988), 34 B.C.L.R. (2d) 167 (S.C.) [Bubas]. In Bubas, the petitioners had applied under s. 996 of the Municipal Act to sub-divide their lot in order to build a home for a relative. The approving officer rejected their application, relying on a policy of the respondent municipality's Official Community Plan, which was to reject such s. 996 applications in all but extremely limited circumstances. Macdonald J. concluded that the policy represented an effort on the part of the municipality to repeal s. 996, something that was beyond its competence. Accordingly, the approving officer was not entitled to rely upon it, or to regard it as expressing the public interest. In the result, he reversed the decision of the approving officer. [96] The Trustees note that in Maple Lodge Farms Ltd. v. Canada, [1982] 2 S.C.R. 2, the Supreme Court of Canada held that a municipality may properly indicate general considerations by which it will be guided in the exercise of its discretion, but cannot fetter its discretion by treating the guidelines as binding upon it and excluding other valid reasons for the exercise of its discretion. It is submitted that in the case at bar, the City unduly fettered its discretion by adopting an inflexible rule: see Hospital Employees' Union, Local 180 v. Peace Arch District Hospital (1989), 35 B.C.L.R. (2d) 64 (C.A.). [97] The Trustees submit that the circumstances in the case at bar mirror those in Hale v. White Rock (City) Board of Variance (1985), 16 Admin L.R. 313 (B.C.S.C.) [Hale]. In Hale, by mistake a builder employed by the applicant built a garage in breach of the city's citing bylaw. The applicant was ordered to remove the structure. He appealed to the Board of Variance for a relaxation of the siting requirements. At the hearing of his appeal, the Chairman of the Board of Variance noted that the Board had a policy not to allow a variance for a building that had been constructed prior to an appeal being made. The applicant's solicitor then made a submission that the policy in question ought not to be applied. The appeal was dismissed. Madam Justice Proudfoot concluded that the Board, by blindly following its policy, had failed to exercise its discretion. In so concluding, she cited with approval at p. 316 the following passage from H.W.R. Wade on Administrative Law, 4th ed. (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1977), at p. 317 that accurately sets out the proposition: OVER-RIGID POLICIES Policy and precedent An authority can fail to give its mind to a case, and thus fail to exercise its discretion lawfully, by blindly following a policy laid down in advance. It is a fundamental rule for the exercise of discretionary power that discretion must be brought to bear on every case: each one must be considered on its own merits and decided as the public interest requires at the time. In enforcing this rule the courts are underlining the difference between judicial and administrative processes. The legal rights of litigants are decided according to legal rules and precedents which are sometimes held to prevail over the court's own opinion. But if an administrative authority acts in this way its decision is ultra vires and void. It is not allowed to 'pursue consistency at the expense of the merits of individual cases'. [footnotes omitted] [98] No reasons other than the Policy were cited by Council to the Congregation for the decision not to grant the exemption. The Trustees submit that, in the circumstances, since reasons were requested and called for, it is appropriate to draw an adverse inference: see Padfield v. Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food, [1968] A.C. 997 at 1006-07 (H.L.). [99] The Trustees rely upon the decision of the Court of Appeal in Alkali Lake Indian Band v. Westcoast Transmission Co. (1984), 53 B.C.L.R. 323 (C.A.) [Alkali]. In that case, under its governing legislation, the British Columbia Utilities Commission had a discretion to award costs to participants at hearings. The Minister had written to the Commission expressing a wish that that practice be discontinued. The appellant band was intervening in a natural gas transmission line application. It was denied costs with reference being made by the Commission to the Ministerial direction, and then later denied again, without reasons, despite having been asked to assist the Commission by preparing a map. The Court of Appeal concluded that the Commission had fettered its discretion. Hutcheon J.A., speaking for the court, noted that there was no clear reason to be found in the record for refusing the order while there were a number of considerations favouring such an order. The court drew an adverse inference, stating at pp. 328-29: When the circumstances demand reasons, a tribunal that fails to state them must be prepared to accept adverse inferences: Re Ross and Bd. of Commrs. of Police for Toronto, [1953] O.R. 556, 107 C.C.C. 134, [1953] 3 D.L.R. 597 (H.C.). The adverse inference that the commission acted in response to the minister's direction, and not in the exercise of an unfettered discretion, arises in this case because there is no clear reason to be found in the record for refusing the order for costs. The best way to escape this inference was by reasons that demonstrated the exercise by the commission of the discretion given to it by the legislature. In the absence of reasons, and with a record that reveals no other ground for refusing costs than adherence to its view of the "policy direction" in the letter of 10th August, 1983, the order cannot stand. [100] In the case at bar, the Trustees submit, there were a number of highly relevant considerations favouring the granting of an exemption that should have been considered by Council. These include that the Congregation had received an exemption for the past 35 years for their previous place of worship, that the Congregation had been forced to relocate by the expropriation of part of their parking lot, and that the place of worship was built and occupied before Council resolved to adopt the Policy. Moreover, Council failed to consider how imposing a burdensome property tax on the Congregations place of worship, while allowing an exemption for all others in the City, would have an adverse effect on the dignity of a minority religious community and offend Charter values. [101] However, the Trustees submit, Council, in determining to follow the Policy, did not give consideration to these factors. In the result, the Trustees submit, the decision was arbitrary and capricious and ultra vires the Council: see Saunders Farms Ltd. v. British Columbia (Liquor Control & Licensing Branch) (1995), 1 B.C.L.R. (3d) 12 (C.A.) [Saunders] and Oakwood Development Ltd. v. St. François Xavier (Rural Municipality), [1985] 2 S.C.R. 164. [102] The City submits that the matter must be considered in light of the presumption of regularity. Mere suspicion that Council has conducted itself improperly is not sufficient to overturn its decisions. There is a presumption of regularity for such decisions in the absence of credible evidence to the contrary; see Aasland v. British Columbia (Ministry of Environment, Lands and Parks) (1999) 19 Admin. L.R. (3d) 154 (B.C.S.C.). Moreover as Mr. Justice Finch, as he then was, stated in MacMillan Bloedel Ltd. v. Galiano Island Trust Committee (1995) 10 B.C.L.R. (3d) 121 at para. 178 (C.A.): In my view courts should be slow to find bad faith in the conduct of democratically elected representatives acting under legislative authority, unless there is no other rational conclusion. [103] The City submits that a decision making body is not prevented from adopting policies or guidelines to assist it in the exercise of its discretion, provided that it does not consider itself bound by those policies and guidelines and as such prevented from considering each case on the merits: see Wedley v. British Columbia (Assessor of Area No. 8 - North Shore/Squamish Valley), [1998] B.C.J. No. 1960 (S.C.), leave to appeal to C.A. refused, December 18, 1998 [Wedley]. Thus, the City submits, it is not sufficient for the Trustees to point to the Policy; the question is rather whether Council was prepared to consider a case for an exemption to the Policy. [104] The City relies upon the decision of the Court of Appeal in Davison v. Maple Ridge (District) (1991), 60 B.C.L.R. (2d) 24 (C.A.) [Davison]. In Davison, the council had adopted a policy that land in a certain area would continue to be kept as agricultural land and that council would not authorize applications to remove such land from the agricultural land reserve. The respondents applied to the council for leave to apply to have the land removed from the reserve. Council rejected the application, based upon its policy. The respondents argued that council had fettered its discretion because it approached the application with a resolution not to depart from its pre-established policy. The Court held that there was no suggestion that the respondents had been denied an opportunity to present their case. The respondents had to be able to point to some positive evidence that council failed in its duty to consider variance or exemption from the policy. In the absence of such evidence, it was to be presumed that council was aware of its duty to consider the matter on the merits. [105] The Alkali decision was distinguished in that the tribunal in that case had initially expressed itself to be bound by the ministerial direction and then failed to give reasons when reaching the same conclusion for a second time. [106] The City submits that in the case at bar there is no evidence of bad faith or improper purpose in Council's exercise of its discretion under the Community Charter. Moreover, there is no evidence that Council failed to consider the application for exemption on its merits. The Congregation was told of the Policy, but was given the opportunity to make a submission to Council and to the Committee for exemption from the Policy. There is, the City submits, simply no evidence that Council did not consider the application on its merits. Accordingly, in the circumstances, the court must conclude that Council did not improperly fetter its discretion. [107] The law in this area is summarized in the judgment of Levine J., as she then was, in Wedley at paras. 34-40. Levine J. quoted from two English cases which illustrate the fine line to be drawn. The first quotation is from R. v. Port of London Authority; ex parte Kynoch, Ltd., [1919] 1 K.B. 176 at 184 (C.A.), where Bankes L.J. said: There are on the one hand cases where a tribunal in the honest exercise of its discretion has adopted a policy, and, without refusing to hear an applicant, intimates to him what its policy is, and that after hearing him it will in accordance with its policy decide against him, unless there is something exceptional in his case. I think counsel for the applicants would admit that, if the policy has been adopted for reasons which the tribunal may legitimately entertain, no objection could be taken to such a course. On the other hand there are cases where a tribunal has passed a rule, or come to a determination, not to hear any application of a particular character by whomsoever made. There is a wide distinction to be drawn between these two classes. [at 184] In British Oxygen Co. Ltd. v. Board of Trade, [1971] A.C. 610 at 625 (H.L.) Lord Reid expanded on Bankes L.J.'s statement of the principle: The general rule is that anyone who has to exercise a statutory discretion must not "shut his ears to an application" (to adapt from Bankes L.J. on p. 183). I do not think there is any great difference between a policy and a rule. There may be cases where an officer or authority ought to listen to a substantial argument reasonably presented urging a change of policy. What the authority must not do is to refuse to listen at all. But a Ministry or large authority may have had to deal already with a multitude of similar applications and then they will almost certainly have evolved a policy so precise that it could well be called a rule. There can be no objection to that, provided the authority is always willing to listen to anyone with something new to say ... [108] I agree with the submission of the City that Council was entitled to adopt the Policy and that it cannot be inferred from the fact that the Policy was adopted that Council fettered its discretion. It is also the case that Council's deliberations are entitled to the benefit of the presumption of regularity. Council did not refuse to give a hearing to the Congregation at all. [109] In my view, the remaining consideration is therefore what to make of the reasons given by the City to the Congregation in response to their requests for an exemption. In Saunders, Southin J.A., concurring, made the following observation with respect to the issue of fettering discretion at para. 2: At the heart of the appellant's case is the principle that a tribunal upon whom, by statute, a discretion is conferred may not fetter its discretion save to the extent the statute expressly or implicitly authorizes. The principle is easy enough to state. But, in truth, it is a principle vague in its limits with a good deal of the chancellor's foot in its application. [111] In Wedley, at issue was a decision of the Assessment Appeal Board. [119] Justice Dickson, as he then was, in the seminal case R. v. Big M Drug Mart Ltd., [1985] 1 S.C.R. 295 at 336-37 [Big M], provided the following definition of the freedom of religion as guaranteed in s. 2(a) of the Charter: A truly free society is one which can accommodate a wide variety of beliefs, diversity of tastes and pursuits, customs and codes of conduct. A free society is one which aims at equality with respect to the enjoyment of fundamental freedoms and I say this without any reliance upon s. 15 of the Charter. Freedom must surely be founded in respect for the inherent dignity and the inviolable rights of the human person. The essence of the concept of freedom of religion is the right to entertain such religious beliefs as a person chooses, the right to declare religious beliefs openly and without fear of hindrance or reprisal, and the right to manifest religious belief by worship and practice or by teaching and dissemination. But the concept means more than that. Freedom can primarily be characterized by the absence of coercion or constraint. If a person is compelled by the state or the will of another to a course of action or inaction which he would not otherwise have chosen, he is not acting of his own volition and he cannot be said to be truly free. One of the major purposes of the Charter is to protect, within reason, from compulsion or restraint. Coercion includes not only such blatant forms of compulsion as direct commands to act or refrain from acting on pain of sanction, coercion includes indirect forms of control which determine or limit alternative courses of conduct available to others. Freedom in a broad sense embraces both the absence of coercion and constraint, and the right to manifest beliefs and practices. Freedom means that, subject to such limitations as are necessary to protect public safety, order, health, or morals or the fundamental rights and freedoms of others, no one is to be forced to act in a way contrary to his beliefs or his conscience. [122] In R. v. Edwards Books and Art Ltd., [1986] 2 S.C.R. 713 at 758-59 [Edwards Books], Dickson C.J. discussed the question of indirect burdens as infringements of the freedom of religion, stating: The first question is whether indirect burdens on religious practice are prohibited by the constitutional guarantee of freedom of religion. In my opinion indirect coercion by the state is comprehended within the evils from which s. 2(a) may afford protection. The Court said as much in the Big M Drug Mart Ltd. case and any more restrictive interpretation would, in my opinion, be inconsistent with the Court's obligation under s. 27 to preserve and enhance the multicultural heritage of Canadians. Although the Court apparently reached a different conclusion in Robertson and Rosetanni in respect of the protection afforded under the Canadian Bill of Rights, I would follow Big M Drug Mart Ltd. in declining to be bound by jurisprudence interpreting an instrument which purported only to reaffirm pre-existing rights and freedoms. It matters not, I believe, whether a coercive burden is direct or indirect, intentional or unintentional, foreseeable or unforeseeable. All coercive burdens on the exercise of religious beliefs are potentially within the ambit of s. 2(a). [emphasis in original] This does not mean, however, that every burden on religious practices is offensive to the constitutional guarantee of freedom of religion. It means only that indirect or unintentional burdens will not be held to be outside the scope of Charter protection on that account alone. Section 2(a) does not require the legislatures to eliminate every miniscule state-imposed cost associated with the practice of religion. Otherwise the Charter would offer protection from innocuous secular legislation such as a taxation act that imposed a modest sales tax extending to all products, including those used in the course of religious worship. In my opinion, it is unnecessary to turn to s. 1 in order to justify legislation of that sort. The purpose of s. 2(a) is to ensure that society does not interfere with profoundly personal beliefs that govern one's perception of oneself, humankind, nature, and, in some cases, a higher or different order of being. These beliefs, in turn, govern one's conduct and practices. The Constitution shelters individuals and groups only to the extent that religious beliefs or conduct might reasonably or actually be threatened. For a state-imposed cost or burden to be proscribed by s. 2(a) it must be capable of interfering with religious belief or practice. In short, legislative or administrative action which increases the cost of practising or otherwise manifesting religious beliefs is not prohibited if the burden is trivial or insubstantial: see, on this point, R. v. Special burdens placed on religious minorities in the operation of their businesses have a venerable and infamous status in the annals of religious persecution. What was at stake in Big M Drug Mart and Edwards Books was nothing less than a state prohibition that put members of minority religions at a disadvantage in gaining their livelihood. This Court, looking at the history and context of such measures, concluded that they might indeed constitute an infringement of freedom of religion. The burden at issue in the case at bar differs from that at issue in Big M Drug Mart and Edwards Books in two ways. First, it does not involve a state prohibition on otherwise lawful conduct. People remain free to educate their children whenever and however they choose, provided they meet prescribed standards. While this may impose costs on them not borne by parents of children attending public secular schools, the cost issue is more appropriately considered under the equality provision of the Charter, s. 15. The second distinction between this case and the Sunday-closing cases is that the sort of absence of benefit complained of in this case has no history of recognition as a violation of freedom of religion. Absence of state funding for private religious practices, as distinct from prohibitions on such practices, has never been seen as religious persecution. In determining the content of the guarantees contained in the Charter, the courts must look to the history of values enshrined. That history provides no support for extending the guarantee of freedom of religion to the provision of equal funding for religious practices, like religious education. Never, to borrow the reasoning of Dubin C.J., has it been suggested that freedom of religion entitles one to state support for one's religion. [132] Justice LeBel, writing for the minority in Congrégation Lafontaine, returned to this theme of state neutrality, stating at para. 65: The concept of freedom of religion, which is not strictly limited to the above definition, includes a positive aspect, that is, the right to believe or not believe what one chooses, to declare one's beliefs openly, and to practice one's religion in accordance with its tenets. This positive aspect also includes the right to proselytize, that is, to teach and disseminate one's beliefs. Freedom of religion also has a negative aspect, that is, the right not to be compelled to belong to a particular religion or to act in a manner contrary to one's religious beliefs (J. [136] The Supreme Court of Canada considered the effect of underinclusive legislation on the exercise of s. 2 freedoms in Dunmore. The claimant in that case challenged government legislation that made it possible for agricultural workers in Ontario to form a union. Although Dunmore dealt specifically with s. 2(d), the freedom of association guarantee, the analysis is applicable to all of s. 2. [137] Justice Bastarache, writing for the majority, provided the context for the analysis as follows at para. 22: Where a group is denied a statutory benefit accorded to others, as is the case in this appeal, the normal course is to review this denial under s. 15(1) of the Charter, not s. 2(d) (see Haig v. Canada, [1993] 2 S.C.R. 995; Native Women's Assn. of Canada v. Canada, [1994] 3 S.C.R. 627 ("NWAC"); Delisle, supra). This was properly recognized by Sharpe J. who noted that "by 'dipping its toe in the water', and affording or enhancing the rights of some", the government is not obliged to "go all the way and ensure the enjoyment of rights by all" (p. 207). However, it seems to me that apart from any consideration of a claimant's dignity interest, exclusion from a protective regime may in some contexts amount to an affirmative interference with the effective exercise of a protected freedom. In such a case, it is not so much the differential treatment that is at issue, but the fact that the government is creating conditions which in effect substantially interfere with the exercise of a constitutional right; it has been held in the s. 2(a) context, for example, that "protection of one religion and the concomitant non-protection of others imports disparate impact destructive of the religious freedom of the collectivity" (see Big M Drug Mart, supra, at p. 337). This does not mean that there is a constitutional right to protective legislation per se; it means legislation that is underinclusive may, in unique contexts, substantially impact the exercise of a constitutional freedom. [emphasis in original] [138] Justice Bastarache then set out the analytic framework for the determination of whether the underinclusive legislation substantially impacts the exercise of a constitutional freedom. To that end, a claimant must establish three elements: 1. the claim of underinclusion must be grounded in fundamental Charter freedoms rather than in access to a particular statutory regime; 2. a proper evidentiary foundation must be provided before creating a positive obligation under the Charter. This evidentiary burden is to demonstrate that exclusion from a statutory regime permits a substantial interference with the exercise of protected section 2 activity; and 3. the state must truly be accountable for the inability to exercise the fundamental freedom. [139] Justice Bastarache emphasized at para. 28 that: In sum, while it is generally desirable to confine claims of underinclusion to s. 15(1), it will not be appropriate to do so where the underinclusion results in the effective denial of a fundamental freedom such as the right of association itself. This is not to say that such claims will be common: they are constrained by both s. 32 of the Charter, which demands a minimum of state action before the Charter can be invoked, as well as by the factors discussed above. However, a claim for inclusion should not, in my view, automatically fail a s. 2(d) analysis: depending on the circumstances, freedom of association may, for example, prohibit the selective exclusion of a group from whatever protections are necessary to form and maintain an association, even though there is no constitutional right to such statutory protection per se. In this sense, the burden imposed by s. 2(d) of the Charter differs from that imposed by s. 15(1): while the latter focuses on the effects of underinclusion on human dignity (Law v. Canada (Minister of Employment and Immigration), [1999] 1 S.C.R. 497, the former focuses on the effects of underinclusion on the ability to exercise a fundamental freedom. This distinction is contemplated by the wording of the Charter itself and is supported by subsequent jurisprudence of this Court (see, e.g., Delisle, supra, at para. 25). [140] In the case at bar, I find that the Congregation's claim is not grounded in fundamental Charter freedoms, but is grounded in the assertion of a right to access to a particular statutory regime. Specifically, the assertion is to a right to access a tax exemption through inclusion in the bylaws. Such an assertion was rejected as a basis for a finding of infringement in Haig v. Canada, [1993] 2 S.C.R. 995 and Native Women's Assn. of Canada v. Canada, [1994] 3 S.C.R. 627. As Justice Bastarache stated in Dunmore at para. 24: Thus, in Haig, the majority of this Court held that "[a] government is under no constitutional obligation to extend [a referendum] to anyone, let alone to everyone" (emphasis in original), and further that "[a] referendum as a platform of expression is ... a matter of legislative policy and not of constitutional law" (p. 1041 (emphasis in original)). Similarly, in NWAC, the majority of this Court held that "[i]t cannot be claimed that NWAC has a constitutional right to receive government funding aimed at promoting participation in the constitutional conferences" (p. 654). [141] With respect to the second element, Bastarache J. stated at para. 25: In my view, the evidentiary burden in these cases is to demonstrate that exclusion from a statutory regime permits a substantial interference with the exercise of protected s. 2(d) activity. Such a burden was implied by Dickson C.J. in the Alberta Reference, supra, where he stated that positive obligations may be required "where the absence of government intervention may in effect substantially impede the enjoyment of fundamental freedoms" (p. 361 (emphasis added)). It was also implied by this Court in NWAC, where Sopinka J. stated that "[i]t will be rare indeed that the provision of a platform or funding to one or several organizations will have the effect of suppressing another's freedom of speech" (p. 657 (emphasis added)). These dicta do not require that the exercise of a fundamental freedom be impossible, but they do require that the claimant seek more than a particular channel for exercising his or her fundamental freedoms. [emphasis in original] [142] Justice Bastarache noted further at para. 39 that the simple fact of exclusion from a regime designed to safeguard a fundamental freedom does not necessarily entail a Charter violation: The fact that a regime aims to safeguard a fundamental freedom does not, of course, mean that exclusion from that regime automatically gives rise to a Charter violation. As I discussed in Delisle, supra, a group that proves capable of associating despite its exclusion from a protective regime will be unable to meet the evidentiary burden required of a Charter claim. In such a case, inclusion in a statutory regime cannot be said to safeguard, but rather to enhance, the exercise of a fundamental freedom. In this case, by contrast, the appellants contend that total exclusion from the LRA creates a situation whereby they are substantially incapable of exercising their constitutional right to associate. Needless to say, this claim must be assessed against the factual record provided by both the appellants and the respondents. In contrast with the evidentiary record in Dunmore, the record in the case at bar does not however disclose that this financial burden has resulted in a substantial interference with the protected right under s. 2 of freedom of religion. In the case at bar, they are not made out. Did Council's Refusals to Grant Permissive Exemptions from Taxation Infringe the Petitioners' Right to Equality? [145] Section 15(1) of the Charter provides: Every individual is equal before and under the law and has the right to the equal protection and equal benefit of the law without discrimination and, in particular, without discrimination based on race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, sex, age or mental or physical disability. [146] The Trustees submit that the denial to the Congregation of the permissive exemption from taxation while providing the exemption to all other places of worship in Coquitlam is an unequal treatment under the law that constitutes an infringement of the members' equality rights. [147] The Trustees rely upon the approach to a s. 15(1) analysis articulated by Chief Justice McLachlin in Auton(Guardian ad litem of) v. British Columbia (A.G.), [2004] 3 S.C.R. 657, 2004 SCC 78 at para. 26 [Auton] as follows: (1) Is the claim for a benefit provided by law? If not, what relevant benefit is provided by law? [emphasis in original] (2) Was the relevant benefit denied to the claimants while being granted to a comparator group alike in all ways relevant to benefit, except for the personal characteristic associated with an enumerated or analogous ground? (3) If the claimants succeed on the first two issues, is discrimination established by showing that the distinction denied their equal human worth and human dignity? [148] The Trustees submit that they have been denied the benefit of a permissive exemption from taxation that has been granted to all other places of worship in Coquitlam. Furthermore, they argue, the members of the Congregation are being discriminated against because their religious community, which is growing, required another place of worship. The exclusion from the benefit of a municipal tax exemption is not consistent with the legislative purpose and is discriminatory. Accordingly, the Trustees submit that the second component of the test in Auton is satisfied. [149] The Trustees submit that discrimination is established by reference to the four contextual factors identified in Law v. Canada (Minister of Employment and Immigration), [1999] 1 S.C.R. 497 [Law]. The first factor, as noted in Law at para. 63, is "pre-existing disadvantage, vulnerability, stereotyping, or prejudice experienced by the individual or group." The Trustees submit that the Supreme Court of Canada has identified Jehovah's Witnesses as one of the "discrete and insular minorities" that has "suffered most severely from the historic disadvantage which has adhered to religious identity." (Adler at para. 80). Denying the Congregation the benefit of a tax exemption that all other places of worship in Coquitlam enjoy, results in further stigmatization for this religious minority. [150] The second factor is the degree of correspondence between the ground on which the claim is based and the circumstances of the claimants. The Trustees submit in this regard that the City has given no consideration to the actual circumstances of the Congregation. [151] The third factor is the ameliorative purpose or effect of the impugned law. In this regard, the Trustees submit that this is underinclusive ameliorative legislation that excludes a historically disadvantaged group. [152] The final factor is the nature and scope of the interest affected. The Trustees submit that the interest affected is access to a fundamental social institution. The singling out of the Congregation's place of worship as the only one to be denied the benefit of an exemption is demeaning. [153] Thus, the Trustees submit, a reasonable person, in circumstances similar to the members of the Congregation, would conclude from these four contextual factors that the dignity of the members if being demeaned; they are being marginalized and devalued. The Trustees submit they therefore satisfy the third component of the test set out in Auton and the Charter protected right to equality before and under the law has been violated. [154] The City submits that, following the approach to the analysis of an equality claim established by the Supreme Court of Canada in Law, there has been no discrimination and no denial of equality. [155] In Law the court identified the approach to be taken at pp. 548-49 as follows: (2) The approach adopted and regularly applied by this Court to the interpretation of s. 15(1) focuses upon three central issues: (A) whether a law imposes differential treatment between the claimant and others, in purpose or effect; (B) whether one or more enumerated or analogous grounds of discrimination are the basis for the differential treatment; and (C) whether the law in question has a purpose or effect that is discriminatory within the meaning of the equality guarantee. The first issue is concerned with the question of whether the law causes differential treatment. The second and third issues are concerned with whether the differential treatment constitutes discrimination in the substantive sense intended by s. 15(1). (3) Accordingly, a court that is called upon to determine a discrimination claim under s. 15(1) should make the following three broad inquiries: (A) Does the impugned law (a) draw a formal distinction between the claimant and others on the basis of one or more personal characteristics, or (b) fail to take into account the claimant's already disadvantaged position within Canadian society resulting in substantively differential treatment between the claimant and others on the basis of one or more personal characteristics? (B) Is the claimant subject to differential treatment based on one or more enumerated and analogous grounds? and (C) Does the differential treatment discriminate, by imposing a burden upon or withholding a benefit from the claimant in a manner which reflects the stereotypical application of presumed group or personal characteristics, or which otherwise has the effect of perpetuating or promoting the view that the individual is less capable or worthy of recognition or value as a human being or as a member of Canadian society, equally deserving of concern, respect, and consideration? [156] In the case at bar, the impugned bylaws do not draw a formal distinction between the Congregation and others on the basis of personal characteristics. As the City notes, another Jehovah Witness Congregation located in Coquitlam continues to receive the permissive tax exemption under the bylaws. The Congregation is not subject to different treatment on the basis of an enumerated ground, but on the basis of timing. Timing is not an analogous ground under s. 15. [157] In my view, it is clear that the court in Auton did not eliminate or diminish the requirement that the differential treatment at issue is on the basis of an enumerated or analogous ground. Accordingly, this is sufficient to dispose of the claim under s. 15(1). REMEDY [158] With respect to the administrative law remedy, the Trustees seek an order in the nature of mandamus directing the City to include the Congregation's property in its Permissive Tax Exemption Bylaws for the 2004, 2005, and 2006 taxation years. The Trustees submit that the Community Charter, s. 137(1), permits municipalities to amend their bylaws. They also seek an order directing the City to refund the amounts paid for municipal taxes in 2004 and 2005. [159] The Trustees rely upon the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in Trinity Western University v. College of Teachers, [2001] 1 S.C.R. 772, 2001 SCC 31 [Trinity Western]. In Trinity Western, the court granted an order for mandamus against the BC College of Teachers, a body with discretionary decision-making power. The order of mandamus was justified because the College's reason for denying the applicant's certification was based entirely on irrelevant considerations. There were no other relevant criteria the applicant had to meet to qualify for certification, therefore there was no reason to return the matter to the College for further review. [160] The Trustees submit that similar circumstances exist in the case at bar in that the only reason cited by the City for the exclusion was the Policy. Accordingly, once the Policy cannot be relied upon to exclude the Congregation from inclusion in the permissive exemptions, the relief should follow. [161] The City submits that in the ordinary course the proper administrative remedy is to remit with directions. However, the City goes further and submits that in the case at bar, there is no point in remitting the matter to Council because there is no remedy available in any event even if the conclusion is that the tax exemption should have been granted. [162] In my view, this is not a case like Trinity Western in which it can be said that the only basis for denial of an exemption was a consideration of discriminatory practices. I have concluded that the Community Charter grants the City the discretion to consider whether an exemption will be granted. Further, Council was entitled to adopt the Policy. Council failed in its duty, with respect to its deliberations in relation to Bylaw No. 3663, 2004, in failing to grant the Congregation a hearing. In relation to Bylaw No. 3663, 2004 and Bylaw No. 3718, 2005, Council also failed in its duty in that it fettered its discretion and failed to address its mind to the merits of the Congregation's application. However, it cannot be said that there is only one possible outcome to the deliberations of Council consistent with the exercise of its duty. The court must not usurp the role of Council. [163] On the other hand, I do not accept that to remit the matter to Council would be a pointless exercise because Council lacks the ability to provide any remedy should it come to a different conclusion with respect to whether the Congregation should have been included in the permissive exemptions. I note that Council was able to fashion a remedy to deal with the particular circumstances of the Legion. [164] In the result I remit that applications of the Congregation for permissive exemptions from taxation to Council to be considered in accordance with these reasons and in observance of the lawful exercise of Council's discretionary authority.
