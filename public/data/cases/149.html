Singh Tribunal Counsel for the persons who are the subject of the appeal Meagan Johnston Conseil des personnes en cause Designated representative XXXX XXXX ReprÚsentant(e) dÚsignÚ(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX (Principal Appellant) and XXXX XXXX (Associate Appellant), citizens of Hungary, appealed a decision of the Refugee Protection Division (RPD), dated June 10, 2019, rejecting their claims for refugee protection. DETERMINATION [2] Pursuant to Section 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA),1 the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD) sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes its determination that the Appellants are Convention refugees. [3] In reaching this decision, I have considered the Guidelines on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-Related Persecution (Gender Guidelines).2 BACKGROUND [4] The Principal Appellant alleged before the RPD that she experienced discrimination and racism in Hungary in education, employment, housing, healthcare, and in her daily life because of her Roma ethnicity and that she and her daughter will continue to face the same or worse if they return. [5] The Appellants' applications for refugee protection were heard on May 3, 2019. By a decision of June 10, 2019, the RPD rejected their claims. The RPD found that, although the Principal Appellant faced instances of discrimination, this did not rise to the level of persecution; the Appellants' allegations that they would face this treatment on a forward-looking basis was speculative, and this prospective treatment, cumulatively, would not rise to the level of persecution. [6] The Appellants argue that the RPD erred by failing to consider the objective country conditions evidence of similarly-situated Roma women and girls and by failing to apply the correct test when assessing the Appellants' forward-facing risk. ROLE OF THE RAD [7] The RAD reviews decisions of the RPD on a standard of correctness after conducting an independent assessment of the evidence before it.3 The exception to this rule is where the RPD enjoys a meaningful advantage in the assessment and weighing of oral testimony heard, which, in particular cases, may attract deference. Unless specifically stated below, I have applied a correctness standard. ANALYSIS Rule 29 application [8] In my view, this appeal can be disposed of without admitting the Appellants' new evidence. The proposed evidence does not affect the disposition, based on my findings below. Therefore, it is unnecessary for me to assess whether the submitted documents are admissible. They were not considered in the determination of this appeal. The Appellants have established a serious possibility of persecution [9] I find that the determinative issues in this appeal are whether the Appellants would face discrimination amounting to persecution should they return to Hungary and whether state protection would be available to them. The RPD's Findings [10] The RPD found the Principal Appellant did not face discrimination in being able to access housing and that the Appellants would be able to find housing upon return to Hungary. The RPD also found that the Principal Appellant experienced some discrimination in education, but that there was no risk of discrimination or persecution in education for either Appellant on a forward-looking basis, as the Principal Appellant did not seek to enrol back in school and as the objective evidence supports the Associate Appellant would be eligible to attend school in Hungary. With regard to employment, the RPD found the Principal Appellant faced discrimination that did not amount to persecution, and that the allegation she would not be able to find work in Hungary on a forward-looking basis was speculative. The RPD also found that the allegations about being unable to access medical care in Hungary and the fear of attacks from nationalist Hungarians were speculative. The RPD found that the Appellants' prospective treatment may include discrimination in certain aspects, but cumulatively it did not rise to the level of persecution. The Appellants' Arguments [11] The Appellants argue that the RPD erred by not conducting a forward-looking assessment and by failing to examine the treatment of similarly situated individuals. They point to several areas in the objective evidence to show the risks the Appellants face in Hungary and argue that the cumulative discrimination would amount to persecution. They also argue that adequate state protection would not be reasonably forthcoming to them. The RAD's Assessment [12] I have reviewed the record and conducted an independent assessment of the evidence. I find the Appellants' arguments regarding the RPD's errors and the cumulative effect of the discrimination they experienced and would experience on a forward-looking basis in Hungary have merit. [13] I find the RPD erred in assessing whether the Appellants' experiences of discrimination amounted to persecution cumulatively, both in the past and on a forward-looking basis. The RPD erred by finding that the Appellants had not established a link between the documentary evidence and their personal circumstances. There is, as the RPD notes, case law which states that not all Roma individuals are refugees based upon the country documents and that the Appellant must establish a link between their circumstances and the country documentation.4 However, I find the RPD's reasons as to why the Appellants have not established a link between the documentary evidence and their specific evidence are incorrect. [14] The documentary evidence, which the RPD accepts, is that Roma individuals in Hungary face discrimination in various areas, including employment, education, housing, medical care, and access to public spaces. The RPD accepted that the Principal Appellant experienced discrimination in several of these areas. Given the evidence which was accepted as credible, I find such a link exists. Here, the Appellants are a young woman with an XXXX XXXX XXXX and some secondary education, who was unable to find employment in Hungary, and her young daughter. Given these circumstances, I find the RPD erred by failing to consider the country evidence as a context in which to determine whether the Appellants have a forward-facing risk in Hungary. [15] For these reasons, I find that the RPD erred in its assessment of cumulative discrimination. [16] On an independent assessment of the record, I find that the Appellants have established a serious possibility of cumulative discrimination amounting to persecution based on their Roma ethnicity. [17] In coming to this decision, I have considered the facts accepted by the RPD; namely, that the Appellants are of Roma ethnicity, the Principal Appellant faced discrimination in education and employment, she suffers from an XXXX XXXX XXXX for which she sees her specialist every 2 weeks in Canada, and the Principal Appellant's parents in Hungary told her about their experiences with nationalist Hungarians and anti-Roma groups. [18] As explained in further detail below, the objective evidence supports that Roma individuals face discrimination in healthcare and that violence against Roma individuals is an ongoing problem. Considering the Appellants' personal circumstances and based on the Principal Appellant's XXXX XXXX medical needs, and the experiences of her parents in Hungary, I find the Appellants would face a risk in accessing medical care and from the nationalist Hungarians and anti-Roma groups. [19] I accept that, when some of these facts are considered separately, they may be considered discrimination, but when I consider these facts cumulatively, together with the Appellants' personal circumstances, the risk they would face in accessing medical care and from the nationalist Hungarians and anti-Roma groups, and the context of the country conditions, I find that the Appellants face a serious possibility of persecution. [20] With respect to the Appellants' personal circumstances, I find that they increase the risk of discrimination in the future. First, the Appellants are a single mother with a young daughter. As explained by the RPD, the Principal Appellant moved from Hungary to the United Kingdom (UK) with her ex-common-law partner but left the UK for Canada because he was abusive. The Principal Appellant is thirty-two years old; she has some secondary education and work history in the UK, but she was unable to get employment in Hungary. These personal circumstances increase the instability that the Appellants will face in Hungary. The documentary evidence shows that women generally, in Hungary, and Romani women in particular, experience higher levels of discrimination and violence.5 Additionally, given the discrimination the Principal Appellant faced in the past and her inability to obtain employment in Hungary, she is more vulnerable to future unemployment or underemployment which will affect her ability to get housing and secure a stable life. [21] Second, the Associate Appellant is a three-year-old girl. In addition to facing extra vulnerabilities as a girl, she also faces additional vulnerabilities based on her age. According to the objective evidence, segregation of Romani children along with misdiagnosis of Romani children as mentally disabled continues in Hungary.6 In fact, according to the most recent United States Department of State report, segregation increased almost ten percent between 2008 and 2016.7 While the Associate Appellant may not be denied education in Hungary outright, the evidence shows that she is at risk of being denied access to the same quality of education as other Hungarian children. This has the ability to impact the rest of her life, as education impacts employment, housing and many other areas of life. [22] Similarly, when I consider the country conditions more broadly, I find this also supports my conclusion that the Appellants face a serious possibility of persecution. As outlined above, the RPD found that the Appellants had not established a link between their personal circumstances and the country conditions, and I find this to be incorrect. The country documents, broadly, show that Hungarian Roma individuals face discrimination "in all fields of life, including education, housing, employment, healthcare ... and political participation."8 Given that the RPD accepted that the Principal Appellant personally faced discrimination in education and employment, and the Appellants' personal circumstances of being a single mother with some secondary education, a XXXX, and a young daughter, I find that this establishes a connection to the country documents. [23] I find the Principal Appellant testified credibly about her experiences of discrimination and her fears for herself and the Associate Appellant should they return to Hungary. I have considered their testimony, as well as the information contained in the Appellants' Basis of Claim forms, and I find that the Principal Appellant experienced incidents of discrimination which ultimately led her to leave her home country. I am guided by the UNHCR handbook which states that: 54. Differences in the treatment of various groups do indeed exist to a greater or lesser extent in many societies. Persons who receive less favourable treatment as a result of such differences are not necessarily victims of persecution. It is only in certain circumstances that discrimination will amount to persecution. This would be so if measures of discrimination lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature for the person concerned, e.g. serious restrictions on his right to earn his livelihood, his right to practice his religion, or his access to normally available educational facilities. 55. Where measures of discrimination are, in themselves, not of a serious character, they may nevertheless give rise to a reasonable fear of persecution if they produce, in the mind of the person concerned, a feeling of apprehension and insecurity as regards his future existence. Whether or not such measures of discrimination in themselves amount to persecution must be determined in the light of all the circumstances. A claim to fear of persecution will of course be stronger where a person has been the victim of a number of discriminatory measures of this type and where there is thus a cumulative element involved.9 [24] In additional to the above, I have also considered the following documentary evidence regarding the situation for Roma individuals in Hungary. The United States Department of State report notes that human rights Non-Governmental Organizations continued to report that Roma individuals suffer social and economic exclusion and discrimination in almost all fields of life.10 It is also noted in this document that Romani woman can suffer multiple forms of discrimination on the basis of gender, ethnicity, class and that they experienced barriers to equal access in education, healthcare, housing, employment, and justice. [25] There exists prejudice by the police against the Roma community and ethnic profiling. It is noted that police response to hate crimes is not adequate.11 There is evidence that right-wing paramilitary groups organize demonstrations against Roma individuals, Jews, and homosexuals.12 Racist violence against Roma individuals remains one of the most pressing issues in the country. Between January 2009 and September 2013 there were more than 100 cases of alleged hate-motivated violence targeting Roma individuals and/or their property. Roma individuals are often subjected to hate speech. These problems have been sustained by the continued hostility Roma individuals face from police forces and officials, reflected in persistent discriminatory practices including ethnic profiling and fines for even the most minor infractions. This also extends to a failure to protect Roma community members from attack.13 [26] Roma individuals in Hungary face discrimination in all fields of life including education, housing, employment, health care, and political participation. The Hungarian Ombudsman has reported that the disadvantageous social situation of Roma individuals is aggravated by discrimination in all of these fields. Direct and indirect discrimination in employment prevents many Roma individuals from breaking the cycle of poverty, and Roma individuals are in the most disadvantaged position in the labour market.14 A report from the Hungarian Central Statistics Office cites that the employment rate for Roma individuals was only 25.9 percent in 2014, as compared to the employment rate for non-Roma individuals, which was 60 percent. This report also states that 67.9 percent of Roma individuals were at risk of poverty. It is noted that 500,000 to 600,000 or 60 per cent of Roma individuals live in rural or disadvantaged regions of Hungary, mostly in segregated residential zones in poor conditions, some of which are classified as "impoverished and overcrowded Roma ghettos."15 [27] It is reported that there is an increasing rate of evictions against disadvantaged people and a decreasing amount of social housing. Roma individuals often face discrimination in accessing social housing and municipalities use a variety of techniques to prevent Roma individuals from living in more desirable urban neighbourhoods.16 [28] Segregation of Roma children is widespread, and there is systemic discrimination against Roma students.17 [29] With regard to health care, there is information that Roma women often lack access to medical services due to direct discrimination and degrading treatment at the hospital. It is reported that Roma women are being segregated in certain medical facilities.18 [30] The documentary evidence references efforts to integrate Roma individuals into the general population. However, there is little information on how the government initiatives have benefited Roma individuals specifically.19 Moreover, the Hungarian state continues to have issues with nationalist groups with anti Roma rhetoric, including members of the government.20 [31] This is not to say that Hungary is not making any efforts to improve the situation of Roma; however, the country conditions show that, currently, the general climate in Hungary for Roma is still rife with discrimination, and it is one which the government is creating a situation of insecurity for Roma people. Moreover, given that the Appellants are young females, these conditions are more likely to have an adverse impact on their lives. [32] Having considered the country conditions evidence concerning the situation for Roma individuals in Hungary, as well as the Appellants' personal circumstances and their past experiences of discrimination cumulatively, I find that the Appellants face an insecure future with regard to accessing adequate education, housing, employment, and medical care. Therefore, I find that the cumulative discrimination they have experienced gives rise to a serious possibility of persecution, should they return to Hungary, and that the Appellants would experience discrimination which would cumulatively lead to consequences of a substantially prejudicial nature that would amount to persecution should they return to Hungary. As this persecution is based upon ethnicity, which is a Convention ground, I find the Appellants meet the test for Convention refugee status. [33] I further find that the documentary evidence does not indicate that the police are providing adequate state protection with regard to crimes against Roma individuals. I therefore find that adequate state protection would not be available to the Appellants in Hungary. I further find that there is no Internal Flight Alternative available to the Appellants in Hungary, as the situation for Roma individuals is consistent throughout the country. CONCLUSION [34] The RAD finds that there is a serious possibility that the Appellants would suffer persecution should they return to Hungary because of their Roma ethnicity. For this reason, the RAD cannot uphold the decision of the RPD. [35] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to section 111(1)(b) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes its own determination that the Appellants are Convention refugees.
