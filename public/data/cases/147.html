The principal Appellant is the single mother of the adult female Appellant and the minor male Appellant. The adult female Appellant is the single mother of the minor female Appellant. XXXX XXXX XXXX was appointed as the Designated Representative for both minor children by the RPD and this designation has been confirmed by the RAD. [2] The Appellants are making an application under Rule 29 of the Refugee Appeal Division ("RAD") Rules for the admission of new evidence and further submissions after the appeal was perfected. They request that the appeal be allowed, that the decision of the RPD be set aside and the Refugee Appeal Division ("RAD") find the Appellants to be Convention refugees or persons in need of protection. Alternatively, they request that the matter be returned to the RPD for redetermination. DETERMINATION OF THE APPEAL [3] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to s. 111(1)(b) of the IRPA, the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes a determination that, in its opinion, should have been made, that the Appellants are Convention refugees. Background [4] The principal Appellant states that she and the other Appellants are Hungarian Roma. She maintains that she and her children and grandchild have been discriminated against in many areas of their lives due to their ethnicity. They fear increasing violence against Roma, encouraged by political parties like Jobbik and carried out by organized groups such as the Hungarian Guard and skinheads. [5] The principal Appellant states that she only finished grade 5. She had to go to a special school that only included Roma after she failed grade 1. She had to drop out of school at age 13 because her mother had passed away, and she had to help her father raise her siblings. [6] The female adult Appellant went to her Roma school that was poorly equipped and the students were taught at a simpler level than in regular schools. She felt intimidated because she was a quiet student, and the teachers yelled at her to talk more. She dropped out of school after grade 9 when she became pregnant. She never lived with the father of her child who did not admit paternity. She always lived with the principal Appellant and had difficulty finding work due to her ethnicity. During one period of time, she worked for the city cleaning streets and then received social assistance. She also received the child benefit which contributed to the Appellants being able to survive. [7] The principal Appellant states that the minor male Appellant was bullied by Hungarian students when he was in school. When he was in grade 5, the principal Appellant was told by school officials that she should take him out of school and home school him. She did this, but she said it was very hard. They received very little assistance from the school or anyone else. He just barely passed, and she feels he has not received a proper education. He completed grade 7. [8] The minor female Appellant went to a Roma kindergarten. The principal Appellant states that her granddaughter was afraid of her teacher because she was yelled at all of the time, and she was slapped on her hands and shoulders. The adults complained several times about this treatment, but the teacher just denied it and nothing was done. They took her out of the kindergarten in December 2014, and she stayed home until the following September. They put her in a different kindergarten in September 2015, that contained Hungarian and Roma children, but the Roma children were segregated. Some of the Hungarian boys would pick on her and hit her in the play yard. They complained, but they were told that no one saw anything happen. They had a difficult time getting her to go to kindergarten. [9] The principal Appellant states that she regularly visited the employment center for help in finding work. She applied for many jobs, but she was unsuccessful. Some told her that they would not hire her because she was a "gypsy". Others used excuses like the job was taken or that she did not have the right training. In 2015, she worked for the city cleaning streets, and later she received social assistance. [10] She says that the minor male Appellant was frequently ill, and she could not buy him medication. He is obese and has high blood pressure and asthma. She does not think he will be properly cared for in Hungary. [11] When the adult female Appellant gave birth, she was segregated with other Roma women away from Hungarian women. She says it was humiliating to be treated that way, and the Roma women received less attention. She asked for painkillers, but she was not given any because they could not afford to pay for it. The principal Appellant says that, in 2002, she had a magnetic resonance image (MRI) done, and it showed that she had cysts in her womb. It turned out that everything was fine, but she found the doctor's words and attitude to her to be frightening. [12] The principal Appellant states that she met her common-law partner in December 1992, and her first child was born in 1993. They called an ambulance when she went into labour, but they had to wait two hours for it to arrive. When it arrived, she was told that they were not obligated to take her to the hospital. Her partner argued with them, but they were forced to call a taxi to go to the hospital. When she arrived at the hospital, there were only Hungarians in the room, and, as she started to unpack, a nurse told her that she would be taken to another room. There were only Roma women in the other room. When she asked for a painkiller, the nurse said it was not included in the hospitalization. If she wanted it, she needed to pay for it, but she could not pay for it as they had no money. [13] She states that, around February 2009, she broke up with her common-law partner. They were always arguing because neither of them had a job, and he started to drink and became an alcoholic. He physically abused her and her child. There were several nights when they ran away and spent the night on the street. They did not call the police because she knew that they did not get involved in family matters "unless there was blood". Her partner threatened that, if she reported him to the police, he would kill her and the children. [14] The principal Appellant states that when her mother died her sister continued renting the home where they lived from XXXX XXXX. Only her sister's name was on the apartment contract, but she lived there and was registered there with her children. In August 2015, all of them were evicted from the apartment, but her sister received a one-bedroom apartment from XXXX XXXX. This apartment was too small for all of them, and they requested a larger apartment. She states that they tried to convince XXXX XXXX that they needed a bigger place, since the principal Appellant and her children were registered in the house from which they were evicted, but they were rejected. [15] Following the eviction, the Appellants rented two other places including one on XXXX XXXX in XXXX, but they could not register themselves at either of these places. She maintains that a doctor reported her and the female adult Appellant to the Children's Aid. He said that they were not properly caring for the children. They wanted to take away the children because they did not have a registered address. She then asked for help from relatives to buy tickets to come to Canada. ROLE OF THE RAD [16] The decision of the Federal Court of Appeal in Huruglica1 provides guidance in relation to the appropriate standard that must be applied by a specialized tribunal, such as the RAD, to decisions of the first-level administrative tribunal. [17] The Court stated that, with respect to findings of fact (and mixed fact and law) which raise no issue of credibility of oral evidence, the RAD is to review RPD decisions by applying the correctness standard. Questions of law are also to be reviewed on a correctness standard. Pursuant to s. 111(2)(b) of the IRPA, the RAD may refer the matter back to the RPD for redetermination if it is "of the opinion" that it cannot make a decision confirming or setting aside the RPD's decision without hearing the evidence presented before the RPD. This recognizes that there may be cases where the RPD enjoys a meaningful advantage over the RAD in making findings of fact or mixed fact and law, because those cases require an assessment of credibility or weight to be given to the oral evidence it hears. It further indicates that, although the RAD should sometimes exercise a degree of restraint before substituting its own determination, the issue of whether the circumstances warrant such restraint ought to be addressed on a case-by-case basis. [18] Accordingly, the RAD will conduct its assessment of the RPD's decision on the basis of the principles outlined above. ANALYSIS OF THE MERITS OF THE APPEAL [19] The RPD determined that the Appellants were not Convention refugees or persons in need of protection for the following reasons: (a) The Appellants were not persecuted by the principal Appellant's former common-law partner after February 2009; (b) The discrimination suffered by the Appellants in Hungary did not cumulatively amount to persecution because it is not a sustained or systemic violation of their human rights; and (c) The Appellants did not rebut the presumption of state protection. Rule 29 Application [20] The Appellants submit the following documents as new evidence: (a) A report published by the Council of Europe entitled "Fourth Opinion on Hungary", dated September 12, 2016; and [21] A Supplementary Memorandum of Argument. RAD Rule 29 provides: 29 (1) A person who is the subject of an appeal who does not provide a document or written submissions with the appellant's record, respondent's record or reply record must not use the document or provide the written submissions in the appeal unless allowed to do so by the Division. (2) If a person who is the subject of an appeal wants to use a document or provide written submissions that were not previously provided, the person must make an application to the Division in accordance with rule 37. (3) The person who is the subject of the appeal must include in an application to use a document that was not previously provided an explanation of how the document meets the requirements of subsection 110(4) of the Act and how that evidence relates to the person, unless the document is being presented in response to evidence presented by the Minister. (4) In deciding whether to allow an application, the Division must consider any relevant factors, including (a) the document's relevance and probative value; (b) any new evidence the document brings to the appeal; and (c) whether the person who is the subject of the appeal, with reasonable effort, could have provided the document or written submissions with the appellant's record, respondent's record or reply record. [22] The Council of Europe Report arose after the RPD rejected the Appellants' claim. The Appellants submit that this new evidence is admissible, pursuant to subsection 110(4) of the IRPA, as it supplements and updates the general information as to the situation of Roma in Hungary. [23] The RAD will admit the documents into evidence as the report of the Council of Europe arose after the RPD's decision in this matter, dated July 7, 2016. The document is credible, as it is prepared by an official organization in Europe, and it is relevant to the issues of discrimination and persecution of Roma in Hungary. [24] The Supplementary Memorandum of Argument will also be admitted as it refers to the new evidence accepted by the RAD. Submissions [25] The Appellants submit that the RPD erred in not taking into consideration what it set out in the Chairperson's Guidelines on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-Related Persecution2 as to the difficulties female victims of domestic abuse have in relating what occurred to them. The RPD made negative credibility findings on the basis that when and where the abuse took place was not included in the Basis of Claim ("BOC") narrative. Precise dates and precise locations where abuse occurred are merely elaborative details that need not be included in the BOC narrative. [26] The Appellants further submit that the RPD erred in considering their experiences and fears regarding access to education, employment, health care, and housing on an individual basis in isolation from each other. These various factors should be considered on a cumulative basis. The RPD made a finding on this issue but failed to provide reasons in support of this conclusion. [27] The Appellants also argue that the RPD erred in finding that their experiences in Hungary did not amount to persecution as a result of sustained or systemic violation of their human rights. When the situation faced by Roma in Hungary such as the Appellants in terms of access to education, employment, health care, and housing is examined on a cumulative basis and in light of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, it is clear that there is a sustained or systemic violation of their human rights. [28] The Appellants also state that the RPD erred in its state protection analysis. It erred in failing to consider whether the efforts of the Hungarian government actually translated into adequate state protection at the operational level. It also erred in placing reliance on governmental and non-governmental bodies and agencies other than the police as evidence of state protection. Specifically, the RPD erred in relying upon the Equal Treatment Authority ("ETA") as an organization that provides protection. The RPD also ignored contradictory evidence and did not consider that the existence and activities of the Jobbik party demonstrates a failure of state protection in Hungary for Roma persons such as the Appellants. [29] The Appellants also submit that the Council of Europe's report entitled "Fourth Opinion on Hungary" shows that Roma continue to suffer systemic discrimination and inequality in all fields of life, including housing, employment, education, access to health and participation in social and political life. The consequences of the combined factors of extreme poverty, unemployment and inadequate housing significantly shorten life expectancy of the Roma. There is also a widely acknowledged perception of an increase in xenophobia and intolerance within the Hungarian society directed in particular against the Roma and the Jewish community. Analysis [30] The RPD found that the Appellants are ethnic Roma. [31] The RPD found that the principal Appellant failed to establish that, on a balance of probabilities, she and the other Appellants were recently persecuted by her former common-law partner or that there is a serious possibility that they would be persecuted by this individual if they returned to Hungary. [32] The RPD made these findings on the basis that the principal Appellant testified that she had been physically assaulted by her former common-law partner in February 2016 and many times in 2015. The RPD pointed out that she had not disclosed in her BOC any incidents of physical abuse after she broke up with her common-law partner in 2009. The principal Appellant responded that she generally stated that her common-law partner abused her, and no one asked her to state when and where she was abused. Counsel submitted that the deficiency was a mere lack of detail. The RPD did not accept these explanations, stating that the BOC was arguably incomplete and inaccurate and not merely lacking in detail. [33] The RPD stated that, while it was sensitive to the difficulty female claimants have in recounting painful episodes of domestic abuse, and it recognized the limited education of the principal Appellant, it was reasonable to expect that she would mention, at the very least, when the last acts of abuse took place, especially if they occurred barely a month prior to arrival in Canada. The RPD drew a negative credibility finding from this omission. [34] In addition, the RPD found that the principal Appellant embellished her testimony with the risk from her former partner. The RPD drew an adverse inference from this embellishment as to the principal Appellant's general credibility. The RPD found that there was insufficient credible evidence to establish a serious risk of persecution on a forward-looking basis from the former common-law partner. The RPD also found that the Appellants were not persecuted by the principal Appellant's former common-law partner after February 2009. [35] The RAD has reviewed the recording of the hearing. The RPD asked the principal Appellant whether she was still frightened of her common-law partner. She replied that she was still frightened because when he is drunk he always contacts her. She stated that he knows where she lives, and he would come into the house and provoke her. If she stood up for herself, he would beat her. She was asked if he does this frequently, and she replied many times. She was asked, "When was the last time he beat you?" And she replied, "February 2016." She stated that he slapped her, pushed and pulled her, and used bad language. She stated that he always gets to know where the family is living. [36] She was asked if she went to the police after he beat her in 2016. She replied "No, because they don't do anything." She was asked why she said that, and she responded that she has acquaintances who have gone through the same thing. "The police don't help them." She was asked if he beat her in 2015. She stated that she could not remember, but it must have occurred because it happened whenever he contacted her. When she was asked if she remembered any incident in 2015, she responded, "It happened several times." She was then asked why she did not put this information in her BOC. She stated that she just said he assaulted her, but they did not ask her exactly what would happen. [37] The RAD does not agree with the RPD's conclusion that the principal Appellant was not persecuted by her former common-law partner after February 2009. She stated in her BOC narrative that her former common-law partner physically abused her and her child. There was no oral or documentary evidence presented as to abuse of any of the children. The principal Appellant testified as to abuse in 2015 and February 2016. The RAD does not agree that an omission of these incidents from the BOC narrative leads to a conclusion that she was not abused after 2009. The RPD finds that the principal Appellant was harassed and abused by her ex-common-law partner after 2009. She testified that she did not report any incident of abuse to the police because she had acquaintances who had been abused and had gone to the police, but they did not receive any help. She was not asked at the RPD hearing to elaborate any further. She stated that the police would not intervene unless there was blood. [38] The Chairperson's Guidelines on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-Related Persecution3 states that: "Women refugee claimants face special problems in demonstrating that their claims are credible and trustworthy." Factors including the "social, cultural, religious, and economic context in which the claimant finds herself should be considered in determining whether it was objectively unreasonable for the claimant not to have sought state protection." For the treatment to likely amount to persecution, it must be a serious form of harm which detracts from the claimant's fundamental human rights. "When considering whether it is objectively unreasonable for the claimant not to have sought the protection of state, the decision-maker should consider, among other relevant factors, the social, cultural, religious, and economic context in which the claimant finds herself." [39] The Criminal Code of Hungary includes "violence within the partnership" (domestic violence) as a separate category of offense. By law, certain cases of regularly committed physical assault, defamation, violation of personal freedom, and coercion are more severely punished if the offender and the victim live together or had lived together or if a child has been born as a result of their relationship. The offense relates not only to current spouses, but also to former spouses, common-law partners, those under guardianship or care, guardians and caretakers. Regulations extend prison sentences for assault (light bodily harm) and defamation to three years if committed in the above context. Police and courts may impose restraining orders. By law, police called to a scene of domestic violence may issue an emergency restraining order valid for three days in lieu of immediately filing charges, while courts may issue up to 60-day "preventive restraining orders" in civil cases.4 [40] Women's rights non-government organizations (NGOs) continue to criticize the law and its application for failing to provide appropriate protection for victims and for not placing sufficient emphasis on the accountability of perpetrators. The Ministry of Human Capacities continues to operate a 24-hour toll-free hotline for victims of abuse to provide information and, if necessary, to coordinate the immediate placement of victims in shelters. The Ministry operated shelters at 14 locations (with 98 beds) for victims of domestic violence, providing immediate accommodation and complex care for abused individuals and families for up to 90 days. The Ministry continued to operate four halfway houses, providing long-term housing opportunities (maximum five years), professional reintegration assistance for families graduated from shelters, and assistance to prevent secondary victimization. Government also sponsored secret shelters for severely abused women whose lives were in danger. [41] According to women's rights NGOs, services for survivors of violence against women either operated with limited capacity or did not meet international standards of good practice. NGOs complained that, despite some positive legislative measures in recent years, the comprehensive prevention, protection, and prosecution approach was missing from the state's response to domestic violence, as well as to other forms of violence against women. NGOs criticize the improper application of existing laws and regulations, the lack of systematic training and protocols for professionals, and the limited availability of proper victim support services.5 [42] Human Rights Watch notes that Roma women are "particularly disadvantaged" in accessing protection from domestic violence, adding that "poverty, unemployment and social exclusion" increase the risk of violence against women. A 2013 study by the European Parliament on the empowerment of Roma women in Hungary states that Roma women are in a condition of "high vulnerability and therefore exposed to violence," including domestic violence. The head of Colorful Pearls Association for Women in Southern Hungary, an organization that works to integrate Roma women, stated that Roma women face societal and family pressure to stay in abusive relationships, and may be less informed about the "scarce" resources for assistance.6 [43] The Human Rights Watch researcher explained that Roma women do not trust the police and did not turn to the police for assistance; furthermore, the police do not respond to calls for assistance, if they are approached. NGOs state that "discrimination and hostility" from law enforcement agencies and the courts against Roma are "likely to have a detrimental effect on the Roma women" calling the police. The head of Colorful Pearls Association indicated that police often do not take cases of domestic violence in Roma family seriously and will only interfere "if blood flows" NANE et al. observed that, while they were providing training to police officers on domestic violence, it was "not uncommon to meet stereotyping (even profiling) attitudes among police regarding Roma women and men in general, "particularly regarding domestic violence, which is perceived as common and "normal" among Roma. NANE et al also explained that, due to their economic situation, Roma women are less likely to be able to access legal redress or initiate criminal proceedings against the perpetrator of the abuse as some procedures cost money, such as medical certificates and criminal procedures for lesser crimes of bodily harm where injuries have a healing time of eight days. The Human Rights Watch researcher stated that it is very, very difficult and rare for Roma women to seek assistance outside their communities and, in particular, from state authorities, whom they do not trust.7 [44] The Human Rights Watch researcher indicated that, although the Criminal Code was changed in 2013 to include domestic violence offenses, since 2013, the new law is not functioning of practice. Attitudes remained unchanged, authorities do not treat domestic violence as a serious crime, and the change of law has not impacted the situation of domestic violence, or the response of the state, which is described is inadequate.8 [45] According to an associate professor, the police are rarely called in situations of domestic violence. The Human Rights Watch researcher indicated that Hungarian police and judicial authorities perceived domestic violence to be a family issue. She explained that, when police are approached for assistance from domestic violence victims, police blame the victim and, for example, will tell a woman who is beaten by her partner to return home to change her behaviour.9 [46] This documentary evidence corroborates the testimony of the principal Appellant as to why it was not reasonable for her to go to the police when she was physically assaulted by her former partner. The latest assault took place in February 2016, and the RPD finds that there is a serious possibility that she will be assaulted if she returns to Hungary, and there is clear and convincing evidence that state protection will not be available to her. The RAD finds that she has rebutted the presumption of state protection. [47] The RPD found that the Appellants were provided with adequate (although not perfect) access to health care. The RPD also found that the Appellants did not establish, on a balance of probabilities, that they were persecuted because of a lack of affordable housing. The RPD did state that it was prepared to accept that the Appellants suffered discrimination in education and employment (and on a balance of probabilities with respect to housing insofar as they encounter racism in renting apartments in general). However, the RPD disagreed that this discrimination cumulatively amounted to persecution because it was not a sustained or systemic violation of their human rights. [48] The Council of Europe's Fourth Opinion on Hungary10 states: "Roma continue to suffer systemic discrimination and inequality in all fields of life including housing, employment, education, access to health and participation in social and political life." According to evidence collected by the authorities, segregation of Roma children in recent years has become more widespread. Alarmingly, discrimination against Roma children has deepened, in particular on account of the concept of "remedial segregation", promoted by the authorities, according to which Roma children are expected to "catch up" in separate Roma classes before inclusion in the mainstream education, which never happens. Although the notion of separate Roma classes has over the years been conclusively proven effective in providing quality education and increasing the chances of inclusion of Roma children in mainstream education, it has survived in Hungary and is even being promoted and justified." The employment rate of the Roma population barely reaches twenty percent. Ten percent unemployment rate amongst Roma women is particularly alarming. The consequence of the combined factors of extreme poverty, unemployment and inadequate housing significantly shortens life expectancy of the Roma. In fact the life expectancy of the Roma is 10 years shorter than the average Hungarian. The Advisory Committee finds the systemic discrimination of Roma children in the field of education deplorable. Not only has there been no progress with the segregation in schools, but on the contrary the proportion of Roma children attending segregated schools has risen in recent years.11 [49] In this particular case, the education difficulties of the two minor Appellants case are corroborated by the above paragraph. The RPD finds that if the children did return to Hungary they would continue to be subject to this discrimination that would, on a balance of probabilities, result in a sustained or systemic violation of their basic human right to an education. [50] The Council of Europe's report also states that the local authorities playing a pivotal role in any such scheme have not always displayed a positive and benevolent attitude towards Roma when addressing their housing needs. In particular, the situation in Miskolc, the fourth biggest town in Hungary, led to a tense stand-off in May 2014 between some 450 Roma residents and local authorities. The local authorities, having decided to relocate residents of a Roma slum located on the outskirts of the town, conducted repeated "raids" of inspectors accompanied by the police controlling the maintenance of the housing units, investigating respect for refuse collection and recycling, checking adherence to pet-keeping regulations and observing the general state of the municipal property in which the apartments were located. Often social workers responsible for child protection would be part of such teams which, according to the Advisory Committee interlocutors, was a clearly implicit threat to "non-cooperative" Roma families of taking children into care on any number of pretexts. The conflict in Miskolc led to the intervention of the Commissioner for Fundamental Rights and the Deputy Commissioner for the Protection of Nationalities who, in a joint report, identified a number of human rights violations and made recommendations to the City Council and other authorities concerned. The Advisory Committee regrets to note that, upon publication of the report and recommendations, the Mayor of Miskolc stated in a press conference that inspections would continue regardless of the expressed concerns and calls for an end to such practices.12 [51] Again, in this particular case, the above paragraph corroborates the evidence given by the Appellants. [52] The RAD finds that, in this particular case, the discrimination suffered by the Appellants cumulatively amounts to persecution, and there is a serious possibility that they will continue to be persecuted if they return to Hungary. DISPOSITION [53] The appeal is allowed. Pursuant to s. 111(1)(b) of the IRPA, the RAD sets aside the determination of the RPD and substitutes a determination that, in its opinion, should have been made, that the Appellants are Convention refugees.
