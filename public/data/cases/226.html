Meyer v. Danka Business Systems Ltd.

2001BCSC0428 Citation: Meyer v. Danka Business Systems Ltd. Date: 20010320 2001 BCSC 428 Docket: C980762 Registry: Vancouver IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA BETWEEN: VICTOR MEYER PLAINTIFF AND: DANKA BUSINESS SYSTEMS LIMITED DEFENDANT REASONS FOR JUDGMENT OF THE HONOURABLE MR. JUSTICE BAUMAN Counsel for the Plaintiff D.J. Mullan, Q.C. and D.J. Wallin Counsel for the Defendant E. G. Phillips and M. S. Boyd Date and Place of Hearing/Trial: 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 11, 12 and 13 December 2000 Vancouver, B.C. I. OVERVIEW [1] Victor Meyer is a 55 year old man who has worked in the business machines industry as a salesperson since 1979. [2] In October 1993, he joined Verster Office Systems Inc. ("Verster"). That company concentrated on the marketing of the Sharp product line of photocopiers. [3] Verster was acquired by the defendant, Danka Business Systems Limited ("Danka"), in January 1995 and Victor Meyer, with his Verster colleagues, simply continued to work in their same capacities for Danka. [4] By 1997, Mr. Meyer's position with Danka was variously termed "Major Accounts Manager" or "Senior Accounts Executive". In any event, it is common ground that Mr. Meyer was a senior salesperson with Danka, but that he did not perform any supervisory functions. [5] On 9 September 1997, Mr. Meyer's employment with Danka was terminated. It is now conceded by Danka that the termination was without cause. [6] Victor Meyer sues for damages for wrongful dismissal. [7] He also seeks damages under various heads of loss which I will later detail but characterize here, compendiously, as Wallace damages: Wallace v. United Grain Growers Ltd., [1997] 3 S.C.R. 701, 152 D.L.R. (4th) 1. II. FACTS [8] Danka is a large international business systems company. It sells, leases and services various business machines, including photocopiers and fax machines, to small and large businesses. [9] At least in Canada, on the evidence before me, it derives the vast bulk of its operating profits from the regular servicing of the machines which it places. [10] In the mid-1990's, Danka expanded into British Columbia by acquiring Verster. At the same time, it acquired Basic Business Systems ("Basic"), a Sharp product dealer in the Fraser Valley. [11] With the acquisition of Basic, Danka inherited two large customers - The Jim Pattison Group of Companies, in particular its Overwaitea grocery division, and Surrey Memorial Hospital. [12] The plaintiff came to service these clients and they became the largest accounts in his successful sales efforts for Danka. [13] Danka's Vancouver operation was divided between sales and service. On the sales side, a number of sales teams were established under various sales managers. The operation in turn was headed by a Vancouver Market Manager. [14] At most relevant times to this litigation, the incumbent Vancouver Market Manager was an individual who was, I can only conclude, the nemesis of Victor Meyer. [15] This individual left Danka in 1999. He did not give evidence at trial. Few of the witnesses who did give evidence at trial had little good to say of this person. But as he wasn't before me to respond, and because I must nevertheless reach unflattering conclusions on the evidence concerning him, I will only refer to him as "S.". [16] Wallace damages potentially arise when an employer's termination of an employee is accompanied by acts of bad faith in the manner of discharge. [17] "Bad Faith", includes conduct which is untruthful, misleading or unduly insensitive. (per Iacobucci J. in Wallace at para. 98). [18] In advancing his case for Wallace damages, the plaintiff effectively adopts the theory that his termination was a drawn out affair which really began in or about the spring of 1996 and culminated on 9 September 1997. In this manner, the plaintiff relies upon a number of workplace incidents during this period in arguing bad faith in the manner of his discharge. [19] I will deal with the various incidents under descriptive headings. In doing so I will lump similar incidents together with the result that my recital will not always be chronological. [20] The incidents will be discussed under these headings: (i) The 1996 Atlanta Conference; (ii) Poaching Sales; (iii) The Sharp Product Launch; (iv) Meter Tampering; (v) Summer of 1997; and (vi) Meetings with Huntington. [21] Under the last heading, I will introduce Garry Huntington. He is now the CEO of Danka (Canada) and he was at all material times relevant to these proceedings the Vice-President, Western Canada. He was stationed in Winnipeg and he was the superior of S. He was the person who terminated the plaintiff's employment. [22] Of the incidents, those discussed under "Meter Tampering" are very important. These include the allegation by Victor Meyer that S., and others in the Vancouver office, turned back the meter counts on various machines in a scheme to sell as "new", used machines. Meyer learned of this practice in the spring of 1996 and over the following months he apparently built a file containing service histories for various machines which demonstrated, he maintains, the misleading practice. [23] It is the plaintiff's theory that S. and Huntington worked together to terminate his employment because he was a potential "whistle blower". (i) The 1996 Atlanta Conference [24] I begin by generally describing S. [25] Various witnesses described his personality and manner as rude and demeaning. [26] Gary Tucker, the plaintiff's sales manager for much of 1996 and early 1997, characterized S. as "unpredictable and immature". He said that S. belittled people and bullied them. [27] Aaron Blacker gave evidence in the plaintiff's case. He said that S. at times was demeaning and verbally abusive. [28] Theresa Ralko said that S. created an uncomfortable and negative atmosphere in the office. [29] Trisha Conlin, a receptionist at Danka, testified that S., in particular, demonstrated a dislike for Victor Meyer; that S. was "trying to do anything he could to get rid of him". [30] From this evidence I conclude that S. was somewhat abusive with many employees and that in particular, he did not like the plaintiff. [31] In 1996, Danka held its Annual Sales Conference in Atlanta. Only the most successful sales people in the last sales year qualified to attend the conference. It was a reward for a good year - a type of bonus. [32] Victor Meyer maintains that he was well over the qualifying sales figure of $300,000, but that shortly before the trip, S. indicated that he did not qualify. [33] That precipitated an incident which saw the plaintiff, or another employee (the evidence was at variance), quickly collecting all of the plaintiff's sales files for a review by S. and Gary Tucker in the Danka conference room. I will not describe events in detail. I conclude that, as one witness put it, S. put Victor Meyer "through the ringer" before S. eventually, and not very gracefully, agreed that Victor Meyer had indeed qualified to attend the conference. [34] In evidence the plaintiff said and I accept, that S. observed to the plaintiff (according to my notes): "I hear your house is worth $750,000. You should sell it and buy a nice house in Surrey, invest the rest and find an easier job." [35] The plaintiff says that he ignored the comment. [36] He maintains that at one point S. asked "What do you people do with all your money?", a comment which the plaintiff, being of the Jewish faith, took to be an anti-Semitic remark. [37] I conclude that S. was intentionally making it difficult for the plaintiff to attend the Atlanta conference and that he unfairly took the position that the plaintiff had not qualified. [38] The plaintiff did go to Atlanta and there he had a brief meeting with Garry Huntington to discuss his problems in the Vancouver office. I will relate further details of that meeting below. (ii) Poaching Sales [39] As the phrase suggests "poaching sales" refers to a serious sin in the sales business - one making a sale within a colleague's exclusive sales territory or to his or her exclusive client account. [40] Three examples of alleged poaching will serve to give a flavour to the plaintiff's complaint. [41] In April 1996, a sales colleague of the plaintiff made a copier sale to the Willingdon Church, an account within the plaintiff's then geographic territory. [42] The sale was facilitated by S. When the plaintiff learned of it, he demanded credit for the sale and the resulting commission. He eventually prevailed, but the incident festered with the plaintiff and he constantly raised it bitterly with his superiors. [43] The second incident involved a sale of a copier to Seaboard Advertising. [44] This company is part of The Jim Pattison Group of Companies and that group was an exclusive account of the plaintiff's. The sale occurred in June 1996 and apparently S. and Gary Tucker arranged it and took the commission on it. [45] The plaintiff soon learned of the sale. Meyer immediately demanded credit for the sale and the file. [46] S. and Tucker maintained at the time that they had not poached a potential sale from the plaintiff; that the Seaboard Advertising sale was a so-called contra sale, that is an exchange of a copier for advertising space. Tucker, who gave evidence in the plaintiff's case, confirmed that. I so conclude. The sale was not a case of pure poaching. In any event the sale was later reversed and the commissions were returned. [47] Once again, the plaintiff bitterly raised this alleged transgression again and again in his dealings with his superiors at Danka and with some of his peers as well. [48] I interject to observe that Victor Meyer's habit of raising historic complaints did, I find, contribute to whatever negative or unsettled atmosphere was present in the Danka workplace. So too did his efforts to track down the various details of the incidents of which he complained by discussing the issues with fellow employees and even secretly taping the conversations. This observation should be recalled when I discuss the events surrounding Victor Meyer's termination. [49] In another incident of poaching, S. and a new sales associate, on Meyer's evidence, made a sale to Security Information Systems, a client of Victor Meyer's and one for whom he had an outstanding proposal. Victor Meyer went to the extreme of directly contacting the client concerning the sale thereby involving the client in an internal Danka dispute. [50] The final poaching incident which I will highlight involved the lease of a copier to the Kaylela Family Centre. [51] Victor Meyer maintained in evidence that the centre was part of the Squamish Nation and, accordingly, his exclusive account. There was an apparent dispute in the evidence on whether this was so. [52] The sale was made by Jeff Hardy and I will discuss the incident in more detail when I discuss the plaintiff's meetings with Garry Huntington. [53] I also relate under this heading, the plaintiff's many complaints that Danka, through S., frequently arbitrarily "shorted" him on commissions otherwise payable on sales and leases. [54] I find that there were a number incidents of this sort. [55] They were in many instances resolved after discussion between the plaintiff and his superiors. I also find that a number of these incidents can be classified as legitimate disputes between the plaintiff and his employer. That is, they were not all instances of arbitrary action. [56] I also note the evidence of the plaintiff's commission calculations being corrected by Danka management to the plaintiff's advantage. (iii) The Sharp Product Launch [57] Victor Meyer gave evidence on this November 1996 incident. It occurred in the presence of the Danka sales staff who were being introduced to a new product by a Sharp Canada representative. [58] The plaintiff says that as S. entered the room, the plaintiff was commenting on the absence of a three hole punch on the new machine. [59] In the entire context of his remarks, the plaintiff was being positive about the new machine, but he says that S. took his comment "out of context, to be a negative one". The plaintiff reports S. as telling the whole group: "Don't listen to him [Victor Meyer]. He's an idiot and doesn't know what he is talking about." [60] Mitch Clancy gave evidence in the plaintiff's case. He was a Danka employee who was present at this incident. [61] His evidence is slightly different from that of the plaintiff. He testified that upon his return, S. heard the reference to the "three hole punch" and angrily asked "who brought that subject up?". [62] Clancy says that S. yelled at the plaintiff that they did not need to discuss the issue. When the plaintiff tried to explain, S. told him to "shut up". [63] On either version, S. was abusive towards the plaintiff in the presence of his workplace peers. (iv) Meter Tampering [64] Much evidence was led at trial on this issue. It is not necessary to discuss it at length. On the whole of the evidence, and to an extent this is not now contested by Danka, I find that there were indeed incidents of meters being rolled back on machines delivered to various Danka clients. The rollbacks effectively misled some customers as to the age and historical usage of the machines in question. [65] I also find on the evidence that some of the recorded meter counts on a number of machines were accidentally and innocently recorded incorrectly. [66] On the evidence it is clear that a number of Danka employees knew of the rollbacks and either facilitated the process by teaching others how to change the meters, or actually undertook to do it on occasion and later decided to refuse to do it again. [67] Again, I do not believe it is necessary to detail this evidence. [68] I find that a number of senior employees in Danka's Vancouver office knew of the practice. In particular, S. knew of it and acquiesced in it. [69] The real issue, as will appear, is what Huntington knew of it and when it was brought to his attention. [70] The real issue is whether the plaintiff's knowledge of meter rollbacks and the fear that he would disclose it, led to his termination as a "whistle blower". (v) Summer 1997 [71] This period, I find, tends to epitomise the plaintiff's tenure at Danka. [72] On the one side, the plaintiff was demonstrating his sales ability and enjoying a very good sales year. He was frequently Danka Vancouver's top sales person and he won national recognition from Sharp and Danka on a significant number of occasions. [73] However, while the plaintiff was in many respects a meticulous record keeper, he did at times chafe, I find, at meeting Danka's policies on the completion of file documentation. [74] This led to a number of the plaintiff's sales files being posted for commissions while still deficient in documentation. [75] Mollie Joestl gave evidence in the defendant's case. [77] This led to a number of memoranda from Joestl to the plaintiff over the summer months of 1997. They were sent in an effort to have the plaintiff complete a number of outstanding matters. THE PARTIES' POSITIONS [124] Victor Meyer seeks damages for wrongful dismissal including Wallace damages, damages for intentional infliction of emotional harm, damages for deceit and punitive damages. In addition to employee benefits, he seeks substantial damages for his alleged loss of various prizes and awards which he maintains he would have won during the 1997 sales year. [125] These prizes and awards include a sum referable to his loss of the Jeep lease to which I have earlier referred. [126] Finally, Mr. Meyer seeks various commissions which he either maintains he earned or was about to earn or which represent "poached" sales. [127] Danka submits that this is a simple case for damages for wrongful dismissal; that Wallace has no application to the facts and that the only awards which should be considered are a sum for the Jeep Cherokee lease and possibly a sum in respect of the 1997 sales award trip. IV. WALLACE DAMAGES [128] Before I turn to consider the appropriate period of notice, I first consider whether whatever period I settle upon should be expanded in accordance with the principles discussed in Wallace [supra]. [129] Justice Esson has highlighted the crux of Justice Iacobucci's reasoning in Wallace in his reasons in Cassady v. Wyeth-Ayerst Canada Inc. (1998), 163 D.L.R. (4th) 1, 3 W.W.R. 74, 54 B.C.L.R. (3d) 68 (C.A.). (I have also read and considered the more recent decision in Clendenning v. Lowndes Lambert (B.C.) Ltd. (2000), 82 B.C.L.R. (3d) 239 (C.A.). [130] Justice Esson emphasizes these portions of the Wallace reasons (at para. 30 of Cassady): [88]... by no means do I condone the behaviour of employers who subject employees to callous and insensitive treatment in their dismissal, showing no regard for their welfare. Rather, I believe that such bad faith conduct in the manner of dismissal is another factor that is properly compensated for by an addition to the notice period. [95]... to ensure that employees receive adequate protection, employers ought to be held to an obligation of good faith and fair dealing in the manner of dismissal, the breach of which will be compensated for by adding to the length of the notice period. [102]... the law must recognize a more expansive list of injuries which may flow from unfair treatment or bad faith in the manner of dismissal. [103]... where an employee can establish that an employer engaged in bad faith conduct or unfair dealing in the course of dismissal, injuries such as humiliation, embarrassment and damage to one's sense of self-worth and self-esteem might all be worthy of compensation depending upon the circumstances of the case. ... [104]... the intangible injuries are sufficient to merit compensation in and of themselves. I recognize that bad faith conduct which affects employment prospects may be worthy of considerably more compensation than that which does not ... [131] What is clear from these extracts is that Wallace damages arise out of bad faith conduct or unfair dealing in the course of dismissal. [132] In the case before me, the incidents of which the plaintiff complains primarily took place well before the actual dismissal by Huntington on 9 September 1997. [133] The plaintiff seeks to avoid this conclusion by submitting that really the dismissal was a continuing act, beginning as early as April 1996 with S.'s conduct before the Atlanta conference. [134] I simply do not accept that that was factually so, even if it is legally possible to so maintain (and of that I have serious reservations). [135] There is no doubt that S. was insensitive in his treatment of Victor Meyer before qualifying him for the Atlanta conference. But that conduct was not "unfair dealing in the course of dismissal". [136] I arrive at the same conclusion with respect to the plaintiff's other complaints (not all of which I have detailed), but I should deal separately with the meter tampering issue. [137] I do not find that Huntington was advised by the plaintiff of his meter tampering allegations before September 1997. [138] The absence of any such disclosure in the plaintiff's letter to Huntington of 1 May 1997 or in the draft letter of 17 May 1997 is telling. [139] So too is the way in which the issue was raised by Victor Meyer in the meeting of 8 September 1997 - as the last item in a long list of matters which the plaintiff complained of. I accept that it was a final effort by the plaintiff to save his job. [140] The meter tampering incidents do not reflect well upon Danka, but the plaintiff does not emerge as the hero either. I find that he carefully went about building his meter tampering file by copying service histories, discussing the issue with fellow employees and by even taping their conversations to create a little leverage in case he needed it. [141] Of critical importance in this analysis, however, is my conclusion that Garry Huntington himself was not tainted by S.'s dislike for the plaintiff and his apparent desire to at least "rein him in", if not dismiss him. [142] On the whole of the evidence and on my assessment of the witnesses, when viewed against the backdrop of the contemporaneous documents, I conclude that Huntington was not part of a team with S. which plotted the dismissal of the plaintiff from an early point. [143] Quite apart from my belief in Huntington's evidence, such a scenario does not make sense. [144] Why wouldn't Danka have dismissed the plaintiff at the first alleged mention of meter tampering? [145] Why leave him around to gather evidence of the practice at his leisure? [146] Why weren't other employees who refused to participate in the scheme harassed and dismissed? [147] The plaintiff submits that Huntington's investigation of the allegations in September 1997 was cursory and therefore evidence that he was simply there for the purposes of damage control. [148] The evidence does not support that submission. It rather supports the conclusion that Huntington was very concerned with the plaintiff's allegations, but that he was persuaded that they were groundless when he diligently checked the facts out with most of the plaintiff's sources and others in the Vancouver office not even mentioned by the plaintiff. [149] I have concluded that Huntington, in consultation with his human resources manager, had legitimate business concerns in September 1997 with the plaintiff's continued employment in the Vancouver office of Danka. While those concerns are not advanced as cause for the termination, they do justify Huntington's actions to the extent of negativing bad faith conduct or unfair dealing in the course of the plaintiff's dismissal. [150] It is true that in its pleadings, until shortly before trial, the defendant did advance a defence of cause and did allege that Victor Meyer "offered to bribe one of his customers, thereby breaching the duty of loyalty and good faith". [151] But there is no evidence that Danka in any way broadcast these allegations within the industry or that they in any way affected the plaintiff's ability to secure employment. [152] In the absence of such evidence, the mere fact that the defendant alleged cause in its pleadings, does not give rise to Wallace damages: Wednesday Enterprises Inc. v. Modern Building Cleaning Inc., [1999] B.C.J. No. 1207 (Q.L.) (B.C.S.C.) at para. 59. [153] I advance to consider the appropriate period of notice. [154] The parties of course both cite Bardal v. Globe & Mail Ltd. (1960), 24 D.L.R. (2d) 140 (Ont. H.C.) and the oft quoted criteria for assessing a reasonable notice period. Central Transport Refrigeration (Man.) Ltd., [1994], 6 W.W.R. 305, 3 C.C.E.L. (2d) 1 (Man. C.A.). and submits that six months is a reasonable notice period for this plaintiff. [156] Counsel for Victor Meyer cites: Bogden v. Purolator Courier Ltd. (1996) 182 A.R. 216, 19 C.C.E.L. (2d) 77 (Alta. Q.B.); Farmer v. Foxridge Homes Ltd. (1992), 134 A.R. 55, 45 C.C.E.L. 144 (Alta. [157] Taking into account all of the relevant circumstances, I fix the period of reasonable notice at 10 months for Victor Meyer. [158] As to calculating the amount of damages, the defendant submits that it should be based on the plaintiff's effective monthly commission earnings for 1997. That is, the sum of $6,560 per month and the defendant submits that this tests well against the plaintiff's commission earnings in previous years. [159] The plaintiff, on the contrary, maintains that he was about to have his best year ever and he filed a forecast of anticipated sales in excess of $1,000,000. The plaintiff's sales quota was only $360,000 for this year. [160] Mollie Joestl testified that the actual sales arising out of the plaintiff's forecast only amounted to $258,225. [161] In my view, the defendant's approach on this issue is to be preferred and it is one supported by the cases: Ceci v. Comdisco Canada Ltd. (1994), 6 C.C.E.L. (2d) 86 (B.C.S.C.); Hamer-Jackson v. McCall Pontiac Buick Ltd. (2000) BCCA 416, 77 B.C.L.R. (3d) 214. Veach v. Diversey Inc. (1993), 1 C.C.E.L. (2d) 242 (B.C.S.C.); and [162] Ten months salary at $6,560 per month totals $65,600. From that must be deducted the income which the plaintiff earned in mitigation. That totals $12,107.25, for a net award of $53,500 (rounded). V. PRIZES AND COMMISSIONS [163] The plaintiff advances a substantial claim for the value of various prizes and commissions which he submits he would have earned but for his dismissal. [164] He also claims for various commissions which he claims arose out of "poached" sales. [165] As to prizes, Larsen v. A & B Sound Ltd. [supra] (para. 23), is cited. [166] The defendant does not dispute the plaintiff's entitlement to damages in lieu of the lost lease of the Jeep Cherokee, although it disagrees with the plaintiff's calculation of the amount. [167] As well, the defendant allows that the plaintiff may be entitled to something for the lost opportunity to enjoy the 1997 Annual Sales Conference Caribbean cruise. [168] On the evidence, the plaintiff was enjoying a good sales year and on the balance of probabilities he has established a reasonable chance or a "real possibility" that he would qualify again for this prize. [169] The defendant suggests an award under this head of $6,000 and I find that to be reasonable. [170] The plaintiff advances a claim for the following "lost" prizes: 1997 Sharp Canada Top Sales Award (a 1998 Chrysler Sebring convertible), value $32,000 to $36,000; and 1997/1998 Danka President's Club Award cruise to Monaco, value $14,000. [171] Whether the plaintiff would have won these prizes, especially the 1997 top sales award, is on the evidence, entirely speculative and they do not form a proper basis for compensation. [172] Next, the plaintiff advances various claims for lost commissions for alleged poaching incidents. These include: INCIDENT VALUE Brian Wade Security Information Systems poaching incident $900.00 - $1,050.00 Seaboard Advertising poaching incident $6,100.00 July 1997 Jeff Hardy Kaylela poaching incident $2,200.00 August 1997 Jeff Hardy Esla7an poaching incident $3,694.59 [173] It is not at all clear on the evidence whether each of these incidents is in fact a poached sale (especially the Kaylela sale). Indeed, the SIS sale was made in May 1996 two months after it was removed from the plaintiff's geographic sales territory. [174] Further, the evidence establishes that the Seaboard Advertising sale was later reversed. [175] Finally, even if any of the sales were improperly poached, it is not clear on the evidence or the law that that gives rise to any enforceable right at the suit of the plaintiff to the lost commission. The exhibit relied upon (Exhibit 2, Tab 137, p. 2) simply does not support this claim. [180] The plaintiff seeks $2,525 representing an allegedly improper price change on fax machines which he sold. I accept the defendant's submission (Argument para. 31), based on the evidence of Mollie Joestl, in denying this claim. [181] Similarly, I accept Mollie Joestl's evidence on the April 1997 Overwaitea sale (Exhibit 2, Tab 132). [182] The defendant does not appear to dispute the claim for $513 in respect of a June 1997 sale to Overwaitea and I allow that. TORT OF INTENTIONAL INFLICTION OF MENTAL SUFFERING [186] Victor Meyer submits that the harassing conduct of S. gives rise to damages in tort for the intentional infliction of mental suffering. [187] Damages for mental or emotional distress were considered in Kalaman v. Singer Valve Co. (1997), 38 B.C.L.R. (3d) 331, 31 C.C.E.L. (2d) 1 (C.A.). [188] Justice Macfarlane (Rowles J.A. concurring) accepted Justice McLachlin's (then of this court) summary of the test in Rahemtulla v. Vanfed Credit Union (1984), 51 B.C.L.R. 200 (S.C.) at para. 63: To support an award for mental distress in tort, the employer's conduct must constitute an independent and actionable wrong and: (a) must be flagrant and extreme; (b) must be plainly calculated to produce some effect of the kind that was produced; and (c) must have caused actual physical harm, a "visible and provable illness". [189] Justice Macfarlane stressed (at para. 73) that in tort the conduct must be "flagrant and extreme" and at paras. 76 and 77, he held: Those cases tend to illustrate that something more than emotional distress over loss of employment or unreasonable and insensitive conduct of the employer preceding dismissal is required to found a claim for damages for mental distress. In my opinion, there was no evidence upon which the trial judge could find that Singer Valve's conduct constituted an independent and actionable wrong. [190] In my view, while S. was by no means the model supervisor and his conduct towards the plaintiff was often insensitive and mean-spirited, there is simply no independent and actionable wrong made out on the facts. [191] This claim suffers further from the manner in which it is pleaded in the Statement of Claim: ... Tort of Intentional Infliction of Mental Suffering 18. The Plaintiff says that the Defendant wilfully and without good and lawful reason or cause terminated the Plaintiff at a time and in a manner aimed at intentionally or recklessly causing emotional distress to the Plaintiff. 19. The Plaintiff pleads that the Defendants knew or ought to have known that the Plaintiff would suffer upset and injury from the time and manner of the termination. 20. As a result of the Defendant's conduct, the Plaintiff has suffered emotional injury. 21. As a result of the Defendant's conduct, the Plaintiff has suffered general and special damages. 22. The actions of the Defendant constitutes wanton and outrageous disregard of the Plaintiff's rights for which the Plaintiff is entitled to an award of aggravated, punitive and exemplary damages. ... [192] It will be seen that the claim as pleaded centres on the alleged wrongful conduct of the defendant in the manner of the plaintiff's dismissal. I have already found against the plaintiff on the facts of that complaint. [193] It follows, as well, from my conclusion that this is not an appropriate case for punitive or aggravated damages: Vorvis v. I.C.B.C., [1989] 1 S.C.R. 1085. VII. TORT OF DECEIT [194] In final submissions the plaintiff advances this claim: ... 37. It is submitted that Danka knowingly, fraudulently and deceitfully misrepresented to Mr. Meyer that he was being terminated from his employment for cause in order to induce Mr. Meyer to accept his dismissal without complaint. It is submitted that such conduct of Danka amounts to the tort deceit. ... [195] On my assessment of the evidence, the plaintiff simply has not made out any factual foundation for this claim. VIII.
