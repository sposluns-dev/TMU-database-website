DETERMINATION [4] I find that the male claimant is not a Convention refugee or a person in need of protection pursuant to sections 96 and 97(1) of the IRPA. The male claimant does not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention reason and his removal to the Czech Republic would not subject him personally to a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment, or a danger of torture. [5] In arriving at my decision, I considered the issue of whether the male claimant's fear is objectively reasonable. ANALYSIS Section 96 - Convention Refugee Grounds State Protection [6] In assessing the issue of state protection, I am guided by a number of cases from the Supreme Court of Canada and the Federal Court. In this regard, the courts have established a number of principles relating to state protection and I have applied those principles to the case at hand. [7] There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens except in situations where the state is in a state of a complete breakdown.3 To rebut the presumption of state protection, a claimant must provide clear and convincing evidence of the state's inability to protect, absent an admission by the national's state of its inability to protect that national.4 While the effectiveness of the protection is a relevant consideration, the test is whether the protection offered is adequate.5 The evidence that state protection is not adequate must be reliable and probative and it must also satisfy me, on a balance of probabilities, that the state protection is inadequate.6 The claimant must approach the state for protection, providing that state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.7 In the absence of a compelling explanation, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim, at least where the state is a functioning democracy with a willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens.8 Where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protect its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not rebut the presumption of state protection.9 A claimant cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state.10 Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection.11 The fact that state protection is not perfect12 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability to provide protection.13 Indeed, if a claimant believes the actions of some police officers are corrupt, the onus is on them to approach other members of the security forces or authorities.14 There is nothing in the documentation before the Board to suggest that the Czech Republic is in a state of complete breakdown. The burden of proof that rests on the claimant increases with the level of democracy of the state in question. The more democratic a state is, the more the claimant must have done to exhaust all courses of action open to them to demonstrate state protection was or would not be forthcoming.15 The Czech Republic is a multiparty democracy16 Therefore, the presumption of state protection is a strong one. The claimant must do more than simply show that he or she went to see some members of the police force and that his or her efforts were unsuccessful.17 Even where the protective services of the home state have gaps or deficiencies, a refugee claimant who alleges a subjective fear based on criminality must, in the absence of a compelling justification, take reasonable steps to access those services.18 Where there is evidence that the government is taking steps to protect the target group, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, it must be presumed that these steps will be effective.19 It is not enough that a government is willing to provide protection and is making serious efforts to do so. In order for state protection to be present, the efforts made must adequately protect citizens in practice.20 In order for adequate state protection to exist, a government must have both the will and capacity to implement effectively its legislation and programs.21 Review of Country Documentation on the Roma in the Czech Republic Refugee Board Documents (Exhibit R-1, NDP April 13, 2011) Country Reports on Human Rights Practices for 2010 [8] In the United States Department of State Report22 covering the period of 2010 in the Czech Republic, it states: ...Notable human rights problems included official corruption, trafficking of persons for commercial sexual exploitation and labor, neo-Nazi and nationalist extremism directed at Roma and other minorities, and societal discrimination against Roma....Salary cuts at the Ministry of the Interior following government-wide austerity measures resulted in a lowered rate of retention among the national police force, especially among experienced detectives, which hampered the effectiveness of their work. Czech Republic Fact Finding Mission Reports-June-July-2009 [9] The Research Directorate of the IRB undertook a fact-finding mission to the Czech Republic from March 23 to March 31 of 2009. The purpose of the mission was to address information gaps related to the situation and treatment of the Czech Roma as well as their access to state protection and the possibility of internal relocation. The Research Directorate produced two Issue Papers reporting its findings, one dated June of 2009 and the other in July of 2009. The Research Directorate met with government officials, representatives of human rights organizations, Romani groups and academics based in Prague, Brno, Chomutov, Rokycany and Usti nad Labem.23 According to the Report of the Research Directorate, dated June of 2009: Interlocutors from governmental and non-governmental organizations (NGO) explained that an estimated 80,000 of the country's 200,000 to 250,000 Roma inhabit some 300 so-called "socially excluded localities". Social excluded localities are residential enclaves mainly populated by Roma that have limited access to formal employment, education, and adequate housing....in several unspecified cases, excluded localities have been "neglected" by local authorities, including the police. Academic, governmental and NGO interlocutors claimed that the crime rate in these areas is considerably above the Czech average...interlocutors referred to legislation that provides for state protection for Roma, in particular, anti-discrimination and hate-crime legislation. The Czech Constitution prohibits discrimination on the basis of national, racial or ethnic background, as do other pieces of legislation, including laws governing employment and education, and the Charter of Fundamental Rights and Freedoms. The Security Police Department of the Ministry of the Interior and the Czech Helsinki Committee noted that Czech law outlines a number of provisions defining hate crimes....when queried about police protection measures established by the state to address acts of discrimination and racially motivated violence, governmental interlocutors pointed to Roma Police Assistants (RPAs), Minority Liaison Officers, and the Ministry of Interior's Anti-Extremism Strategy. The Roma Police Assistant (RPA) program was created in 2003 with seed money from the Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs, in the eastern city of Ostrava where the first two RPAs aided the police in an investigation into allegations of usury. RPA's are now mainly funded by municipalities via national government grants. As of March 2009, seven RPAs were operating in municipalities across the country, and an eighth position was planned for the Janov neighbourhood of Litvinov. RPA duties include establishing contact between Roma and the Police, accompanying Romani victims of crime who wish to file a police report, educating victims on their legal rights and responsibilities, helping police investigate unreported crime and assisting in the provision of social services in socially excluded localities....Several NGO interlocutors also identified the existence of the RPA program as a sign of progress and two interlocutors claimed that RPAs have increased Romani trust in the police....Holomek and Brno-based NGO IQ Roma Servis agreed that RPAs have improved relations between Roma and police, but stated that RPAs are sometimes perceived as informants seeking information about criminal activity in Romani communities....Minority Liaison Officers are employed by the Ministry of the Interior to help introduce police to minority groups and to communicate with NGOs and local authorities with a focus on crime prevention. MLOs also participate in projects intended to combat the social exclusion of Roma. The hiring of MLOs began in 2004 in areas with a high concentration of Roma, such as Litvinov. In March 2009, eight MLOs were operating in the Czech Republic at the regional level. Hule, an NGO with several regional branches that fights against poverty and social exclusion, claimed that MLOs are overextended due to the size of their jurisdictions. Nevertheless, Hule also noted that the MLO in Prague was in regular contact with the Ministry of the Interior requesting information about cases involving Romani victims of violence. Two NGO interlocutors commended the effectiveness of MLOs, and the Ministry of the Interior stated that MLO activities had improved communication between police and Roma, contributing to a reduction of crime within Romani communities....In the mid-1990s, the Ministry of the Interior launched an initiative to combat extremism, allowing for police monitoring of extremist group activities and trends. The initiative began with the creation of a specialized anti-extremist department within the Police Presidium of the Czech Republic in 1995, followed by the deployment of anti-extremist specialists at the regional and district level in 1996. There are between 130 and 140 extremist crime investigators across the country....According to the Ministry of the Interior, anti-extremist measures have shown increasing signs of success, for instance, the number of extremist crimes declined from 473 in 2002 to 196 in 2007. However, Vales of the Czech Helsinki Committee described extremist crime investigators as "overburdened with work", and also noted that there is limited coordination between anti-extremism agencies and local police, the latter of which he viewed as "inefficient and unprofessional"....According to Holomek of the Romani Association of the Romanies in Moravia and to Miroslav Mares, a professor of political science at Masaryk University in Brno, police response to racially motivated demonstrations targeting Roma is generally adequate. Holomek noted that in May 2008, police successfully prevented a major clash between anarchists and neo-Nazis in Brno, expelling the latter from the city. Governmental and NGO interlocutors also described the police response to the November 2008 Workers' Party march in Litvinov as satisfactory and noted that the police were able to break up the demonstrations. Vales, of the Czech Helsinki Committee, described the police response to neo-Nazi demonstrations as inadequate. For example, he believed that violence at the November 2008 Litvinov demonstration was averted "by chance" rather than organized police efforts. Other NGO interlocutors claimed that the police tended to address acts of extremism only when they were considered serious or too high-profile to ignore....Governmental and NGO interlocutors varied in their assessment of police relations with Roma. Police in Brno pointed to high levels of crime and an uncooperative attitude with the police in Roma communities as deterrents in fostering better interactions between police and Roma....In contrast, several NGO and academic interlocutors commented that the police generally have a negative view of Roma that sometimes includes "discriminatory" or "disrespectful attitudes. Several governmental interlocutors claimed that, in general, the police response to Roma complaints or calls of distress is equal to that of non-Romani Czechs. In addition, Mares and Holomek stated that the police response to racially motivated crime against Roma is generally adequate. However, other NGO interlocutors were more cautious in their assessment stating that despite some signs of progress, negative perceptions of the police persist among Roma. A number of NGO interlocutors asserted that Roma are often hesitant to file criminal complaints because they lack confidence in the police and doubt that their complaints will be effectively addressed. Zvule prava, a Prague-based civic association advocating for Romani rights, noted that Roma generally believe that the police do not take their cases seriously. Roma are said to report crimes to the police only in the most serious circumstances, such as when they believe that they are in imminent physical danger...Some interlocutors also claimed that the police have been known to dissuade Roma, especially those from socially excluded localities, from filing a complaint....NGOs and academic interlocutors noted that various ministries differ in their approaches to address social exclusion and that there is a lack of coordination. In an effort to resolve these issues, the Social Inclusion Agency was established by the national government in January of 2008. The objectives of the Agency are to coordinate social integration efforts, combat social exclusion, evaluate social integration programs, monitor cases of discrimination, provide legal advice, and promote education and employment at the local level. In March of 2009, the Agency was active in 12 cities as part of its pilot phase and is expected to take on a nationwide mandate by 2010. IQ Roma Servis believed the Agency is hampered by poor management.... According to the Report of the Research Directorate, dated July of 2009: Karel Holomek, Chairperson of the Association of Romanies of moravia and Zuzana Kailova of Usti and Labem City Hall indicated that while the police and government are working on curbing open discrimination against Roma, a more pervasive problem is hidden discrimination, which is difficult to tackle directly. Several interlocutors cited public opinion surveys which found that between 60 and 95 percent of Czech citizens held negative attitudes toward Roma. Miroslav Mares explained that political extremism in the Czech Republic is weak in comparison with some neighbouring countries. Despite negative public perceptions of Roma, he asserted, extremist political parties remain unsuccessful in attracting sufficient support to win elections...However, interlocutors asserted that some mainstream national parties occasionally espouse platforms considered anti-Roma, especially during elections. In addition, several interlocutors indicated that discrimination manifests itself politically at the local level. For example, they pointed to municipal politicians who have adopted policies perceived as anti-Roma in order to gain votes. According to interlocutors from IQ Roma Servis, local governments that adopt policies to address Romani concerns are often criticized by their non-Roma constituents, who view these policies as ineffective.....Mares indicated that while there has been increased visibility of extremist activities, such as demonstrations, in the past five years, he reported that it was not clear if such activism has resulted in increased violence against Roma. He maintained that, as of March 2009, extremist attacks against Roma were low compared to earlier in the decade....According to the SPD of the Ministry of the Interior, violent crimes committed in an extremist context such as homicide, attempted homicide or assault resulting in serious injury, have occurred, but are uncommon...Frantisek Vales, Executive Director of the Prague-based Czech Helsinki Committee, state that while official statistics recorded no racial murders between 2002 and 2006, three neo-Nazis killed a Romani person in 2006. The three men were prosecuted for non-racially motivated murder, and one of them was sentenced to a prison term. Vales went on to say that in 2007 there were 11 crimes resulting in the physical harm of Roma as well as one murder which was attributed to the victim's socio-racial background. The perpetrator of the killing was charged with murder and hooliganism, but these were not classified as hate crimes...When questioned about particular groups that target Roma, interlocutors explained that far-right wing extremist groups have instigated "hate" crimes, not only against Roma, but also against other minority groups. NGO and government interlocutors described physical attacks targeting Roma, particularly those resulting in serious injury, as not prevalent. For instance, when queried about the extent of anti-Romani activism by extremist groups, Mares indicated that the Roma do not face significant physical danger from such groups in Prague. Cerny of People in Need's Usti nad Labem office observed that, despite vocal extremist agitation, Roma living in the Janov district of Litvinov do not suffer from physical violence...A 2008 report provided by the Ministry of the Interior's SPD indicated that Roma are the most frequent victims of verbal and physical assaults by extremists, followed by "foreigners of dark skin", persons of Asian origin and other foreign nationals, though drug users, homosexuals and homeless people also face threats....Extremist groups are engaged in various activities, including organizing meetings and concerts and publishing manifestos and promotional material. However, the extremist activity most frequently cited by interlocutors was public demonstrations. Interlocutors reported an increase in the mobilization of anti-Roma extremist groups in recent years, as exemplified by the rise of the Worker's party in northern Bohemia..... Response to Information Request number CZE103682.E, March 1, 2011 [14] In the above Response it states:28 A European Network Against Racism (ENAR) shadow report on racism and discrimination in the Czech Republic describes police investigation of crimes against Roma citizens as "inadequate", and adds that such investigations are "presumed" to be the result of "ethnic profiling". The report, which the Research Directorate obtained in advance of its March 21, 2011 publication date, also describes the police reaction as a form of "secondary victimization" stemming from the "assumption that the Roma committed the criminal act or provoked it with their behaviour". As the ENAR explains, the phenomenon is "particularly prominent in cases of hate crimes; for example, many arson attacks on Roma families were not investigated or the investigation was adjourned." However, the Embassy of the Czech Republic in Ottawa, in February 2011 correspondence with the Research Directorate, counters that "ethnicity...has no effect on the quality of the services rendered by the Police of the Czech Republic: all are treated equally, without discrimination". The Embassy of the Czech Republic acknowledges that although there can be "individual cases of failure to comply" with its standards, the police "have the power, in such cases, to employ means that identify and punish discriminatory conduct of police officers". The Inspection of the Police of the Czech Republic, an independent body under the Minister of the Interior undertakes the criminal investigation of police officers, reports the Embassy of the Czech Republic. Citizens can also register "complaints about police inaction and non-serious offenses by police officers" with the Office of the Internal Control, which is part of the Police of the Czech Republic. Information on the Police of the Czech Republic website also states that complaints about the police force generally or individual policemen in particular are to be sent to what it translates as the "Office of Internal Inspection". Citizens do so by sending an email or calling. The complaints will either be immediately addressed or "submitted for inspection" to the responsible bodies. Liga Lidskych Prav, a human rights non-governmental organization (NGO) in the Czech Republic points out that there is no "single, independent police complaints commission" that can investigate all types of complaints against police, including accusations of misconduct or criminal charges against the police. Instead, these types of complaints "are investigated by the police's own internal monitoring body"; and the Interior Minister's Inspectorate, which is "exclusively made-up of police officers, all of whom still retain their rank," determines "the most important stage of the investigation"-whether a crime was actually committed...According to the Police of the Czech Republic, citizens who wish to submit a complaint against the police should report the complaint to the director of the unit "who is the supervisor of the members or staff of the Police you are filing y our complaint against" and report the complaint to thee supervising unit of the director does not address it appropriately. Analysis of Board's Documents [18] I find that the documentary evidence above establishes that, despite the numerous measures, policies, and legislation that the Government of the Czech Republic has adopted and implemented with regard to its stated objectives of eliminating racial discrimination against the Romani population, protecting their rights, and improving their socio-economic status, there continues to be widespread discrimination against the Roma in the Czech Republic in the areas of employment, education, housing, and health care. According to the documentary evidence, in some instances, it is the municipal governments at the local level that have been frustrating the well-intentioned plans of the Central Czech Government to bring about social and economic equality for members of the Romani community. I find that the preponderance of the documentary evidence above establishes that the anti-discrimination measures, policies, and plans of the Central Czech Government have not been implemented effectively. [19] I also acknowledge, based on my review of the above documentation, that anti-Roma sentiments and hate-speeches at marches and demonstrations organized by racist groups have become more prevalent of late. However, I find that the documentary evidence regarding the level of racially motivated acts of violence perpetrated by Neo-Nazis and extremists against the Roma is conflicting. According to some of the documentary sources of information above, there has been an increase in racially motivated attacks on the Roma in the Czech Republic. I find, given the lack of reliable statistical or empirical data regarding racially motivated attacks on members of the Roma community, that I am unable to find, either way, that violent attacks on the Roma have either increased or decreased. However, I find that the preponderance of the documentary evidence establishes, on a balance of probabilities, that members of the Romani population in the Czech Republic continue to be targets of racially motivated violence carried out by Neo-Nazis and national extremists.
