Introduction [1] In seeking to protect citizens and the viability of health care systems against the COVID-19 ("Covid") pandemic, Canadian governments, health officers, and other governmental entities restricted everyday liberties to a degree unseen since the Second World War. It is conceivable that a carefully focused and drafted legal proceeding could challenge those measures, including seeking damages awards for losses flowing from any of those restrictions found to be unlawful.[1] Unfortunately, this particular proposed class proceeding, framing these particular claims, with this particular proposed class and sub-classes, as constantly reformulated by the plaintiff some eight times since its initial filing, suffers from so many self-inflicted problems, as outlined in these reasons, that the present claim is not the one. [2] The plaintiff Society brings this action against the Province and its Health Officer (the "Health Officer"), alleging that restrictive measures enacted to address the Covid pandemic pursuant to the Emergency Program Act, RSBC 1996, c 111 (the "EPA"); the Public Health Act, SBC 2008, c 28, Part 5 (the "PHA"); and the COVID-19 Related Measures Act, SBC 2020, c 8 (the "CRMA") (collectively, "health measures" or "restrictions") breached the section 2 (fundamental freedoms), 7 (life, liberty, and security of the person), 9 (detention), and 15 (equality) rights protected under the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (the "Charter").[2] [3] At this stage, the plaintiff applies to have its action certified as a class proceeding under the Class Proceedings Act, RSBC 1996, c 50 (the "Act"[3]). The plaintiff also applies to amend its present amended notice of civil claim (the "ANCC"), and to add three proposed individual representative plaintiffs to the claim, in addition to the present lone plaintiff: the Society. [4] As evidenced by 10 days of hearings in this matter, with multiple post-hearing supplementary written submissions to address the plaintiff's further proposed amendments, as well as jurisprudential and pandemic developments, and the wall of binders of materials presented to the Court by the parties, the proposed class proceeding is almost certainly the largest and most complex in Canadian legal history.[4] Plaintiff's counsel describes it as "ambitious" and "unprecedented." The plaintiff challenges almost every order issued under the EPA, the PHA and the CRMA: some 50 to 70 orders, depending on the version of the plaintiff's notice of civil claim. The plaintiff proposes to bring the class proceeding on behalf of all adult British Columbians--roughly 4 million people--in three subclasses. The plaintiff envisions a common issues trial, followed by multiple--perhaps tens or hundreds or thousands or millions--of individual sub-trials to assess individual issues, including the damages suffered by each of the individual plaintiff class members. At the end of the day, as the defendant is the government, taxpayers would in effect finance the likely massive damages award that would then be payable back to themselves. [5] The government defendants oppose certification. They also bring an application to strike the present notice of civil claim as an abuse of process and as disclosing no cause of action. [6] The present certification and strike applications are purely procedural pre-trial motions. The Court does not evaluate in depth the merits of the claim or make any factual determinations or findings at this stage. It is not the role of the Court at this stage to decide whether the plaintiff's claim is valid or not, let alone decide whether the actions of the Province or the Health Officer were good, bad, or otherwise. The task of the Court at this stage is to decide whether this specific action makes practical sense to be brought as a class proceeding, based on the Act and judicial applications of the Act. Many proposed class proceedings that are found not to be appropriately certified as a class proceeding may nonetheless proceed as an ordinary action. [7] An action that is certified as a class proceeding receives various procedural benefits, including facilitation of evidence, suspension of limitation periods, immunities against costs awards, and logistical streamlining, as in distributing funds to class members. Certification also imposes consequences and responsibilities on people who may not be aware of the present proceeding: if certified, the litigation and trial orders will bind all British Columbians who do not expressly opt out of the proceeding under the procedure set out in the Act, and will likely bar them from advancing their own claims if the plaintiff's claim is dismissed. They would be unable to themselves challenge any provincial health order, or ministerial order on the basis of Charter violations. Unless the plaintiffs and petitioners who have already started their own proceedings against these defendants opt out of this proceeding, their own lawsuits will likely be stayed as duplicative of the present proceeding and thus abusive. [8] Given these significant benefits and consequences, the Act creates no right to certification. As stated by Justice Branch, a noted jurist and author on class proceedings,[5] in Kett v. Mitsubishi Materials Corporation, 2020 BCSC 1879: [54] The court has an important gate-keeping role requiring it to carefully screen proposed claims to ensure they are suitable for class action treatment: [117] The goal of the CPA is to be fair to both plaintiffs and defendants "it is imperative to have a scrupulous and effective screening process, so that the court does not sacrifice the ultimate goal of a just determination between the parties on the altar of expediency." Thorburn v. British Columbia, 2012 BCSC 1585, appeal dismissed, 2013 BCCA 480 [9] As set out below, the claim is struck as an abuse of process. At its core, the claim seeks to challenge and set aside governmental actions: specifically, the various orders and directives issued by the defendants in response to the pandemic. It thus seeks an end run around the more stringent and deferential judicial review of those enactments, by casting the claim as one in Charter damages. The plaintiff has in fact brought unsuccessful judicial reviews of Covid-related restrictions, the subject matter of which petitions overlap with the present claim: another abuse of process. Further, despite judicial warnings, and despite eight versions of its claim, the plaintiff persists with pleadings that are replete with vexatious argument and other violations of pleadings rules: another abuse of process. In any case, the present and proposed claims do not disclose a cause of action, in part because many of the claims have been dismissed in other Covid-related litigation. [10] The claim also fails the test for certification. Subsection 4(1) of the Act requires an applicant for certification to establish all five conditions for certification: (a) the pleadings disclose a cause of action; (b) there is an identifiable class of 2 or more persons; (c) the claims of the class members raise common issues, whether or not those common issues predominate over issues affecting only individual members; (d) a class proceeding would be the preferable procedure for the fair and efficient resolution of the common issues; (e) there is a representative plaintiff who (i) would fairly and adequately represent the interests of the class, (ii) has produced a plan for the proceeding that sets out a workable method of advancing the proceeding on behalf of the class and of notifying class members of the proceeding, and (iii) does not have, on the common issues, an interest that is in conflict with the interests of other class members. [11] The applicant bears the persuasive burden of showing that it has met all five of these prerequisites: Kett at para 56. If an applicant fails to establish any one of those requirements, the proceeding will not be certified as a class proceeding. [12] Subsection 4(2) requires the court to consider all relevant matters in determining under s. 4(1)(d) whether a class proceeding would be the preferable procedure for the fair and efficient resolution of the common issues, including: (a) whether questions of fact or law common to the members of the class predominate over any questions affecting only individual members; (b) whether a significant number of the members of the class have a valid interest in individually controlling the prosecution of separate actions; (c) whether the class proceeding would involve claims that are or have been the subject of any other proceedings; (d) whether other means of resolving the claims are less practical or less efficient; (e) whether the administration of the class proceeding would create greater difficulties than those likely to be experienced if relief were sought by other means. [13] For the reasons that follow, the plaintiff's failure to establish any of the required s. 4(1) certification preconditions is fatal to its application: 1) s. 4(1)(a): the claim is bound to fail, largely for the same reasons that require it to be struck. 2) s. 4(1)(b): the proposed class and sub-classes are simultaneously imprecise and yet overbroad. It would be unclear whether any one of the 4 million potential class members would be members of, or bound by decisions concerning each subclass. 3) s. 4(1)(c): there is no central issue that would apply to all class members and advance all claims. Instead, the proposed claim lumps together three unrelated subclasses, with a cumbersome 26 common issues, each of which would only apply to a sub-class, or a sub-sub-class of the entire class, under the broad thematic umbrella of Covid restrictions. 4) s. 4(1)(e): the present proposed representative plaintiff is inappropriate: a Society rather than a human being. The plaintiff provides scarce evidence to establish that the newly-proposed individual representative plaintiffs would adequately represent the interests of 4 million class members, who would be bound, absent opt-out, by their litigation decisions and judicial pronouncements in this case. The pro forma and scanty litigation plan for this most complicated of proposed class proceedings indicates their inability to do so. [14] With respect to s. 4(1)(d), and generally, these manifold difficulties with the plaintiff's case reverberates and culminates in the analysis of the fourth precondition: whether a class proceeding would be the preferable procedure for the fair and efficient resolution. Individual issues overwhelmingly predominate. The class proceeding would involve claims that are or have been the subject of other proceedings. A class proceeding would be a less practical or less efficient means of resolving the claim. And, to a staggering degree, the administration of the class proceeding would create greater difficulties than those likely to be experienced if relief were sought by other means. To employ the oft-cited phrase of the late Chief Justice Esson, in Tiemstra v. Insurance Corp. of British Columbia, 22 BCLR (3d) 49 at para 20, 1996 CanLII 2819 (SC), aff'd 38 BCLR (3d) 377 at para 13, 1997 CanLII 4094 (CA), the proposed class proceeding would further morph into the ultimate "monster of complexity and cost." [18] The first defendant is referred to as "His Majesty the King in right of the Province of British Columbia". As a preliminary concern, the plaintiff has misnamed the defendant. Weisenburger v. College of Naturopathic Physicians of British Columbia, 2024 BCSC 1047, considering a similar pleading deficiency, surveys the jurisprudence[7]: [129] Finally, the NCC and PANCC misname "His Majesty the King in Right of the Province of British Columbia" as the defendant. To the extent the plaintiff is seeking a declaration that aspects of the PH Act and orders made by the PH Officer are unconstitutional, the proper defendant is the Attorney General of British Columbia: Allen v. Superintendent of Motor Vehicles and Attorney General of British Columbia (1986), 2 BCLR (2d) 255 (SC) at 260-61, per Madam Justice Southin; Maddock at para. 5, per Chief Justice Hinkson; Klassen v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2021 BCSC 2254 at para. 11, per Mr Justice Jenkins; the last two cases were in the context of the pandemic. Further, "His Majesty the King in right of the Provincial Health Officer" is not a legal entity. The PH Officer is an independent office appointed by Order in Council and ought to be named separately as a defendant: PH Act, ss. 64-69. As stated in Karen Horsman & Gareth Morley, Government Liability: Law and Practice (Toronto: Thomson Reuters) (loose-leaf updated 2023, release 3), at 2:2: In order to enter into contracts, own property, sue or be sued, an entity must have "civil personality". Individuals and business corporations have civil personality, as does the Crown. Public sector organizations may have their own civil personality, they may be part of the Crown or they may only have public law existence. While sometimes it will be clear from the statute that creates the organization which category it falls into, sometimes it is a difficult question. A governmental entity can "exist" legally for either public-law or private-law purposes. If a statute gives an individual or group a power of decision, then a person affected by the decision may seek an order in the nature of certiorari, prohibition or mandamus if the body has acted outside its jurisdiction, threatens to act outside its jurisdiction or refuses to perform a mandatory statutory duty. The statute may also give persons affected a right of appeal, either to another body or to a court. Finally, a person may seek a Charter remedy against the body if it is part of "government" as that term is defined in s. 32 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. However, the legal existence of an entity for administrative and constitutional purposes must be kept strictly distinct from whether it exists for civil purposes. For example, if a Minister makes a statutory decision, and an affected person wishes to challenge it for administrative or constitutional reasons, the Minister is the proper respondent to a petition or application for judicial review. However, all of the executive acts of the Minister will be attributed to the Crown, all of his or her department's employees will be employees of the Crown (and not the Minister) and the appropriate institutional defendant in a civil action against decisions of the Ministry will be the Crown. On the other hand, if a governmental organization does have its own legal personality (unlike a Ministry or Department), then that organization, and not the Crown, is the appropriate defendant in a civil action. Failure to appreciate this can lead to the action being struck out. [130] The Court accordingly grants the order sought by the provincial defendants: that the Attorney General of British Columbia and Dr Bonnie Henry in her capacity as Provincial Health Officer for the Province of British Columbia be substituted in place of His Majesty the King in Right of the Province of British Columbia as defendants in this proceeding, and the style of cause be amended accordingly. [19] In this, the plaintiff has stuck to its guns and persisted in its present misnomer despite repeated notice from the defendants and Weisenburger. [20] Apart from misnaming the defendant as a general representative of the government of British Columbia, the proposed claim also fails to name all of the correct defendants. Many of the restrictions complained of are not attributable to the present named defendants: that is, the Province and the Health Officer. Rather, they are the edicts of the five regional health authorities, designated under the Health Authorities Act, RSBC 1996, c 180 to implement and enforce the PHA and its regulations within each region. Many of the impugned actions attributed to the present defendants were those of the BC Centre for Disease Control. Many of the impugned actions are the result of decisions by multiple entities unnamed as defendants. For example, delayed or cancelled surgeries are primarily determined by more local decisions and factors: by health authorities, administrators, and surgeons (as well as the patients themselves and their families). All of these are separate legal entities who should or may have to be added as defendants in the proposed proceeding. III. The legislative apparatus and health orders [21] Beaudoin v. [34] The circumstances in which a person affected by an order may request reconsideration of the order are set out in s. 43 of the PHA [T]he relevant provisions of s. 43 are set out below: 43(1) A person affected by an order, or the variance of an order, may request the health officer who issued the order or made the variance to reconsider the order or variance if the person (a) has additional relevant information that was not reasonably available to the health officer when the order was issued or varied, (b) has a proposal that was not presented to the health officer when the order was issued or varied but, if implemented, would (i) meet the objective of the order, and (ii) be suitable as the basis of a written agreement under section 38 [may make written agreements], or (c) requires more time to comply with the order. Constantly changing notice of claim [23] The assessment of whether a claim is appropriately brought as a class proceeding is largely determined by the pleadings set out in the plaintiff's notice of civil claim: Kett at para 56. As indicated by the statutory test quoted in the introduction, clear identification of the representative plaintiff, the class, the common issues, and the relief sought, is critical. [46] Needless to say, the ever-moving target of the ever-evolving proposed pleadings was significantly prejudicial to the defendants, whose lengthy and detailed certification arguments were based on the pleadings as they existed before the start of the certification hearing. The plaintiff's own arguments were also based on the December 2022 version of the FANCC. This in turn made the task of the Court exponentially more difficult in assessing whether certification is appropriate: an exercise largely based upon the pleadings. At each step of drafting these reasons, the Court had to second-guess whether the certification arguments of both sides still applied to any given portion of the multiple versions of the ANCC and FANCC. [47] To be fair to the plaintiff, to some extent the factual basis has evolved in real time: as more public health orders were issued, it was not wholly unreasonable for the plaintiff to amend its pleadings to particularise those new orders. Further, the evolving Covid jurisprudence rendered many of the plaintiff's legal claims and arguments untenable. That said, many of the amendments could have been anticipated and pleaded at an early stage of the litigation. To give two critical and fundamental examples: as discussed below, the plaintiff was in a position to properly identify and define the subclasses at an early stage, and it was always ill-conceived to have the Society serve as the proposed representative plaintiff. [48] It is also true that the task of defining a class is sometimes "a fluid exercise in fine tuning": Heward v. Eli Lilly & Co. (2007), 39 CPC (6th) 153, 2007 CanLII 2651 at para 11 (Ont SCJ), aff'd (2008), 295 DLR (4th) 175, 2008 CanLII 32303 (Ont SCJ Div Ct). The jurisprudence recognises that a plaintiff has the right to shape its case so as to make it more amenable to certification, provided the defendant is not prejudiced: Burnett v. St. Jude Medical, Inc., 2008 BCSC 148 at para 40, quoting Rumley v. British Columbia, 2001 SCC 69. [49] But the present case has not been an exercise in fine-tuning. Law [122] The defendants apply to strike the claim in its entirety, under Rule 9-5(1): Scandalous, frivolous or vexatious matters (1) At any stage of a proceeding, the court may order to be struck out or amended the whole or any part of a pleading, petition or other document on the ground that (a) it discloses no reasonable claim or defence, as the case may be, (b) it is unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious, (c) it may prejudice, embarrass or delay the fair trial or hearing of the proceeding, or (d) it is otherwise an abuse of the process of the court, and the court may pronounce judgment or order the proceeding to be stayed or dismissed and may order the costs of the application to be paid as special costs. [123] In R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42, the Supreme Court of Canada explained the important housekeeping function provided by civil procedure strike provisions: [20] This promotes two goods -- efficiency in the conduct of the litigation and correct results. Striking out claims that have no reasonable prospect of success promotes litigation efficiency, reducing time and cost. The litigants can focus on serious claims, without devoting days and sometimes weeks of evidence and argument to claims that are in any event hopeless. The same applies to judges and juries, whose attention is focused where it should be -- on claims that have a reasonable chance of success. The efficiency gained by weeding out unmeritorious claims in turn contributes to better justice. The more the evidence and arguments are trained on the real issues, the more likely it is that the trial process will successfully come to grips with the parties' respective positions on those issues and the merits of the case. [emphasis added] [124] Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 SCR 959 at 980 states with respect to Rule 19(24)(a), the predecessor of Rule 9-5(1)(a): Thus, the test in Canada governing the application of provisions like Rule 19(24)(a) of the British Columbia Rules of Court is the same as the one that governs an application under R.S.C. O. 18, r. 19: assuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it "plain and obvious" that the plaintiff's statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action? [47] Irrelevancy and embarrassment are both established when pleadings are so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is being pleaded . To demonstrate a breach of s.15(1) of the Charter, the claimant must establish that "the impugned law or state action on its face or in its impact, creates a distinction based on enumerated or analogous grounds; and imposes burdens or denies a benefit in a manner that has the effect of reinforcing, perpetuating, or exacerbating disadvantage": Fraser v Canada (Attorney General), 2020 SCC 28 at para 27. Some Plaintiffs appear to suggest that they were subject to differential treatment by their colleagues and superiors as a result of declining to receive the vaccine. The alleged distinction is not particularized, and no material facts are pled to establish a connection between the alleged distinction and the Directives. Specifically, in both seeking to set aside and challenge administrative orders while at the same time seeking damages flowing from those actions, the claim represents an impermissible collateral attack on those governmental actions. A challenge to those actions are properly the subject of a judicial review brought by way of petition, not by way of action, as is in the present proceeding: see Canada (Attorney General) v. TeleZone Inc., 2010 SCC 62 at paras 60-62. As described in Weisenburger: [28] Under subrules 9-5(1)(b) and (d), the defendants argue that the claim is, in essence, an impermissible collateral attack on the decisions and actions of the provincial defendants and the College, which are properly the subject of a judicial review brought by way of petition, not by way of action, as is in the present proceeding: see Canada (Attorney General) v. TeleZone Inc., 2010 SCC 62 at paras. 60-62 [TeleZone]. They also argue that it should be struck under the flexible and broad doctrine of abuse of process, as expressly codified in subrule 9-5(1)(d). Were the proceeding properly brought as a judicial review, the Court would be obliged to approach those governmental decisions and actions under the deferential approach mandated by Loyola High School v. Quebec (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 12 and Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v. Vavilov, 2019 SCC 65, asking whether the decisions and actions were reasonable in light of the relevant statutory mandate, and evaluated solely on the record underlying those decisions. To permit this action for damages and declarations to proceed would bypass the decisions and actions of the PH Officer on highly technical scientific issues with which she has been entrusted by the democratically-elected legislature. It would assess the defendants' decisions and actions on a standard of correctness, rather than reasonableness. It would eschew the deference to which she and other designated administrative decision makers, particularly those in a highly technical field of expertise, as here, are entitled. It would place the reviewing court in the untenable position of assessing matters afresh, on an expanded record, as an inappropriate and ill-equipped "armchair epidemiologist": Beaudoin v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2022 BCCA 427 at para. 156, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 40622 (10 August 2023). [emphasis added] [153] Greengen Holdings Ltd. v. British Columbia (Ministry of Forests, Lands and Natural Resource Operations), 2018 BCCA 214, explains the two ways in which a civil action may constitute an impermissible collateral attack: [32] However, a civil action will not be a collateral attack only because it alleges that a government decision is unlawful. TeleZone confirms that a civil action may constitute a collateral attack in two ways. [33] The first (and most obvious) way is where the civil action seeks to invalidate the decision or avoid its consequences. A plaintiff in a civil action cannot claim relief that is only available on judicial review, such as an order setting aside the decision. To seek such a remedy in a private law action effectively circumvents the deference that is usually given to statutory decision makers. It is important to note, however, that the doctrine applies only where the action attacks the legal force of the decision; it does not apply where the action attacks the factual basis for the decision: Toronto (City) at para. 34. [34] The second is where the plaintiff fails to plead a valid cause of action for damages. The superior courts' inherent jurisdiction includes a residual discretion to stay a claim for damages where in its "essential character it is a claim for judicial review with only a thin pretence to a private wrong": TeleZone at para. 78. [emphasis added] [154] Accordingly, "a party in these circumstances cannot proceed with both a civil action and a judicial review simultaneously", as the "two seek inconsistent remedies": Greengen at para 47. [155] As explained in Weisenburger: [34] Both doctrines of collateral attack and abuse of the court's process engage "the inherent power of the court to prevent the misuse of its procedure in a way that would be manifestly unfair to a party to the litigation before it or would in some other way bring the administration of justice into disrepute": Canam Enterprises Inc. v. Coles (2000), 51 OR (3d) 481 (CA) at para. 55 (per Goudge JA), rev'd 2002 SCC 63 (aff'g dissent of Goudge JA). The purpose of the doctrines is "to prevent unfairness by preventing 'abuse of the decision-making process'": Figliola at para. 34; Quinn v. British Columbia, 2018 BCCA 320 at para. 77, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 38375 (21 March 2019). [35] TeleZone and its subsequent jurisprudence establish that an individual who disagrees with an administrative decision has two options: (a) seek judicial review to have the decision overturned, quashed, or otherwise invalidated; or (b) abide by the decision and let it stand, and file an action raising a viable private law cause of action, if one exists in the circumstances. [36] As stated in TeleZone: [19] If a claimant seeks to set aside the order of a federal decision maker, it will have to proceed by judicial review, as the Grenier court held. However, if the claimant is content to let the order stand and instead seeks compensation for alleged losses (as here), there is no principled reason why it should be forced to detour to the Federal Court for the extra step of a judicial review application (itself sometimes a costly undertaking) when that is not the relief it seeks. Access to justice requires that the claimant be permitted to pursue its chosen remedy directly and, to the greatest extent possible, without procedural detours. [emphasis added] [156] Myers v. Canada (Attorney General), 2022 BCCA 160 emphasises that plaintiffs will only be permitted to pursue an action rather than seek relief via judicial review if they are content to accept the legal force of the governmental decision: [33] When TeleZone speaks of a party being content to "let the decision stand", it refers to a party who accepts the legal force of the decision in the sense that they accept that they are bound to abide by the decision and do not seek in the provincial superior court to overturn the decision or nullify its effects. But TeleZone does not say that the party may not, as part of a properly framed civil cause of action, seek to prove the unlawfulness of that federal decision as a material fact supporting their claim for damages. [emphasis in original] [157] As set out in Weisenburger at para 46, permissible damages claims based on putatively unlawful governmental actions will generally limit the relief sought to damages, and will not seek declarations or injunctions that seek to have those actions set aside. The present claim, as with the Weisenburger claim, seeks both damages and administrative law relief, revealing that the plaintiff is not content to "let the administrative decision stand" (Myers at para 33). Rather, in effect, the plaintiff seeks to do an end run around the proper avenue for judicial review of administrative actions by appending a damages claim. [158] The plaintiff in effect conceded the correctness of the defendants' position by withdrawing the administrative law relief at the head of its prayer for relief, in the 2023 proposed FANCC six weeks after the conclusion of the certification hearing. [159] That last-ditch attempt to save the claim is not determinative, even if this Court were to grant leave to file a late application to amend the ANCC, and even if the Court were to grant the order to amend in the form of the June 2023 FANCC. [160] The remedy sought is not in itself determinative of whether a claim is at its core an administrative challenge appropriately brought as a petition. The court must consider the essential nature of the claim. As stated in Scow [75] The essential nature of the claim must be based on a realistic appreciation of the practical result sought by the claimant. A judge must look beyond the words used in the pleading, the facts alleged, and the remedy sought to be satisfied that the claim is not an "artfully pleaded" but thinly disguised attempt to pursue a claim in an inappropriate forum . [emphasis in original] [161] In finding the present claim to be an abuse of process, I reach the same conclusion as with respect to the claim advanced in Weisenburger, which, as here, sought to avoid a judicial review process by framing its claim as a claim for Charter damages: [59] I reach a conclusion similar to that in Northern Cross at para. 68. The causes of action advanced in the pleadings raise issues that are precisely the kinds of issues that would be addressed on judicial review: Northern Cross at para. 61. Abuse of process: overlapping proceedings [163] As a further basis for striking the claim as an abuse of process, the plaintiff Society has filed three proceedings against the same defendant--Dr. Bonnie Henry--each concerning various health orders. It is an abuse of process for the plaintiff to bring this action against these defendants when it has already brought other proceedings in relation to the same subject matter (one petition of which has now been dismissed, and the other abandoned). [164] "It is well established that it is an abuse of process for the same plaintiffs to bring multiple proceedings against the same defendant in relation to the same subject matter": Drover v. BCE Inc., 2013 BCSC 1341 at para 41 [emphasis in original]. [165] Saskatchewan (Environment) v. Métis Nation - Saskatchewan, 2025 SCC 4 ["Métis Nation"] describes the doctrine of abuse of process: [33] The doctrine of abuse of process is concerned with the administration of justice and fairness (Behn, at para. 41). The doctrine engages the inherent power of the court to prevent misuse of its proceedings in a way that would be manifestly unfair to a party or would in some way bring the administration of justice into disrepute (Toronto (City) v. C.U.P.E., Local 79, 2003 SCC 63, [2003] 3 S.C.R. 77, at para. 37; Behn, at para. 39; Abrametz, at para. 33). [34] In Abrametz, this Court reiterated that abuse of process is a broad concept that applies in various contexts (para. 34, citing Toronto (City), at para. 36, and Behn, at para. 39). The Court noted that the doctrine of abuse of process is "characterized by its flexibility. It is not encumbered by specific requirements, unlike the concepts of res judicata and issue estoppel" (para. 35, citing Behn, at para. 40, and Toronto (City), at paras. 37-38). [35] One way in which an abuse of process can arise is by relitigation, that is, "where the litigation before the court is found to be in essence an attempt to relitigate a claim which the court has already determined" (Behn, at para. 40, quoting Canam Enterprises Inc. v. Coles (2000), 51 O.R. (3d) 481 (C.A.), at para. 56, rev'd 2002 SCC 63, [2002] 3 S.C.R. 307; see also Abrametz, at para. 34; and D. J. Lange, The Doctrine of Res Judicata in Canada (5th ed. 2021), at pp. 1-5). Relitigation will be an abuse of process if it violates "such principles as judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice" (Toronto (City), at para. 37; Behn, at para. 41). Where warranted, the doctrine of abuse of process can be relied on to strike pleadings so as to prevent relitigation of an issue (see Behn; Canam Enterprises Inc.). [166] In Métis Nation, as here, the abuse of process argument was directed towards the multiplicity of proceedings filed: [38] A multiplicity of proceedings which engage the same issues can amount to an abuse of process. In the foundational case of McHenry v. Lewis (1882), 22 Ch. D. 397, Sir George Jessel observed that: ". . . it is prima facie vexatious to bring two actions where one will do" (p. 400). Examples of where a multiplicity of proceedings has amounted to an abuse of process include: where two parallel class actions involving the same parties were brought in two different jurisdictions (Englund v. Pfizer Canada Inc., 2007 SKCA 62, 284 D.L.R. (4th) 94, at paras. 38-40); where plaintiffs initiated multiple actions claiming Aboriginal and treaty rights over the same land and natural resources (Dixon v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 ABQB 565); and where the plaintiffs provided "no viable explanation" for bringing a second action that duplicated the issue of ownership of a trade name which encapsulated the original defendants (Cashin Mortgages Inc. v. 2511311 Ontario Ltd., 2024 ONCA 103, 170 O.R. (3d) 107, at para. 14). [39] However, the fact that there are two or more ongoing legal proceedings which involve the same, or similar, parties or legal issues, is in itself not sufficient for an abuse of process. As this Court recognized in Toronto (City), "[t]here may be instances where relitigation will enhance, rather than impeach, the integrity of the judicial system" (para. 52). Similarly, there may be instances where parties have a valid reason for bringing separate, but related, proceedings; in such cases, a multiplicity of proceedings can serve to enhance the administration of justice (see, e.g., Birdseye Security Inc. v. Milosevic, 2020 ONCA 355, at paras. 20-22). The inverse can also be true: pleadings do not need to be identical in order for a multiplicity of proceedings to amount to abuse of process (see, e.g., Dixon, at para. 85; Fillion v. Degen, 2005 MBCA 58, 195 Man. R. (2d) 2, at para. 23). [40] Thus, the abuse of process analysis does not end when multiple or similar proceedings exist. Rather, the analysis needs to focus on whether allowing the litigation to proceed would violate such principles as judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice, as discussed above. Where, for example, having duplicative proceedings would waste the resources of the parties, courts and witnesses, or risk inconsistent results and therefore undermine the credibility of the judicial process, this can amount to an abuse of process. Stare decisis: was it reasonable to declare a state of emergency? [174] The claim is also bound to fail, as this Court has already determined the central plank of the plaintiff's proposed claim: whether the defendants have failed to establish the legally binding conditions necessary to declare a state of emergency under the EPA and PHA. [175] In Hoogerbrug, heard with two other proceedings, the petitioners were healthcare workers who had been terminated from their jobs as they were unvaccinated against Covid. They challenged the Healthcare Orders on administrative law and Charter grounds, arguing that the Healthcare Orders were an unreasonable exercise of the Health Officer's powers under the PHA, and that they unjustifiably infringed ss. 2(a) and 7 of the Charter. [176] The plaintiff in the present proceeding advances the first ground argued by the Hoogerbrug petitioners: "by October 2023, COVID-19 was no longer "an immediate and significant risk" to public health in British Columbia, and therefore the statutory preconditions for the continued use of the PHO's emergency powers no longer applied": para 4. [177] In Hoogerbrug, Justice Coval found that, as of October 5, 2023, the Health Officer's continued reliance on the declaration of a public health emergency under PHA, s. 52 was reasonable: para 198. [178] Hoogerbrug sets out the statutory preconditions for the use the emergency powers in response to a "regional event": Challenge 1: No Immediate and Significant Risk to Public Health [179] Under s. 52 of the PHA, for the PHO to use the emergency powers in response to a "regional event", there must be "an immediate and significant risk to public health throughout a region or the province", which the PHO "reasonably believes" satisfies two of the four criteria in s. 52(2). [180] The "regional event" declared by the PHO on March 17, 2020 was "the transmission of the infectious agent SARS-CoV-2". [181] The four criteria in s. 52(2) are: (a) the regional event could have a serious impact on public health; (b) the regional event is unusual or unexpected; (c) there is a significant risk of the spread of an infectious agent or a hazardous agent; and (d) there is a significant risk of travel or trade restrictions as a result of the regional event. [182] Recital A of the Orders says the PHO believed the s. 52(2) criteria continued to be met for the following reasons: (a) In view of the history and ongoing mutation of SARS-CoV-2, and the uncertainty which exists about its future behaviour, there continues to be a reasonable risk that it could have a serious impact on public health; (b) There is a continued reasonable risk of an unusual or unexpected occurrence of a new variant of SARS-CoV-2 which could cause serious disease among the population; [and] (c) The infectious agent, SARS-CoV-2 continues to mutate and new variants continue to spread in British Columbia, Canada and around the world. [183] In my view, the summaries above demonstrate abundant support in the record for the PHO to reasonably conclude that COVID-19 continued to represent a regional event that satisfied ss. 52(2)(a), (b), and (c). [emphasis added] [179] Hoogerbrug found that during the time period in question in that case, as well as the present, the Health Officer reasonably concluded that Covid continued to pose a pressing threat justifying statutory restrictions and health orders: [186] Another important consideration for this assessment is the "precautionary principle," which the petitioners acknowledged applied to the PHO's COVID-19 decision-making. The principle being that, in the face of serious threats, scientific uncertainty must be resolved in favour of protection of the healthcare system. It would be a dereliction of duty for the PHO to await the actual grips of another severe COVID-19 wave before taking steps to protect the healthcare system. As the point is sometimes put in the cases, to wait for certainty about the risks is to wait too long. . [188] As summarized above, the circumstances evident in the record included: (i) the three-year COVID-19 experience of an unprecedented and unpredictable virus, with the ability to create new variants, and to attack in waves causing widespread serious illness, death, and harm to the functioning of the healthcare system which stressed it beyond capacity to protect and care for the health needs of the population; (ii) the extreme contagiousness of Omicron and its variants, including within healthcare settings; (iii) the particular vulnerability of patients within the healthcare and long-term care settings; and (iv) the key negative indicators, leading up to October 2023, of rising COVID-19 severe outcomes and deaths, back to levels seen in the fall of 2021, as the annual onset of flu and other respiratory illnesses was about to arrive. [189] Turning to the s. 52(2) pre-conditions to the exercise of emergency powers, and beginning with s. 52(2)(a), for the reasons summarized in the previous paragraph, there was ample support in the record for the reasonableness of the PHO's conclusion that the transmission of SARS-CoV-2 continued to pose a significant and immediate risk that "could have a serious impact on public health", including the health of vulnerable patients and the capacity of our healthcare system to continue providing essential care for those afflicted by the virus or other serious illnesses or conditions. [190] Regarding s. 52(2)(b), I agree with counsel for the PHO that, despite the pandemic having commenced more than three and a half years earlier, in October 2023 COVID-19 continued to be an "unusual and unexpected" immediate and significant risk to public health. As counsel submitted, the overdose drug crisis is another example of an unusual and unexpected event giving rise to an extended emergency. [191] British Columbia had not previously faced the type of health crisis posed by this highly communicable, dangerous, global disease. The negative indicators, reasonable risk of new variants, previous waves of infections causing serious illness and harm to the public health system, and applicability of the precautionary principle all contributed to the reasonableness of continuing to characterize COVID-19 in this way. [192] Finally, regarding s. 52(2)(c), the petitioners acknowledged that at the time of the Orders, there continued to be a significant risk of the spread of SARS-CoV-2, and therefore s. 52(2)(c) was satisfied. I agree that is uncontroversial. [emphasis added] [180] Hoogerbrug found that even after the threat of Covid somewhat abated, the Health Officer's maintenance of heightened restriction for health care workers remained justified: [196] Outside of healthcare, the PHO saw it as safe to transition away from much of the former emergency regime, such as public masking, distancing, vaccine passports, and restrictions on gatherings and travel. As the record indicates, in large part this was due to the high percentage of the population that was vaccinated. At the same time, the record indicated good reason for continued vigilance within the healthcare system itself for the reasons that I have summarized above. [197] An important aspect of the within-healthcare context is that, as the record repeatedly indicated, hospital patients and long-term care residents are more vulnerable than the general population to COVID-19. This is due to pre-existing conditions, suppressed immune systems, and less protection from their own vaccination due to reduced antibody production. The petitioners did not contest this. It must also be borne in mind that, generally speaking, such patients do not choose to be in these healthcare settings. If there were no vaccine mandate, they could not simply choose to avoid receiving treatment from unvaccinated healthcare workers. This differentiates their situation from outside-healthcare settings, such as restaurants, gyms, etc., which the vulnerable can choose to avoid if the staff are unvaccinated. [198] In sum, in light of the record as of October 5, 2023 and applying the appropriate legal deference, in my view the record supported the reasonableness of the PHO's decision to continue to exercise the emergency powers in the PHA, to protect public health, and in particular the functioning of the healthcare system and the health of those most vulnerable to COVID-19. H. Section 92 challenge: Health Officer statutory immunity from Charter damages claim [183] All claims against the Health Officer are bound to fail. (2) Subsection (1) does not apply to a person referred to in that subsection in relation to anything done or omitted in bad faith. (3) Subsection (1) does not absolve the government or a health authority from vicarious liability arising out of anything done or omitted by a person referred to in that subsection for which the government or health authority would be vicariously liable if this section were not in force. [emphasis added] [184] In this, I reach a conclusion identical to that reached in Weisenburger, in respect of a similar claim directed against the Health Officer: [107] In a strike application, the court should consider and give considerable weight to such immunity provisions. If it is plain and obvious that an immunity applies in light of the facts pleaded, the claim should be struck: Ernst at paras. 19 (per Cromwell J), 67 (per Abella J, concurring), 149 (per McLachlin CJ dissenting); Walkom v. Law Society of British Columbia, 2019 BCCA 391 at para. 19. [108] In Ernst, the Supreme Court of Canada confirms the efficacy of such immunity clauses, striking a claim seeking Charter damages for alleged breaches of the plaintiff's s. 2(b) expression rights by the Alberta Energy Regulator. The Court notes that such clauses should be read expansively, to protect administrative decision-makers from the distraction of litigation flowing from the decisions they make in their legislatively-designated roles: [57] Immunity is easily frustrated where the mere pleading of an allegation of bad faith or punitive conduct in a statement of claim can call into question a decision-maker's conduct: Gonzalez, at para. 53. Even qualified immunity undermines the decision-maker's ability to act impartially and independently, as the mere threat of litigation, achieved by artful pleadings, will require the decision-maker to engage with claims brought against him or her. As Lord Denning M.R. held, to be truly free in thought, judges should not be "plagued with allegations of malice or ill-will or bias or anything of the kind": Sirros, at p. 136, cited by Morier, at pp. 739-40. [109] It is conceded that the Ernst Court was considering the Alberta Energy Regulator, serving a quasi-adjudicative function, with an ostensibly absolute immunity clause. But these words also speak for the most part to the present defendants, legislatively entrusted to carry out their important public functions, here as well as below, in the discussion of factors countervailing against the justice and appropriateness of a Charter damages claim. As noted by the Ernst Court, the immunity clause, there, as here, does not bar judicial oversight and intervention by way of judicial review; it only bars damages claims: para. 33. [111] Section 92 is drafted in the broadest possible terms. There is no pleaded allegation that the PH Officer is acting in any other capacity than "in the exercise or intended exercise of a power under this or any other enactment" (s. 92(1)(a)), and it is difficult to conceive how one could plead or prove such a proposition.[13] [emphasis added] [185] In its argument predating the now-binding authority of Weisenburger, the plaintiff seeks to avoid Ernst v. Alberta Energy Regulator, 2017 SCC 1 by noting the Court split 4-4-1. Despite the split, a majority of five justices agreed it was plain and obvious that the immunity clause immunises the Alberta Energy Regulator from Charter damages. Justice Cromwell, writing for four justices, expressly "agree[d] with Abella J. that we should hold that it is 'plain and obvious' that the immunity clause on its face bars Ms. Ernst's claim for Charter damages": para 15. The agreement of five justices represents the majority decision of the Court. [186] After the defendants noted in their application response that the plaintiff would need to challenge the constitutionality of PHA s. 92 to seek Charter damages from the Health Officer, the plaintiff proposed an amendment to its pleadings to do just that: FANCC Part 3, para 31. The plaintiff pleads no factual or legal basis, however, to support this challenge. [187] In short, there is no viable Charter damages claim against the Health Officer, on any legal theory. While the plaintiff may seek Charter damages from the Province on the basis that the Province is vicariously liable for the actions and omissions of the Health Officer, it is plain and obvious that the plaintiff cannot seek Charter damages from the Health Officer herself. I. Charter damages are neither appropriate nor just [188] In its submissions, the plaintiff acknowledges that its Charter claims are novel. They are novel in the sense that they are generally not supported by precedential authorities based on similar facts. They are unusual in the sense that they are solely founded on alleged breaches of the Charter, unaccompanied by other actions constituting independent torts. [189] These novel Charter claims are bound to fail based upon the Supreme Court of Canada jurisprudence governing Charter damages. In this, I reach a similar conclusion to that reached in Weisenburger at paras 113-123, where the Charter damages claim arising from Covid-related restrictions was struck, as judicial review would be an alternate and more effective remedy than would be Charter damages. As set out below, in the present case, the futile, complex, and circular damages distribution scheme provides yet another reason to find that a Charter damages claim as bound to fail, and that the proposed class proceeding should be filtered at the certification stage. [190] In Canada (Attorney General) v. Power, 2024 SCC 26 at para 42, the Supreme Court confirmed the four-step approach to the award of damages based upon ss. 24 and 52 of the Charter set out in Vancouver (City) v. Ward, 2010 SCC 27 at para 4: a) Has a Charter right been breached? b) Would damages fulfill one or more of the related functions of compensation, vindicating the right, or deterring future breaches? c) Has the state demonstrated that countervailing factors defeat the functional considerations that support a damage award and render damages inappropriate or unjust? d) What is the appropriate quantum of damages? [191] The plaintiff bears the burden of establishing a prima facie case by establishing the first two steps. If established, the onus shifts to the defendant to demonstrate that countervailing considerations render damages inappropriate or unjust. In the context of an application to strike under subrule 9-5(1)(a), the court must assess whether the plaintiff's claim has a "reasonable prospect of success" at each of the steps set out in Ward: Quinn v. British Columbia, 2018 BCCA 320 at para 49, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 38375 (21 March 2019). [192] All that said, Ernst tempers the availability of Charter damages. Cromwell J, writing for the majority, at para 25 quoted the following from Henry v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 24 at para 91: "when it comes to awarding Charter damages, courts must be careful not to extend their availability too far." Cromwell J further reminded that: [26] Not every bare allegation claiming Charter damages must proceed to an individualized, case-by-case consideration on its particular merits. Ward held that Charter damages will not be an appropriate and just remedy where there is an effective alternative remedy or where damages would be contrary to the demands of good governance. These considerations, taken together, support the conclusion that the proper balance would be struck by holding that damages are not an appropriate remedy. [emphasis added] [193] Ernst notes that Charter damages may be inappropriate where more effective avenues--such as judicial review--exist, or where allowance of Charter damages would interfere with concerns for effective governance: [27] Section 24(1) of the Charter confers on the courts a broad remedial authority. As has been said, "[i]t is difficult to imagine . . . a wider and less fettered discretion": Mills v. The Queen, [1986] 1 S.C.R. 863, at p. 965. This broad discretion should not be narrowed by "casting it in a straight-jacket of judicially prescribed conditions": Ward, at para. 18. But this does not mean that Charter breaches should always, or even routinely, be remedied by awards of Charter damages. The remedy of damages is limited to situations in which it is "appropriate and just" because it serves one or more of the compensatory, vindicatory and deterrent purposes which support that choice of remedy: Ward, at para. 32. Countervailing factors may establish that damages are not an appropriate and just remedy even though they would serve these ends: Ward, at para. 33. [28] The list of countervailing factors is not closed. So far, two have been identified: the existence of alternative remedies and concerns for good governance: Ward, at para. 33; see also para. 42. I conclude, therefore, that Ward does not preclude the immunity of the Board to Charter damages. Rather, Ward set out two countervailing factors that could negate the appropriateness of Charter damages and specifically left open the development of others. [29] The jurisprudence does not require that every pleaded claim for Charter damages be assessed on an individualized, case-by-case basis. Ward, for example, specifically contemplates the development of new defences to Charter damages claims and these defences are not limited to enhanced liability thresholds. Countervailing factors against granting Charter damages may be of a more generalized nature, reflecting the availability of other remedies, the accumulated wisdom of the common law and strong indications of public policy. [emphasis added] [194] Here, as in Ernst and Quinn, the claim founders on the third Power/Ward enquiry: countervailing factors--specifically the availability and greater appropriateness of other avenues--defeat any functional considerations that would support a damage award, and render damages inappropriate and unjust. Specifically, there is both an effective (and more appropriate) alternative remedy in the form of judicial review. Further, damages would be contrary to the demands of good governance. We will consider each of these in turn. [195] First, as set out above, the present claim is a thinly-veiled challenge to administrative decisions that would be appropriately brought as a judicial review. In Ernst, the existence of judicial review as a more appropriate remedy to address the essence of the plaintiff's complaints weighed heavily against the continued survival of a claim based in Charter damages: [30] First, there is an alternative remedy -- judicial review -- that substantially addresses the alleged Charter breach. Judicial review is available to vindicate Charter rights and to clarify the law so as to prevent similar future breaches. Second, good governance concerns are also engaged as granting damages undermines the effectiveness of the Board and inhibits effective governance. Third, to determine the appropriateness of Charter damages against this type of board on a case-by-case basis in a highly factual and contextual manner largely undermines the purposes served by an immunity. [31] When these countervailing factors are considered collectively -- that is, when one looks at their cumulative effect -- they negate the appropriateness of an otherwise functionally justified award of Charter damages against this Board. In short, damages are not an appropriate and just remedy for the Board's Charter breaches. [196] Ernst placed considerable weight on the greater efficacy of judicial review for addressing the plaintiff's complaints: [34] The availability of judicial review is important for two reasons. [35] First, judicial review can provide substantial and effective relief against alleged Charter breaches by a quasi-judicial and regulatory board like this one. The facts of this case strikingly illustrate the utility of the remedy of judicial review. The basis of Ms. Ernst's complaint is that the Board abused its discretion and breached the Charter by refusing to deal with her. If that claim were established in the context of judicial review, a superior court could set aside the directive which Ms. Ernst alleges was issued to stop interaction with her and could order corrective action. Such orders would go a long way towards vindicating Ms. Ernst's Charter rights. [36] Moreover, judicial review would in all likelihood provide vindication in a much more timely manner than an action for damages. Again, the facts of this case provide a good example of how this could be so. Ms. Ernst did not start her action for damages until some two years after the alleged breach, and several months after the Board had rescinded the directive which she challenged. A prompt application for judicial review had the potential to achieve practical relief much sooner. While an application for judicial review would not have led to an award of damages, it might well have addressed the breach much sooner and thereby significantly reduced the extent of its impact as well as vindicated Ms. Ernst's Charter right to freedom of expression. Finally, judicial review would have provided a convenient process to clarify what the Charter required of the Board. That sort of clarification plays an important role in preventing similar future rights infringements. [37] Thus, judicial review of the Board's decisions and directives has the potential to provide prompt vindication of Charter rights, to provide effective relief in relation to the Board's conduct in the future, to reduce the extent of any damage flowing from the breach, and to provide legal clarity to help prevent any future breach of a similar nature. While the remedies available under judicial review do not include Charter damages, Ward directs us to consider the existence of alternative remedies, not identical ones: para. 33. [emphasis added] [197] The Ernst observations dovetail with those discussed above with respect to the abuse of process posed by the plaintiff's claim. The plaintiff neither pleads nor argues any compelling basis for establishing that Charter damages are "appropriate and just", to address Ward, or that judicial review is not a more appropriate means to address her complaints, to address Ernst. Advancing the claims as Charter damages claims does not exempt those claims from a consideration of the appropriateness of proceeding by way of judicial review. Rather, the formulation of the plaintiff's grievances as Charter damages claims further emphasises and illustrates why these claims are nothing but thinly-veiled, artfully-pleaded attacks on administrative decisions that must be pursued by way of judicial review. The plaintiff in essence seeks damages on the basis that it disagrees with the manner in which the defendants are conducting their statutory decision-making authority: the quintessential focus of judicial review. [198] Second, good governance concerns weigh against Charter damages. At worst, on the facts pleaded by the plaintiff, the Health Officer and the Province did too much to protect the public from a novel communicable disease. This kind of good faith policy-making does not sound in damages: Ward at para 40. [199] Declaratory relief is a more appropriate remedy than Charter damages in a case like this one that deals with broad questions of policy, public health, and medical and epidemiological judgment. Of course, the plaintiff would prefer to avoid that administrative law remedy, as it appropriately requires deference to the expert decision maker entrusted by the legislature with those decisions, as seen in Hoogerbrug and Beaudoin, and many other Covid-related decisions. [200] Finally, again, in the unusual circumstances of this proposed proceeding, awarding Charter damages to class members would be circular, because the class consists of all British Columbia adults. Charter damages, like all expenditures of public funds, are funded by the public. This case is not like the few cases in which Charter damages have been awarded, where the public as a whole must compensate an individual or relatively small group of individuals who experienced harm. Given that the class is the entire public, the public as a whole would be compensating the public as a whole. The payees would be the payors. [201] In Kimpton v. Canada (Attorney General), 2002 BCSC 1645 at para 64-65, aff'd 2004 BCCA 72 the Court cited "the impact of such a finding on the taxpayers of the province who must ultimately fund any award of damages," among various reasons for declining to certify a proposed class proceeding alleging that the governmental defendants were negligent in drafting building codes. Section 7 Charter claims (life, liberty, and security of the person) [215] The plaintiff alleges that health measures have restricted s. 7 Charter rights in several manners. The plaintiff argues that the "[o]rders, directives, or decrees that prohibit or limit medical procedures infringe on the s. 7 Charter right of life, liberty and security of the person .": FANCC Part 3, para 29.c. The plaintiff specifically cites delayed access to medical procedures, limited reconsideration of PHA Orders, quarantines, and vaccine requirements for access to certain locations such as bars and cultural events.[14] The plaintiff also submits that the restrictions and the orders themselves engage class members' liberty under s. 7 of the Charter by restricting their ability to move about freely and by preventing them from having the same "access to property" as other members of the public. The plaintiff argues that the s. 7 liberty interest includes the "freedom to go to a restaurant or go shopping, to go for a walk, the freedom to meet your friends in the park, the freedom to recognise facial expressions, the freedom to smile and be smiled at." Finally, the plaintiff argues that the health measures have limited the ability of some class members, such as Ms Leppky, to work in their chosen professions. [216] Canadian appellate courts have repeatedly confirmed that s. 7 "is not synonymous with unconstrained freedom": R. v. Edwards Books and Art Ltd., [1986] 2 SCR 713, at pp 785-86; Blencoe v. British Columbia (Human Rights Commission), 2000 SCC 44 at para 54. The s. 7 liberty interest "encompasses only those matters that can properly be characterized as fundamentally or inherently personal such that, by their very nature, they implicate basic choices going to the core of what it means to enjoy individual dignity and independence": Godbout v. Longueuil (City), [1997] 3 SCR 844 at para 66. [217] In Taylor v. Newfoundland and Labrador, 2020 NLSC 125 at para 396, for example, the Court found that Covid-related travel restrictions did not violate the plaintiff's right to liberty under s. 7.[15] The Court helpfully summarises Supreme Court jurisprudence providing examples of the kind of fundamental personal choices that engage s. 7 at para 389: · In Carter [v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 5] a majority of the Court concluded that an individual's response to a grievous and irremediable medical condition is a matter critical to one's dignity and autonomy. As such, a law which interferes with the ability to make decisions concerning medical care and bodily integrity trenches on liberty under s. 7. · In B. (R.) v. Children's Aid Society of Metropolitan Toronto, [1995] 1 S.C.R. 315 the appellants argued that their right to choose (refuse) medical treatment for their child was a liberty interest protected by s. 7. The treatment in question being a blood transfusion. Justice LaForest, for the majority, agreed, but concluded on the facts that there had been no violation of the principles of fundamental justice. · In R. v. Morgentaler, [1988] 1 S.C.R. 30 Justice Wilson concluded that a woman's liberty interest was engaged in deciding whether or not to have an abortion, as the "decision is one that will have profound psychological, economic and social consequences for the pregnant woman" (at para. 300). · In R. v. Smith, 2015 SCC 34 restrictions on the manner medical marijuana could be ingested was held to violate the liberty interest of the appellant, by exposing him to a threat of imprisonment for violation and by foreclosing reasonable medical choices through the threat of criminal prosecution. By forcing a person to choose between a legal, but inadequate treatment (smoking dry marijuana), and an illegal but more effective choice (ingestion), the law also infringed security of the person. [218] In R. v. Malmo-Levine; R. v. Caine, 2003 SCC 74, the Court cautioned against stretching s.7 to cover all personal preferences. The accused argued that "smoking marihuana is integral to his preferred lifestyle, and that the criminalization of marihuana [ ] is an unacceptable infringement of his personal liberty". The Supreme Court of Canada rejected this broad and unconstrained conception of s.7: 86 While we accept Malmo-Levine's statement that smoking marihuana is central to his lifestyle, the Constitution cannot be stretched to afford protection to whatever activity an individual chooses to define as central to his or her lifestyle. One individual chooses to smoke marihuana; another has an obsessive interest in golf; a third is addicted to gambling. The appellant Caine invokes a taste for fatty foods. A society that extended constitutional protection to any and all such lifestyles would be ungovernable. Lifestyle choices of this order are not, we think, "basic choices going to the core of what it means to enjoy individual dignity and independence" (Godbout, supra, at para. 66). [219] Mr Parihar's choice to go to a bar, restaurant, or social event falls far short of the "core of what it means to enjoy individual dignity and independence": Godbout at para 66. [220] Beaudoin BCSC similarly rejected the petitioner's submission that s. 7 extended to protect against the loneliness, depression, anxiety, and other psychological and theological effects of restrictions on religious assembly, as evidence of those phenomena fell short of a threat to life or serious psychological harm: Beaudoin BCSC at paras 180, 184-85. The plaintiff made the identical submission in the Gatherings Petition, where it was rejected in CSASPP Gatherings BCSC: [147] The jurisprudence does not support a s. 7 right to publicly-accessible private establishments. In R. v. Heywood, [1994] 3 S.C.R. 761 [Heywood], Cory J. considered s. 179(1)(b) of the Criminal Code (now repealed). Section s. 179(1)(b) imposed lifetime bans from school grounds, playgrounds, public parks and bathing areas on individuals convicted of sexual assault. Violating the restriction would result in imprisonment. Cory J. held that s. 179(1)(b) indeed restricted the liberty of those it applied to and ultimately found the provision to be unjustifiably overbroad. In the result, the case law does not assist the petitioners. [149] On the evidence before me, I do not find that the impugned Orders breached s.7 of the Charter and I dismiss this aspect of the petition. [emphasis added] [222] Warner BCCA also specifically confirmed the rejection of the plaintiff's reliance on Heywood: para 48. Section 7 "encompasses fundamental life choices, not pure economic interests": Siemens v. Manitoba (Attorney General), 2003 SCC 3 at para 45. Edwards Books and Art Ltd., [1986] 2 SCR 713 at 785-86. The Charter also does not confer a "constitutional right to practise a profession unfettered by the applicable rules and standards which regulate that profession": see Mussani v. [277] Instead, they lost their jobs because they chose not to accept vaccination against a highly contagious virus which posed the risk of serious illness and death to vulnerable patients and other healthcare workers. In my view, this loss did not engage their s. 7 right to liberty because of the well-established principle that s. 7 does not protect the right to work in any specific employment or particular profession, particularly when the job-loss arises from non-compliance with its governing rules and regulations. This is not a constitutionally-protected fundamental life choice. [278] In my view, their s. 7 security of the person rights were also not engaged. The fact that they experienced serious consequences, including stress and hardship, from choosing to follow their personal convictions about vaccination does not make the Orders a state interference with their physical or psychological integrity. In effect, their position amounts to security of the person being engaged unless vaccination were a matter of free choice without any serious state-imposed consequences for refusal. As stated in by Chief Justice Lamer in J.G. at para. 59: It is clear that the right to security of the person does not protect the individual from the ordinary stresses and anxieties that a person of reasonable sensibility would suffer as a result of government action. If the right were interpreted with such broad sweep, countless government initiatives could be challenged on the ground that they infringe the right to security of the person, massively expanding the scope of judicial review, and, in the process, trivializing what it means for a right to be constitutionally protected [underlined emphasis in original, bold emphasis added] [225] As stated recently in Tatlock BCCA: [38] With respect to s. 7 of the Charter, the judge relied on a well-known line of authorities holding that state-imposed limitations on employment or the practice of an occupation do not engage the constitutionally protected right to liberty: see Siemens v. Manitoba (Attorney General), 2003 SCC 3; Walker v. Prince Edward Island, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 407; Green v. Law Society of Manitoba, 2017 SCC 20; B.C. Teachers' Federation v. School District No. 39 (Vancouver), 2003 BCCA 100 (Prowse J.A. dissenting); Tanase v. College of Dental Hygienists of Ontario, 2021 ONCA 482; Mussani v. College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario (2004), 248 D.L.R. (4th) 632 (ONCA); Maddock v. British Columbia, 2022 BCSC 1605, appeal dismissed as moot in Kassian 2023. The weight of authority certainly favours that view. [226] Further, the above passages and cited cases also confirm that the Healthcare Orders requiring vaccination for work in the health care field were not tantamount to compelling the petitioners to receive unwanted medical treatment, specifically vaccination, and therefore did not engage their bodily integrity and medical self-determination: Hoogerbrug at paras 18, 276 and 291; Maddock at paras 78-79. This Court's jurisprudence has made abundantly clear that Health Officer orders that impose consequences for failure to take a Covid vaccination (including significant consequences like the loss of employment) do not engage s. 7. These conclusions are binding on the plaintiff, and preclude its s. 7 Charter claims in respect of all of the impugned PHO orders. Quebec (Attorney General), 2005 SCC 35 for the proposition that "[e]xcessive waiting times in the public health care system" engage security of the person. It cites Carter v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 5 at paras 64-67 for the proposition that s. 7 protects "'a notion of personal autonomy involving . . . control over one's bodily integrity free from state interference' and also includes the right to 'fundamentally important and personal medical decision making'." [228] Again, the plaintiff made this same submission in the Gatherings Petition. [141] Pursuant to s. 7 of the Charter and the common law, the petitioners are clearly entitled to accept or decline even life-saving medical treatment: B. (R.) v. Children's Aid Society of Metropolitan Toronto, [1995] 1 S.C.R. 315; Malette v. Shulman (1990), 72 OR (2d) 417; Fleming v. Reid (1991) 4 OR (2d) 74 and Carter v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 5 [Carter]. It includes the decision of whether to take a COVID-19 vaccine. [142] In Carter, at para. 64, the Court discussed liberty and security of the person rights under s. 7: [64] Underlying both of these rights is a concern for the protection of individual autonomy and dignity. Liberty protects "the right to make fundamental personal choices free from state interference": Blencoe v. British Columbia (Human Rights Commission), 2000 SCC 44, [2000] 2 S.C.R. 307, at para. 54. Security of the person encompasses "a notion of personal autonomy involving . . . control over one's bodily integrity free from state interference" (Rodriguez, at pp. 587-88, per Sopinka J., referring to R. v. Morgentaler, 1988 CanLII 90 (SCC), [1988] 1 S.C.R. 30) and it is engaged by state interference with an individual's physical or psychological integrity, including any state action that causes physical or serious psychological suffering (New Brunswick (Minister of Health and Community Services) v. G. (J.), 1999 CanLII 653 (SCC), [1999] 3 S.C.R. 46, at para. 58; Blencoe, at paras. 55-57; Chaoulli, at para. 43, per Deschamps J.; para. 119, per McLachlin C.J. and Major J.; and paras. 191 and 200, per Binnie and LeBel JJ. [143] However, this line of cases does not assist the petitioners. The petitioners claim that the impugned Orders mandated vaccination and engaged the s. 7 liberty and security interests. This argument ignores the fact that vaccination was never mandatory. Rather, there was a period during which discretionary activities were restricted to those who had become vaccinated against COVID-19. [emphasis added] [229] Warner BCCA confirmed the Chief Justice's analysis: [45] Underlying Mr. Warner's inability to access privately-accessed establishments was his decision not to be vaccinated. In our view, the Chief Justice was correct in deciding that Mr. Warner's liberty interest was preserved, as the choice to become vaccinated or not was always available. [46] The Chief Justice properly noted that s. 7 encompasses the right to accept or decline medical treatment; and, as he observed, Mr. Warner was not compelled to receive or prohibited from obtaining any medical treatment and nor was any medical decision imposed upon him. This makes Mr. Warner's arguments concerning the health risks associated with vaccination beside the point. He remained free to choose whether or not to become vaccinated. Indeed, he exercised that freedom of choice by deciding not to get vaccinated. [emphasis added] [230] Chaoulli, like Cambie Surgeries Corporation v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2022 BCCA 245 ["Cambie Surgeries BCCA"] challenged legislative provisions barring individuals from using their own resources to obtain private health care for themselves through private payment. In contrast, the present claim asserts in essence that the government was required to provide health care for class members more promptly than it did, which it failed to do due to cancelled and postponed treatments and surgery. [231] Cambie Surgeries BCCA at para 10 and Chaoulli at para 104 confirm that there is no "freestanding constitutional right to healthcare." Cambie Surgeries BCCA, at para 240 confirms that, "[w]e think it stretches the definition of "liberty" too far to suggest the choice of private or public medical care is a fundamental choice related to autonomy and human dignity." Even if either defendant were responsible for postponing or cancelling publicly-funded medical procedures, such actions would not engage s. 7 security of the person rights. As there is no s. 7 right to publicly funded health care, there can be no s. 7 right to publicly funded health care provided within any particular time. [232] The plaintiff further argues that health orders threaten the s. 7 liberty of class members because a breach of an order is a provincial offence that could in theory result in imprisonment of up to six months: 53.E.b. PHA Orders in respect of vaccination against COVID-19 created an offence punishable by imprisonment for failure to comply, thereby depriving the Vaccination Subclass of liberty interests protected by s. 7 of the Charter. 53.E.c. The deprivations of liberty interests set out above were arbitrary and overbroad as the PHA orders were of the broadest possible scope, and could have been used to imprison persons in circumstances that were fundamentally unjust. The deprivation of liberty interests did little or nothing to enhance the state's interest in reducing the impact of COVID-19, which interest could have been sufficiently and better addressed while considering accommodations or exemptions such as an exemption requiring proof of a negative COVID-19 test or natural immunity. Further, the state interest in increasing the uptake of COVID-19 vaccines was a lesser state interest (compared to reducing the impact of COVID-19), and the deprivations of liberty interests were grossly disproportionate to this object. [June 2023 FANCC Part 1] [233] In Warner BCCA, the Court specifically confirmed that a remote possibility of imprisonment will not engage the s. 7 liberty interest. At paras 53-57 the Court endorsed the statement in Ontario (Attorney General) v. Bogaerts, 2019 ONCA 876 at para 49: "where there are intermediate steps between the operation of a provision and deprivation of liberty, the court will not engage in speculation as to the possible eventual outcome to bring the case within s. 7." [234] For a person to be imprisoned for breaching a Health Officer order, several contingent and indeed unlikely intermediate steps would have to first occur, as per Bogaerts. First, the peace officer would decide to recommend charges by way of information, rather than simply issuing a violation ticket. Second, the Crown would decide to approve the charge. Third, the trial judge would have to convict the individual. Fourth, the trial judge would have to decide to sentence the accused to imprisonment: a wholly unlikely event, given general sentencing principles that seek to avoid incarceration if other reasonable options are available, and the bounty of those other options for non-violent crimes, in PHA ss. 105-10, and generally. [235] The plaintiff did not cite any examples of any person being imprisoned for a breach of any of the impugned orders.[16] The Court is not aware of any instances. Given this, and the intermediate steps above, the possibility of imprisonment for a breach is beyond speculative. As all health orders have now been repealed, there is no ongoing threat or possibility of future imprisonment. [236] If and when a person is actually charged and prosecuted for breaching health measures, with a real risk of incarceration (as was the case in Hillier, as set out above), that person would have standing to invoke s. 7 on the basis of the real and imminent possibility, for that individual, of imprisonment. That s. 7 challenge would be heard as part of the prosecution of the individual, not as a freestanding action for Charter damages in a class proceeding on hypothetical and unparticularised facts. It is plain and obvious that the class will not be entitled to Charter damages on the basis that class members could hypothetically have been imprisoned, even assuming that the particular intermediate steps had in fact occurred. [237] The plaintiff also argues that certain measures are arbitrary, overbroad, or have consequences that are grossly disproportionate to their object. The plaintiff submits that unspecified orders are overbroad and thus arbitrary because they do not contain "reasonable exemptions", including exemptions for persons who have "natural immunity", and because they prohibit access to certain venues while allowing access to others: 2023 FANCC, Part 1, paras 53, 53.E.c., 53.J. and Part 3, para 11.a. The plaintiff submits that overbreadth should be inferred from an alleged "absence of evidence regarding both effectiveness, and comparative effectiveness, in relation to achieving objectives " [238] In the s. 7 context, arbitrariness means that "there is no connection between the effect and the object of the law": Canada (Attorney General) v. Bedford, 2013 SCC 72 at para 98. It is plain and obvious that all the Health Officer's orders had a rational connection to the public health objectives. In the s. 7 context, "gross disproportionality" means that the effects of the impugned law or order "are so grossly disproportionate to its purposes that they cannot rationally be supported": Bedford at para 120. The principle against gross disproportionality "only applies in extreme cases where the seriousness of the deprivation is totally out of sync with the objective of the measure": Bedford at para 120. In other words, "The connection between the draconian impact of the law and its object must be entirely outside the norms accepted in our free and democratic society" [emphasis added]. In Bedford at para 120, the Supreme Court of Canada gives such an example: a clean streets law that imposes a life imprisonment sentence for spitting on the sidewalk. [239] Further, the plaintiff's submissions overlook the fact that the plaintiff, not the defendants, bears the persuasive burden of proving overbreadth: Bedford at paras 97-123, 127. At this stage, the plaintiff must plead facts that, if true, could support a finding of overbreadth. It has not done so. Again, none of the present pleadings come close to that standard. [240] In any case, again, this pleading butts up against the findings of the Court of Appeal in Beaudoin BCCA at paras 267, 274, 276, and 309 that the health orders restricting religious gatherings were proportionate. It also butts up against the Hoogerbrug holding that the Healthcare Orders were consistent with principles of fundamental justice: para 295. More specifically, Justice Coval held that the Healthcare Orders were neither arbitrary, overbroad, nor grossly disproportionate: paras 296-299.[17] Given the findings in Hoogerbrug and Beaudoin, and almost every other Covid-related decision, no conceivable pleading, let alone evidence at trial, could approach the high Bedford standard. [241] Thus even if the plaintiff could show that s. 7 was engaged, it will not be able to show that any deprivation of a s. 7 right was inconsistent with the principles of fundamental justice. Its s. 7 claims are bound to fail. [242] The recent Warner BCCA decision also bars the s. 7 claims. At para 52, the Court of Appeal stated that "Mr. Warner failed to adduce evidence sufficient to establish that the Impugned Orders interfered with his ability to make fundamental personal decisions or otherwise interfered with his personal autonomy so as to constitute a breach of s. 7." [243] Finally, no s. 7 breach is possible under the present ANCC. The sole plaintiff is the corporate Society. Section 7 applies only to natural persons: Irwin Toy Ltd. v. Quebec (Attorney General), [1989] 1 SCR 927 at 1004. [244] The claim, as framed, discloses no reasonable cause of action under s. 7. L. It then rescinded them in light of the defendants' correct observation that those orders had nothing to do with quarantining, but rather addressed intra-provincial travel in certain regions of the province between April and June 2021.[18] [246] The June 2023 proposed FANCC now challenges unspecified "[o]rders, directives or decrees that require quarantine", suggesting that such orders constitute arbitrary detention or imprisonment under s. 9 of the Charter: Part 1, para 53.B.b, Part 3, para 29(d). The most recent FANCC fails to provide requisite particulars, instead vaguely asserting: i. Verbal orders, directives or decrees made at various times, including but not limited to September 8, 2020, March 28, 2020, December 31, 2021, and statements found on the BC CDC website from time to time relating to self-isolation requirements; ii. Such further orders as may be advised at trial. [June 2023 FANCC Part 3, para 29(d)] [247] In argument, the plaintiff referred not to enactments but rather to interviews and media releases issued by the Health Officer that make passing reference to federal quarantine requirements and recommendations to self-isolate. The plaintiff provides no authority for the proposition that these statements are justiciable. In any case, Beaudoin BCCA at para 169 makes clear that the Health Officer's passing recommendations in media briefings and interviews do not in themselves constitute justiciable orders. In the specific context of Covid restrictions, courts have confirmed that even actual quarantine requirements (in contrast to the metaphorical concept advanced by the plaintiff) do not constitute "arbitrary detention or imprisonment" under s. 9. [249] In Spencer v. Canada (Health), 2021 FC 621 at paras 161-168, appeal dismissed as moot: Spencer v. Canada (Attorney General), 2023 FCA 8, for example, the Federal Court considered a federal order that required persons entering Canada to quarantine in a government-approved hotel for up to 72 hours while awaiting the result of a Covid test. The Court held that the effect of the order was to detain persons, but not arbitrarily, given the public health rationale for the order. Section 9 of the Charter was thus not infringed.[19] A similar Ontario challenge was also dismissed: Canadian Constitution Foundation v. Attorney General of Canada, 2021 ONSC 4744. [250] In any case, any British Columbia orders or directives were far less restrictive than the federal order at issue in Spencer, as they merely limited travel between different regions of British Columbia, as opposed to requiring persons to remain in a government-approved hotel. In any case, any detention was not arbitrary but was rationally related to public health objectives, as the Federal Court and Ontario cases confirm. Section 15 Charter claim (equality) [252] The ANCC advances two s. 15 claims: one by the proposed religious subclass and one by "those that require a second dose of vaccination within the manufacturer's recommended time intervals" (the latter since abandoned). The proposed 2023 FANCC identifies as offending s. 15 orders, directives, or decrees that prohibit or limit medical procedures or that mandate vaccination, "because by treating everyone equally created a distinction on an enumerated ground and were discriminatory in their effect": Part 3, para 29.c. [253] The claim by persons who allegedly require a second dose of vaccination sooner than others is bound to fail. [254] Requiring a second dose of vaccination sooner than others is not an enumerated or analogous ground. [255] Section 15 prohibits discrimination on prohibited grounds, i.e., "race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, sex, age or mental or physical disability" and analogous grounds such as sexual orientation. [256] Beaudoin BCSC (see also Beaudoin BCCA at para 227), sets out the two steps the claimant must prove to establish to establish a breach of s.15: [188] Section 15(1) of the Charter protects the equality rights of, inter alia, religious individuals. Establishing a violation of s. 15(1) requires the claimant to pass the following two-stage analysis: (a) Does the impugned law, on its face or in its impact, create a distinction on the basis of an enumerated or analogous ground? (b) If it does draw a distinction, does the impugned law fail to respond to the actual capacities and needs of the members of the group and instead impose burdens or deny a benefit in a manner that has the effect of reinforcing, perpetuating, or exacerbating their disadvantage? [257] Corbiere v. Canada (Minister of Indian and Northern Affairs), [1999] 2 SCR 203 sets out the approach for considering a new proposed analogous ground: 13 What then are the criteria by which we identify a ground of distinction as analogous? The obvious answer is that we look for grounds of distinction that are analogous or like the grounds enumerated in s. 15 -- race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, sex, age, or mental or physical disability. It seems to us that what these grounds have in common is the fact that they often serve as the basis for stereotypical decisions made not on the basis of merit but on the basis of a personal characteristic that is immutable or changeable only at unacceptable cost to personal identity. This suggests that the thrust of identification of analogous grounds at the second stage of the Law analysis is to reveal grounds based on characteristics that we cannot change or that the government has no legitimate interest in expecting us to change to receive equal treatment under the law. To put it another way, s. 15 targets the denial of equal treatment on grounds that are actually immutable, like race, or constructively immutable, like religion. Other factors identified in the cases as associated with the enumerated and analogous grounds, like the fact that the decision adversely impacts on a discrete and insular minority or a group that has been historically discriminated against, may be seen to flow from the central concept of immutable or constructively immutable personal characteristics, which too often have served as illegitimate and demeaning proxies for merit-based decision making. [258] Those persons who allegedly "requir[ed] a second dose of vaccination within the manufacturer's recommended time intervals" is not analogous to any of the enumerated grounds. It is not like race, religion, or disability: it carries no evidenced history of stereotyping or discrimination. It is not immutable or constructively immutable. The analogous ground newly proposed in the FANCC--people who eschew vaccination or who have experienced delayed medical procedures--does not constitute an analogous ground flowing from an immutable characteristic. [259] In Koehler, similarly, the Court at para 70 found that individuals prevented from visiting their seasonal homes and businesses in Newfoundland, cast as unequal treatment under the law based on place of residence, was not an analogous ground protected by s. 15. [260] The defendants concede that differing treatment and effects on individuals with certain medical conditions could constitute analogous grounds. Again, were the proposed claim not otherwise defective on multiple grounds, the Court might have contemplated leave to amend on that narrow point. The constitutional challenge then centrally focuses on whether the measures were "reasonable limits prescribed by law as can be demonstrably justified in a free and democratic society", distilled to the enquiry as to whether they were reasonable and proportionate in the circumstances: Charter s. 1, Beaudoin BCCA at para 290. [263] As set out above, in Hoogerbrug, Coval J applied the Doré v. Barreau du Québec, 2012 SCC 12, s.1 framework to conclude that the Healthcare Orders "represent a proportionate balancing of the public health objectives of the PHA and the petitioners' Charter-protected freedom of religion": para 314. [264] Similarly, in Beaudoin BCCA the Court of Appeal concluded that containing the spread of Covid and the protection of public health is a legitimate objective which can support limits on religious freedoms as set out in the gathering and events restrictions presently challenged in this proceeding: [267] In my view, the limitation on the religious freedom of the appellants stemming from the G&E orders has been shown to be a proportionate one in light of the unprecedented risk to public health that arose during the second wave of the virus, the need to take precautions to stop preventable deaths from occurring, and the need to protect the capacity of the healthcare system. [268] I emphasize that hindsight has no place in this analysis: Trinity Bible Chapel at para. 6(2). Regard must be had to what was known about the potential for the virus to cause widespread death and disable the delivery of essential services, including health care services to British Columbians. The analysis must recognize that, when the orders were made, vaccines were not widely available. The prospect of the exponential growth of COVID-19 cases was very real. Failing to act in a timely and reasonable way to prevent transmission in settings identified as high-risk could lead to the imposition of more extreme measures at a future date to curb the spread of the virus. . [307] The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted our interdependence as a community. It forced us to confront the reality that the pursuit of some activities, including the exercise of some constitutionally protected rights, would increase the risk of exponential spread of the disease and the loss of human life. In the exercise of her responsibility to safeguard public health and access to our healthcare system, the PHO made time-limited and setting-specific orders restricting activities she considered to be most likely to foster widespread transmission of the virus. She was uniquely qualified to make these decisions and the exercise of her judgment must be afforded deference. [emphasis added] [265] All of the bases for the plaintiff's Charter challenges will butt up against these broad pronouncements about the rationality and proportionality of the health measures taken in the face of the real-time and evolving threat posed by the pandemic. [266] It is conceded that the plaintiff may be able to successfully argue that the measures were not rational or proportionate with respect to the certain specific restrictions and effects. For example, while in Hoogerbrug Coval J dismissed the petition with respect to most categories of employees, he found that it was disproportionate to require administrative remote managerial health care employees to show proof of vaccination: para 225. [267] That said, such arguments would face two very high hurdles. First, most of the restrictive effects presently complained of are far less fundamental than those raised in Beaudoin BCCA: a restriction on gathering in restaurants falls far short of restrictions of employment or religious gathering. Second, Beaudoin BCCA itself anticipates that its broad pronouncements on the rationality and proportionality of the measures would be of broad application, regardless of the alleged infringement: [234] In the case at bar, I can identify no analytical route that could conceivably lead to a different application of the proportionality analysis if, in addition to the s. 2 breaches, the orders were also found to have violated s. 15 . VI. Proposed class proceeding certification [269] Given the conclusions above, in striking the claim, there is no need to continue with the analysis under the Act. For completeness, however, these reasons will identify other difficulties with the claim, to illustrate how it would have failed the certification test in any case. A. Law: class proceeding certification [270] As set out in the introduction, the plaintiff bears the onus of satisfying each of the five certification requirements in s. 4(1) of the Act. [271] The party seeking certification of a class action bears the burden of showing some basis in fact for every certification criterion: Hollick v. Toronto (City), 2001 SCC 68 at para 25; AIC Limited v. Fischer, 2013 SCC 69 at para 48. There must be sufficient evidence to satisfy the Court that the conditions for certification have been met to a degree that should allow the matter to proceed on a class basis: Pro-Sys Consultants Ltd. v. Microsoft Corporation, 2013 SCC 57 at paras 99-104. [272] Class certification does not concern the merits of the action, but merely its form. The certification motion is not a trial. It is entirely procedural. As stated in Hollick: [16] the certification stage is decidedly not meant to be a test of the merits of the action: see Class Proceedings Act, 1992, s. 5(5) ("An order certifying a class proceeding is not a determination of the merits of the proceeding"); see also Caputo v. Imperial Tobacco Ltd., (1997), 34 O.R. (3d) 314 (Gen. Div.), at p. 320 ("any inquiry into the merits of the action will not be relevant on a motion for certification"). Rather the certification stage focuses on the form of the action. The question at the certification stage is not whether the claim is likely to succeed, but whether the suit is appropriately prosecuted as a class action . [emphasis in original] [273] Hollick establishes that the evidentiary burden on the plaintiff on a certification motion is low: [25] ... In my view, the class representative must show some basis in fact for each of the certification requirements set out in s. 5 of the Act, other than the requirement that the pleadings disclose a cause of action. That latter requirement is of course governed by the rule that a pleading should not be struck for failure to disclose a cause of action unless it is "plain and obvious" that no claim exists . [274] Specifically, the applicant need not establish its case on the evidentiary level of "balance of probabilities". As stated in Pro-Sys: [102] Had McLachlin C.J. intended that the standard of proof to meet the certification requirements was a "balance of probabilities", that is what she would have stated. There is nothing obscure here. The Hollick standard has never been judicially interpreted to require evidence on a balance of probabilities. Further, Microsoft's reliance on U.S. law is novel and departs from the Hollick standard. The "some basis in fact" standard does not require that the court resolve conflicting facts and evidence at the certification stage. Rather, it reflects the fact that at the certification stage "the court is ill-equipped to resolve conflicts in the evidence or to engage in the finely calibrated assessments of evidentiary weight" . The certification stage does not involve an assessment of the merits of the claim and is not intended to be a pronouncement on the viability or strength of the action; "rather, it focuses on the form of the action in order to determine whether the action can appropriately go forward as a class proceeding". [citations omitted] B. Issue (a): do the pleadings disclose a cause of action? [275] The test under s. 4(1)(a) is the same as the test for striking pleadings under Rule 9-5(1)(a): Pearce v. 4 Pillars Consulting Group Inc., 2021 BCCA 198 at para 55. As recently summarised by Justice Fleming (before her translation to the Court of Appeal) in Sutherland v. Electronic Arts Inc., 2024 BCSC 2202: [33] The s. 4(1)(a) requirement is assessed on the same standard that applies to motions to strike under R. 9-5(1) of the Supreme Court Civil Rules, B.C. Reg. 168/2009. The question is whether, assuming the pleaded facts to be true, it is plain and obvious the claim cannot succeed or is bound to fail: Microsoft at para. 63; Sun-Rype Products Ltd. v. Archer Daniels Midland Company, 2013 SCC 58 at para. 31. A claim will be bound to fail if the pleading does not set out the elements of the cause of action and the material facts in support of them: R. v. Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42 at para. 22. The standard is a low one with a view to allowing claims to go forward if they have any chance of success or if an amendment would resolve the problem: Finkel v. Coast Capital Savings Credit Union, 2017 BCCA 361 at para. 17; Sandhu v. HSBC Finance Mortgages Inc., 2016 BCCA 301 at para. 44. In the same way, the test is applied by reading the pleadings generously. [276] As with strike applications, the certification jurisprudence has become more stringent and less forgiving, emphasising in more recent times the court's duty to serve as a gatekeeper, scrupulously screening proposed class proceedings to ensure their suitability and conserve finite judicial resources: WCB v. Sort at para 100; Sutherland at para 34, Bowman v. Kimberly-Clark Corporation, 2023 BCSC 1495 at para 14, Atlantic Lottery Corp. Inc. v. Babstock, 2020 SCC 19 at paras 18-19 ["Babstock"]; Wakelam v. Wyeth Consumer Healthcare/Wyeth Soins de Sante Inc., 2014 BCCA 36 at para 64, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 35800 (4 September 2014). [277] Although Hunt confirms at 980 that "the novelty of the cause of action" should not in itself bar a claim (whether on a strike application or a certification hearing), the more recent Supreme Court of Canada authority of Babstock, a case specifically discussing class proceeding certification, emphasises that hopeless claims should be put out of their misery at the certification stage. As stated in Kett: [59] In applying the pleadings requirement, the court must keep in mind the "cultural shift" mandated by the Supreme Court in Hryniak v. Mauldin, 2014 SCC 7, which emphasizes "resolving questions of law by striking claims that have no reasonable chance of success": Babstock at para. 18. The Hryniak decision has been interpreted as giving judges "the power to be bold...to strike hopeless claims": Bruno v. Samson Cree Nation, 2020 ABQB 504 at para. 26. As Justice Brown states in Babstock: [19] [A] claim will not survive an application to strike simply because it is novel. It is beneficial, and indeed critical to the viability of civil justice and public access thereto that claims, including novel claims, which are doomed to fail be disposed of at an early stage in the pleadings. This is because such claims present "no legal justification for a protracted and expensive trial". If a court would not recognize a novel claim when the facts as pleaded are taken to be true, the claim is plainly doomed to fail and should be struck. In making this determination, it is not uncommon for courts to resolve complex questions of law and policy. [Citations omitted. Emphasis in original.] [278] The Federal Court of Appeal emphasises this judicial duty, again in the class proceeding context, in Mohr v. National Hockey League, 2022 FCA 145, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 40426 (20 April 2023): [48] The fact that the law has not yet recognized a particular claim, interpretation, or cause of action is not determinative of the outcome of the motion. Novel but arguable claims must be allowed to proceed to trial as new developments in the law often find their provenance in surviving motions to strike . [49] There is, however, a countervailing principle. Motions to strike serve an important screening or gatekeeping function. They are essential to effective and fair litigation and prevent unnecessary effort and expense being devoted to cases that have no reasonable prospect of success. This is particularly true in the context of class actions, where plaintiffs may have fundraised to cover their expenses and where they are relieved from paying costs when they are unsuccessful on interlocutory matters along the way. [50] There is also a broad cost to access to justice. The diversion of scarce judicial resources to cases which have no substance diverts time away from cases that require attention. The point was well made by Stratas J.A. in Coote v. Lawyers' Professional Indemnity Company, 2013 FCA 143, 229 A.C.W.S. (3d) 935, at paragraph 13 when he wrote that "[d]evoting resources to one case for no good reason deprives the others for no good reason." Legal principles [280] Section 4(1)(b) requires that there be an identifiable class of two or more persons. Western Canadian Shopping Centres v. Dutton, 2001 SCC 46 ["Dutton"], describes the requirement for an identifiable class: [38] First, the class must be capable of clear definition. Class definition is critical because it identifies the individuals entitled to notice, entitled to relief (if relief is awarded), and bound by the judgment. It is essential, therefore, that the class be defined clearly at the outset of the litigation. The definition should state objective criteria by which members of the class can be identified. While the criteria should bear a rational relationship to the common issues asserted by all class members, the criteria should not depend on the outcome of the litigation. It is not necessary that every class member be named or known. It is necessary, however, that any particular person's claim to membership in the class be determinable by stated, objective criteria. [citations omitted] [281] Jiang v. Peoples Trust Company, 2017 BCCA 119 summarises the principles governing the second requirement: [82] In sum, the principles governing the identifiable class requirement may be summarized as follows: · the purposes of the identifiable class requirement are to determine who is entitled to notice, who is entitled to relief, and who is bound by the final judgment; · the class must be defined with reference to objective criteria that do not depend on the merits of the claim; · the class definition must bear a rational relationship to the common issues -- it should not be unnecessarily broad, but nor should it arbitrarily exclude potential class members; and · the evidence adduced by the plaintiff must be such that it establishes some basis in fact that at least two persons could self-identify as class members and could later prove they are members of the class. [emphasis in original] [282] The plaintiff must show that there is some basis in fact that (a) the common issue exists; and (b) the proposed issue can be answered in common across the class: Lilleyman v. Bumble Bee Foods LLC, 2024 ONCA 606 at paras 67-70, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 41489 (27 March 2025); Jensen v. Samsung Electronics Co. Ltd., 2023 FCA 89 at paras 71-91. This is a low evidentiary standard: Lilleyman at para 71; Flesch v. Apache Corporation, 2022 ABCA 374 at para 28. 2. Discussion and decision [288] Even in its most recent iteration, the class and sub-classes are vague and yet overbroad. As defined, they undermine objective self-identification of potential members. For example, it is not clear if class membership is limited to those presently resident in British Columbia, or resident in British Columbia during the impugned governmental measures, or whether it would include those who have since moved. The phrase "subject to" is vague: does it require the class member to actually be affected, or affected adversely, by the specific public health order? "Orders, directives, or decrees" is also broad. [289] Further, subclasses should only be certified where there are both common issues for the class members as a whole, and other issues that are common to some but not all of the class members: Caputo v. Imperial Tobacco Ltd., 236 DLR (4th) 348, 2004 CanLII 24753 (Ont SCJ) at para 45; Merck Frosst Canada Ltd. v. Wuttunee, 2009 SKCA 43 at paras 125-132, lv to appeal to SCC refused, 32905 (22 October 2009). The plaintiff must provide some basis in fact for an overarching central commonality among class members. As stated in Merck Frosst: [125] What the statutory provisions would appear to envision, however, is not that the identifiable class should be entirely composed of an array of subclasses, each with its own distinct set of claims and common issues, but, rather, that there should be a single, over-riding class, with its set of issues common to all members of a class, some of whom might form a subclass with a distinct additional set of issues common to its members but not other members of the class as a whole. This seems to be required by the statutory definition of "class" in s. 2 of the Act as "two or more persons with common issues respecting a cause of action or a potential cause of action," together with the certification requirement in s. 6(1)(b) that there be an identifiable class. Clearly, the more the subclasses are seen to have in common only issues distinct from one another, the less likely it is that a single class, or, indeed, a single class action, has been identified. [emphasis added] [290] The proposed proceeding lacks meaningful commonality amongst the members of the class, and amongst the members of each subclass. The only commonality among class members is that they were each affected in some way by the pandemic: as was every British Columbian. There is no evidence that there was any common means as to how each person within the class or subclass was affected. As expanded below, under the discussion of the proposed claims, the plaintiff's own affiants experienced pandemic-related orders, directives, and decrees in different manners and degrees. [291] Even the proposed subclasses lack commonality, and suffer from further difficulties. [292] The plaintiff has failed to acquit its onus of establishing a basis in fact to indicate that the medical subclass even exists, let alone its efficient commonality. As set out above, the plaintiff has not established a basis in fact that the named defendants themselves cancelled or postponed any medical procedures in the face of the defendants' affirmative evidence that while some surgeries were postponed, they were not postponed due to the actions or directives of either defendant. The uncontradicted evidence is that regional health authorities and officers, hospitals, and surgeons were independently responsible for determining which surgeries would be postponed and which would not.[20] [293] Even if there were a basis in fact to conclude the medical subclass exists, there would be no meaningful commonality within it. Inherently, every person whose surgery was postponed had a specific medical condition that affected them in specific ways depending on their individual circumstances; the effects of each postponed surgery also accordingly differed individually, undermining the s. 4(1)(c) common issues requirement. [294] The proposed vaccination subclass is also vague. The defendants' rhetorical questions in their submissions highlight this difficulty. Does it include persons who in the end received a second dose between September 13, 2021 and April 8, 2022? Or is it limited to persons who were still without a second dose on April 8, 2022? If it is the former, different vaccination subclass members will have been restricted by the impugned orders for different durations, indicating, again, a lack of commonality in their claims. If it is the latter, there is still no meaningful commonality amongst members of the proposed subclass: different people have chosen not to get vaccinated for different reasons, and different people have been affected differently by the restrictions on unvaccinated persons. Again, this undermines the 4(1)(c) common issues criterion, discussed below. [295] The proposed religious subclass is also vague. "Religious belief" is one of the most amorphous and variegated concepts in the history of human thought. Does it refer to organised religions, or idiosyncratic beliefs intensely (or otherwise) held by an individual. Does it refer to mandatory dogma of a given religion, or an individual belief ascribed to a religion of some form, organised or otherwise? [296] The definition of the proposed religious subclass is specifically incoherent: persons "whose religious beliefs prevented them from attending in-person gatherings" clearly have no claim. If this pleading is a typographical error, and the intended pleading was "whose religious beliefs required them to attend in-person gatherings", there is unacceptable heterogeneity among subclass members: different subclass members will have materially different religious beliefs and therefore claims. There is no single workable subclass with sufficient commonality. The heterogeneity of religious beliefs results in a wide variety of experience within the subclass, as defined. Even more profoundly than the other proposed subclasses, the proposed religious subclass definition clearly will predominate individual issues over common issues. [297] In this, the proposed class suffers the same fatal individualised assessment difficulty as that identified in the KO mental illness-related stigma proposed class proceeding: [49] The proposed class definition also lacks objective clarity. A subjective preliminary inquiry will be required about individual personal characteristics before membership in the main class may be established: Monaco v. Coquitlam (City), 2015 BCSC 2421 at para. 152. It would not only be just and fair, but also vital from the viewpoint of protecting our public finances, for the defendant to be permitted, at a minimum, to cross-examine putative class members about whether they have actually been diagnosed with a mental disorder stipulated in the class definition, or a "related disorder" if such is the case, and whether they have ever sought treatment from anyone for whom the defendant might be responsible. In my view, the inevitability of such substantial threshold inquiries is an insurmountable obstacle to certification . D. Issue (c): do the claims of the class members raise common issues? 1. Legal principles [298] To satisfy s. 4(1)(c), the action must raise common issues of fact or law. The common issues need not be determinative of liability. The issues must, however, advance the litigation in a material way for the benefit of all class members. The essence of a common issue is that it be a substantial ingredient of each class member's claim: Harrington v. Dow Corning Corp., 2000 BCCA 605 at para 24; Campbell v. Flexwatt Corp., 44 BCLR (3d) 343 at para 53, 1997 CanLII 4111 (CA), lv to appeal to SCC ref'd 26433 (14 May 1998); Hollick at para 18. [299] The Act, s. 1 defines common issues as "(a) common but not necessarily identical issues of fact, or (b) common but not necessarily identical issues of law that arise from common but not necessarily identical facts". [300] Dutton describes the commonality requirement: [39] ...[T]here must be issues of fact or law common to all class members. Commonality tests have been a source of confusion in the courts. The commonality question should be approached purposively. The underlying question is whether allowing the suit to proceed as a representative one will avoid duplication of fact-finding or legal analysis. Thus an issue will be "common" only where its resolution is necessary to the resolution of each class member's claim. It is not essential that the class members be identically situated vis-à-vis the opposing party. Nor is it necessary that common issues predominate over non-common issues or that the resolution of the common issues would be determinative of each class member's claim. [emphasis added] [301] As an indicia of a common issue, success for one class member must bring with it benefit for all the other class members: Dutton at para 40. [302] Sutherland further summarises this principle: [161] Subsequently the Court of Appeal has identified the commonality threshold as low, setting out that a triable factual or legal issue which advances the litigation (for or against the class) when it is determined is sufficient: Charlton v. Abbott Laboratories Ltd., 2015 BCCA 26 at para. 85; Service v. University of Victoria, 2019 BCCA 474 at para. 59; Finkel at para. 22. [162] Put another way, a proposed common issue will not move the litigation forward if it is dependent on individual findings of fact that must be made for each class member, framed in overly broad terms, or not capable of benefitting all class members if successfully prosecuted: MacKinnon v. Pfizer Canada Inc., 2021 BCSC 1093 at para. 88 (appeal allowed on other grounds). [emphasis added] 2. Proposed common issues [303] Most proposed class proceedings propose five or six common issues. In its June 2023 FANCC, the plaintiff proposes no fewer than 26 common issues, set out in Part IV.E, above.[21] 3. Discussion and decision a) Introduction [304] Again, given the conclusions in these reasons, and given the plaintiff's proposed amendments, this section will only briefly discuss some of the plaintiff's failures to satisfy this requirement of the test. b) Administrative law issues (#1-9) [305] These administrative law issues should not be certified because they are unrelated to the relief sought and thus would not advance class members' claims, for the reasons set out above. Again, the plaintiff has largely, but not wholly, abandoned the administrative law relief: it is no longer seeking an order setting aside the impugned public health orders as unreasonable. [306] In any case, the proposed common issues 1 through 9 are not appropriate common issues because their resolution would not do anything to advance the claims of class members. As set out above, a person who has been affected by an order that is later shown to be unreasonable is not, for that reason, entitled to Charter damages. The administrative law reasonableness of the impugned orders is irrelevant to whether the orders have infringed class members' Charter rights. That question falls to be determined on the basis of the effects of the orders on class members, not the administrative law reasonableness of the order. [307] Further, as noted in Sutherland at para 161 and Kett at para 121, common issues "need to advance the litigation in a material way for the benefit of class members. The essence of a common issue is that it is a substantial ingredient of each class member's claim." At para 134, Kett confirms that "[a] common issue should be one in which all class members at least have an interest." Given the re-abandonment of the administrative law relief, proposed common issues 1 to 9 would not advance the litigation for class members. They are not an ingredient of each class member's claim. Those issues have nothing to do with the other claims, apart from a broad thematic connection to Covid measures. As stated in Kett at para 139 about the broadly-cast claim before it: "[t]he breadth of this claim degrades its commonality." c) Charter issues (#10-24) [308] We come to the central fatal flaw in the proposed class proceeding: the claims, based on alleged Charter breaches, and seeking damages based on those breaches, require highly individualised inquiries that would be antithetical to efficient adjudication of common issues. The impugned health measures affected different class members differently. Each issue will require the Court to hear evidence and make findings about whether and to what extent the relevant order or orders affected individuals on a class member-by-class member basis. A common issue is a question that can be answered for all class members (or subclass members, as applicable) "without individualized investigation": Ewert v. Canada (Attorney General), 2022 BCCA 131 at para 25. [309] For this reason, Charter claims are rarely certified as class proceedings: Ward K. Branch & H. Michael Rosenberg, Class Actions in Canada, at 6-20 ["Branch Text"]. Two decisions of our Court of Appeal, arising from exceptional searches of inmates, allegedly contrary to their Charter rights, demonstrate how the necessarily individualised enquiry will elude common determinations and certification, even with a smaller proposed class with greater homogeneity than in the proposed proceeding. [310] In Thorburn v. British Columbia (Public Safety and Solicitor General), 2013 BCCA 480 ["Thorburn BCCA"], the proposed class proceeding sought Charter damages for the alleged violation of the s. 8 Charter rights of all persons who were subjected to routine strip searches of all new arrivals to the city jail over a five-year period. The Court of Appeal noted that "[r]esolution of the common issues is the heart of a class proceeding" (para 35), and stated: [39] Certification of a common issue will not move the litigation forward if: (i) it is dependent on individual findings of fact that must be made for each class member (Lam v. University of British Columbia, 2010 BCCA 325 at paras. 26 and 75, 321 D.L.R. (4th) 150); (ii) it is framed in overly broad terms (Rumley at para. 29); or (iii) it is not capable of benefitting all members of the class if successfully prosecuted (Western Canadian Shopping Centres Inc. v. Dutton at para. 40). In these instances the practical effect of certifying a class action would break down into numerous individual proceedings and therefore would not serve the ends of fairness or efficiency. [311] At para 41 the Court held that individualised investigation would be needed to determine whether each search was reasonable: While a warrantless search is presumptively unreasonable, a Charter right is individual in nature. Individual assessments would be necessary to determine if reasonable grounds existed [ ]. Each of these legal and factual determinations would require a consideration of the multifarious circumstances of each class member (e.g., the reason for the arrest, any prior criminal record or acts of violence and/or possession of weapons, and the extent of the possibility he or she might be remanded into custody). An unreasonable policy alone could not provide the foundation for determining each class member's cause of action of an unreasonable search; only an individual assessment of the relevant circumstances unique to each class member would allow a judge to determine if a cause of action had been established. [emphasis added] [312] In the facts leading to Ewert, on two dates in January 2010, all 215 inmates of the Kent Institution were subject to certain exceptional searches. Mr Ewert commenced a proposed class action, in tort and under ss. 7 and 12 of the Charter, on behalf of the inmates. The chambers judge certified as common issues broad questions based on the elements of the torts and Charter breaches alleged: paras 1-3. The Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, holding at para 6 that the issues were "unacceptably entangled with factual matters requiring individualized investigation". The issues in Ewert required individualised investigation because the alleged effects of the impugned searches were not the same for everyone: "each inmate will likely have had a somewhat different experience": para 49. Although there was likely some commonality among the experiences of at least some class members, individualised investigation would be required to find that commonality: "it would only become clear at trial which issues are truly common": paras 39-42. [313] Similarly, in Monaco v. Coquitlam (City), 2015 BCSC 2421 at para 142, Justice Abrioux (before his translation to the Court of Appeal) dismissed a certification application seeking damages arising out of invalid property searches in asserted breach of s. 8 of the Charter. Each class member would need to establish that their reasonable expectations were breached: [127] I agree with the defendants' submission that each member of the putative Class will have to establish that he/she had an expectation of privacy which was breached. That may well be accomplished in some circumstances, impossible to in others, and there may well be a number of circumstances which are not readily obvious. [314] Abrioux J distinguished the case before him from two others (Murray v. East Coast Forensic Hospital, 2015 NSSC 61, rev'd in part Capital District Health Authority v. Murray, 2017 NSCA 28 and Good v. Toronto Police Services Board, 2014 ONSC 4583, aff'd 2016 ONCA 250, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 37050 (10 November 2016)) where "the claims for Charter damages flowed from a single event which affected all putative class members at the same time, occurred on one occasion and was predicated on a single decision by the relevant authorities or state actors": para 184. In contrast, Monaco necessitated a highly individualised process, reflecting the individual nature of each allegedly unconstitutional search: para 128. To this end, at para 115 Abrioux J cited Thorburn BCCA at para 41 and Thorburn v. British Columbia (Public Safety and Solicitor General), 2012 BCSC 1585 at para 76: [41] An unreasonable policy alone could not provide the foundation for determining each class member's cause of action of an unreasonable search; only an individual assessment of the relevant circumstances unique to each class member would allow a judge to determine if a cause of action had been established. [76] it is the lawfulness of the search and not the policy itself which must be considered in an action for damages. [315] More recently, in KO, Justice Baird declined to certify a claim seeking Charter damages for the provincial government's failure to take adequate steps to combat mental health stigma, on behalf of "all persons who, on or after August 1, 1974, suffered from mental illness while resident in British Columbia": para 46. While noting that mental health stigma, and its harmful effects, are generally recognised, Baird J. held the harm suffered by individual members of the class would vary widely amongst the class members, and elude common determination: [52] Class action certification does nothing to move litigation forward if, in reality, the alleged common issues are dependent on individual findings of fact: Thorburn v. British Columbia (Ministry of Public Safety and Solicitor General), 2013 BCCA 480 at para. 39. No matter how the common issues might be framed in the present case, the practical reality, one that I foresee with great clarity, is that the proposed class proceeding will necessarily and infallibly devolve into a myriad of separate trials in which infinitely varied issues of fact, credibility, fault, causation, injury and damages will have to be independently evaluated and proved, as in the overwhelming majority of cases where damages are claimed for individual personal injury or Charter rights violations. [emphasis added] [316] Recently, in Dang v. Canada (Attorney General), 2025 BCSC 1143, Justice Giaschi declined to certify a claim seeking damages for alleged breaches of ss. 2(b), (c), (d), 7, 8 and 9 of the Charter for RCMP actions in enforcing Fairy Creek injunctions: a much more discrete event in time and space, with hundreds, not millions, of potential class members. In declining certification, the Court noted the lack of homogeneity amongst class members and the need for individualised assessment of Charter breaches and damages: [180] As in Thorburn and Cirillo, individualized and particularized assessments are required to resolve the Charter issues in this case. Indeed, given the nature of the protests at Fairy Creek, the multiple and disparate locations where enforcement actions took place, the differing circumstances for the different enforcement operations, the evolving nature of the protests and enforcement actions, and the time period involved, there is a very high degree of individualized and particularized assessments that are required in this case to resolve the Charter issues. There is no commonality that allows them to be resolved on a class wide basis. [317] Like the issues in Ewert, Thorburn, Monaco, KO, and Dang, the Charter issues in this case are inescapably entangled with factual matters requiring individualised investigation. The legal tests for each of the invoked Charter provisions will inevitably require individual analyses of each class member's particular circumstances in order to determine if that class member's rights were infringed. [318] In this, I agree with the defendants that the Charter issues contain at least the following embedded individual issues: a) What is the type and severity of the harm alleged by the class member? For example, was the class member prohibited from attending a social gathering, sporting event, bar, or restaurant? Is the class member not permitted to practice their preferred profession or work in a certain setting? Has the class member been dismissed from their employment? Has the class member lost business revenue, relative to time periods before Covid? Has the class member suffered negative physical or mental health impacts? b) Is the class member's harm caused by one or more of the impugned orders? Or, for example, is the harm caused by a decision made by a Medical Health Officer, regional health authority, hospital, doctor, or other health professional? c) Is there an exemption available to the class member under the impugned order? For example, would the health of the class member be seriously jeopardised if the class member was to be vaccinated such that they may request an exemption from the requirement of providing proof of vaccination? d) If the impugned order relates to vaccination and the class member is not vaccinated, why has the class member chosen not to be vaccinated? Is it a sincere religious objection? Is it a concern about vaccine safety or efficacy? Is it a conspiracy theory? e) Did the class member request a reconsideration of the impugned order under s. 43 of the PHA, such that it is the class member's particular reconsideration decision, rather than the impugned order, that is properly reviewable by the Court? f) If the class member did not request a reconsideration of the impugned order under s. 43 of the PHA, can the class member establish, based on their particular circumstances, that s. 43 does not provide an adequate alternative remedy? Or, based on the class member's particular circumstances, should the Court grant an individual exception to the general rule about exhausting internal remedies? [319] The plaintiff's own affidavits illustrate how the health measures affected different individual in different manners, requiring individualised investigations. Ms Leppky averred that she was unable to work as a care attendant as her religion prohibits vaccination. In contrast, Ms Martinson was vaccinated early, but claims that she experienced stress and anxiety because she did not receive her second dose sooner. Ms D'Amico had to close her yoga studio. Ms Lale had to cancel her piano lessons. Ms Gauthier had to wait three months for a hysterectomy. Mr Warner could not take a planned vacation, and had to miss a performance of a string quartet. Mr Parihar could not attend yoga classes or hockey games. [320] These effects, and the severity or triviality of these effects, and the cause of these effects, differ by individual. Even assuming that each affiant's assertions can be established at trial, each individual will have to prove that the health measures in fact caused these effects and in fact inflicted damages and had effects that would not otherwise have occurred in any case. These individual analyses defy the common issues requirement. There is no basis in fact for the existence of class-wide harm. There is no basis in fact for a class-wide methodology that would allow that question to be determined commonly for all the class members. [321] The plaintiff's own evidence from its seven selected would-be class member affiants highlights the lack of factual commonality that would require individual inquiry. Multiplied by the roughly 4 million class members, it illustrates not only the lack of predominant commonality, but also how a class proceeding would be wholly unworkable. [322] Similarly, the specific issues proposed by the plaintiff illustrate the absence of common issues, even within each of the plaintiff's own proposed subclasses. [323] For members of the religious subclass (common issue #10), the effect of not attending in-person gatherings will vary by religion, the practices and formalism of each religious subsect, and the individual adherence to those principles. For some, an inability to worship in person may be profound; for others, it will have no effect. [324] For members of the medical subclass (common issue #12), the effect of delayed or cancelled surgery will be profoundly idiosyncratic. For substantial surgery, delay could be fatal; for cosmetic surgery, delay could be inconsequential or even beneficial. [325] In this, the condition and complaint of the proposed representative plaintiff Ms Gauthier illustrates the lack of commonality amongst her class: the effects of a medical condition, and thus the effects of a delay in surgery, will depend on a patient's unique circumstances. Her affidavit states that in March 2020, she was suffering from a uterine disease which caused her chronic pain and stress and affected her ability to work. Her hysterectomy scheduled for March 27, 2020 was postponed to late June 2020. Her doctor prescribed a pain medication. But Ms Gauthier could not afford that medication, in part because restaurants (including the pub where she worked as a server and manager) had to close in March 2020. [326] The essence of Ms Gauthier's claim is that the decision to postpone her hysterectomy from late March 2020 to late June 2020 caused her to suffer unnecessary pain. Her claim depends on several idiosyncratic facts that elude common adjudication. Her condition, age, medical history, and amenability to pain medication would all be relevant in determining whether and to what extent the health measures caused that pain. [327] A similarly individualised analysis would have to be carried out with respect to the remaining roughly 27,999 postponed or cancelled surgeries. For some patients, delay could have been consequential. For others, delay could have been inconsequential, or solely an inconvenience not quantifiable in damages. Some patients may have welcomed postponement of a hospital stay during the pandemic, or because the previously scheduled surgery or appointment was personally inconvenient. As stated in Cambie Surgeries Corporation v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2020 BCSC 1310 at para 1744, aff'd 2022 BCCA 245, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 40412 (6 April 2023), "[d]ifferent patients respond differently to wait times".[22] [328] For members of the vaccination subclass (common issue #16), different people have opted against vaccination a first or second time for different reasons. Some feared the safety of the vaccine; some considered it unnecessary, some did so for religious or philosophical reasons; some do not believe in vaccinations generally, some feared side effects; some expected that a previous Covid infection would guard them from future infection; some suffered from pre-existing health conditions making vaccination inappropriate; some suffered an adverse reaction from an earlier vaccination. Some declined because of more fanciful explanations: fear that the vaccine injected microchips, to allow population control and tracking by various political and business figures, or that vaccines cause autism. [329] The reasons for which a person has chosen not to be vaccinated are centrally relevant to their claim. A claim by one of the few people in British Columbia who experienced a genuine adverse reaction to immunisation differs markedly from a claim by someone who believes the vaccine contains microchips designed to allow population tracking or control. There is heterogeneity within the vaccination subclass because there is heterogeneity among the reasons for which subclass members have chosen not to be vaccinated. [330] On top of each of these variables, different people have been affected differently by the restrictions on unvaccinated persons. This heterogeneity is illustrated by the proposed representative plaintiff, Ms Leppky, who worked as a care attendant. She claims that she lost her job as her religious beliefs prevented her from adhering to health care provider vaccination requirements. But other vaccination subclass members who did not work in health care, or who had different religious beliefs, or religious beliefs of different intensity and dogma, would not have been not affected by the impugned orders in the same manner as Ms Leppky. [331] More broadly, many members of the proposed subclass would not have been affected by any restrictions on employment or attending restaurants or bars due to unvaccinated status. Many would have avoided public congregations during a pandemic in any case. Many would not have frequented bars or restaurants for financial or social reasons, regardless of any public restrictions. The effect on impugned health measures on these subclasses and sub-subclasses and sub-sub-subclasses would differ considerably from that on those individuals who frequented bars and restaurants before the restrictions. [332] In sum, a class proceeding will inevitably fracture into individualised investigations: different subclass members had different reasons for choosing not to get vaccinated, and different subclass members were affected differently by the restrictions on unvaccinated persons. There is no basis in fact that the claims of the vaccination subclass members raise common issues which are properly resolved across the subclass, without regard to the circumstances of any individual member. [333] Finally, some of the complaints of the members within the plaintiff's proposed sub-classes are contradictory and thus lack commonality. For example, Ms Martinson complains in her affidavit that she was not allowed to get vaccinated swiftly enough. In contrast, most of the other plaintiff affiants complain about pandemic directives that encourage or require vaccination in the first place. [334] Similarly, the existence and calculation of Charter damages are also inherently individual issues. In assessing the appropriateness of Charter damages, the Court would have to consider the nature and severity of the Charter infringement. This is not just an issue of quantum. In deciding whether to award Charter damages at all, the Court would have to consider the effects on class members, which are individual. As stated by the Court of Appeal in Thorburn at para 42 and Ewert at para 30, "the appropriateness of Charter damages [ ] requires individual findings specific to the proposed class member." [335] I conclude this section by addressing the cases primarily relied upon by the plaintiff, where Ontario Courts certified class proceedings that included Charter claims: Brazeau v. Canada (Attorney General), 2020 ONCA 184, Johnson v. Ontario, 2016 ONSC 5314, and Good, supra. The present claim is readily distinguishable from those three cases with smaller, more focused, discrete, and homogenous issues and classes. [336] Brazeau concerned inmates in a specific federal penitentiary subjected to solitary confinement over at least 15 consecutive days or while experiencing mental illness. Good concerned protestors who had been arrested by police at a G20 summit, allegedly as a result of a single police order to arrest everyone without regard to their specific conduct. Johnson concerned inmates of a correctional facility that was allegedly overcrowded and failed to provide adequate medical care. In each of these cases, class members shared a narrow common experience: being detained in administrative segregation under the same policy in the same location, in the same period (Brazeau), being arrested at the same protest (Good), or being detained at the same correctional facility (Johnson). Although each class member's experience would have been slightly different, there existed a discrete unifying baseline establishing common issues. Further, the central issue was not whether they had suffered a Charter violation, or whether they had been solitarily confined, but simply to what degree. Further, in contrast to the present class of millions of British Columbians, Brazeau (roughly 2,000 class members), Johnson (roughly 9,000 class members), and Good (roughly 1,000 class members) were much smaller and simpler cases than that proposed here, with millions of plaintiffs. [337] In this, the KO distinguishment of Brazeau speaks equally to the present case: [53] As for the Charter claims, moreover, this is not a case analogous to Brazeau v. Canada (Attorney General), 2020 ONCA 184, in which, without any serious dispute as to the essential facts, a law already judicially determined to be unconstitutional was found to have a commonly quantifiable negative impact upon the rights of a neatly circumscribed class of citizens (federal inmates with diagnosed mental illnesses known to prison officials) within a specific controlled setting to which they were all exposed (penitentiary segregation units). Here, instead, the wrong alleged is the impact of a policy gap or omission upon a potentially enormous and ill-defined demographic of differently situated members of the public at large, and liability under the Charter is rationally and vigorously denied. In these circumstances, I maintain that the evaluation of claims for damages under s. 24(1) must necessarily be an individual exercise. [54] In my view, in other words, there can be no realistic talk of commonality from one plaintiff to another in this very different context. Each case will depend on the gravity of the specific Charter infringement alleged and, if proved, the seriousness of its impact on the subjective plaintiff's Charter-protected rights, as well as a showing that damages will be a just and appropriate remedy, and if so, in what amount. Any such claim, furthermore, will be subject to countervailing considerations that the defendant may legitimately raise, and that were not available on the facts in Brazeau, including the existence of alternative remedies and concerns for good governance: Vancouver (City) v. Ward, 2010 SCC 27, especially at para. 33. [55] One of the central purposes of the CPA, namely the avoidance of duplication in fact-finding or legal analysis, cannot be achieved across a multiplicity of claims for damages which, as I have already said, are bound to be as distinct and various as the claimants themselves. In my view, therefore, there will be no gain to the judicial system or its users through employment of the class procedure, and there is no point in postponing the recognition of this obvious fact out of an excess of judicial reticence: Parsons v. Coast Capital Savings Credit Union, 2007 BCCA 247 at para. 38. [emphasis added] [338] In this, the Dang distinguishment of Good also speaks equally to the present case: [156] There are obvious similarities between the circumstances in Good and those before me in that both concern demonstrations or protests and the lawfulness of detentions and arrests by police. However, there are also marked differences. Specifically, the class definition that was certified in Good was much more circumscribed than the definition before me. In Good, the class comprised only individuals who were arrested at specific locations on specific dates. This is in marked contrast to the class definition proposed before me. [169] There was commonality in each subclass in Good because the putative members of the location subclasses were arrested/detained in the same event. In contrast, here the searches, seizures, arrests, detentions, and/or restrictions of movement occurred at different times and locations and under different circumstances. There is no unifying or common factor in the case before me as there was in Good. [emphasis added] d) The plaintiff's aggregate damages proposal [339] As indicated by proposed common issues #20 and #21, the plaintiff proposes that an aggregate damages award, rather than individual damages calculations, could avoid the spectre of thousands or millions of individual damages trials. [340] The Act allows the court to order such damages if "the aggregate or a part of the defendant's liability to some or all class members can reasonably be determined without proof by individual class members", among other factors: s. 29(1)(c). [341] In Fulawka v. Bank of Nova Scotia, 2012 ONCA 443, lv to appeal to SCC ref'd, 34932 (20 March 2013), Chief Justice Winkler described the nature of aggregate damages: 126. Finally, s. 24(1)(c) states that the aggregate of the defendant's liability "can reasonably be determined without proof by individual class members." This provision is directed at those situations where the monetary liability to some or all of the class is ascertainable on a global basis, and is not contingent on proof from individual class members as to the quantum of monetary relief owed to them. In other words, it is a figure arrived at through an aggregate assessment of global damages, as opposed to through an aggregation of individual claims requiring proof from individual class members. I would describe the latter calculation as a "bottom-up" approach whereas the statute envisages that the assessment under s. 24(1) be "top down". [emphasis added] [342] Thus ultimately, aggregate damages bring the issue back to the same problem: the lack of commonality and the necessity of individual assessment. That is, the damages scheme is inefficient if the individual issues outnumber the common issues. In particular, Charter damages claims are generally unsuitable for aggregate assessment, as canvassed above, due to the necessity of individualised enquiry. [343] Similarly, in Bennett v. Hydro One Inc., 2018 ONSC 7741, the Court refused to certify aggregate damages as a common issue in a proposed class action based on systemic electricity overcharges. To assess damages, there would inevitably be individual issues trials to determine, for each of the 1.3 million class members, what loss was suffered and what compensation each had received, as not every class member was exposed to the risks of systemic negligence: paras 27, 40. [344] Recently, and very much on point, in Ingram v. Alberta, 2024 ABKB 631 ["Ingram 2024"], Justice Feasby soundly rejected the plaintiff's proposal that aggregate damages be certified as a common issue in a proposed class proceeding on behalf of individual (rather than corporate) business owners in Alberta, for losses caused by Covid-related business restrictions: [101] The Proposed Representative Plaintiffs assert that damages may be assessed on an aggregate class-wide basis using "statistical evidence based on random sampling." There is no basis in fact for this assertion. The Proposed Representative Plaintiffs adduced no evidence to support their claim that damages can be assessed on an aggregate basis. Alberta has provided expert evidence that an assessment of damages requires an examination of each individual business, its structure, and its accounting methodology. [102] The damages sought by the Proposed Representative Plaintiffs are mainly to compensate for what may be characterized as business losses. The proposed class is comprised of owners of businesses that participated in different industries and the businesses operated at different locations within Alberta during the relevant period. The CMOH Orders affected different industries and different businesses within industries differently. Different industries, different businesses, and different locations were subject to different market conditions, trends, and forces. Moreover, different businesses adopted different mitigation strategies. The task of assessing aggregate damages on a class-wide basis in this case is a fool's errand. The present case is one that cries out for individual assessment of damages. [emphasis added] [345] Those words speak even more profoundly in the present case, with 4 million members. Ingram 2024 was limited to several thousand individual business owners. Further, the Ingram 2024 class proceeding was facilitated by the prior judicial finding that the health orders were issued ultras vires: by the provincial cabinet rather than independently by the Chief Medical Officer: Ingram v. Alberta (Chief Medical Officer of Health), 2023 ABKB 453 at para 520. As noted by Feasby J at para 5 in Ingram 2024, the "[p]roposed class proceeding cannot be understood apart from its predecessor action" given that the ultra vires orders "are the common thread that links the causes of action pleaded by the Proposed Representative Plaintiffs." That prior judicial determination provided baseline commonality, as well as liability, and simplicity: all factors lacking in the present claim. [346] Again, similar problems arise with this present multifaceted case, as canvassed throughout these reasons. For example: a) Not every British Columbian was exposed to the alleged harms relating to the impugned health measures. b) Aggregate damages are inappropriate for members of the medical subclass as the harms suffered are not uniform. It would be necessary to scrutinise whether the rescheduling of each medical procedure caused actual loss, the degree of that loss, and whether recompense had already been achieved. c) For the religious subclass, the basis for the various religious beliefs, the sincerity of each class member's beliefs, and any resultant harm would need to be evaluated in order to determine their entitlement to damages. d) Exemptions and accommodations for the vaccination subclass are made on a case-by-case basis. While the reasonableness of each exemption may be outside the scope of the certification assessment, the fact that this analysis would inevitably break down into individual assessments if certified speaks volumes to the fact that this a class proceeding not the preferable procedure. [347] Attempting to overcome the individualised assessment difficulty with aggregate damages, the plaintiff cites the broad opening to Ramdath v. George Brown College, 2014 ONSC 3066, rev'd in part on other grounds 2015 ONCA 921, a proposed class proceeding arising from alleged misrepresentations made to predominantly foreign students about the qualifications they would receive at the end of their programme: [1] Aggregate damages are essential to the continuing viability of the class action. If all or part of the defendant's monetary liability to class members can be fairly and reasonably determined without proof by individual class members, then class action judges should do so routinely and without hesitation. Aggregate damage awards should be more the norm, than the exception. Otherwise, the potential of the class action for enhancing access to justice will not be realized. [348] The plaintiff does not, however, quote the immediate next paragraph in Ramdath, which adds an important qualification: [2] If, however, all or part of the defendant's monetary liability cannot be fairly and reasonably determined without proof by individual class members, then individual assessments must be undertaken. [349] Later, Ramdath emphasises at para 46 that the court may award aggregate damages "if the evidence put forward by class counsel is sufficiently reliable to permit a just determination of all or part of the defendant's monetary liability without proof by individual class members." (emphasis added.) [350] The Ontario Court of Appeal recently revisited Ramdath in Spina v. Shoppers Drug Mart Inc., 2024 ONCA 642 at paras 196-201. The Court confirmed that aggregate damages are only appropriate where it is possible to conduct a "'top down' global damages assessment, as opposed to a 'bottom up' aggregation of individual claims": para 197. The Court noted that Ramdath itself refused aggregate damages with respect to income lost during and after the programme, as those categories of loss could not be reasonably determined without individualised evidence: Ramdath at paras 60-66. Spina confirmed the trial judge's refusal to award or certify as a common issue aggregate damages in that case, based on the necessity of individual assessment of idiosyncratic harms: paras 219-225. [351] The plaintiff also cites Ontario cases where aggregate damages have been awarded in a Charter damages claim: Brazeau and Good, cited above. Again, they are distinguishable on this point for the same reasons above: the numbers of class members were much smaller, and the Charter breaches arose out of the same discrete policy and actions, and did not require significant individualised assessments. E. Issue (d): would a class proceeding be the preferable procedure for the fair and efficient resolution of the common issues? 1. Legal principles [352] The plaintiff must establish that certifying this action as a class proceeding is the preferable method of resolving the claims. As the Supreme Court of Canada stated in AIC Limited: [48] The party seeking certification of a class action bears the burden of showing some basis in fact for every certification criterion: Hollick, at para. 25. In the context of the preferability requirement, this requires the representative plaintiff to show (1) that a class proceeding would be a fair, efficient and manageable method of advancing the claim, and (2) that it would be preferable to any other reasonably available means of resolving the class members' claims: Hollick, at paras. 28 and 31. [353] Subsection 4(2) requires the court to consider all relevant matters in determining under s. 4(1)(d) whether a class proceeding would be the preferable procedure for the fair and efficient resolution of the common issues, including: (a) whether questions of fact or law common to the members of the class predominate over any questions affecting only individual members; (b) whether a significant number of the members of the class have a valid interest in individually controlling the prosecution of separate actions; (c) whether the class proceeding would involve claims that are or have been the subject of any other proceedings; (d) whether other means of resolving the claims are less practical or less efficient; (e) whether the administration of the class proceeding would create greater difficulties than those likely to be experienced if relief were sought by other means. [354] In Bowman, Madam Justice Matthews discusses the preferability analysis: [241] In Finkel BCCA at para. 25, the Court of Appeal confirmed that when comparing a class proceeding to other realistically available means for resolving the claims, a practical cost-benefit approach applies, citing AIC Limited at paras. 21, 23 and Marshall v. United Furniture Warehouse Limited Partnership, 2013 BCSC 2050 at para. 230, aff'd 2015 BCCA 252, leave to appeal to SCC ref'd, 36584 (17 March 2016). [242] Generally speaking, the preferability analysis must be applied through the triple lenses of the objectives of class proceedings: access to justice, judicial efficiency, and behaviour modification: Hollick at para. 27. Related to this, when considering whether a class proceeding will be fair, efficient, and manageable, the common issues must be considered in the context of the action as a whole, and their relative importance taken into account: Hollick at para. 30. [355] In Escobar v. Ocean Pacific Ltd., 2021 BCSC 2414, Matthews J provided further guidance on assessing whether common issues predominate: [219] The preferability analysis is not a matter of numerosity either in the numbers of common issues versus individual issues, or in the number of individual inquiries that will be required for a very large class. There are many cases where the individual issues will be many and time consuming for a very large class. In personal injury cases, for example, the individual inquires pertaining to causation and multiple heads of damages will involve individual phases that may require trials for each class member. Many cases of this nature have been certified in British Columbia including: Rumley; Harrington v. Dow Corning Corp. (1996), 22 B.C.L.R. (3d) 97 (S.C.); and Endean v. Canadian Red Cross Society (1997), 36 B.C.L.R. (3d) 350 (S.C.), rev'd on other grounds (1998), 48 B.C.L.R. (3d) 90 (C.A.). [220] The problem arises where the individual issues are such that the case is bound to break down into a series of individual trials where the outcome will be driven by the individual issues and for which the answers to the common issues are not substantially important. These are cases where, after considering the common issues in the context of the whole of the action and the goals of class proceedings, the common issue or issues are "negligible" in relation to the individual issues (Hollick at para. 32). [356] Matthews J similarly stated in Bowman that "[t]he existence of potentially complex individual issues does not usually stand in the way of certifying a claim where the substantial ingredients of the liability issues are central and can be resolved in a common way thereby eliminating the need to litigate them for each class member": para 247. 2. Discussion and decision [357] The plaintiff argues that a class proceeding is the preferable means of resolving these claims. The plaintiff argues that: a) common issues predominate over any individual issues; b) there is no evidence that any putative class members wish to pursue such claims on an individual basis; c) although there are other proceedings in British Columbia challenging the constitutional validity of some of the orders, none seek Charter damages pursuant to s. 24(1) of the Charter on a class basis; and d) individual litigation of these claims would be expensive and repetitive, and in many cases impossible. [358] A class proceeding would not be the preferable means of resolving these claims. The proposed proceeding would not be an efficient or manageable method of advancing class members' claims. Nor would a class proceeding achieve the three principles underpinning class actions: access to justice, judicial economy, and behaviour modification. In this, the plaintiff concedes, properly, that "the requirement that a class proceeding be the preferable procedure for resolution of issues is premised on the existence of common issues": Ewert at para 32. [359] With respect to s. 4(2)(a), as set out above, the issues of Charter breach and damages indicate overwhelmingly that individual issues vastly predominate over common issues, indicating the inefficiency and inappropriateness of certification. As stated in Tiemstra (CA) at para 17: "[a] class action which will break down into substantial individual trials in any event does not promote judicial economy or improve access to justice, and is not the preferable procedure." In addition to Thorburn; Ewert; Monaco; KO, and Dang (all surveyed above in respect of Charter-based proposed class proceedings), and Kett and Caputo, courts usually reject proposed class proceedings that would require individualised inquiries based on significantly different experiences and findings with respect to proposed class members. A few further examples: a) Hollick: for the proposed class of 30,000 people, it would be necessary to determine individually to what extent each person was affected by noise and pollution from a landfill: para 32; b) McKay v. CDI Career Development Institutes Ltd., 64 BCLR (3d) 386, 1999 CanLII 5599 (SC): the alleged misrepresentations would have affected differently, if at all, each of the 455 proposed plaintiff students, of a wide variety of backgrounds and experiences enrolled across six campuses: paras 5, 22, 49; c) Vaugeois v. Budget Rent-A-Car of B.C. Ltd., 2015 BCSC 802, aff'd 2017 BCCA 111: it would likely be necessary to cross-examine each of the thousands of proposed class members to confirm the nature and source of the rental car damage for which the class members were charged: para 46. [360] Those rejected class proceedings involved only tens or hundred or thousands of class members. This general concern over multiple individualised enquiries is amplified in this proposed claim, acknowledged by counsel to be unprecedented in Canadian history, with roughly 4 million class members, and 26 purportedly common issues. In this, while not determinative, the court may consider the size of the class in determining whether a class proceeding is the preferable procedure: Branch Text at §4:6 at 4-15 to 4-17. [361] Further, as set out above, while the proposed claim arises from the common context of the Covid pandemic, it does not raise common issues in the sense required by the jurisprudence: that a decision on each common issue will be determinative with respect to each class member, in that each member has an interest in the outcome: Kett at para 134. In this, it resembles the Frankenstein amalgams of thematic class proceeding claims rejected in Caputo and in Kett. As stated in Kett: [183] In my view, this case really seeks to tie together many potential class actions. The CPA is not designed to stich together a case with so many dangling threads. It is designed for cases with a strong factual and legal bond. Justice Winkler, as he then was, raised a similar concern in a proposed tobacco injury class action, Caputo v. Imperial Tobacco Ltd. (2004), 42 B.L.R. (3d) 276, 236 D.L.R. (4th) 348, (Ont. S.C.J.): [72] Here, notwithstanding the inability of the plaintiffs to define an acceptable class in relation to the causes of action alleged, it appears that any class would be comprised of at least several million people. The eight remaining legal bases for asserting claims allegedly arise from multiple fact situations spanning at least 50 years, during which prevailing circumstances changed dramatically. The legal principles underlying the claims asserted require inquiry into the circumstances of each individual class member in order even to ascertain liability, let alone damages. This would be necessary on a procedural basis to ensure that the defendants are treated fairly but would also be necessary from the perspective of the members of the class so that each would receive fair compensation. Further, even if the defendants were to only contest a portion of the individual claims, and each dispute could be concluded in one day, simple mathematics indicate that such a process would require the equivalent of 1,000 years of litigation, if it were to be conducted sequentially. [73] In my view, a class proceeding based on the present action would not be "fair, efficient and manageable" and, therefore, it does not meet the fourth element of the test for certification [80] In my view, the action as advanced is not appropriate for certification as a class proceeding. The best estimates of the number of class members is a range between 2.4 and 15 million persons. The plaintiffs proceed on the basis that tobacco is inherently dangerous and therefore the class should include everyone who smokes or has ever smoked in Ontario, whether alive or deceased, and regardless of individual differences pertaining to smoking habits or the effects of smoking on any particular individual class member. It is apparent on any careful analysis of the proposed class in the context of the conduct complained of and the allegations set out in the Amended Statement of Claim, that the plaintiffs have combined at least five, and possibly more, classes, not to mention innumerable subclasses, into one globally defined class for the purpose of seeking certification. In adopting this strategy, the plaintiffs have presented an action lacking a core of commonality that would permit the court to approve, or frame, common issues that would significantly advance the proceeding. [emphasis in Kett] [362] With respect to s 4(2)(b), the fact that many individuals in British Columbia have already filed Covid-based claims, as surveyed in the jurisprudential survey above, itself indicates that members of the class have an interest in individually controlling the prosecution of separate actions. Amongst the millions of class members, likely others would have their own interest in individually controlling their claim, if they elected to bring a claim at all, or at least preferring the present plaintiff not to be authorised to advance a claim in which they would be presumptive members. [363] With respect to s. 4(2)(c), again, as canvassed above, the class proceeding would involve claims that have been the subject of several other proceedings. [364] With respect to s. 4(2)(d), other means of resolving the claims would be more practical and efficient. No class proceeding is necessary to challenge the constitutionality of PHA s. 92 or to determine whether the preconditions for the EPA had been met: such could be determined on a test case basis, or a petition seeking a declaration, or a claim with a single or few plaintiffs. Such an approach was successfully followed in Ingram 2024, the only Covid-related class proceeding certified in Canada: a simple initial challenge to the legality of the governmental measures, followed by a class proceeding for thousands, rather than millions, of plaintiffs. More tailored claims, with common issues affecting a smaller number of plaintiffs, would be far more suitable and efficient than the present omnibus amalgam. Hoogerbug, Kassian, Weisenburger and other Covid-related claims set out above, provide examples of more focussed proceedings seeking these goals. [365] With respect to s. 4(2)(e), the individualised analysis required for this broadly-cast proposed class proceeding would make the remaining trial and hearings amongst the longest and most complicated in Canadian history, for the reasons set out above. Even setting aside the issue of individual inquiries into Charter breaches and damages, trying the disparate subclasses together will not be efficient: much of the evidence will concern only one of those subclasses, or a portion of those subclasses. [366] Finally, the proposed class proceeding would not advance the class proceeding objectives: access to justice, judicial economy, or behaviour modification. It combines millions of potentially distinct claims, in three disparate subclasses linked only by the overbroad theme of Covid health measures, in a single bloated action that would prove utterly unmanageable, and stretch over years and perhaps decades. Absent opt-out, millions of British Columbian would be drawn into the claim. Again, Kett urges rejection of such certification applications: [196] In terms of judicial economy, I find that the risk of the proceeding collapsing under its own weight is simply too great. This risk will not only be visited upon the court, but also upon the defendants and individual class members, who expect and deserve prompt access to justice once the proceeding has been certified. The court should avoid certifying an action where the proceeding will inevitably over-promise and under-deliver. [367] Apart from inefficient and futile prosecution of these claims, in a claim doomed to failure, as set out above, it would selfishly consume massive amounts of scarce judicial resources. As noted in Jensen v. Samsung Electronics Co. Ltd., 2021 FC 1185 at para 62, aff'd 2023 FCA 89 "preventing baseless class actions from monopolizing the judicial system to the detriment of other litigants' actions is also part of preserving access to justice for all litigants." [368] The plaintiff has failed to establish that a class proceeding would be the preferable procedure for resolving common issues. F. Issue (e): is there an appropriate representative plaintiff? 1. Legal principles [369] Subsection 4(1)(e) requires the applicant to establish that the proposed representative plaintiff meets all three of the following requirements: (i) would fairly and adequately represent the interests of the class, (ii) has produced a plan for the proceeding that sets out a workable method of advancing the proceeding on behalf of the class and of notifying class members of the proceeding, and (iii) does not have, on the common issues, an interest that is in conflict with the interests of other class members. [370] In Dutton at para 41, Chief Justice McLachlin outlined the approach for assessing whether a class representative adequately represents the proposed class: In assessing whether the proposed representative is adequate, the court may look to the motivation of the representative, the competence of the representative's counsel, and the capacity of the representative to bear any costs that may be incurred by the representative in particular (as opposed to by counsel or by the class members generally). The proposed representative need not be "typical" of the class, nor the "best" possible representative. The court should be satisfied, however, that the proposed representative will vigorously and capably prosecute the interests of the class. [citations omitted] 2. The proposed representative plaintiffs a) The present proposed representative plaintiff [371] The claim as drafted has fundamental problems with the proposed representative plaintiffs, and fails s. 4(1)(e) on multiple grounds. [372] Generally, a class proceeding will, from the start, name one or more representative plaintiffs who are natural persons: that is, individuals. [373] Here, the sole present pleaded plaintiff is the corporate Society, which would not be a member of the proposed class, which consists of all British Columbia adults. [374] Naming individuals as the representative plaintiffs is not a matter of convention, but is required under the Act, which prohibits plaintiffs who are not members of the class: 2(4) The court may certify a person who is not a member of the class as the representative plaintiff for the class proceeding only if it is necessary to do so in order to avoid a substantial injustice to the class. [375] The s. 2(4) burden is high. The applicant must establish that necessity with reference to both the suitability of the plaintiff and the circumstances of the class members themselves: Bhangu v. Honda Canada Inc., 2021 BCSC 794 at paras 161-163; Cantlie v. Canadian Heating Products Inc., 2017 BCSC 286 at paras 364, 366, 369. [376] The plaintiff was only able to provide one precedent where a court authorised a society to bring a class proceeding instead of real persons: Elder Advocates of Alberta Society v. Alberta, 2008 ABQB 490, aff'd 2009 ABCA 403, rev'd on other grounds 2011 SCC 24. There, the society was authorised and certified to bring a claim on behalf of some 12,500 elderly and disabled residents of Alberta long-term care facilities. The Court found that the society met the test under the essentially identical Alberta statutory provision: allowing a society to serve as the plaintiff would avoid the substantial injustice to the class, consisting of people who were aged and disabled by chronic health problems to the point that they were institutionalised: paras 545-46. [377] The plaintiff Society provides no evidence that establishes the necessity of having the litigation conducted by the Society, as opposed to individuals who are actual members of the class. A workable plan must be comprehensive and provide sufficient detail corresponding to the complexity of the litigation. However, the Act does not require a representative plaintiff to prepare a plan covering, in detail, every step that has to be taken from the beginning of the class proceeding to the end. Rather, the plan must set out a workable method of advancing the proceeding on behalf of the class. The plan should set out the expected procedural steps, a timeline for these steps, and a discussion of the proposed case management program. Where confidentiality issues are raised by the proceeding, the plan should address those issues. The purpose of the plan is to provide a framework within which the case may proceed and to demonstrate that the representative plaintiff and class counsel have a clear grasp of the complexities involved in the plan and to address them. The court need not scrutinize the plan at the certification hearing to ensure that it will necessarily be capable of carrying the case through to trial and resolution of the common issues without amendment. It is anticipated that plans will require amendments as the case proceeds and the nature of the individual issues are demonstrated by the class members. Certification of a proceeding does not convert the proposed plan into an order of the court. Rather, certification is simply an acceptance that the plaintiff has a comprehensive plan for how the plaintiff hopes the action will proceed. The plan should not be outdated, boilerplate, or rudimentary. In Bellaire v. Independent Order of Foresters, the court rejected a plan presented by the proposed representative plaintiff and suggested that, the litigation plan should address the following issues: (a) the steps to be taken to identify and locate necessary witnesses and gather their evidence; (b) the collection of relevant documents from members of the class, as well as other persons; (c) the exchange and management of documents produced by all parties; (d) ongoing reporting to the class members; (e) mechanisms for responding to inquiries from the class members; (f) whether discovery of individual class members is likely to be need, and, if, so, the process for conducting same; (g) he need for experts, and, if need, how those experts are to be identified and retained; (h) if individual issues will remain after the determination of the common issues, the plan for resolving the individual issues; and (i) how damages, or any other forms of relief, are to be assessed or determined once the common issues have been decided. [footnotes omitted] [406] The litigation plan is not only relevant to the adequacy of the representative plaintiff, but also the preferability analysis. As Winkler J. (as he then was) stated in Caputo: [75] The Act mandates that the representative plaintiffs produce a "plan" that sets out a "workable method of advancing the proceeding on behalf of the class ".McLachlin C.J. held in Hollick that the preferability analysis must be conducted through a consideration of the common issues in the context of the claims as a whole. (para. 30) In this context, the litigation plan is often an integral part of the preferability analysis. Frequently, in more complex cases, it is only when the court has a proper litigation plan before it that it is in a position to fully appreciate the implications of "preferability" as it pertains to manageability, efficiency and fairness. [407] Where the complexity of the proposed litigation raises prima facie practical concerns, the Court expects a commensurately detailed plan addressing the logistical and analytical issues. The further passage in Caputo could speak directly to the present case: [78] In my view, in a proceeding of this size and complexity, a proper litigation plan should reflect a clear acknowledgement of the massive undertaking involved. Thus, the plan should contain, at a minimum, information as to the manner in which individual issues will be dealt with, details as to the knowledge, skill and experience of the class counsel involved, an analysis of the resources required to litigate the class members claims to conclusion, and some indication that the resources available are sufficiently commensurate given the size and complexity of the proposed class and the issues to be determined. In respect to the latter point, I do not wish to be taken as holding that only large firms can prosecute class actions nor that the proposed representative plaintiffs must be themselves sufficiently wealthy to finance litigation. Rather, the plan should satisfy the court as to how the resources available to the plaintiffs can be brought to bear to ensure that the litigation can be conducted in such a way so as to protect the interests of the class members. The detail required with respect to each of these elements is relative to the nature of the action. With respect to the financing of the action, it is to be kept in mind that the purposes of certain sections of the CPA, and related legislation, is to permit proposed representative plaintiffs to commence and maintain class proceedings through means not available in ordinary litigation. [79] In consideration of the foregoing, and given the paucity of information proffered in the certification motion record, the plaintiffs have not met the requirement of providing a "workable" litigation plan to the court. Were it the case that this was the only defect, I would normally be inclined to grant a conditional certification, subject to the plaintiffs producing an acceptable litigation plan. However, in this case, the other deficiencies are such that, without changing the entire theory of the case, it is not possible for the plaintiffs to satisfy the requirement. Plaintiff's application to amend [415] For the reasons set out above, even if the Court were to allow the plaintiff's amendments, which application is denied, it would make no difference to the feasibility of the claim. [416] The plaintiff's claim cannot be saved by the generally liberal approach to amendments. In British Columbia, the law for pre-certification amendment applications is the same as the rules for amending regular applications, as helpfully summarised by G.P. Weatherill J. in Peterson v. 446690 B.C. Ltd., 2014 BCSC 1531 at para 37: 1) Amendments to pleadings ought to be allowed unless pleadings fail to disclose a cause of action or defence. 2) Amendments are usually permitted to determine the issues between the parties and ought to be allowed unless it would cause prejudice to party's ability to defend an action. 3) The party resisting an amendment must prove prejudice to preclude an amendment, and mere, potential prejudice is insufficient to preclude an amendment. 4) Costs are the general means of protecting against prejudice unless it would be a wholly inadequate remedy. 5) Courts should only disallow amendments as a last resort. [citations omitted] [417] A key consideration is whether the other party has suffered real prejudice: Burnett at para 58. Where delays are long and the explanations for such delays are inadequate, some prejudice will be presumed: Tate v. Bishop, 2015 ONSC 742 at para 27. For example, in Defazio, after the certification motion had ended but before the reasons were released, the plaintiff sought to restate the common issues and redefine the class in its amended statement of claim. The amendments were struck because it would be unfair to deny the defendants an opportunity to make further submissions: paras 4 and 17. The plaintiff also conceded that the amendments were brought to address deficiencies in their case highlighted by the defendants, rendering their admission even more unjust: para 49. [418] Again, the defendants' submissions were based upon a version of the proposed FANCC delivered almost a year before the deadline for the certification pleadings. The plaintiff's current proposed FANCC was delivered six weeks after the end of those hearings. Prejudice could not be more clear than in the present circumstances. [419] More importantly, for the reasons set out above, based upon an analysis of the current proposed June 2023 FANCC, those amendments would still leave the plaintiff's claim short of each of the s. 4(1) requirements for class certification. [420] The plaintiff argues that courts are generous in giving the plaintiff an opportunity to fix deficiencies in class proceedings pleadings, and notes that class proceedings pleadings are often amended, tightened, and tailored both before and after the certification process, citing Babstock at paras 88-90 (but citing the dissent, not the majority decision); Basyal v. Mac's Convenience Stores Inc., 2018 BCCA 235 at para 78; and Escobar at paras 13-14. [421] The history of the eight proposed notices of civil claim does not, however, evidence a coherent and focussed basis for a claim that has been clarified and honed through the addition of particulars and the tightening of claims, issues, and classes, in the manner surveyed in the class proceeding jurisprudence. Rather, as set out in the procedural history above, the plaintiff has wildly recast its claim throughout the proceeding, largely to avoid the defendants' arguments, with which the Court has largely agreed. [422] The plaintiff has had eight opportunities and several years to decide what claim it wishes to advance. [423] The words of WCB v. Sort, where certification was denied, with no leave to amend, speak equally to the present application: [185] In my opinion, there is a significant difference between an amended pleading clarifying or modifying claims which have been properly certified and allowing amendments to cure one which is fundamentally deficient. That is particularly so if the proposed amendments include entirely new causes of action. While the courts "tend to be generous in making available the possibility of amendments to fine tune the pleadings and to bring clarification to obscure issues", it is also the case that "the essentials of a cause of action must be pleaded else the pleadings may be found to be fatally lacking"; also, in considering this issue, the court will consider "the length of time the plaintiff has had to 'get it right'": Sandhu [v. HSBC Finance Mortgages Inc., 2016 BCCA 301] at para. 44.[23] [emphasis added; authorities omitted] [424] Further, as set out above, the plaintiff continued to hew to its defective and vexatious pleadings in the face of multiple specific warnings from the defendants and the Court itself of those defects. The camera and operating personnel remained stationary while the Court was in session. With respect to broadcast, comment functions were disabled. A watermark stating "Reproduction Prohibited" was superimposed behind the video, and a formal notice was placed above the streaming video that the recording must not be further broadcast or reproduced without the prior written authorisation of the Court, and that any unauthorised use of this recording in breach of the authorising order shall expose the person doing so to legal proceedings for contempt of court. At para 43 of that decision the Chief Justice described the camera set-up in the courtroom and provided a report of sorts on the filming experience: [43] The media set up two web cameras in the courtroom which provided virtually live webcast of the entire closing submissions. I say "virtually" because it was a condition of my order that there be an approximate 10 minute delay in broadcast to permit recourse in the event of inadvertent reference to certain protected evidence. While I cannot speak for counsel, I did not find the cameras to be obtrusive or otherwise distracting. No concerns arising from the webcast have been brought to my attention. [428] This Court's assessment of the filming experience in the present hearing matched that of the Chief Justice in the Polygamy Reference. The camera and camera operator sat in the prisoner's dock. They imposed no distraction on the proceedings. There was a snafu on the first day: the camera angle was excessively broad, and captured potentially privileged material on counsel's computer screens; in a laudably practical manner, counsel set up a protocol to review and contain this problem, and that portion of the screen was pixelated before broadcast. [429] Counsel originally agreed to stream the recordings on the YouTube platform.
