Oh Tribunal Counsel for the person who is the subject of the appeal Ali Yusuf Conseil de la personne en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION OVERVIEW [1] XXXX XXXX ("the Appellant") is a dual national of Chile and Syria. He fears harm from gang members in Chile, who robbed him until he was forced to close his business. He also fears persecution due to his profile as an Arab and Muslim. The Refugee Protection Division ("RPD") rejected his claim, finding that the Appellant had not experienced persecutory treatment in Chile due to his ethnic and religious profile. It also found that he did not face a risk of harm from the gang, as their primary motivation was to target businesses that possessed cash. The Appellant disputes the RPD's decision. He argues that the RPD ignored evidence of the discrimination he experienced in Chile, and that the RPD failed to address whether the presumption of state protection had been rebutted. [2] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act ("IRPA"), the appeal is dismissed. On an independent review, I too determine that the Appellant does not face treatment amounting to persecution in Chile. I also see no error in the RPD's failure to address the issue of state protection. I agree with the RPD's assessment of the Appellant's risk. He does not face a serious possibility of persecution; nor, on a balance of probabilities, does he face a danger of torture, a risk to life, or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment in Chile. As the Appellant is a Chilean national and as he has not established a claim against this country of nationality, he is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. The Refugee Appeal Division ("RAD") confirms the RPD's negative decision. BACKGROUND [3] The Appellant was born in Syria but moved to Chile in 1994, where he later obtained citizenship. In 2009, the Appellant took over the lease of a XXXX XXXX XXXX in Estacion Central, a suburb of Santiago. The next year, a group of Chilean men began to frequent his business, demanding food and drink free of charge. The Appellant forced them to leave, but they later returned and robbed him. From this point, a pattern of robberies emerged, with men returning to the shop to demand money. After speaking with neighbours and friends, the Appellant ascertained that these individuals were affiliated with a violent gang known as Los Chubis. Other business owners had been stabbed, and some neighbours recommended that the Appellant simply pay them. The Appellant reported some of these incidents to the police. Though the police took a report each time, they did not take further action. In November 2014, a robbery for the equivalent of approximately CAD $2000 forced the Appellant to close his business. [4] The Appellant then became involved in some small investments, which failed. In XXXX 2016, he moved to Valdivia, another city in Chile. However, he had difficulty finding work there, and on some occasions, employers explicitly told him that they would not employ Arabs. Some of the Appellant's friends told him of how the gang members had asked about him. As the Appellant's common-law relationship had ended by that point, the Appellant returned to Syria and married. However, he soon encountered problems when Syrian authorities began to question him about his Chilean nationality. The Appellant returned to Chile. After obtaining a Canadian visa, he traveled to Canada on XXXX XXXX, 2017, and initiated his claim for refugee protection several months later. [5] The RPD heard the Appellant's claim on March 28, 2019. As the Appellant had multiple nationalities, he was required to establish a claim against each country of nationality. The Appellant's failure to establish his claim against Chile was determinative. The RPD found that the risk of harm from the gang in Chile did not meet the requisite threshold. Although the Appellant heard of how gang members had asked about him, the RPD found that the lack of pursuit against the Appellant for a significant period of time was indicative of an absence of risk, and reflected how the gang was merely targeting the Appellant because he carried cash, as a small business owner. With the closure of his business, the risk had diminished. As for his residual profile as a Muslim and Arab, the RPD examined several factors, but the panel believed that there was no evidence to indicate that Arabs or Muslims faced discrimination. For these reasons, the RPD rejected the claim. Role of the Refugee Appeal Division [6] The jurisprudence establishes that the RAD is required to undertake an independent assessment of the evidence and reach its own determination. Deference is owed to the RPD only where the RPD held a meaningful advantage in assessing the evidence.1 I see no reason, in the present case, to show deference to the RPD's findings. I have therefore applied a correctness standard throughout this decision. ANALYSIS [7] The Appellant raises the following issues on appeal: a) Whether the RPD erred in its finding there was no evidence of discrimination in Chile; and b) Whether the RPD erred by failing to assess whether state protection was available for the Appellant in Chile. [8] These will be addressed in detail below. a) Whether the RPD erred in its finding there was no evidence of discrimination in Chile [9] The Appellant submits that the RPD ignored evidence about his experience of discrimination in Chile. I agree that it was an error for the RPD to fail to address all the relevant evidence. However, I determine that the Appellant does not face treatment amounting cumulatively to persecution in Chile due to his ethnic and religious profile. The RPD's findings [10] The RPD concluded that the Appellant did not face a risk of discrimination as a Muslim and as a Syrian Arab. The panel pointed to numerous factors: * There was a sizeable Arab population in Santiago; * There was a large Sunni Muslim mosque in Santiago, which the Appellant attended, and it appeared that Muslims attended the mosque without fear of harm; * Women wore the hijab freely and there was no evidence that they were harmed for dressing in Islamic attire; * There was a Sunni Muslim elementary school in Santiago; * The Appellant was able to access government services, for example by acquiring public funds for low-income families to buy his home; * The Appellant was able to obtain a job with the municipality of Santiago; * Many Arabs were able to open businesses in Santiago, which were supported by the local population; * The Appellant had given his son a Muslim name, without fear of any consequences; and * The Appellant's children attended public schools. [11] Citing a lack of evidence about prejudice or discrimination against Sunni Muslims and Arabs from Syria, the RPD found that this aspect of the Appellant's claim could not succeed. The Appellant's arguments [12] The Appellant highlights the RPD's statement that there was "no evidence" of prejudice or discrimination, and how this was directly contradicted by the Appellant's testimony of his own experiences in the country. As the RPD found no reason to doubt the Appellant's credibility, it is submitted that the instances of alleged discrimination must be accepted as true. [13] The Appellant points to how the gang members made racist comments toward him, and how a senior police officer responded to his complaint by telling him that he should be thankful to have received Chilean citizenship. As these are instances of racism and discrimination, the Appellant argues the RPD was wrong to find that there was no evidence of prejudice or discrimination against Arabs. The RAD's independent assessment [14] I agree that the RPD's analysis of this issue should have addressed the Appellant's description of the problems he faced due to his ethnicity and religion. I accept the Appellant's description of the incidents of discrimination experienced by him in Chile as an Arab and Muslim. However, the Appellant has not gone further to explain how the instances of discrimination entitle him to refugee protection. I am not satisfied that the Appellant faces mistreatment in Chile, including discrimination, that would amount cumulatively to persecution. [15] The jurisprudence defines persecution as a sustained or systematic violation of basic human rights. The treatment must be serious, and it must be repetitive, or systematic. The quality of the incidents should be assessed in terms of whether they constitute a fundamental violation of human dignity.2 [16] In the present case, the Appellant testified to how he experienced discrimination in several different situations. When he relocated to Valdivia, he had difficulties finding employment due to his religious and ethnic profile. He explained how he tried to find work, but was denied employment after being asked his name and where he comes from. According to the Basis of Claim form, business owners told him they do not employ Arabs. The Appellant also expressed having difficulty finding a place to rent in Valdivia. In addition, as the Appellant points out in his memorandum, the gang members used racist expressions in their dealings with him, and the police told him to be thankful for his Chilean citizenship. I accept this as evidence of discrimination, however I do not consider these to be reflective of a sustained or systematic violation of basic human rights. [17] The Appellant lived in Chile from 1994 to 2017. Apart from the above, he has not complained of any other discriminatory treatment. The RPD understandably examined whether discrimination existed in his life in a way that interfered with his ability to exercise his basic rights. I see no error in the RPD's examination of various relevant factors, which addressed the Appellant's ability to practice his religion freely, his ability to work in a manner free of discrimination, and his ability to access housing and public services. Apart from the few unfortunate instances referred to above, there was little to indicate that the Appellant had experienced any persistent, repetitive, or systematic mistreatment. [18] The country conditions evidence also provides little evidentiary basis to support a pattern of discriminatory treatment towards Arabs and Muslims in Chile. The United States Department of State ("USDOS") report for Chile only mentions a single incident, involving a soccer game between Israeli-Chilean and Palestinian-Chilean clubs that ended in physical violence. In that incident, Palestinian leaders complained that the entrance of their community's soccer field had been marked with a Star of David with the words, "Palestine doesn't exist, Arabs are terrorists".3 Jewish community leaders in turn filed a complaint with the public prosecutor over anti-Semitic chants by Palestinian fans. I see little evidence of any other anti-Islamic or anti-Arab discrimination in Chile in the country conditions evidence. [19] According to the USDOS report, equal treatment and non-discrimination were explicitly protected in the constitution, and the labour code prohibited discrimination. Laws and regulations prohibited employment discrimination on several grounds, including race, nationality, national extraction, social origin, language, and religion.4 There were civil legal remedies for victims of employment discrimination. The USDOS report indicated that the Chilean government effectively enforced applicable laws and regulations prohibiting employment discrimination. Nonetheless, it was reported that discrimination in employment continued to occur. A large proportion of immigrants, mostly Latin American and Caribbean migrants, reported experiencing discrimination.5 However, the evidence lacks specifics as to the nature of the treatment experienced. On the whole, the documentary evidence does not indicate that anti-Arab and anti-Islamic discrimination is a serious problem in Chile. [20] In short, though I accept the Appellant's assertion that he has experienced some discrimination in Chile due to his ethnicity and religion, these incidents have been very few, considering the many years he has resided in Chile. The treatment experienced by the Appellant has not been sufficiently persistent, repetitive, or systematic. It has not resulted in a violation of his basic human rights. Even considering the evidence cumulatively, I am not satisfied that the Appellant faces treatment amounting to persecution in Chile on account of his profile as a Muslim and Arab. b) Whether the RPD erred by failing to assess whether state protection was available for the Appellant in Chile [21] According to the Appellant, the RPD erred in failing to determine whether state protection is available to him in Chile. I am unpersuaded by this argument. [22] The Appellant highlights how he approached police over ten times and yet they did not assist. He even approached a senior police officer, who merely suggested that he cooperate with his persecutors, and that he should be thankful for his Chilean citizenship. The Appellant emphasizes that the issue of the state's inability to protect is a crucial element in determining whether a refugee claimant's fear is well-founded. The Appellant believes the likelihood of persecution is closely linked to state protection and the well-foundedness of his fear. He says it was therefore an error for the RPD to fail to engage in some analysis of whether the presumption of state protection had been rebutted. [23] I disagree with the Appellant. The issue of state protection is indeed an important one in determining whether a claimant has a well-founded fear of persecution. It is a determinative, threshold issue under both section 96 and subsection 97(1) of the IRPA. However, there are other elements under section 96 and subsection 97(1) that are also determinative, threshold issues that can be considered separately from the state protection issue. [24] One such issue is the likelihood of the risk. The test under section 96 of the IRPA for a well-founded fear of persecution is forward-looking. It requires a reasonable chance, or a serious possibility, of persecution.6 Under section 97(1), an even higher standard applies, as a claimant must establish the risk on a balance of probabilities.7 Though it is easy to see how the likelihood of harm can be factually related to the state's ability and willingness to protect, it is also not difficult to see that the two concepts do not always converge. Where an agent of harm is no longer motivated to harm a person, the threshold for risk may not be met, regardless of the state's ability and willingness to protect. To illustrate a clear example, a man who flees his agent of harm only to later discover that the agent of harm is deceased, will no longer face a risk of harm, even where the evidence establishes the inadequacy of state protection in the country. [25] In the present case, the RPD correctly examined the motives of the agent of harm, and found those motives were based on the Appellant's possession of cash as a small business owner. This was reflective of a pattern of targeting small businesses in the neighbourhood. Once the Appellant closed his business, the violent interactions stopped. Though I acknowledge the Appellant's description of how the gang members were asking about him, it is also important to consider that the Appellant continued to reside in Santiago for more than a year after the closure of his business, and he also returned to Santiago and lived there for a year before coming to Canada. During all this time, the gang members did not seek him out. Despite testifying how the gang was aware of the location of his home, they did not go to his home while he was living in Chile. Importantly, the Appellant explained in testimony that they did not come to his house because they knew he did not have money anymore. [26] The RPD correctly determined that the agents of harm were not motivated to pursue the Appellant. In this particular case, the police's inaction in response to previous reports of criminal activity does not mean the Appellant still faces a significant risk from his agent of harm. Even if I were to apply the lower threshold of a serious possibility or reasonable chance of persecution, the requisite threshold for risk would not be met. Once the RPD determined that the agent of harm was no longer motivated to pursue the Appellant, it was not obliged to assess the separate issue of state protection. I see no error in the RPD's approach. CONCLUSION [27] For these reasons, I reach the same conclusion as the RPD. The Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. I therefore confirm the RPD's negative decision. [28] The appeal is dismissed, pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the IRPA. (signed) M. Oh M.
