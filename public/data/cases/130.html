Weintraub Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia January 30, 2018 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, British Columbia February 16, 2018 Summary: The appellant, Zoltan Andrew Simon, applies for an order extending the time to file and serve his notice of appeal. If that order is granted, he applies for a range of other pre-hearing relief. Held: Applications dismissed. It is not in the interest of justice to grant an extension of time in this case. Although the respondents were promptly informed of the appellant's bona fide intention to appeal and they would not be prejudiced by the extension of time, the appeal is doomed to fail. Reasons for Judgment of the Honourable Madam Justice Bennett: [1] Mr. Simon has been before the Courts in Canada since 2007 attempting to litigate the same circumstances under different auspices. He now applies for an extension of time to file and serve his notice of appeal against an order striking his notice of civil claim in its entirety and without leave to amend, and declaring him a vexatious litigant. The reasons for judgment are indexed at 2017 BCSC 1438. [2] In these applications, Mr. Simon seeks: · An extension of time to file and serve his notice of appeal; · Leave to appeal the order or a direction that leave is not required; · A no-fees application under R. 56 of the Court of Appeal Rules; · An application to "stay" the order, as it relates to costs, pronounced by this Court in Simon v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2016 BCCA 52 ("Appeal #1") as he wishes to review the Registrar's order in relation to the assessment of costs; · Special costs from the respondents Attorney General of Canada ("AGC") and Attorney General of British Columbia ("AGBC"; collectively, "AGs"); · An application to for an extension of time to vary a direction of the Registrar; and · A variance of a direction of the Registrar (October 26, 2017). [3] Mr. Simon's notices of motion also seek various other directions which are not further articulated and which were not raised in oral submissions: (1) to clarify whether the AGBC is a party to the appeal; (2) to clarify how to "enter into an agreement with the Court Services Branch" to file documents electronically; (3) an order directing the Registrar to issue written costs reasons, which were previously delivered orally, for Mr. Simon's previous unsuccessful appeal to this Court, Appeal #1; and (4) to order that Mr. Simon's applications be heard before the AGs' applications. [4] The AGs each seek security for the costs of the appeal ($3,500 each) and security for trial costs ($2,000 each), amounting to a total of $11,000. Procedural history [6] Decades ago, Mr. Simon was the immigration sponsor for his first spouse, which included providing an undertaking to provide her financial support. Shortly after she arrived in Canada, the two separated, she began to collect social assistance, and Mr. Simon ultimately defaulted on his immigration undertaking because he did not repay the social assistance payments to the Province. Years later, Mr. Simon attempted to sponsor a second spouse and her son (who are the other named plaintiffs/appellants) for immigration to Canada. Because Mr. Simon was disqualified from being a sponsor due to previously breaching a sponsorship undertaking, the application was refused. In these actions, he has sought damages from the AGs related to his sponsorship debt and to the refusal to sponsor his second wife. Most of his claims have been struck for having no chance of success or for being vexatious or an abuse of process. POSITION OF THE PARTIES Extension of time to file notice of appeal [13] Mr. Simon submits that he did not file his appeal on time because he had difficulty filing them in proper form. On the ground of merit, he says that no court has ever decided his case on the merits because his claims have been repeatedly struck. Accordingly, he says that calling his case "meritless" based solely on the pleadings is artificial. While he acknowledges his case may be ambitious, he says that it cannot be said to be without merit when no adjudication on the facts has ever taken place. [14] The AGs received unfiled documents from Mr. Simon purporting to begin the appeal within the appeal period. However, they were not filed in time for unclear reasons. The AGs do not claim any prejudice arising from an extension of time. However, they say that the extension of time should be rejected because Mr. Simon's appeal is meritless. Leave to appeal / direction that leave not required [15] Mr. Simon filed a notice of application for leave to appeal because he did not want to be in contempt of the order of the chambers judge declaring him a vexatious litigant. [16] The AGs take the position that leave is not required because orders striking a claim and declaring Mr. Simon a vexatious litigant are not "limited appeal orders" under s. 7 of the Court of Appeal Act. Accordingly, they submit that Mr. Simon's notice of application for leave to appeal should stand as a notice of appeal if he is granted an extension. He is not barred from filing a notice of appeal by the vexatious litigant order made below. No-fee application [17] Mr. Simon deposes that he is unemployed, is the sole contributor to his household expenses, and supports his wife and her son in China. His estimated net monthly income is $1,960 and his estimated monthly expenses are $1,835. He has no assets, and has a $5,000 debt owing to his niece. Although not mentioned in his application, the order of the chambers judge also issued $2,000 lump sum costs awards in favour of the AGs for an additional $4,000 in debt. Mr. Simon has post-secondary education in surveying and industrial design. He was a design draftsman, land surveyor, and worked in civil engineering until 1997. From 1997 to 2017, he worked in the service industry and as a security guard. [18] Mr. Simon does not speak to the merits of his appeal in the no-fee application, but he appends his notice of appeal. His appeal against the vexatious litigant declaration argues that he never pursued vexatious legal proceedings, but was protecting "his Charter rights in order to reunite his family in Canada by lawful means". On the order striking his pleadings, he says that counsel for the AGs obtained the judgment by fraud by misleading the courts for three years regarding whether Mr. Simon's pleadings in this action are materially the same as his pleadings in other actions, and by repeatedly contravening the Supreme Court Civil Rules. He also says the chambers judge did not consider crucial submissions regarding his "application to strike out the [AGs'] applications to strike". [19] Mr. Simon has pursued many actions and appeals over the past decade. Due to this, the AGBC submits that this Court should infer that, where an applicant has the resources to pursue multiple court proceedings, he or she will not suffer undue hardship if required to pay fees in this one: Langston v. Teamsters, Local 155, 2010 BCCA 310 at para. 14. [20] The AGBC also notes that Mr. Simon sent a $280 cheque to the Registry, indicating his ability to pay filing fees. The Registrar returned that cheque to Mr. Simon, as the Registry's practice is to not proceed with charging fees until a filed no-fee application is determined. While Mr. Simon has filed some evidence of modest finances, the AGBC says the amount he has already spent on legal proceedings, coupled with the $280 cheque, weigh against a finding of undue hardship. [21] On the ground of merit, the AGBC notes that Fitch J.A., in Appeal #1, found the chambers judge's order striking Mr. Simon's pleadings to be "unassailable". The chambers judge found in this case that Mr. Simon's pleadings in this action repeated, incorporated and expanded upon past pleadings, and he adopted the decision of the judge who was affirmed in Appeal #1. The chambers judge then went on to consider whether any part of Mr. Simon's pleadings were materially different from the pleadings that were the subject of Appeal #1 and from pleadings filed in other registries. He found that they were not. Accordingly, the AGBC says that this appeal, just like Appeal #1, is clearly without merit and thus the Court should not grant Mr. Simon no-fee status under R. 56. Application to "stay" the costs order issued after Appeal #1 [22] Mr. Simon requests a stay of proceedings "at least regarding costs" for Appeal #1. Elsewhere in the materials, Mr. Simon also seeks a direction that the Registrar prepare a written decision regarding costs in Appeal #1, which he pronounced orally after the hearing. [23] The AGs oppose this relief sought, and take the position that the Court has no jurisdiction to make the order. They point to s. 18(1) of the Court of Appeal Act, which only gives the Court discretion to stay proceedings "in the cause or matter from which the appeal has been taken". Special costs [24] Mr. Simon says that the AGBC should pay him special costs if he must pay registry fees and security for costs "if the Registry wishes to proceed by contravention of the Rules and the Act." [25] The AGs state that they oppose the relief sought. Security for costs of the appeal [26] The AGBC seeks $3,500 in security for costs of the appeal, and a stay pending a posting of the security. They note that where there is a serious question of whether recover may be difficult, there is "almost a presumption" in favour of granting security for costs: Braich (Re), 2008 BCCA 354 at para. 6. They say that, in this case, Mr. Simon's financial difficulties (as outlined in his no-fees application) and his failure to pay the costs assessed in Appeal #1 demonstrate a difficulty of recovery. The AGBC says the appeal has no merit. [27] The AGC seeks $3,500 in security for costs of the appeal, which they calculate to be approximately equivalent to the lowest allowable units under the tariff. They note that recovery of costs would likely be difficult, especially in light of the fact that Mr. Simon has not paid a costs award made in favour of the AGC in Appeal #1. Canada also submits that the appeal is bound to fail. The chambers judge reviewed in detail Mr. Simon's 132-page notice of civil claim, finding he attempted to relitigate previously decided matters and advanced claims that were bound to fail. He also found that Mr. Simon's pleadings included "spurious ad hominem attacks" that are "an affront to the dignity of the court". Further, the chambers judge concluded that Mr. Simon "habitually and persistently" brought claims before courts and tribunals, amounting to a litany of unsuccessful proceedings, including previous dismissals of his claims that were found to be scandalous, vexatious, and an abuse of process. [28] Mr. Simon says that he is prepared to deposit $2,500 in security for costs flowing from his Service Canada "one-time death benefit", and that he is bound to die within three decades or so. He says that the AGs may also place a lien on his EI and Canada Pension Plan benefits to come up with the security for costs. He also argues that the AG motions are "vexatious, frivolous, oppressive, and constitute an abuse of process". His affidavit continues in this prolix manner for eight single-spaced pages. Security for trial costs [29] The AGBC seeks $2,000 in security for trial costs. They submit that the underlying rationale making such orders less common (that trial costs are generally more substantial than appeal costs) does not apply here, because the chambers judge ordered a lump sum costs award of $2,000. By contrast, they say they will be prejudiced if Mr. Simon does not post security because his past conduct indicates collection will be difficult, and because the appeal is bound to fail (as outlined above in the AGC's security for appeal costs position, which the AGBC adopts). While Mr. Simon is of limited financial means, the AGBC says that such circumstances will not defeat an application where the appeal is meritless, because a successful party in the court below should not be required to respond to an unmeritorious appeal where there is no real prospect of recovery: Morbank Financial Inc. v. 0476779 B.C. Ltd., 2014 BCCA 265 at para. 20. [30] The AGC seeks $2,000 as security for trial costs awarded to them. They adopt the submissions of the AGBC on this point. DISCUSSION Extension of time to file and serve notice of appeal [31] The criteria for an application to extend time to begin an appeal are set out in Davies v. C.I.B.C. (1987), 15 B.C.L.R. (2d) 256 (C.A.) at 259-60: · Was there a bona fide intention to appeal? · When were the respondents informed of the intention? · Would the respondents be unduly prejudiced by an extension of time? · Is there merit in the appeal? · Is it in the interest of justice that an extension be granted? [32] When an appeal is not brought within time, the fact that it has no merit will be a decisive factor in granting an extension of time: Clock Holdings Ltd. v. Braich, 2009 BCCA 269 at para. 31. [33] The overriding question is whether it is in the interest of justice to grant the extension: Perren v. Lalari, 2009 BCCA 564 at para. 33. The burden is on the applicant to establish that the criteria are met: Rapton v. British Columbia (Superintendent of Motor Vehicles), 2011 BCCA 71 at para. 19. [34] Mr. Simon expressed a bona fide intention to appeal, by sending the AGs copies of a document purporting to start his appeal within the appeal period. In oral submissions on the application, the AGs informed the Court that they received the document, so they were informed of Mr. Simon's intention within the appeal period. The AGs would not be unduly prejudiced by the extension of time. The main question is whether the appeal has merit. In my view, Mr. Simon's appeal is meritless. [35] The test for merit on an application to extend time is low. The threshold question is whether the appeal is "doomed to fail" or, alternatively, whether "it can be said with confidence that the appeal has no merit": Stewart v. Postnikoff, 2014 BCCA 292 at para. 6; Clock Holdings Ltd. v. Braich, 2009 BCCA 269 at para. 31, aff'd 2009 BCCA 437; Seiler v. Mutual Fire Insurance Co., 2003 BCCA 696 at para. 18; Gourmet Gallery v. Pacific International, 2000 BCCA 681 at para. 9. [36] An extension of time to begin an appeal by filing a notice of appeal, as opposed to an extension to continue prosecuting an appeal, should not be granted if the appeal is without merit. In Franks v. British Columbia (B.C. Benefits Board), 1999 BCCA 165, aff'd 1999 BCCA 407, leave to appeal ref'd, [1999] S.C.C.A. No. 361, Proudfoot J.A. in Chambers stated (at para. 10): [10] There is ample authority for the proposition that an extension of time [to file a notice of appeal] should not be granted if the appeal is without merit. [37] This principle was affirmed in Deutschmann Estate v. Fallis, 2011 BCCA 404 at para. 5 and MacLanders v. MacLanders, 2012 BCCA 218 at para.19. [38] This principle applies even where the other Davies factors are met. See e.g., Stewart v. Postnikoff, 2014 BCCA 292, where Goepel J.A. in Chambers dismissed an application for an extension of time to file a notice of appeal solely on the basis that the appeal was "doomed to fail". See also Kedia International Inc. v. Royal Bank of Canada, 2008 BCCA 305 at para. 27. [39] The absence of merit also informs the interests of justice factor, the overriding criterion, as discussed in Seiler v. Mutual Fire Insurance Co. of British Columbia, 2003 BCCA 696, leave to appeal ref'd [2004] S.C.C.A. No. 60, at para. 18: [18] While refusal to extend the time for service in these circumstances may seem like a harsh result, the fact is that there is no prospect of the plaintiffs succeeding on their appeal. Extending the time for service of notice for an appeal that is doomed to fail would put all parties to unnecessary expense, and clearly would not be in the interests of justice. [40] On this point, see also Moore v. Moore, 2012 BCCA 266 at para. 35, where the application to grant an extension of time to file a notice of appeal was refused because it was "not in the interest of justice to prolong this meritless litigation ". [41] Mr. Simon had been before the Courts many times. He continues to try to pursue litigation that has already been decided. The chambers judge concluded that the pleadings in this case are not sufficiently different than the pleadings in Appeal #1, which have already been struck with approval from this Court. Mr. Simon's appeal is, frankly, hopeless with respect to the appeal in relation to the pleadings. [42] The vexatious litigant order is framed as follows: 2. Zoltan Andrew Simon is declared to be a vexatious litigant. 3. Zoltan Andrew Simon must not, without first obtaining leave of this Court, institute or commence legal proceedings in any British Columbia court, or file applications in any existing legal proceedings in any British Columbia court. 4. Any application brought by Zoltan Andrew Simon for leave, as aforesaid, shall be served, in addition to any other intended defendant or respondent parties, on the Attorney General of British Columbia with at least twenty-one (21) clear days' notice prior to scheduled hearing of the application. 5. Zoltan Andrew Simon is not by this order precluded from defending himself in criminal or civil proceedings. [43] While broadly worded, the order does not prevent Mr. Simon from defending himself in any action, it only prevents him from commencing an action without leave of the Supreme Court. Despite its wording, the order does not apply to prevent Mr. Simon from filing a notice of appeal in this Court. See Bryfogle v. British Columbia (Ministry of Children and Family Development), 2016 BCCA 199 at paras. 23-31. [44] Given Mr. Simon's very lengthy litigation history, and the fact that he is trying to litigate matters that have already been dealt with in the Courts, I do not see that there is any merit with respect to his appeal of the vexatious litigant order. [45] My conclusion that this appeal is meritless is fatal to the test of whether to extend time and grant indigent status. Accordingly, it is not in the interests of justice to allow the applications. [46] The refusal to grant an extension of time to appeal ends the appeal and most of the pending applications. However, I will address the other applications in any event. Leave to appeal / direction that leave not required [47] If leave to appeal is not required, the presiding justice may simply order that the notice of application for leave to appeal stand as a notice of appeal alongside any necessary extension of time: Commencing an Appeal When Uncertain if Leave to Appeal is Required (Civil Practice Directive, 8 May 2017). [48] Mr. Simon only filed a notice of application for leave to appeal out of caution, to avoid breaching the order of the trial judge that declares that he must not, "without first obtaining leave of this Court, institute or commence legal proceedings in any British Columbia court ". The AGs both take the position that leave is not required, and based on Bryfogle, I agree with that position. If I had granted the extension of time, I would direct that Mr. Simon's notice of application for leave to appeal stand as a notice of appeal. No-fee application [49] Under R. 56(1) of the Court of Appeal Rules, a justice who finds that a person cannot pay the required fees without undue hardship may order that no fees are payable. Rule 56(2) provides that a justice may not make a no fee order if the justice concludes that the position being argued lacks merit. [50] Thus, there are two tests that must be met in order to obtain an order that ordinary court fees are not required: the applicant must show that he cannot afford to pay the fees without undue hardship, and there must be a reasonable basis for the appeal: Shoolestani v. Ichikawa, 2018 BCCA 11 at para. 14. [51] I do not think Mr. Simon has demonstrated that he cannot pay fees without undue hardship. He sent a $280 to the Registry when he filed his original materials. Additionally, in the past, has pursued many actions in a variety of courts, including applications for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada. However, the main impediment, as noted above, is that his appeal has no merit. [52] Thus, if I had granted an extension of time to appeal, I would not have granted the application for no fees payable on the basis that he did not meet either the undue hardship test or the merit test. Application to "stay" the costs order issued after Appeal #1 [53] Under s. 18(1) of the Court of Appeal Act, a justice may order "that all or part of the proceedings, including execution, in the cause or matter from which the appeal has been taken are stayed in whole or in part". [54] This Court does not have jurisdiction to stay an order unrelated to "the proceedings, including execution, in the cause or matter from which the appeal has been taken": Court of Appeal Act, s. 18(1) (emphasis added). Accordingly, I would dismiss this application. If Mr. Simon wishes a transcribed copy of the Registrar's reasons on costs assessment, he should order it from the Registry. Mr. Simon indicates that he intends to appeal the Registrar's assessment. If so, any relief sought needs to be under the auspices of Appeal #1. Special costs [55] The standard to award special costs is that the conduct in question must be reprehensible. This term encompasses scandalous or outrageous conduct, and also milder forms of misconduct deserving of rebuke: Garcia v. Crestbrook Forest Industries Ltd. No. 2 (1994), 9 B.C.L.R. (3d) 242 (C.A.) at para. 17. [56] The AGs have not demonstrated anything approaching "reprehensible" conduct within the meaning of the case law in navigating these chambers applications. I would dismiss this application. Security for costs of the appeal [57] An order for security for appeal costs is expressly authorized under s. 24 of the Court of Appeal Act. [58] In Creative Salmon Company Ltd. v. Staniford, 2007 BCCA 285, Lowry J.A. provided a concise statement of the relevant criteria on an application for security for costs of the appeal: [9] The jurisdiction to order security for costs of an appeal is found in s. 24 of the Court of Appeal Act, R.S.B.C. 1996, c. 77. The appellant against which such an order is sought bears the onus of showing why security should not be required: Kedia v. Shandro Dixon Edgson, 2007 BCCA 57 at para. 4 (C.A. Chambers), Smith J.A. Generally, the considerations are the appellant's ability to post security and the likelihood of costs awarded being recovered from it, as well as the merits and the timeliness of the application: Southeast Toyota Distributors, Inc. v. Branch (1997), 45 B.C.L.R. (3d) 163 (C.A.); Milina v. Bartsch (1985), 5 C.P.C. (2d) 124 at 125 (B.C.C.A); and M.(M.) v. F.(R.) (1997), 43 B.C.L.R. (3d) 98 at 101. [59] Where the applicant fails the merits test, an order requiring security for costs will not create an injustice: Buchy v. Villars, 2008 BCCA 237 at para. 15; Clare's Cove Marina Ltd. v. Salmon Arm (City), 2013 BCCA 290 at para. 23. [60] The onus is on the appellant to establish that the interests of justice require that security not be ordered: Creative Salmon Company Ltd. v. Staniford, 2007 BCCA 285 at para. 9. [61] If I granted the extension of time to appeal, I would grant the AGs security for costs of the appeal and direct a stay until those costs are posted, with leave to apply to dismiss within 60 days of the order. Mr. Simon has not demonstrated why security for appeal costs should not be granted, his appeal has no merit, and there is a real risk that the AGs would otherwise experience difficulty in collecting in the event that Mr. Simon's appeal is dismissed, as he still has not paid costs for Appeal #1. Security for trial costs [62] The jurisdiction to order security for trial costs is found in s. 10(2)(b) of the Court of Appeal Act, which allows a justice to "make an interim order to prevent prejudice to any person." [63] The overriding question is whether, in all the circumstances, the interests of justice require that some or all of the costs of the underlying proceeding be posted: Chung v. Shin, 2017 BCCA 355 at para. 35. [64] Security for costs of a trial will be ordered less readily than of an appeal: Siekham v. Hiebert, 2008 BCCA 229 at para. 13. The applicable principles on an application for security for costs for a trial judgment or for trial costs is found in Creative Salmon Company Ltd., citing Kedia v. Shandro Dixon Edgson, 2007 BCCA 57 at para.14, quoting from Aikenhead v. Jenkins, 2002 BCCA 234 at para. 30: 1. The onus is on the applicant to show that it is in the interest of justice to order posting for security of a trial judgment and/or of trial costs. 2. The applicant must show prejudice if the order is not made. 3. In determining the interests of justice the chambers judge should consider the merits of the appeal and the effect of such an order on the ability of the appellant to continue the appeal. [65] Again, had I granted the extension of time, I would grant the AGs security for trial costs. While Mr. Simon is of limited financial means, such circumstances may not defeat an application where the appeal is meritless. Conclusion [66] I would dismiss the application to extend the time to file a notice of appeal, and as a result, the remaining applications relating to this appeal are dismissed. The application with respect to Appeal #1 is dismissed. The application for special costs against the AGs is dismissed. "The Honourable Madam Justice Bennett"
