McKee v. Farnworth

2022 BCSC 2246 McKee v. Farnworth IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: McKee v. Farnworth, 2022 BCSC 2246 Date: 20221213 Docket: S-224454 Registry: Vancouver Between: Steven McKee Plaintiff And Honourable Mike Farnworth Defendant Before: Master Robertson Oral Reasons for Judgment In Chambers Appearing on his own behalf: S. McKee Counsel for the Defendant: J. Ruttle Place and Date of Trial/Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. December 13, 2022 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. December 13, 2022 [1] THE COURT: The application before the court today is to strike a notice of civil claim on the basis that it is frivolous and vexatious and otherwise an abuse of the court, as well as dismissing the action in its entirety without leave to amend, with costs to be assessed at $500. [2] For the purpose of these reasons I will reproduce the contents of the notice of civil claim, as the determination of the application is largely dispositive on the face of its contents: [3] The statement of facts reads: 1. Definition(s) of words such as: mandate, dictatorship, tyrant 2. News stories that claim there are still mandates in effect 3. Examples of tyrant's/dictators [4] The legal basis of the claim in full is as follows: 1. The Canadian law enforcement enforced rules that are not laws. 2. Presently there are only mask/vaccination mandates in specific places but they are still not laws. [5] Whether or not a claim ought to be struck has been set out in various cases including Willow v. Chong, 2013 BCSC 1083, at para. 18 to 21: [18] The test for striking a claim as disclosing no reasonable claim under Rule 9-5(1)(a), set out in Hunt v Carey Canada Inc., 1990 CanLII 90 (SCC), [1990] 2 SCR 959 and reiterated more recently in R. v Imperial Tobacco Canada Ltd., 2011 SCC 42, is whether it is "plain and obvious," assuming the facts pleaded are true, that the claim discloses no reasonable cause of action, has no reasonable prospect of success, or if the action is "certain to fail." If there is a chance that the plaintiffs might succeed, then they should not be "driven from the judgment seat." No evidence is admissible on an application under Rule 9-5(1)(a). [19] The rule that material facts in a notice of civil claim must be taken as true does not mean that allegations based on assumption and speculation must be taken as true. This was discussed in Operation Dismantle Inc. v The Queen, 1985 CanLII 74 (SCC), [1985] 1 SCR 441, where Dickson J. (as he then was) stated that "[n]o violence is done to the rule where allegations, incapable of proof, are not taken as proven". In Young v Borzoni, 2007 BCCA 16, the court stated (at paras. 30-31) that great caution must be taken in relying on Operation Dismantle as a general authority that allegations in pleadings should be weighed as to their truth, but it is not fundamentally wrong to look behind allegations in some cases, and it may be appropriate to subject the allegations in the pleadings to a sceptical analysis. It was considered appropriate in Young, where the plaintiff made sweeping allegations of things like intolerance, deceit, harassment, intimidation and falsifying documents against the defendants, which the court concluded could only be viewed as speculation. [20] Under Rule 9-5(1)(b), a pleading is unnecessary or vexatious if it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action, if it does not advance any claim known in law, where it is obvious that an action cannot succeed, or where it would serve no useful purpose and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v Canadian Jewish Congress, 1999 CanLII 5860 (BC SC), [1999] BCJ No. 2160 (SC); Skender v Farley, 2007 BCCA 629. If a pleading is so confusing that it is difficult to understand what is pleaded, it may also be unnecessary, frivolous or vexatious. An application under this sub-rule may be supported by evidence. [21] Abuse of process under Rule 9-5(1)(d) or the court's inherent discretion is a flexible doctrine. It allows the court to prevent a claim from proceeding where to do so would violate principles of judicial economy, consistency, finality and the integrity of the administration of justice. A claim may be struck where it is a collateral attack on an administrative decision that is subject to appeal or judicial review: Cimaco International Sales Inc. v British Columbia, 2010 BCCA 342; Stephen v HMTQ, 2008 BCSC 1656; Varzeliotis v British Columbia, 2007 BCSC 620; Gemex Developments Corp. v City of Coquitlam, 2002 BCSC 412; Berscheid v Ensign, 1999 CanLII 6494 (BC SC), [1999] BCJ No. 1172 (SC). A claim may also be struck as an abuse of process where it is an attempt to re-litigate an issue that has already been decided: Toronto (City) v Canadian Union of Public Employees (CUPE), Local 79, 2003 SCC 63. [6] These provisions have been cited with approval in Kent v. Translink, 2016 BCSC 224 at para. 9; as well as Mohebbi v. North Vancouver RCMP, 2015 BCSC 2083, at para. 17. In Mohebbi, the court also noted as follows at para. 18: [18] In Borsato, at para. 24, Master Baker stated that "[a] pleading is frivolous if it is without substance, is groundless, fanciful, 'trifles with the court' or wastes time." A pleading is vexatious or unnecessary where it does not go to establishing the plaintiff's cause of action or fails to advance any known legal claim: Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform, at para. 47. [7] In summary, a claim will be struck where it is plain and obvious, even assuming the facts pleaded are true, that the claim discloses no reasonable cause of action. A pleading is unnecessary and vexatious if it does not go to establishing a cause of action, does not advance any claim known in law, and where it is obvious that the action cannot succeed or would serve no useful purpose, and would be a waste of the court's time and public resources. [8] I am satisfied on the face of this notice of civil that this claim meets each of those definitions. [9] I understand, based on the plaintiff's oral submissions, what his intention was in filing this action and in seeking "$50,000,000 of Honourable Farnworth's Money", namely to "make a statement towards the rest of Canada and countries of the world, because if we let governments say the country is shutdown, you have to have a vaccine, or a vaccination passport, then we are not in a free world. We are in a dictatorship." [10] Filing a frivolous action that has absolutely no prospect of success is not the proper way to make such a statement. It wastes the court's time and resources. [11] Unless there is a cause of action properly disclosed and a reasonable claim, then the court is left with no alternative but to strike it. [12] I am also satisfied that the errors with it are irredeemable such that it is appropriate to not grant leave to amend the claim, but rather to dismiss the whole of the action as was done in Parhar v. British Columbia (Attorney General), 2021 BCSC 700 at para. 34. [13] With respect to the assessment of costs on a lump sum basis, in accordance with Rule 14-1(15), I agree with counsel for the defendant that this is an appropriate matter to award a lump sum amount to avoid further costs being incurred with respect to an assessment, and I fix those as sought in the amount of $500. [14] I will also dispense with approval as to form of the order by the plaintiff. "Master Robertson"
