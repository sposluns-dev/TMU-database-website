Grewal Place and Date of Hearing: Vancouver, British Columbia February 19, 2026 Place and Date of Decision: Vancouver, British Columbia February 19, 2026 Summary: Application to settle the contents of the transcript in an appeal engaging constitutional arguments relating to freedom of religion under s. 2(a) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. Held: The transcript of the relevant hearings and the parties' evidence is needed because the constitutional question(s) raised, if entertained, must be grounded in the adjudicative facts required to resolve the issues on appeal. [1] REGISTRAR OUTERBRIDGE: The appellant applies to settle the contents of the transcript in this appeal. His appeal is centred on his claim that certain orders below infringed his freedom of religion under s. 2(a) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms. [2] The parties are of the Jewish faith and were married in a religious ceremony at a synagogue in early 2009. Just over 10 years later, they separated. After separation, the respondent began family proceedings in the Supreme Court. Following a trial in 2023, final orders were made including child and spousal support, parenting time, property division, and related relief. [3] The trial judge also dealt with the issue of a "Get," which is a Jewish divorce. The traditional terms of a Get were discussed by the Supreme Court of Canada in Bruker v. Marcovitz, 2007 SCC 54, a case also cited by the trial judge: [3] A get is a Jewish divorce. Only a husband can give one. A wife cannot obtain a get unless her husband agrees to give it. Under Jewish law, he does so by "releasing" his wife from the marriage and authorizing her to remarry. The process takes place before three rabbis in what is known as a beth din, or rabbinical court. [4] The husband must voluntarily give the get and the wife consent to receive it. When he does not, she is without religious recourse, retaining the status of his wife and unable to remarry until he decides, in his absolute discretion, to divorce her. She is known as an agunah or "chained wife". Any children she would have on civil remarriage would be considered "illegitimate" under Jewish law. [4] In her affidavit on this application, the respondent recalls the appellant taking different positions at trial on giving the Get. She says his position fluctuated from agreeing (with or without conditions) to outright refusing to give the Get. The appellant may take a different position on this evidence. [5] Eventually, the judge made an order facilitating the Get as part of the final order at trial. It was scheduled before a rabbinical court, and the court below ordered the appellant to attend the ceremony. He did so but advised the officiating Rabbis that he was not attending by his own free will and, as a result, the Get was not granted. [6] In July 2023, the appellant was found in contempt for unreasonably refusing to give the Get. He was fined and instructed to attend a rescheduled ceremony and voluntarily participate. Eventually, the appellant gave the Get. [7] The appellant filed this appeal in February 2024. Factums were filed and the appellant's appeal came before this Court in October 2024. The Court adjourned that hearing generally and ordered the appointment of amicus curiae to assist the Court. However, on 12 November 2024, the appellant obtained independent representation, and the amicus order was stayed. [8] The appellant then applied to extend time to amend his notice of appeal. The respondent also applied to file a cross-appeal. Both applications were granted in May 2025. The appellant amended his notice of appeal seeking to vacate the trial judge's orders in relation to the Get, including orders convicting and sentencing him for contempt. He seeks to have these orders set aside because they infringe his religious rights under s. 2(a) of the Charter and cannot be justified by s. 1. The respondent now cross-appeals on the trial judge's decisions to not award retroactive child support, and the dismissal of claims to both basic child support and in special and extraordinary expenses. [9] Rule 24(3) of the Court of Appeal Rules governs the appellant's responsibilities when obtaining transcripts. An appellant must obtain transcripts of any oral testimony and transcripts other than oral testimony, if required to resolve the grounds of appeal. The parties may either agree to exclude material or, if they cannot agree, may apply to have the transcript settled under Rule 28(1) or in case management under Rule 48(1). [10] The central question when settling a transcript is whether the appeal can still be properly considered by a division if the transcript is limited in size: see Foote v. Canada, 2016 BCCA 188 (Registrar) at para. 8; Rabanes v. Pureza, 2014 BCCA 393 at paras. 10-12. Given the cost of transcripts, they must be limited in size where reasonably possible, but cautiously to not deprive the Court of what is needed to decide the appeal: J.P. v. K.S., 2023 BCCA 374 (Registrar) at para. 12, aff'd 2023 BCCA 408 (Chambers), aff'd 2024 BCCA 26. More caution is required when limiting the transcript in "fact-rich" or "fact intensive" appeals: J.P. at para. 12. [11] The practice of settling transcripts was further described by the Court in Salloum v. Smith, 2023 BCCA 175: [8] The Court of Appeal Rules, B.C. Reg 120/2022 [Rules] give the registrar a wide discretion to direct parties to organize documents for purposes of an appeal. Rule 28 allows the registrar to settle the contents of an appeal record, transcript, and appeal book. The registrar is permitted to settle or limit the contents of the document, direct parties to add or remove materials, direct that certain documents not be used in the appeal, and provide any other directions that may be required to settle the contents of a document. The registrar has considerable experience in these matters. [12] In this appeal, the appellant argues that very limited transcripts are required because he is advancing only "legal and constitutional questions" in relation to the Get orders. I agree that, generally, grounds of appeal that involve only legal questions do not require a transcript or, indeed, "much of any evidence": Foote; Hutton v. Lapka Estate (1988), 10 A.C.W.S. (3d) 151 (B.C.C.A. Chambers). [13] But, as conceded, this appeal does not involve only legal questions. The appellant also advances his Charter rights under s. 2(a). Analysis concerning any Charter right requires an appellant to provide adjudicative facts to support their arguments. Here, the appellant will need to establish the sincerity of, and non-trivial interference with, the religious belief in question: see, e.g., Syndicat Northcrest v. Amselem, 2004 SCC 47 at paras. 53-58. [14] To the extent evidence supporting the appellant's Charter argument is available, I agree that some of that evidence will be in the transcripts of the contempt and sanction orders, which the appellant has agreed to obtain. [15] However, other relevant evidence is likely in the parties' testimony leading up to those orders. In addition to providing the factual background to his Charter argument, that testimony may inform both the sincerity of, and extent of the interference with, the appellant's religious beliefs. This evidence is particularly important if his position on obtaining the Get was, as the respondent alleges, inconsistently advanced at trial. [16] Also, because the appellant is advancing his Charter argument for the first time on appeal, this Court will be called upon to decide whether to entertain these arguments at all: see Court of Appeal Rules, Rule 32; and Jean Louis v. Jean Louis, 2021 BCCA 324 (Chambers) at para. 32, leave ref'd 2022 CanLII 38787 (S.C.C.). Though the test for advancing an argument for the first time on appeal involves a consideration of all circumstances, part of the Court's analysis will include whether the record provides the necessary legislative and adjudicative facts: Jean Louis at para. 33. Including the testimony of the parties provides the essential record for the Court to exercise its discretion on this question as well. [17] As the appellant has raised his financial circumstances, I would reiterate the challenge faced by litigants in this Court to obtain transcripts in support of their appeals, particularly from trials. For this appeal, the cost to obtain the limited testimony required will be over $14,000. Over two decades ago, this Court observed that the cost of providing a transcript can be " so far beyond the means of a litigant to make his or her right of appeal illusory": Dhillon v. Dhillon, 2005 BCCA 529 (Chambers) at para. 9. Twenty years later, "the cost of manual transcription still represents an expensive and very real barrier to advancing [an] appeal": Annable v. Devencore Company Ltd., 2025 BCCA 221 (Registrar) at para. 23. [18] The appellant has deposed that the cost of obtaining the transcripts will defeat his appeal. While the Court will always attempt to reduce the size of a transcript to achieve cost savings and efficiency, as mentioned above, caution must be exercised to ensure the Court is not deprived of the evidence needed to decide the appeal: Foote at paras. 8-10. In this case, the evidence of the parties is required to support the appellant's proposed Charter argument. [19] There is also the matter of the cross-appeal. Here, the respondent has properly acknowledged that the parties' testimony at trial will be required to support her cross-appeal. That evidence will be drawn from the same body of evidence required for the appellant to advance the main appeal. Normally, the transcripts for a cross-appeal are not the subject of debate because they are supplied by the appellant, given both appeals flow from the same order and record. [20] The responsibility to order transcripts thus first vests with the appellant, given there is no freestanding requirement for a cross-appellant to file transcripts in the Rules: Garcha v. 690174 B.C. Ltd., 2022 BCCA 178 (Registrar) at para. 30. Although s. 14 of the Court of Appeal Act, S.B.C. 2021, c. 6 requires a cross-appeal to be "treated as an appeal for all purposes of the Act", that requirement is also subject to the Rules. While the Rules provide that a cross-appellant can file an appeal book, it makes no such allowance for a transcript: Rule 30(2). [21] It follows; therefore, that the appellant is to obtain a transcript of the parties' testimony at trial, with the understanding that the parties may still limit that transcript further by agreement under Rule 24(6).
