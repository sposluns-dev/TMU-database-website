He testified that he owned his own business and took any job posted. He testified that his rates were lower than the average Slovak. [38] I have found, on a balance of probabilities, that the alleged incident of XXXX 10, in which the claimant was beaten by thugs engaged by his employer, did occur. However, I found the claimant embellished his claim with allegations that he was refused medical care following this incident. In his amended narrative the claimant alleges that he was refused medical care after he was beaten at a gas station in XXXX 11.32 I have found, on a balance of probabilities, that this incident did not occur. I, therefore, conclude that the claimant has not been denied medical care in Slovakia. [39] The claimant lived his entire life in Slovakia over a period of approximately sixty-one years. I find that the difficulties that the claimant may have experienced in the Slovak Republic during that time because of his Roma ethnicity, although they qualify as acts of discrimination and harassment, do not individually or cumulatively, rise to a level of seriousness of harm amounting to persecution. Persecution is defined as the systematic or sustained or repeated violations of one's fundamental human rights. I find that the insults, physical attacks, and denial of employment opportunities do not meet the criteria of this definition. [40] While the test for persecution or a risk to life or a risk of cruel and unusual treatment or punishment is forward-looking, past experiences are often a good indicator of what might happen in the future. [41] I find that while some of these actions, previously noted, can be viewed as discriminatory, they do not rise to a level of harm amounting to persecution according to the United Nations Human Rights Commission (UNHCR). When discrimination rises to such a level that it prevents a person from accessing health care or education, or the protection of the state, or housing or employment to allow a person to survive, discrimination can become persecution. I find that the claimant has experienced harassment and discrimination in the Slovak Republic. [42] I find, based on all of the evidence, including the objective country evidence, that there is no serious possibility that the claimant will face treatment in the Slovak Republic, were he to return there, that would rise to a level of seriousness of harm tantamount to persecution. Therefore, I find that his fear of persecution at the hands of racist Slovakians and skinheads in the Slovak Republic should he return there is not well-founded. Although I agree that ethnic Roma face discrimination in the Slovak Republic, I find that preponderance of the documentary evidence does not establish that ethnic Roma are being persecuted in the Slovak Republic. State Protection [43] I find, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant has not established that state protection is unavailable for him in Slovakia. There is a presumption that a state is capable of protecting its citizens. The claimant may rebut this presumption by providing "clear and convincing" proof of lack of state protection in their country of origin. The claimant must approach his or her state for protection, providing state protection might be reasonably forthcoming.33 [44] Evidence that protection being offered is not necessarily perfect34 is not clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, as no government can guarantee the protection of all its citizens at all times. However, where a state is in effective control of its territory, has military, police and civil authority in place and makes serious efforts to protects its citizens, the mere fact it is not always successful at doing so will not be enough to justify a claim that the victims are unable to avail themselves of protection.35 The onus is on the claimant to establish, on a balance of probabilities, that state protection is inadequate.36 [45] No government can be expected to guarantee perfect protection of all of its citizens at all times, and the fact that a state is not always successful in protecting its citizens is not enough to justify a claim. This is particularly true when a state is in effective control of its territory and has military, police and civil authorities in place and is making serious attempts to protect its citizens.37 [46] The burden of proving an absence of state protection is directly proportional to the level of democracy that exists in the state.38 The independent documentary evidence indicates the Slovak Republic is a functioning democracy.39 It has functioning political and judicial systems in place and an official apparatus sufficient to provide a measure of protection to its citizens. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a pattern of state's inability or refusal to provide protection.40 Therefore, a claimant must do more than approach one police officer unsuccessfully before deciding that state protection would not be forthcoming. There is an onus on the claimant to exhaust all courses of action reasonably available to him.41 [47] The Slovak Republic is in control of its territory and has functioning security forces, both military and police, in place. There is no evidence of a total breakdown of state authority in the country. Therefore, the burden of attempting to show that one should not be required to exhaust all avenues of domestic recourse is a heavy one.42 The Slovak security forces are hierarchical and allow a person to complain to a higher authority, particularly the Bureau for the Inspection Service of the Police Corps, if dissatisfied with the services provided.43 [48] I have found, on a balance of probabilities, that in XXXX 10 the police responded to a call and escorted the claimant and his co-workers to a hospital but the police did not follow up with an investigation of the assault upon the claimant and his co-workers. According to the claimant's original narrative, the police advised the claimant not to return to the employer where the assault had occurred.44 As disappointing and frustrating as this might have been to the claimant, the failure of the police on one occasion to not follow up on an assault is not evidence of a lack of state protection. As previously stated, local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a pattern of the state's inability or refusal to provide protection.45 The claimant testified that he did not seek protection from another police department. He testified that he did not take the matter of lack of payment by his employer to court. To qualify for refugee status, a claimant must satisfy the Board that he or she sought, but was unable to obtain, protection from their home state, or alternatively, that their home state, on an objective basis, could not be expected to provide protection.46 In the absence of a compelling explanation, a failure to pursue state protection opportunities within the home state will usually be fatal to a refugee claim, at least where the state is a functioning democracy with a willingness and the apparatus necessary to provide a measure of protection to its citizens.47 [49] I have made a credibility finding regarding the alleged incident at a gas station in XXXX 11. The claimant stated that the police refused to accept his report. There is no reliable evidence before me that the police in the Slovak Republic refused protection to the claimant with respect to this particular incident. [50] I have also made a credibility finding with respect to the alleged incident concerning an assault on the claimant and his three family members in XXXX of 12. [51] I made a credibility finding regarding the alleged extortion attempts including a kidnapping. Even if I were to believe the claimant, which I do not, he testified that he did not seek state protection after having been kidnapped, beaten which resulted in broken toes, and food withheld for a number of days. If such were the case, this is not evidence that the state refused to provide protection. [52] I have made a credibility finding regarding an alleged attack on the claimant at a supermarket in XXXX 11. [53] If I were to accept the allegations with regards to the various incidents in which the claimant alleges that the police refused to help, which I do not, these incidents would not prove that there is no state protection for the claimant were he to return to the Slovak Republic. Even when taken together, these incidents do not prove that the claimant has a well-founded fear of persecution if he were to return to the Slovak Republic because he can avail himself of the protection offered by the state.48 [54] I find, on a balance of probabilities, that the claimant has failed to rebut the presumption of state protection. I find that there is state protection for members of the Roma community in the Slovak Republic. [55] Both the documentary evidence of the Board49 and counsel's two-page, on-line article from The Daily Slovakia50 do indicate that Roma and other minorities suffer discrimination and violence in Slovakia and that police mistreat Romani suspects and detainees. Counsel's two page article provides a brief outline of some of the issues presented by the Center for Civil and Human Rights (Poradna) and People in Need Slovak Republic which submitted written comments concerning the Slovak Republic for consideration by the United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination.51 These issues are also reported in the Board's documentary evidence. The evidence also supports the claimant's contention that organized neo-Nazi groups and sympathizers harass and attack minorities including the Roma. Further, police might not intervene or investigate properly when there are Roma involved, although this varies by jurisdiction. The Roma generally face discrimination in health care, education, housing and employment in the Slovak Republic as well.52 Many Roma face severe difficulties and discrimination accessing adequate housing and employment and they experience segregation in schools and health care facilities.53 [56] However, the documentary evidence indicates that the Slovak Republic has taken, and is taking, steps to combat these deficiencies and there are signs of real progress. For example, there is a deeply entrenched legal framework as a basis from which the Slovak Republic can combat these problems. The legal framework includes the Constitution, legislation and international obligations as well. [57] The Slovak Republic is an active party to the Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities, being a signatory since 1995. In its report to the Council of Europe delivered in 12, the Slovak Republic describes a number of improvements and achievements in legislation and social practice. This includes combating some forms and expressions of racism and xenophobia by means of criminal law. The report was prepared with the participation of a number of minorities through their delegated representatives in the Government Council for National Minorities and Ethnic Groups.54 [58] The Council of Europe reports that the amended Anti-Discrimination Law introduced positive measures aiming to redress social and economic inequalities or disadvantages facing persons belonging to more vulnerable groups, such as Roma. Specific programs have been elaborated and some financial resources allocated to improve the situation of the Roma in the fields of housing, employment, education and social care. [59] If the claimant experiences difficulties in obtaining police cooperation, as he has alleged happened in the past, there is legislation in place since 06 that allows the Public Defender of Rights to participate in bringing the persons holding public authority to accountability if these persons violate a fundamental right or freedom, and all public authorities must provide the Public Defender of Rights all co-operation as necessary. The National Council of the Slovak Republic instituted the first Public Defender of Rights (ombudsman) in March 02, and now the office has nine regional branches and employs 38 staff. Further, the Slovak National Centre for Human Rights collects and monitors complaints of discrimination and provides legal assistance to victims.55 [60] While there continues to be criticism of police, the documentary evidence indicates that the authorities have taken serious measures to deal with police misconduct and there are signs of some success. Police continued to provide special training on Romani culture and language to officers working in districts with Romani communities in the Kosice and Presov regions. Police training on policing in a multiethnic environment, focusing on policing in areas inhabited by the Roma minority, has been carried out in recent years in order to build trust between the police and the Roma. One hundred and twenty policemen have been trained so far. The Bratislava branch of postsecondary schooling for police also offered an elective course in Romani language and culture. In 06, the Council of Europe's Committee for the Prevention of Torture (CPT) noted that there were significant allegations of mistreatment of detainees by law enforcement agencies and in a "notable proportion" of cases the victims were Roma. The CPT conducted a follow-up visit from March 24 to April 2, 09 and found, in general, the situation had improved.56 This is clear evidence that the efforts of the state are working. [61] The documentary evidence indicates that police service may vary depending on the jurisdiction but the Commissioner for Human Rights of the Council of Europe (COE) reported that police response to racially motivated crimes has improved, although it continues to face challenges. The COE credits this improvement to the Slovak Republic's establishment of a Commission for Co-ordinating Actions to Eliminate Racially Motivated Crime, which is tasked with sharing information on racially motivated crime and eliminating racial discrimination, including police mistreatment. The Minister of the Interior has also introduced police specialists for Roma communities, but there are few Romani officers in the police force.57 Police do take action when they are requested to do so and that state does prosecute. [62] Although there were some indications that impunity within the police was a problem, there is also evidence that the state is serious about combating impunity and disciplining authorities that do not perform their function or break the law. For example, in September 08, the Supreme Court upheld the February 08 Banska Bystrica Regional Court conviction of seven former police officers for torture and inhuman treatment in connection with the 01 death of a Romani man in police custody. In another example, in March 08, police officers abused six Romani boys who were detained. When the abuse was made public police authorities immediately suspended nine officers, seven of whom subsequently lost their jobs, accused the officers of abuse of office and of intimidation, and the officers faced criminal charges. The most common charge authorities brought against police officers was abuse of power; other charges included battery, assault and battery, and illegal intrusion into private homes. In 09 authorities charged 171 police officers with crimes; in sixty-one of these cases, the crime was abuse of power. Disciplinary action ranged from fines to expulsion from the police force.58 [63] The Slovak Republic has anti-discrimination legislation as well. In terms of obtaining remedy at court, in 08 provisions of the Anti Discrimination Act were repealed. Now a person no longer needs to present the court with evidence proving that there was a violation of the principle of equal treatment. This makes it easier for a person like the claimant who feels he was not treated fairly because he is Roma. There is criticism that the anti-discrimination law has not been implemented consistently due to insufficient training of judges. [64] The Penal Code, amended in June 09, provides for better protection against crime motivated by racial discrimination, and for crimes committed on the grounds of national, ethnic or racial hatred or hatred based on skin colour. Moreover, the Slovak government has adopted the Action Plan on the Prevention of Discrimination, Racism, Xenophobia, anti-Semitism and other forms of Intolerance for the years 09-11, containing proposals for improving the legislative and policy frameworks in this field.59 [65] The priorities of the Action Plan for 06 to 08 included: the systematic training of members of professional groups; systematic training and opinion-making in the area of prevention of discrimination in relation to migrants; focusing on professional groups and general public; intensifying the fight against extremism in the form of drafting and implementing the legislation; improving the efficiency of the detection; clarification, proving and punishment of crimes motivated by racial and other intolerance, by means of systematic training and opinion-making in the area of the prevention of extremism; intensifying the monitoring, systematic training and opinion-making in the area of the prevention of anti-Semitism; implementation of activities aimed at addressing the problems of disadvantaged groups of the population; support for cultural and social-science activities devoted to the issues of respect for human rights; and prevention of all forms of discrimination, racism, xenophobia, anti-Semitism, and other expressions of intolerance. [66] In 1999, the Slovak government established the Office of Plenipotentiary for Roma Communities as an advisory body that recommends action to assist in the integration of Roma. The role of the Plenipotentiary is to address issues of concern to Roma, such as education, employment, housing and healthcare, in coordination with other Slovak ministries. In 04, the office was expanded and provided with more funding to allow for five regional branches as well as increased staff.60 The Ministry of Labour, Social Affairs, and Family assigned specially trained social workers to Romani settlements to assist with government paperwork and to advocate the importance of education and preventative health care. The Ministry of Health continued to train Romani-speaking health care assistants to improve the community's access to health services.61 [67] In terms of discrimination in employment, employees may protect their rights not only with their employer, but also through the intermediary of employee representatives, labour inspectorates, or the court if they consider themselves affected by failure to apply the principle of equal treatment. They may further demand that the entity that failed to apply the principle of equal treatment refrain from such conduct and, where possible, rectify the unlawful situation or provide adequate satisfaction. In case the employer violates the principle of equal treatment with regard to access to employment the applicant has the right to seek adequate pecuniary compensation by filing a claim with the court. With respect to education, right to education in a national minority language is enshrined in the Constitution. The national project on "Improving the qualification potential of the Roma community by introducing a new course on Roma studies in the system of secondary schools" was carried out under the professional guidance of the National Institute for Education was aimed at eliminating the limited access of the Roma to education and employment due to the language barrier, cultural handicaps, low mobility, and social and economic situation of families and raising educational attainment of the Roma in the Slovak Republic as a basic prerequisite for their participation in the labour market and integration into society in general. [68] Further, the Constitution guarantees the right of the citizens belonging to national minorities to create and maintain their educational institutions. The teaching of the languages of national minorities is provided also in state-run institutions of higher education. These schools are part of Slovak Republic's system of education and instruction and are financed from the state budget. Education and instruction of children and pupils from socially disadvantaged environments is carried out in schools under this Act using specific methods and forms to meet the needs of these disadvantaged students. The state employs compulsory education for all students. There are subsidies on food, school materials and subsidy on motivational allowance for children attending preschool facilities or schools and living in families that receive benefits and allowances or whose income does not exceed the level of subsistence minimum. Under these programs school attendance of pupils has improved but educational attainment of pupils from socially disadvantaged environment has not improved. [69] The evidence clearly shows that the Roma still suffer from higher rates of unemployment and lower educational achievement. The Roma are still excluded from regular life in terms of housing and health care. However, the documentary evidence indicates that the state is making serious efforts and although progress is slow, there are signs of progress. It is unreasonable to expect that these measures should have prevented or eliminated all racism or acts of violence related to race. However, this is an indication of the serious efforts the state has made to combat racial discrimination in every aspect of society. The progress is not as rapid and there have been set-backs and obstacles, but the commitment of the state to continue the battle is not in question. The quality of existence for the Roma is not what it should be, but their existence is not threatened by the state. While skinheads and extremists seek to threaten the existence of the Roma, the state takes serious actions against that as well. [70] There is information in the documentation to indicate that bribery and corruption remain issues in Slovakian security forces. However, weighted against this is persuasive evidence that indicates the Slovak Republic candidly acknowledges its past problems and is making serious efforts to rectify the corruption and impunity that exists. [71] The preponderance of the objective evidence regarding current country conditions suggests that, although not perfect, there is adequate state protection in the Slovak Republic for victims of crime, that the Slovak Republic is making serious efforts to address the problems of criminality, and that the police are both willing and able to protect victims. Police corruption and deficiencies, although existing and noted by the Board, are not systemic. I am of the view in canvassing the documentary evidence, that, as a whole, the issues of corruption and deficiencies are being addressed by the Slovak Republic. [72] The documentary evidence currently before the Board indicates the following:62 The national police has sole responsibility for internal and border security and reports to the Ministry of Interior. The head of the police force reports directly to the minister of interior, who has the authority to recall any member of the police. Human rights observers believed that police were occasionally reluctant to accept the testimony of certain witnesses, particularly Roma, women, and homeless persons and often failed to investigate cases involving Roma and other minorities promptly and thoroughly. Instances of police corruption and misconduct were reported, primarily the extortion of bribes during traffic stops. Headed by a director who reports directly to the minister of interior, the Bureau for the Inspection Service of the Police Corps is responsible for investigating police abuses. Cases may be initiated by, among others, the inspection service, the police corps, the police's organized crime unit, and individual citizens. Authorities arrested six police officers following an investigation that indicated they had been working as security guards in a brothel in Senec. At year's end the officers, who were members of teams in Bratislava and Trnava, awaited trial. As previously stated, the most common charge authorities brought against police officers was abuse of power; other charges included battery, assault and battery, and illegal intrusion into private homes. In 08 authorities charged 158 police officers with crimes; in sixty-eight of these cases, the crime was abuse of power. Disciplinary action ranged from fines to expulsion from the police force. Several non-governmental organizations (NGOs) criticized the actions of police at a June protest in Bratislava during the visit of Chinese president Hu Jintao. During violent clashes between Slovak protesters and supporters of the Chinese president, police did not actively intervene. Subsequently, police detained nine protesters: six Slovaks and three Chinese. In July five NGOs filed a complaint against the Ministry of Interior, alleging that police did not protect the rights of protesters and expressing concern that the investigation of the event was inadequate. Prime Minister Fico publicly supported the police action. In September the ministry dismissed the NGOs' complaint. There were some indications that impunity was a problem, as evidenced in the continuing case of Radoslav Puky, a Slovak citizen of Romani origin. In 04 Puky's body was found in a Trebisov canal following his disappearance during a police operation. A CPT investigation indicated that police took only perfunctory action to investigate reports of police assault against Puky. In 07 the Constitutional Court dismissed a new complaint filed by the League of Human Rights Activists on behalf of the Puky family. In April 08 the European Roma Rights Center submitted the case to the European Court of Human Rights (ECHR), where it was pending at year's end. Human rights training remained on the curriculum at police training facilities. [73] Further documentary evidence indicates the following:63 Sources indicated that corruption in the Slovak police and judiciary system is an ongoing problem as there were reports of corruption and misconduct in the police forces. For instance, police corruption at the border "may have hampered" efforts to reduce human smuggling. For the year 05, Transparency International (TI) puts Slovakia at the forty-seventh rank out of one hundred and fifty-eight evaluated countries ranked from the least to the most corrupt. TI stated that Slovakia was among the countries to improve the most in comparison with the previous year. In 1999, a program of judicial reforms that increased state action against corruption began. According to Country Reports 04, 90 per cent of the Slovak "Government Action Plan to Fight Corruption" was implemented at year's end. However, a European Union (EU) summary of legislation stated that "Slovakia must pursue its efforts to implement its national programme of action." As well, although the United States' International Narcotics Control Strategy Report 05 reported that "Slovakia has made major legislative strides in the fight against corruption," it also indicated that the execution of the new measures "remain[ed] a challenge." The Slovak government created its "Anti-Corruption Department" in December 02 and put it in charge of applying its national anti-corruption program. This department develops policy, concepts and programs, and proposes laws on combating corruption. It also monitors information on corruption and advances its own projects to reduce corruption. The International Narcotics Control Strategy Report 05 provided a comprehensive description of the recent state measures put forward in the fight against corruption: Officials serious about creating transparent rules and prosecuting abuses have been put in key positions, a new conflict of interest law passed in 04, and a special prosecutor and court to fight corruption were set up. The head of the [Government of Slovakia] anticorruption office is a noted human rights lawyer who encourages the use of "sting" operations and introduced a "whistle blower" statute to protect government employees who talk to investigators about corruption in their offices. The Slovak government indicated that the special court for the prosecution of corruption crimes with nationwide jurisdiction was to be established in Bratislava and was to be operational in September 04. In November 05, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) confirmed that the Special Court and prosecutor had started their work and that they had "exclusive jurisdiction over domestic and foreign bribery." The justice minister of Slovakia recognized that confidence in the Slovak justice system was "low." He stated, however, that a reform through which judges are assigned cases randomly had helped to "improv[e] transparency in the judicial system." Country Reports 04 added that the computerized system assigning cases was implemented at almost all court levels. As well, a program which permits plea bargaining with people who have inside information about organized crime and "the legalization of the use of undercover agents" in the fight against corruption was also put forward. Laws regulating professional codes of conduct were amended to make sure that the principle of "zero tolerance" would be applied in cases of corruption. Another legislative change, criminalizing "failure to disclose corruption," was implemented in December 03. Freedom House added that the Law on Free Access to Information increased transparency. In May 04, the appointed "special prosecutor to fight corruption" brought charges against the mayor of Bratislava-Raca, who was suspected of accepting a bribe, and referred other cases to an anticorruption court. Over the year 04, the director of the "alien detention facility" was arrested as a suspect in human smuggling and "several corruption charges" were pressed against members of the customs and immigration police. [74] The claimant has not established that were he to return to Slovakia today, protection would not be reasonably forthcoming or that it is objectively unreasonable for him to seek that protection. [75] I find that the claimant has not established, on a balance of probabilities, that there is inadequate state protection for him in the Slovak Republic. I find that the claimant has failed to rebut the presumption of state protection with clear and convincing evidence and the claim fails under sections 96 and 97 of the IRPA. CONCLUSION [76] I, therefore, determine that the claimant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. [77] The Refugee Protection Division, therefore, rejects his claim.
