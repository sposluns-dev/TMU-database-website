XXXX XXXX) Personne(s) en cause Appeal considered / heard at Toronto, ON Appel instruit / entendu à Date of decision December 7, 2018 Date de la décision Panel James Sloan Tribunal Counsel for the person(s) who is(are) the subject of the appeal Aleksandar Jeremic Barrister & Solicitor Conseil(s) (de la/des) personne(s) en cause Designated representative N/A Représentant(e) désigné(e) Counsel for the Minister N/A Conseil du ministre REASONS FOR DECISION [1] XXXX XXXX (the Appellant) claims that if returned to Ukraine, her country of nationality, she will be killed or otherwise harmed by her abusive ex-husband and persecuted based on her Jewish religion. She claims that her ex-husband was physically and emotionally abusive to her during their nearly ten-year marriage. In addition, she claims he is anti-Semitic, and would not allow her to practise her religion or to raise their son XXXX as a Jew. The Appellant claims that her husband came to her apartment and assaulted her in January and November 2014, some three years after their divorce. In XXXX 2014, she flew to the US to meet friends and, on XXXX XXXX XXXX XXXX, flew to Toronto. (She had earlier applied for a Canadian visa to visit friends and it was issued on July 29, 2014.) The Appellant made an inland refugee claim and signed her Basis of Claim form on March 17, 2015. [2] Her claim for refugee protection was heard by the Refugee Protection Division (RPD) on July 6 and August 4, 2017 and refused on September 20, 2017 because it found that a viable internal flight alternative (IFA) existed in the Ukrainian city of Poltava. She now appeals to the Refugee Appeal Division (RAD). DETERMINATION [3] Pursuant to paragraph 111(1)(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA), I confirm the decision of the RPD that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. The appeal is dismissed. REQUEST FOR THE ADMISSION OF POST-PREFECTION EVIDENCE [4] The Appellant's Record was perfected on November 22, 2017. On February 12, 2018, she requested the admission of post-perfection evidence in the form of an affidavit from the Appellant, dated January 30, 2018. I find this affidavit to be admissible under RAD Rule 29. The sole purpose of the affidavit was to introduce an email from her son by her first marriage, Sergey, dated November 5, 2017. The email by her son is discussed below. NEW DOCUMENTS AND A HEARING Documentary evidence [5] As noted, the Appellant has requested the admission of an email from her son by her first marriage, XXXX, dated November 5, 2017. [6] Subsection 110(4) of the IRPA limits the admission of evidence in an appeal before the RAD to evidence that: 1) arose after the RPD's rejection of the claim; 2) was not reasonably available at the time of the RPD's rejection of the claim; or 3) an Appellant could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented at the time of the RPD's rejection of the claim. If I find that the new evidence is admissible under subsection 110(4), I must then consider whether the evidence meets the factors elaborated by the Federal Court of Appeal in the Singh case, i.e. credibility, newness, and relevance.1 Only then am I able to admit the new evidence. The threshold for the admission of new evidence before the RAD is high.2 [7] While the November 5, 2017 email relates to events that took place in or before August 2017, the Appellant argues that it was not reasonably available to her prior to the September 20, 2017 rejection of her claim by the RPD. First, she argues that "even if the events occurred in the very first few days of August, it would have been unreasonable to expect that the Appellant's son would immediately communicate the occurrence to his mother." In addition, she argues that she only learned of the information in the email when her son was in touch on November 5, 2017 because he withheld the information for benevolent reasons. [8] As to the first explanation, no reason is given why it would be unreasonable for the son to communicate the information regarding the Appellant's ex-husband's conduct before the RPD's rejection of the claim. The son's email states that the Appellant's ex-husband "came to us several times, most recently in August of this year". No date in August is given. Without more, it seems to me perfectly reasonable for the son to have advised his mother of her ex-husband's conduct in advance of the RPD's September 20, 2017 decision to reject the claim. [9] As to the explanation that the son was acting benevolently-"I did not tell you, did not want to upset you"-I will assume, without deciding, that this representation is the truth. Consequently, the explanation provides a basis on which to find that the information was not reasonable to the Appellant and/or that she could not reasonably have been expected in the circumstances to have presented it at the time of the RPD's rejection. The email from XXXX meets the requirements of subsection 110(4). [10] I find, however, that the evidence lacks credibility for the reasons that follow and, as such, does not meet one of the Singh tests for admissibility. First, the timing of the letter is overly-fortuitous: the Appellant learned of the rejection of her claim on October 19, 2017 and less fifteen days later, an email from her son arrives. [11] Second, the letter appears tailor-made to address the misgivings of the RPD as expressed in its Decision: it directly undermines Poltava as an IFA by claiming that the Appellant's ex-husband is working there; it rebuts the RPD's finding that the Appellant's ex-husband could not find her in Poltava by portraying him as someone connected to the police chief of Poltava and the police more broadly; it rebuts the RPD's finding that the Appellant's ex-husband would not attempt to find her in Poltava ("I will not let her live); it portrays the Appellant's ex-husband as having an ability to track arrivals into the country ("'I will know the minute she returns to Ukraine!'") due to his connections at the airport; it states that the Appellant's ex-husband has acquired some weapons; and it underlines the Appellant's ex-husband's loathing for Jews. [12] Finally, the letter is suspect because it is from XXXX, who was born in XXXX and was the child of the Appellant and her first ex-husband. He was a teenager when his mother remarried and nothing in the evidence indicates that XXXX had a relationship with the Appellant's second husband, whether as a child or as an adult. In fact, when the Appellant was asked if her ex-husband had been in touch with XXXX ("Has your ex-husband contacted your other son?") she testified in the negative ("No"). Therefore, I find XXXX claim that his step-father had come to XXXX family home "several times" and advised him of his work repairing a house in Poltava for a police chief and of the weapons he had in his possession to be unbelievable. [13] I find that the letter is not credible and, consequently, find it inadmissible under Singh. Hearing [14] Because no new evidence has been admitted, the request for a hearing pursuant to subsection 110(6) of the IRPA must be denied. DETERMINATIVE ISSUES [15] The determinative issues in this case are the existence of an IFA and the credibility of the Appellant as it pertains to the proposed IFA. ANALYSIS [16] The RPD found that the Appellant had a viable IFA in the city of Poltava. The Appellant argues that the RPD erred and that she will be subject to persecution under section 96 or other harm as envisaged under subsection 97(1) of the IRPA if she moves to Poltava. She claims she will be attacked or killed by her ex-husband and persecuted by society due to her religion as a Jew. [17] The Federal Court has made clear that the onus is on the Appellant to demonstrate that it is unreasonable to seek refuge in a different part of her country before resorting to international protection.3 The Court has further held that, in determining the viability of an IFA, a two-pronged test must be considered. First, the Immigration and Refugee Board must be satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that there is no serious possibility of the individual being persecuted in the part of the country where it has suggested an IFA may exist.4 Second, the conditions in the part of the country proposed as an IFA must be such that it would not be unreasonable, in all the circumstances, including those particular to the claimant, for her to seek refuge there.5 [18] Before turning to the two prongs of the test, it must be noted that, in deciding this appeal, I am guided by the Guidelines on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender-Related Persecution.6 First Prong: Is there a serious possibility of risk in the proposed IFA? [19] The RPD held that the Appellant did not face a serous possibility of persecution in Poltava, because a) she had not shown that her ex-husband had a serious interest in pursuing her; b) that, even if he had such an interest, he did not have the capacity to do so and c) that she was not at risk of persecution in Poltava based on being Jewish. a) Intention to find the Appellant in Poltava [20] The RPD noted that from the time she and her ex-husband separated in 2009 until the time he attacked her in 2014, the Appellant saw him only once, when they filed for divorce. It also noted that the Appellant's ex-husband had never sought out the Appellant at her workplace when she was in Ukraine and had not taken much action to pursue her since she came to Canada in January 2015. The Appellant testified that her ex-husband did not contact XXXX, did not contact her family members or her friends-people whom the ex-husband would have known given their long-term relationship. The only exception is that he was in touch with their son, XXXX. In view of this, the RPD was not persuaded that the ex-husband would seek out the Appellant in Poltava. [21] The Appellant argues that the RPD was unreasonable in relying on the fact that her ex-husband did not seek her out at work as a basis for a finding that he lacked interest in pursuing her in Poltava. After all, he did not need to pursue her at work, given that he knew where she lived and could pursue her there, and given that he would be more likely to be able to attack her without consequences in the privacy of her home. I agree with the Appellant; the RPD's reasoning on this point was in error. [22] The other aspects of the RPD's finding on this point are uncontested. I agree with them. The RPD's uncontested finding that the Appellant has credibility problems in relation to her testimony regarding her ex-husband (discussed immediately below) gives me reason to doubt the veracity of her testimony. I find that her testimony is not sufficient to establish, on a balance of probabilities, that her husband would attempt to find her and kill or otherwise harm her in Poltava. I do not find an email from her son XXXX dated May 7, 2015, in which he states that his father threatens the Appellant "as before" and most likely will never give her peace,7 to be sufficiently persuasive to overcome this credibility finding. b) Ability to find the Appellant in Poltava [23] The Appellant testified that her husband told her he had connections with police officers and others in high positions, who had helped him to look for her using the address registration system in operation in Ukraine. First, the RPD noted that it did not make sense for the Appellant's ex-husband to have made such a claim; after all, he had no reason to look for her in Ukraine as he knew where she was until she left for North America. The RPD did not find the Appellant's testimony credible in this regard. Second, the RPD noted that the ex-husband fled the scene after the police were called during his January 2014 attack on the Appellant. When asked about this, the Appellant said he had left because he was drunk and likely feared the police would arrest him. The RPD did not accept this explanation and noted that if the ex-husband had connections to the police, he would not have feared arrest. Third, the RPD noted that the ex-husband sounds like a "broken man with mental health issues" and, on this basis, held that he was unlikely to have sufficiently powerful friends-or the wherewithal-to find the Appellant. [24] The Appellant argues that there was nothing unreasonable about the Appellant's testimony that her ex-husband would have fled the scene of his January 2014 assault because the officers who arrived may have arrested him because they did not know him (the Appellant did not say he knew every police officer) or because he would prefer not to engage with the police (even if he was confident his connections would lead to him being released). She also argues that her ex-husband's possible mental health problems are not a factor in his ability to cultivate a relationship with the police. Finally, the Appellant makes an argument based on the new evidence she has requested the submission of. [25] I agree with the Appellant's argument that someone with powerful friends in the police might, nevertheless, flee the scene of a drunken attack. I also agree with the Appellant's argument that the ex-husband's mental health is not relevant to whether or not he has powerful friends. As to the Appellant's argument based on her proposed new evidence, I note that that evidence has not been admitted; as such, the argument cannot be considered. [26] The Appellant does not contest the RPD's finding that the Appellant lacked credibility based on her testimony that her ex-husband had pursued her using the address registry even before she left the country. I agree with the RPD that this testimony was illogical and find that it undermines the Appellant's credibility and indicates that she was embellishing her story to make it more convincing. I do not believe the Appellant's testimony that her husband is sufficiently powerful that he can take measures to find people, despite the registration system in Ukraine being inaccessible to the public. c) Persecution in Poltava based on being Jewish [27] The RPD considered the Appellant's claim to fear persecution in Poltava based on her Jewish religion. It reviewed the objective documentation and found that societal anti-Semitism was rare in Ukraine. Consequently it held that she did not have a well-founded fear of persecution in Ukraine on the basis of her religion. Based on its earlier findings that her ex-husband did not have the desire or the ability to find her in Poltava, it found that there was no serious possibility that he would persecute her based on her religion there. [28] The Appellant suggests that the RPD was selective in its analysis of the objective documentation relating to anti-Semitism in Ukraine. She refers to a US Department of State report8 that outlined a number of incidents of anti-Semitic vandalism between 2013 and 2015 and one instance relating to the Jewish community's concerns over the use of an ancient Jewish cemetery. This, the Appellant argues, provides evidence "that anti-Semitic attacks continue and that the authorities are either unwilling or unable to find and persecute the perpetrators."9 The Appellant also argues that her ex-husband's "attacks were always motivated by anti-Semitism".10 [29] I have reviewed the RPD's analysis regarding whether a Jewish person would face a serious possibility of persecution in Ukraine. I find that the RPD was not selective in its analysis of the objective documentation. It is clear from the documentation that, in the most recent year discussed in the Department of State report-2015-there was a single incident of suspected anti-Semitic violence in the country.11 Even a single incident is, of course, deeply disturbing. However, given that the number of Jews or persons of Jewish heritage in Ukraine is estimated at between 103,600 and 370,000,12 I find that there is not a serious possibility of the Appellant being persecuted based on her religion if she returns to Ukraine. I note that the incidents highlighted by the Appellant relate to acts of vandalism or to concerns about the use of a cemetery. Though deeply loathsome, they do not involve physical attacks against Jewish people and do not indicate a serious possibility of persecution. [30] As regards the Appellant's argument that the Appellant's ex-husband's attacks were motivated by anti-Semitism, given the above finding that he had neither the will nor the ability to locate the Appellant in Poltava, I need not address this. Second Prong: Is the proposed IFA reasonable in the circumstances? [31] In making its decision that it was not unreasonable for the Appellant to seek refuge in Poltava, the RPD noted that she had a support network there (her eldest son, XXXX, lives there with his family), that she was in receipt of a government pension, that she was extremely well-educated (with diplomas in Economics and in Psychology) and that she had owned and operated her own successful business. [32] The Appellant does not contest the findings of the RPD that it would not be unreasonable for the Appellant to return to Poltava. [33] I have reviewed the RPD's finding on the second prong and do not find that it would be objectively unreasonable13 for the Appellant to live in Poltava.14 CONCLUSION [34] I agree with the decision of the RPD and find that the Appellant is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. The appeal is dismissed. (signed) "James Sloan" James Sloan December 7, 2018 Date 1 M.C.I. v. Singh, Parminder, (F.C.A., no.
