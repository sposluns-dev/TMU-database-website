Keezio Group, LLC v. The Shrunks' Family Toy Company Inc.

2021 BCSC 1042 Keezio Group, LLC v. The Shrunks' Family Toy Company Inc. IN THE SUPREME COURT OF BRITISH COLUMBIA Citation: Keezio Group, LLC v. The Shrunks' Family Toy Company Inc., 2021 BCSC 1042 Date: 20210331 Docket: S1914059 Registry: Vancouver Between: Keezio Group, LLC Plaintiff And The Shrunks' Family Toy Company Inc. and David Cirjak Defendants Before: The Honourable Madam Justice Choi Oral Reasons for Judgment Counsel for the Plaintiff: E.M. Feil-Fraser Counsel for the Defendants: D. Bowden Place and Date of Trial/Hearing: Vancouver, B.C. March 8 & 10, 2021 Place and Date of Judgment: Vancouver, B.C. March 31, 2021 [1] THE COURT: Keezio Group, LLC ("Keezio"), a defendant by counterclaim, applies for security for costs pursuant to s. 236 of the Business Corporations Act, S.B.C. 2002, c. 57 [BCBCA] in the amount of $74,354.90 on the basis that The Shrunks' Family Toy Company Inc. ("The Shrunks"), the plaintiff by counterclaim, may not be able to pay costs if its counterclaim fails. [2] The Shrunks argues that Keezio will not incur any additional costs defending the counterclaim because the two claims are mirror images of the other. It claims the orders sought are tantamount to requiring it to post security to advance its own defence in the original action. [3] The individual defendant, Mr. Cirjak, is the sole shareholder, officer, and director of The Shrunks. Keezio does not seek security for costs against Mr. Cirjak. [4] The parties are direct competitors in the children's inflatable bed market. They both sell their products, mostly through Amazon, to big consumers in the same marketplace. [5] Keezio asserts that The Shrunks and Mr. Cirjak have repeatedly interfered with Keezio's business, alleging intellectual property infringement by making false infringement complaints to Amazon, which caused Keezio's product listings to be taken down, making what it claims are slanderous comments to Keezio's manufacturer, and posting a negative one-star review alleging infringement regarding Keezio's products and businesses. [6] Based on these incidents, Keezio claims defamation, libel, contravention of the Trademarks Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. T-13, s. 7 (false or misleading statements), and contravention of the Competition Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-34, s. 52 (false or misleading statements). [7] The Shrunks says its statements are true and, therefore, not defamatory. The Shrunks' counterclaim alleges the following: a) Keezio contravened the Trademarks Act, s. 7(b), by drawing attention to Keezio's inflatable beds in a way that is likely to cause confusion between Keezio's beds and The Shrunks'; b) copyright infringement, asserting that the bed design is an artistic work--namely a sculpture--such that Mr. Cirjak and possibly The Shrunks as licensee have intellectual property right to the design, which Keezio infringed; and c) contravention of the Trademarks Act, s. 22, by using The Shrunks' registered trademark in a manner likely to depreciate their goodwill via the "Rhymes with The Skunks" comparison chart. [8] I now turn to the legal framework. Section 236 of the BCBCA states: Court may order security for costs 236 If a corporation is the plaintiff in a legal proceeding brought before the court, and if it appears that the corporation will be unable to pay the costs of the defendant if the defendant is successful in the defence, the court may require security to be given by the corporation for those costs, and may stay all legal proceedings until the security is given. [9] The principles respecting security for costs against a corporate defendant are set out at para. 17 in Kropp v. Swaneset Bay Golf Court Ltd. (1997), 29 B.C.L.R (3d) 252 (C.A.): 1. The court has a complete discretion whether to order security, and will act in light of all the relevant circumstances; 2. The possibility or probability that the plaintiff company will be deterred from pursuing its claim is not without more sufficient reason for not ordering security; 3. The court must attempt to balance injustices arising from use of security as an instrument of oppression to stifle a legitimate claim on the one hand, and use of impecuniosity as a means of putting unfair pressure on a defendant on the other; 4. The court may have regard to the merits of the action, but should avoid going into detail on the merits unless success or failure appears obvious; 5. The court can order any amount of security up to the full amount claimed, as long as the amount is more than nominal; 6. Before the court refuses to order security on the ground that it would unfairly stifle a valid claim, the court must be satisfied that, in all the circumstances, it is probable that the claim would be stifled; and 7. The lateness of the application for security is a circumstance which can properly be taken into account. [10] Keezio relies on the four-part test from the Citizens for Foreign Aid Reform Inc. v. Canadian Jewish Congress (1999), 91 A.C.W.S. (3d) 362 (B.C.S.C.) [Canadian Jewish Congress]: [14] Based then, on the decision in Kropp, supra as well as the decision of Spencer J. in Ruko, supra, the test to be applied on an application for security for costs is as follows: 1. Does it appear that the plaintiff company will be unable to pay the defendants' costs if the action fails? 2. If so, has the plaintiff shown that it has exigible assets of sufficient value to satisfy an award of costs? 3. Is the court satisfied that the defendants have an arguable defence to present? 4. Would an order for costs visit undue hardship on the plaintiff such that it would prevent the plaintiff's case from being heard? [11] Keezio argues that The Shrunks cannot pass the first or second part of the test, and that The Shrunks' argument focuses entirely on the third part of the test, being whether Keezio has an arguable defence. [12] The Court of Appeal has held that, once an applicant for security for costs has shown that a corporate plaintiff will not be able to pay costs should its claim fail, security is generally ordered unless the court is satisfied that there is no arguable defence: Ocean Pastures Corporation v. Old Masset Economic Development Corporation, 2016 BCCA 12 at para. 18, citing Fat Mel's Restaurant Ltd. v. Canadian Northern Shield Insurance Co. (1993), 76 B.C.L.R. (2d) 231 (C.A.) at 235. [13] The Shrunks argues that the court must consider other factors from Gray v. Powerassist Technologies Inc., 2001 BCSC 1208 at para. 19: (a) whether the failure to order security for costs will work an injustice on a defendant by counterclaim (who is also a plaintiff) by being unable to recover litigation costs or whether the defendant by counterclaim will incur those cost in any event in the prosecution of its claim; (b) the extent of the overlap between the claim and the counterclaim - this factor is related to the previous factor; (c) the extent to which the plaintiff's impecuniosity may be due to the actions of the defendant which form the basis for the plaintiff's claim. [14] The Shrunks also points to Trak Energy Corp. v. Happy Valley Resort, 2009 BCSC 1375 [Trak]. When the counterclaim is intertwined or overlaps with a plaintiff's claim, two matters arise: [11] First, it is appropriate to consider the degree to which the costs that the defendant seeks to have the plaintiff secure will be incurred by the defendant in any event in the prosecution of its counterclaim. Second, the potential for injustice is greater where there are significantly overlapping claims and where the effect of ordering security for costs may be to stifle one party's ability to advance their claim. [15] The Shrunks contends that, to find entitlement, I must find that there is an incremental costs to Keezio because of the counterclaim. [16] For the reasons that follow, I accept Keezio's argument that this is a case where entitlement is not an issue, only quantum. [17] The Shrunks has provided no evidence that it will be able to pay an adverse costs award. Requests have been made with no meaningful response respecting its ability to pay. [18] The case law is clear and settled that The Shrunks must show that it has exigible assets to satisfy an award of costs. In the case at bar, The Shrunks has not argued hardship or that security would unfairly stifle a valid claim. Therefore, those issues will not be considered in this analysis. [19] Each party has submitted one affidavit, both of which are from personnel at their respective law firms. The affidavit in support of Keezio largely consists of correspondence between counsel, corporate and land title searches and other searches to discern The Shrunks' financial assets in a draft bill of costs. [20] The affidavit in support of The Shrunks includes correspondence between counsel, the parties' list of documents, and some documents including The Shrunks' certificate of U.S. registration for a trademark of bed rails and an inflatable bed. [21] Keezio conducted various searches to gain some insight into The Shrunks' finances and its ability to pay a costs award. The Shrunks does not own any land, and it has not filed its annual return for two years. When a company fails to file its annual report for two consecutive years, the company may no longer be in good standing, and the Registrar of Companies may dissolve it. Due to the pandemic, dissolutions have been suspended. [22] The Shrunks' argument focused on whether Keezio had an arguable defence and the merits of each party's position. I accept that the threshold for whether Keezio has an arguable defence is low. As held in Canadian Jewish Congress at para. 19: [19] The plaintiff may also avoid an order for costs if it is evident on the record before the court that the defendant has not an arguable defence: Fat Mel's Restaurant Ltd. v. Canadian Northern Shield Insurance Co. (1993), 76 B.C.L.R. (2d) 231 (C.A.). In Ruko, supra, the court discussed the merits of the defence and the requirements thereof in a claim for security for costs. The court explained that all that is required is that a judge be satisfied that the defence is bona fide and presents an arguable case, not that it should win in any event. Spencer J. made these comments at p. 109: With respect to the merits of the defence, both plaintiff and defendants swear that their cases have a good chance of success. All that is required now is that I should be satisfied that the defence is bona fide and has an arguable case to present, not that it should win in any event. The strength or weakness of a defendant's case is a factor to be considered in ordering security, to be balanced with the factors of financial strength, delay, prejudice to the plaintiff and whether the plaintiff may be prevented by the financial burden of posting security from presenting its case at all. [23] The Shrunks argues that the counterclaim completely overlaps with the main claim, such that no extra work or time will be required to prosecute the counterclaim. The copyright claim is new to the counterclaim, but The Shrunks argues that the copyright claim is carved out of the scope of the security for costs application, since it is only advanced by the individual defendant, Mr. Cirjak, who is not subject to this application. [24] After examining some of the wording of the counterclaim, I agree with Keezio that it is not clear that copyright is not advanced by The Shrunks: Part 1, para. 6, and Part 2, Relief Sought at para. 15 (c). In any event, I accept that there will be additional costs in defending the counterclaim. [25] Keezio argues that if I find that there is an overlap between the claims, I could consider awarding 50 percent of the draft bill of costs in the amount of $37,177.45, similar to what occurred in Trak. [26] In reviewing the bill of costs, I agree with The Shrunks that the expert's report in the amount of $30,000 for accounting of profits and infringement will be required in any event for the main claim. Similarly, I note that the maximum is sought for many of the items and that many of the steps would be taken in any event for the main claim, such as the ten-day trial or discovery of documents. [27] Therefore, I order that The Shrunks post security in the amount of $25,000 within 14 days of this order. Until the security is posted, The Shrunks' counterclaim against Keezio is stayed. Keezio is permitted to apply for further security if the security posted becomes inadequate in relation to Keezio's anticipated costs as a defendant in the counterclaim. Keezio is entitled to its costs of this application. "Choi J."
