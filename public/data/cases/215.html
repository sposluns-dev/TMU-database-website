INDRASIGAMANY Agent(e) des tribunaux Designated Representative(s) Représentant(e)(s) désigné(e)(s) Counsel for the Minister Conseil du ministre INTRODUCTION XXXXX XXXXX is an 85-year-old citizen of Russia who has made a claim to Convention refugee status in Canada on the basis of her nationality, specifically, a member of a Jewish community. She has also claimed protection under Section 97.of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Act (IRPA). DETERMINATION Based on the evidence, documents, observations and submissions, I have determined that XXXXX XXXXX is neither a Convention refugee nor a person in need of international protection. ALLEGATIONS Ms. XXXXX alleges that: 1. Prior to Perestroika in 1992 she experienced no anti-Semitic discriminatory behaviour during her lifetime to that point. 2. With the advent of Perestroika in 1992, anti-Semitic social behaviour became evident and grew to a level she believes to be persecutorial in nature. 3. The anti-Semitic behaviour was exhibited by her fellow citizens and was not enshrined in any laws of which she was aware. 4. Her daughter was married in 1982 to a Jewish male and they relocated to Israel in 1994; then to Canada in 1999. The claimant was not aware of the circumstances that led to her daughter's acquiring Canadian citizenship. 5. Their family and the claimant's prior family during her formative years were non-religious. The identity of Jewishness was attached as an appendage nationality and was unimportant and largely insignificant until 1992. She had lived her entire life in Moscow, but travelled extensively in her job. 6. The claimant has no idea regarding the demographics of the "Jewish" population of Russia. 7. Following the death of her husband in 2003, the claimant became "more fearful" and for the first time mentioned it to her daughter. 8. The claimant has become progressively less mobile in the past 10 years resulting in the use of a wheelchair to attend the hearing. 9. The claimant wanted to get out of Russia to improve her lifestyle and had never considered relocating within the country. 10. She and her husband were never specifically targeted for harassment but were treated in a manner similar to other "Jewish" families in the apartment building. Telephone reports of incidents to the police yielded no results. She never filed a written report. 11. Verbal anti-Semitic harassment is "normal" public behaviour in her experience. This included harassing phone calls. 12. She applied for and received a visa to visit her daughter in Canada in XXXXX 2004 and made a refugee claim in March 2005. Prior to the March 2005 CIC interview, she had never mentioned any fear of any kind to any official of the Government of Canada. As a matter of fact, the CIC interviewer first suggested the refugee claim during that interview. 13. Her husband's death in 2003 was a turning point in her life and her fear and anxiety increased until she moved to her daughter's residence in Canada in 2004. She found her anxiety level significantly decreased when living with her daughter. She attributed this calming effect to the elimination of the source of her fear. She could now sleep restfully. 14. The IFA was not viable due to her limited mobility and interest in remaining in Russia. In addition to credibility, the key issues to be resolved are: 1. Who or what are the agents of persecution? 2. What caused the delay in making the claim? 3. An IFA in Vladivostock. ANALYSIS 1. The claimant's fear has arisen from the increasing public anti-Semitism she has experienced. This anti-Semitic behaviour on the part of her fellow citizens was not targeted at her (and her husband), but of a general nature. This is supported in both Exhibit C-4, item 35 and Exhibit R/A-1, section 13. While verbal and other forms of harassment are anti-social in substance, the behaviour Ms. XXXXX reported does not rise to the intensity of persecution. Other than two phone calls to the police, she took no action and reported that her neighbours experienced the same apparent indifference. Russia is not a lawless state and this type of harassment is discouraged1 but not acted upon usually. It does not constitute state complicity that the police are unable or unwilling to resolve these matters. 2. Her attribution to the removal of fear as being the cause of her "calmness" disregards completely the salutary effect of family reunification in Canada. Moreover, this tension or anxiety did not arise until her husband's demise suggesting the loneliness and fear normally associated with mortality might reasonably be considered to be the cause of her restiveness. 3. Not considering any relocation within Russia is consistent with her seeking to reunite with her daughter. Her limited mobility, she alleged, precluded such internal relocation. However, such limited mobility did not preclude her from travelling thousands of miles to reunite with her daughter in Canada. I am satisfied that she has not rebutted the potential IFA in Vladivostock. 4. That she first thought of a refugee claim as a result of her CIC interview in Etobicoke in March 2005 belies her assertions that she had a subjectively genuine fear in Russia, or that such fear was objectively reasonable. 5. The claimant, by her own testimony, was seeking a better place to live and raised the refugee claim as a means of staying in Canada; otherwise, she would have advised the Government of Canada that she was a person in need of protection at some time prior to her CIC interview. A reasonable person, holding a genuine fear, would not need to be prompted to express such a need especially in the presence of the protector she seeks. CONCLUSION For the aforementioned reasons, I have determined that XXXXX XXXXX is not a Convention refugee nor a person in need of protection. (signed) "Robert H. Rushowy" Robert H.
