DECISION [3] The panel finds that the claimants are not Convention refugees as they do not have a well-founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground in Hungary. The panel also finds that the claimants are not persons in need of protection in that their removal to Hungary would not subject them personally to a risk to their lives or to cruel and unusual treatment or punishment. Furthermore, on substantial grounds, there is insufficient evidence to believe that their removal to Hungary would subject them personally to a danger of torture. In determining this claim, the panel followed the Chairperson's Gender Guidelines. on Women Refugee Claimants Fearing Gender Related Persecution.3 IDENTITY [4] On a balance of probabilities, the panel finds that the claimant are citizens of Hungary based on certified copies of their passports as seen in Exhibit R/A-2.4 COUNSEL'S APPLICATIO PURSUANT TO RULE 50 [5] Counsel for the claimants submitted an application pursuant to Rule 50 on April 8, 2013 seeking the Board to make a fair determination and that the Board not endorse the actions of the then Minister of Immigration, that the designation of Hungary as a Designated Country of Origin contradicts significant country evidence and jurisprudence and that it will not be a factor in the assessment of Hungarian Roma refugee claims and that the Singh5 decision as well as section 7 of the Charter are inviolable parts of the refugee determination process to be considered in regards to the two previous points made. ANALYSIS [11] The determinative issues in these claims are credibility of the evidence submitted and state protection. On the issue of credibility, the panel is guided by the Federal Court of Appeal case in Sellan8 which stated the following: Where the Board makes a general finding that the claimant lacks credibility, that determination is sufficient to dispose of the claim unless there is independent and credible documentary evidence in the record capable of supporting a positive disposition of the claim. The claimant bears the onus of demonstrating there was such evidence. [12] Furthermore, the Federal Court notes the following: The Board is in a much better position than a reviewing court to gage the credibility and plausibility of a refugee claimant's story. A credibility finding is not a finding of mixed fact and law. It is a finding of fact, pure and simple. Findings of fact made by the Board may only be interfered with by a reviewing court if the finding was made in a perverse or capricious manner or without regard for the material before it (see the Federal Courts Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. F-7, paragraph 18.1(4)(d)). Indeed, it was Parliament's express intention that administrative fact finding would command this high degree of deference (see Canada (Citizenship and Immigration) v. Khosa, 2009 SCC 12, [2009] S.C.J. No. 12 (QL) at paragraph 46). Thus, credibility findings of the Board are to be reviewed against the statutory standard of review provided for in paragraph 18.1(4)(d) of the Federal Courts Act (see Diabo v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2004 FC 1772, [2004] F.C.J. No. 2168 (QL) at paragraph 3). [13] The majority of the evidence pertaining to the claims was provided by the adult male claimant with some evidence from the female claimant. The adult male claimant indicated that from 1991 to 2011 he worked temporary seasonal jobs in XXXX for 3 months at a time for the municipality in XXXX. When he was not employed, the claimant was in receipt of social assistance. Although the claimant states he tried, he was not successful in obtaining permanent work. He blames this due to the fact he is Roma. [14] The panel notes that the claimant stated he does not know if non Romas are engaged in temporary construction work and whether Romas are engaged in permanent construction work. Consequently, the claimant was asked how he knows he did not get permanent jobs because he is Roma. The claimant responded, "I believe when they saw our skin they said no more jobs." The panel is well aware by reading the documentation in Hungary that Romas are a disadvantaged group and suffer acts of discrimination. [15] The panel has insufficient credible and trustworthy evidence to conclude that the adult claimants and in particular the adult male claimant suffered acts discrimination that is tantamount to persecution, in regards to the inability to secure permanent employment. The claimant sought and obtained temporary employment and when the employment ended, he applied for and appeared to have met the requirements to receive social assistance by the municipality of XXXX. The panel has no evidence that the claimants, as Romas, were ever denied the opportunity to receive social assistance and left destitute due to being Roma. The panel makes a negative credibility finding as a consequence which negatively affects the claim as submitted. The panel does not have sufficient credible and trustworthy evidence to believe that the minor claimant who attended school or the adult claimants, when they attended school, suffered acts of discrimination that can be seen to be tantamount to persecution due to allegedly being segregated in the educational system in XXXX or anywhere else in the country for that matter. An absence of corroborating evidence, then, permitted it to make a negative inference against credibility of the applicant. The respondent relies on Sheik v. Canada (M.E.I.) The panel makes a negative credibility finding and believes that the adult son who returned to Hungary may have and will experience acts of racism. However, insufficient evidence was submitted that would show that the claimants' lives are at risk and that such acts of racism cumulatively would amount to persecution. [31] The burden of proof rests with the claimants in proving a well founded fear of persecution based on the allegations raised. In this case, the claimants have failed to meet their burden where it would be reasonable to conclude that they suffered persecution due to being Roma. [32] Quite frankly, the panel has no idea that the claimants were threatened by police officers or anyone else for that matter and that they suffered persecution and would continue to suffer persecution due to being Roma. STATE PROTECTION [33] In the alternative analysis, if the claims were credible which the panel does not believe it to be the case, the panel is of the view the claimants could reasonably seek and obtain state protection based on the evidence adduced. The burden rests with the claimants to show that in their particular circumstances state agents in Hungary could not or would not offer adequate state protection. The claimants bear the legal burden of rebutting the presumption that adequate state protection does not exist, by adducing clear and convincing evidence, that is reliable and probative, which satisfies the Board, on a balance of probabilities.10 [34] In looking at the documentary evidence, that being the United States Department of State report, as noted in item 2.111 of the index the following is noted. Hungary is a multiparty parliamentary democracy. Legislative authority is vested in the unicameral National Assembly (parliament). Parliament elects the president (the head of state) every five years. The president appoints a prime minister from the majority party or coalition following national elections every four years. Effective January 1, a single round national election system replaced the previous two-round system. The most recent parliamentary elections in April 2010 were assessed as free and fair. The conservative Fidesz-Christian Democrat coalition won a two-thirds parliamentary majority. Fidesz's prime ministerial candidate, Viktor Orban, took office in 2010. Security forces reported to civilian authorities. Among the most important human rights problems during the year were societal discrimination and exclusion of the Romani population, as well as violent right-wing extremism that included a strong anti-Semitic component. Discrimination against Roma continued to limit significantly their access to education, employment, health care, and social services. Right-wing extremism, including public campaigns by paramilitary groups to intimidate and incite hatred against Roma and other minorities, persisted. [35] The evidence before this panel is replete with documentation regarding the plight of Romas in the nation and the discrimination perpetrated against them primarily by right wing extremists and by some members of Hungarian society. It is despicable that there are persons in Hungary and elsewhere in the world who believe for whatever reason that they have an inherent right to impose their views on others as well as insulting, assaulting and belittling persons like the Roma or are connected to Romas. [36] The evidence in this case has to be assessed on its own merits with a view of determining whether there is a well founded fear of persecution or whether the claimants' lives are at risk pursuant to section 97 of the IRPA. [37] While the claimants stated that contact was made with the police when the alleged problems occurred, the panel has little credible and trustworthy evidence that the claimants approached state agents above the police, if the police in fact did little to assist them, with a view of the appropriate state agents to take action. [38] While it is disconcerting to the panel that right-wing extremist groups target and attack Romas, the panel was not provided with credible and trustworthy evidence that the claimants as Romas suffered acts of aggression from right-wing extremist groups. [39] The panel is not in a position to conclude that the claimants could not obtain adequate state protection and that sufficient steps were taken to exhaust state protection. [40] In a functioning democracy as Hungary is, a claimant will have a heavy burden when attempting to show that they should not have been required to exhaust all of the recourses available to them domestically before claiming refugee status.12 Therefore, it is not unreasonable to expect the claimants to do more, other than merely show that contact was made with members of the police force and that those efforts were not forthcoming or were not successful. The claimants must show that they have taken all reasonable steps in the circumstances to seek protection, taking into account the context of the country of origin, as well as the claimant's interactions with the authorities.13 [41] The panel notes that the issue pertaining to state protection has been thoroughly addressed by the courts, and in particular, the Supreme Court in Ward14. In order to succeed on the notion that the state is unable to offer protection, the claimants must show, on a balance of probabilities, that they are unable, or by reason of such fear of persecution, unwilling to avail themselves of the protection of their country of nationality. [42] Furthermore, the protection offered by the state need not be perfect nor can a state protect its citizens all the time.15 The issue of state protection goes to the objective portion of the test of fear of persecution.16 There is a presumption that every state is able to protect its citizens unless the state is unable due to a complete breakdown of the state apparatus which is not the case in Hungary. In this particular case, the claimants have not rebutted the presumption of state protection and the panel does not support the notion that the claimants could not obtain adequate protection from the police and higher state agents in Hungary in a forward looking analysis. The panel comes to this view by also factoring in some of the documentary evidence which highlights the problems of the police and the like in Hungary as they address the situation of Romas. [43] While reviewing the documentary evidence from the Board and counsel, the panel recognizes the problems as it relates to discrimination and racist attacks against Romas, those affiliated with Romas in Hungary as well as the future possibility of discrimination in all spheres of life for Romas in Hungary. However, the claimants cannot rebut the presumption of state protection in a functioning democracy by asserting only a subjective reluctance to engage the state or a brief attempt to seek state protection. Doubting the effectiveness of the protection offered by the state when one has not really tested it does not rebut the existence of a presumption of state protection17. [44] The fact that state protection is not perfect18 does not constitute clear and convincing proof of the state's inability to protect its citizens, since no state can guarantee the protection of its citizens at all times. Local failures to provide effective policing do not amount to a lack of state protection unless they are part of a broader pattern of the state's inability or unwillingness to provide protection.19 In this particular case, the panel has insufficient evidence to conclude that the claimant and or family members contacted the police for assistance and that adequate assistance was not provided. [45] There could have been a variety of reasons, unbeknownst to the panel why the police were not able to adequately assist the claimants. Even with police failures as described by the claimants, insufficient evidence was submitted to indicate that there was a lack of will or effort from the state to appropriately assist the claimants. [46] In looking at the evidence further, it is clear that there are serious concerns about how the government of Hungary addresses the problems that Romas face in many areas in the nation. It is also noteworthy that Hungary could do more regarding the plight of Romas. [47] Even though the claimant alleges to have sought state protection which was not allegedly forthcoming, Hungary is a functioning democracy with a willingness to provide a measure of protection to its citizens. The documentary evidence on Hungary in regards to the issue of Romas is mixed. However, the panel is pleased that there are increased efforts to assist and provide Romas with adequate protection as well as employment, educational and other social assistance services. The presumption of state protection applies equally to cases where the state is alleged to be a persecutor20. [48] The panel recognizes that the Federal Court has stated that it is not enough to say that steps are being taken which one day may result in adequate state protection. It is what state protection is actually provided at the present time that is relevant21. The panel also recognizes that regard must be given to what is actually happening and not what the state is endeavouring to put in place.22 Any efforts must have "actually translated into adequate state protection" at the operational level.23 [49] In response to the guidance from the Court, the documentary evidence reports that: Hungary has one of the most advanced anti-discrimination laws and a system for minority protection in the Central and Eastern European region. A number of mechanisms have been developed to ensure that minority groups enjoy civil and political rights. However, the central government's general failure to maintain strong and effective control mechanisms over rights violations takes its toll on Hungary's largest minority, the Roma.24 [50] Hungary has recently launched a number of new initiatives. The documentary evidence does not indicate whether or not these measures are successful at the operational level. The panel is encouraged though by these initiatives implemented as the government recognizes that more work is required to assist Romas in a variety of ways. However, at this point and time it is too early to make such a determination since these are brand new initiatives: During the year the state secretary for social integration at the Ministry of Public Administration and Justice, Zoltán Balog, continued to play a critical role in advancing Roma affairs within the government. The office harmonized the government's inclusion policy as well as that governing Roma-related government programs (e.g., scholarships, Decade of Roma Inclusion Program). The Ministry of National Resources continued to offer financial incentives to encourage schools to integrate Romani and non-Romani children in the same classrooms and to reintegrate Roma inappropriately placed in remedial programs. On September 26, the government established the 27-member Roma Coordination Council, chaired by the minister for public administration and justice and co-chaired by the head of national Roma self-government, Florian Farkas, who was elected on January 20. The new council includes representatives of local Roma self-governments, NGOs, and churches. Most ministries and county labor affairs centers had special officers for Romani affairs focused on the needs of the Romani community. On November 30, the cabinet approved the National Social Inclusion Strategy. The national strategy identifies specific actions the government aims to take to reduce the percentage of the population living under the poverty line, integrate Roma into the labor market, and increase the level of education of Roma. On December 13, the cabinet adopted the Governmental Action Plan for the implementation of the National Social Inclusion Strategy for 2012-2014. The action plan determines specific tasks, identifies responsible members of the cabinet, and sets deadlines in the areas of child welfare, education, employment, health care, housing, raising awareness, and fighting discrimination against Roma. On December 19, parliament passed a new law on "nationalities," scheduled to enter into force in January 2012. The new law defines the cultural autonomy of the nationalities and recognizes as collective rights the fostering and enrichment of historic traditions, language, culture, educational rights, as well as establishing and operating institutions and maintaining international contacts. Roma and the other 12 official minorities are entitled to elect their own minority self-governments to organize minority activities and handle cultural, educational, and linguistic affairs. The president of each minority self-government has the right to attend and speak at local government assemblies.25 [51] In reviewing the above documentary evidence, there is an indication that the government is motivated and willing to implement policies and procedures to protect the Roma as much as reasonably possible. The panel does acknowledge that policies and procedures are not always implemented efficiently and effectively at the local or municipal level. [52] Again as referenced earlier, the documentary evidence relating to government efforts to protect the Roma and to legislate against broader forms of discrimination and persecution is mixed. However, recognizing that the documentary evidence is mixed, in the circumstances particular to these claims as so presented, the claimants have not presented evidence in a clear and convincing fashion that state protection in Hungary is so inadequate that they need not have approached the authorities at all. [53] The claimants did not make reasonable efforts to seek and exhaust state protection from people higher in authority, or take complaints to organizations and bodies with powers of oversight, such as the Minorities Ombudsman's Office or the Independent Police Complaints Board (IPCB),26 before seeking international protection in Canada. [54] It is recognized that the Board's documents indicate that the systems in place to make appropriate complaints pertaining to police inaction for example, do so exist with a view to preparing reports and make and report findings back to the appropriate authorities for their response. The documents also indicate that final resolutions to complaints appear to have a smaller number of resolutions in favour of the complainant. This is not a matter that is before the panel since it does not have the facts relating to these cases, only the reported numbers27. [55] The Board recognizes that there are some inconsistencies among several sources within the documentary evidence. However, the objective evidence regarding current country conditions suggests that, although not perfect and knowing perfection is not required, there is insufficient evidence in these cases that adequate state protection in Hungary for the claimants does not exist. In the panel's view, Hungary is making serious efforts to address these problems, and that the police and government officials are both willing and able to protect victims even taking into account some police inaction as so alleged by the claimants. The following is noted from item 2.1 of the index. The National Police Headquarters (NPH), which operates under the direction of the Ministry of Interior, is responsible for maintaining order nationwide. Nineteen county police departments and the Budapest police headquarters are directly subordinate to the NPH; city police are subordinate to the county police and have local jurisdiction. Two hierarchically equal units are directly subordinate to the minister of interior: the Counterterrorism Center, which is responsible for protecting the prime minister and the president and for preventing and investigating terrorist acts, including kidnapping and hijacking, and the National Defense Service (NDS). The Hungarian Defense Force is subordinate to the Ministry of Defense and is responsible for external security as well as aspects of domestic security and disaster response.28 [56] While there are reports of police corruption and that police sometimes use excessive force against Roma, the evidence29 also shows that the state takes action when complaints are made. On January 1, the new NDS commenced operations aimed at eliminating corruption within law enforcement agencies, replacing the former Defense Service of Law Enforcement Agencies. The new NDS had increased authority, including the authority to use covert intelligence tools, and operated under the direct supervision of the minister of interior and the prosecutor general. Organized citizen groups, such as neighborhood and town watches, played an important role in helping police prevent crime. At the beginning of the year, far-right extremists continued to take advantage of the law to form vigilante groups and conduct patrols in smaller towns in eastern Hungary, apparently to intimidate the local Roma population. On April 23, the government issued a decree providing for fines of up to 100,000 forints ($414) for any failure of local neighbourhood watch members to cooperate with the police. On May 2, parliament amended the penal code to increase sentences for unauthorized law enforcement activities. According to the amended code, anyone who organizes an unauthorized law enforcement effort commits an offense punishable by up to two years in prison. On November 29, parliament amended the law in order to require neighbourhood watch groups to complete a written agreement with relevant police stations. The prosecutor's office maintained legal control over the operation of the neighbourhood watch groups and could initiate legal proceedings at court upon the lack of the written cooperation agreement with the police. Civilian authorities maintained effective control over police, the NDS, and the armed forces, and the government has effective mechanisms to investigate and punish abuse and corruption. There were no reports of security forces acting with impunity. While there were no reports of impunity, the HHC noted that there was a great disparity between the number of indictments of members of security forces alleged to have committed abuses and the indictment of persons alleged to have committed violent acts against officials. In the first six months of the year, only 6 percent of complaints of abuse by members of the security forces resulted in an indictment, while 76 percent of alleged acts of violence against an official person resulted in an indictment. There was also a significant disparity between the conviction rate of members of the security forces charged with a crime (60 percent) and the conviction rate for persons indicted for violence against an official person (96 percent). The Military Prosecutor's Office is responsible for conducting proceedings involving any member of the armed forces charged with a criminal offense. On November 28, parliament amended the law integrating the formerly independent Military Prosecutor's Office into a united Prosecutor's Office under the supervision of the Central Investigative Chief Prosecutor's Office. The law was scheduled to come into effect in 2012. In the first nine months of the year, authorities found 3,022 police officers responsible for breaches of discipline, 766 guilty of petty offenses, 283 guilty of criminal offenses, and 10 unfit for duty. In the same period, courts sentenced four police officers to prison terms, gave suspended sentences to 39, fined 106, and dismissed 12. In the same period, courts convicted 37 officers of corruption. No information was available on the number placed on probation. Victims of lesser police abuses may complain either to the alleged violator's unit or to the Independent Police Complaints Board (IPCB), which investigated violations and omissions by the police that affected fundamental rights. The five-member body, appointed by a two-thirds majority of parliament, functions independently of police authorities. At year's end the board had received 805 reports from the public. It reviewed 458 complaints (including some cases filed in 2010) and found serious legal violations in 67 and minor legal violations in 33. The board forwarded the 67 cases to the national police chief, who agreed with the findings in two cases, partially accepted the findings in three, and rejected the findings in three. The rest remained pending. The IPCB's authority is limited to making recommendations to the NPH and reporting its findings to parliament.30 [57] Therefore, regarding the totality of the evidence before the panel, while there is evidence to indicate that police do still commit abuses against people, including the Roma, the evidence also demonstrates that it is reasonable to expect authorities to take action in these cases and that the police are willing, able and capable of protecting Roma and that there are organizations in place to ensure that the police are held to account. STATE RESPONSE TO DISCRIMINATION AGAINST ROMA [58] Aside from the issue pertaining to the adequacy of state protection in Hungary, Human rights reports state that Romas continue to face discrimination in education, employment, housing, and access to social services. In addition, Romas reportedly experience discrimination in accessing public places such as bars and restaurants, the Equal Treatment Authority (ETA), an independent organization established by the Hungarian government that is tasked with investigating complaints of discrimination, notes that denial of service in commercial establishments is almost exclusively experienced by the Roma. Failures to act by local authorities and, in particular, failures in implementing centrally enacted legislation in conformity with the prohibition on discrimination continue to lie at the heart of much of the discrimination experienced by Romas in daily life.31 [59] The Parliamentary Commissioner for National and Ethnic Minority Rights takes complaints from any person that feels that, as a result of a government agency's decision, proceedings or negligence, a violation has taken place in her/his national or ethnic minority rights, or that the direct threat of such a violation is imminent. The documentary evidence indicates that many other similar initiatives have been undertaken by the Hungarian government to address the problems of corruption within its police forces and to provide measures or avenues of complaint to the public.32 [60] There is also recourse to the Equal Treatment Authority. Since 2005 it has provided individuals with a direct avenue of redress for violations of the prohibition of discrimination in a variety of public and private law relationships. In the first year, there were nearly 500 complaints lodged and the number has risen steadily ever since. There are also other remedies such as seeking compensation through the courts, or turning to one of the Parliamentary Commissioners where public authorities are concerned.33 There is also the Roma Police Officers' Association in Hungary where Romas are able to file a complaint to the association. The complaints mostly concern discrimination in employment, discriminatory treatment, and discrimination by law enforcement authorities or police officers.34 In practice, Roma continue to face both a disproportionately high rate of unemployment and discrimination in access to employment. In the fields of education and housing, the efforts of the central authorities are frequently hampered by the manner in which local authorities translate the measures taken into practice: numerous abuses have been reported, and the central authorities appear somewhat hamstrung in their efforts to achieve change. Roma families are deprived of access to social housing by discriminatory rules and practices of local authorities; and Roma children are still confronted with segregation in schools, which has a devastating impact on education outcomes for these children and leaves them with correspondingly limited future life choices and employment prospects.35 [61] The panel has little or no credible evidence that the claimants filed complaints to the appropriate body or agency as it pertains to discrimination in educational institutions, employment, housing and the like due to being Roma. If the claimants continues to face discrimination in the future, which is likely, they have access to a variety of avenues for assistance should they need it. The Hungarian government has taken a number of legal and institutional measures to improve the situation of the Romas in the nation. [62] According to the documentary evidence, there are criticisms on how Romas are treated. In light of criticisms, the documentary evidence indicates the following: A substantial reform of education, which included renewal of pedagogical methods, revision of legislation on education concerning multiple disadvantages and financial incentives, began in 2003. According to the Minorities Ombudsman, through the Roma Integration Policy, the Hungarian government provides financial support for schools that have special programs for integration of disadvantaged students, including Roma, non-native, and disabled children. A report of the working group on the UN Universal Periodic Review states that in order to fight segregation and decrease school dropout rates, Hungary implemented several programmes in 1,800 education institutions; the budget assigned for this purpose was about US$ 40 million. Similarly, the Hungarian government indicates that by 2010, those programs "covered almost 300,000 pupils and reached approximately 13,000 teachers in 1,800 schools". Hungary's Social Renewal Operation Programme (SROP) 2007-2013, co-financed by the European Union, includes the Promotion of Implementation of Equal Opportunities Programmes that are designed to improve the situation of children with multiple disadvantages. For example, SROP co-ordinates and supports programs related to the development of methodological documents, teacher training courses, and extracurricular activities that encourage opportunities of informal learning. The Open Society Foundations (OSF), an international organization that works to build democracies with accountable governments and implements initiatives at the local level in its study on Roma integration strategies, points out that the SROP project is a positive policy example.36 [63] Hungary faces criticism regarding the implementation of the laws it has enacted to address the discrimination and persecution of its minorities, especially the Roma. While there may be motivation within the central government to have its laws enforced, there is difficulty in implementing the enforcement of these laws at the local level, and resources routinely fail to reach the groups with the greatest needs. The criticism against Hungary may be appropriate in some circumstances. However, it is noted that Hungary is a part of the European Union and is therefore responsible for upholding a number of various standards and initiatives to maintain its membership in the Union. [64] For example, the European Commission against Racism and Intolerance (ECRI) was established by the Council of Europe. It is an independent human rights monitoring body specializing in questions relating to racism and intolerance. It is composed of independent and impartial members, who are appointed on the basis of their moral authority and recognized expertise in dealing with racism, xenophobia, anti-Semitism and intolerance. The ECRI published a report on Hungary in which it gives praise to Hungary for its accomplishments, cites issues of concern, and gives recommendation for future action.37 Hungary is a responsible member of the European Union and reports regularly to the appropriate structures within that Union. The Republic of Hungary was one of the first signatories to the Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities of the Council of Europe and deposited its instrument of ratification on 25 September 1995. The Parliament of the Republic of Hungary ratified the Framework Convention in 1999.38 [65] Even if criticism of Hungary's measures to combat racism is warranted, particularly against the Romani population, on a balance of probabilities, Hungary is taking measures to implement the standards that are mandated as a member of the European Union. [66] The panel has reviewed the Chairperson's Guidelines on gender related persecution. However, given the concerns raised earlier in these reasons pertaining to the issue of credibility and state protection, the panel cannot come to the conclusion that the adult female claimant meets the elements as indicated in the Gender Guidelines which may off-set any deficiencies referenced earlier in these reasons. [67] The panel notes a recent decision from the Federal Court, from Justice Zinn dated December 7, 2012, Orgona,39 who writes that there is inadequate state protection for Romas. [68] While the panel has taken the decision of the Court under advisement, every case must be assessed on its own merits. The panel notes a more recent decision from the Court on this issue, Kotai40. [69] Simply, after reviewing all the evidence, inclusive of counsel's written submissions as it pertains to a well-founded fear of persecution or that the claimants are in need of protection pursuant to section 97 of the IRPA, there has not been a successful argument made to suggest a positive determination in this regard. [70] In Li,41 the Federal Court stated "pursuant to subsection 97(1) there must be persuasive evidence that is on a balance of probabilities establishing the facts on which a claimant relies, to say that he or she faces a substantial danger of being tortured on his or her return. Second, the danger or risk must be such that it is more likely than not that he or she would be tortured or subjected to other cruel or other degrading treatments." This is not the case in these claims. [71] In Bouaouni,42 the Federal Court stated "a claim under section 97 must be evaluated with respect to all the relevant considerations and with a view to the country's human rights record." In evaluating these claims under section 97 and considering the human rights record of Hungary, the panel has not been persuaded that the claimants would face a substantial danger of being tortured, or that the risk and/or danger is such that it would be more likely than not that the claimants would be tortured or subjected to cruel or other degrading treatments, based on what has already been mentioned earlier in these reasons. [72] Therefore, after a thorough review of these cases, the claimants have failed to meet their onus that they have a well founded fear of persecution for a Convention ground or that they are in need of protection in a forward looking analysis. CONCLUSION [73] Based on the evidence adduced at this hearing, inclusive of counsel's written submissions of July 22, 2013, the panel finds that the claimants are neither Convention refugees nor persons in need of protection. The cumulative reasons referred to above as it relates to the issue of credibility and state protection does not establish that the claimants have a reasonable chance or serious possibility of persecution for a Convention ground if required to return to Hungary today. The panel also finds for the same reasons that the claimants are not in need of protection. The panel rejects these claims pursuant to both sections 96 and 97(1) of the IRPA. (signed) "K.
