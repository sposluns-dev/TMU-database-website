Decision

[1]
The Member of the Social Security Tribunal, General Division, Employment Insurance Section found that the Commission did not prove that the Appellant departed his employment voluntarily on a balance of probabilities.

Introduction

[2]
The Appellant filed an initial claim for benefits on August 12, 2013 (GD3-19). The claim was established effective August 11, 2013 (GD4-1).
[3]
The Canada Employment Insurance Commission (the “Commission”) decided on September 12, 2013, that it could not pay the Appellant employment insurance benefits because he did not prove that he had just cause for departing his employment voluntarily (GD3-26).
[4]
The Appellant filed a request for reconsideration. On October 8, 2013, the Commission reconsidered its original decision and decided to maintain it (GD3-33).
[5]
The Appellant filed an appeal to the Tribunal on October 28, 2013 and submitted a copy of the reconsideration letter on March 7, 2014 (GD-2).
[6]
On June 19, 2014, (by way of an interlocutory decision), the Tribunal granted the Appellant an extension of time and allowed the appeal to proceed (GD7).
[7]
On August 5, 2014, the Tribunal added the Employer as a party to the appeal because it determined that the Employer had a direct interest in the outcome of the appeal pursuant to section 10 of the Social Security Tribunal Regulations SOR/2013-60 (the “SST Regulations”) (GD8).

Form of hearing

[8]
The hearing was scheduled to be held by teleconference for the reasons indicated in the Notice of Hearing dated August 29, 2014.

Issue

[9]
Whether or not the Appellant voluntarily departed his employment with just cause pursuant to paragraph 29(c) of the Employment Insurance Act, S.C. 1996, c. 23 (the “Act”)?

The law

[10]
Section 30(1) of the Act provides for an indefinite disqualification when a claimant voluntarily leaves his/her employment without just cause. It provides as follows:

30. (1) A claimant is disqualified from receiving any benefits if the claimant lost any employment because of their misconduct or voluntarily left any employment without just cause, unless

a) the claimant has, since losing or leaving the employment, been employed in insurable employment for the number of hours required by section 7 or 7.1 to qualify to receive benefits; or b) the claimant is disentitled under sections 31 to 33 in relation to the employment.

[11]
Subsection 29(c) of the Act provides that just cause is held to exist where the claimant had no reasonable alternative to leaving or taking leave, having regard to all of the circumstances, including, the circumstances which are enumerated in subparagraphs (i) to (xiv) of subsection 29 (c), which provide as follows:

(i) sexual or other harassment, (ii) obligation to accompany a spouse, common-law partner or dependent child to another residence, (ii) discrimination on a prohibited ground of discrimination within the meaning of the Canadian Human Rights Act, (iv) working conditions that constitute a danger to health or safety, (v) obligation to care for a child or a member of the immediate family, (vi) reasonable assurance of another employment in the immediate future, (vii) significant modification of terms and conditions respecting wages or salary, (viii) excessive overtime work or refusal to pay for overtime work, (ix) significant changes in work duties, (x) antagonism with a supervisor if the claimant is not primarily responsible for the antagonism, (xi) practices of an employer that are contrary to law, (xii) discrimination with regard to employment because of membership in an association, organization or union of workers, (xiii) undue pressure by an employer on the claimant to leave their employment, and (xiv) any other reasonable circumstances that are prescribed.

[12]
Section 30(2) and (3) specify the following regarding the effect of the disqualification:

(2) The disqualification is for each week of the claimant’s benefit period following the waiting period and, for greater certainty, the length of the disqualification is not affected by any subsequent loss of employment by the claimant during the benefit period.

(3) If the event giving rise to the disqualification occurs during a benefit period of the claimant, the disqualification does not include any week in that benefit period before the week in which the event occurs.

[13]
According to the jurisprudence, the Commission first has to prove that the claimant voluntarily departed from his or her employment on a balance of probabilities. Once the voluntary separation has been established, the burden then shifts to the claimant to prove on a balance of probabilities that s/he had just cause for leaving the employment and that considering all of the circumstances, that s/he had no reasonable alternative to leaving (White, 2011 FCA 190; Patel 2010 FCA 95; Rena Astronomo A-141-97; Tanguay A-1458-84; Bell A-450-95; Landry A-1210-92).

He was earning $14.01 an hour. He was no longer working because he quit due to health or other medical reasons (Application for Benefits, GD3-2 to GD3-2 and record of employment dated October 2, 2013 (“RO E” ) the reason was listed as Code “E”, GD3-22).

There was no acknowledgement of his letter. On August 1, 2013, one week later, he informed the Employer that he was withdrawing his letter of resignation as he had written that he was suffering from depression. The Employer advised that it would get back to him. He wanted the letter to constitute official notice that he was withdrawing his resignation and would be remaining with the Employer. The Employer told him verbally that his employment would be terminated effective August 9, 2013. His health condition impacted his ability to work on July 3, 2013. It was too much work to do and it was not possible to complete his work. He had to resolve his problem and not aggravate it. Although he felt slightly better and was able to work, he could not find work and he and his family were under a great deal of financial stress. He was really hoping to rely on his employment insurance benefits to assist him in that difficult time.
[27]
The Appellant confirmed that he did not discuss the situation or his stress level with his Employer prior to submitting his letter of resignation. The Appellant also confirmed that he did not seek other employment or ask for an earlier vacation or period of leave.

The Employer said that in the days following the Appellant’s resignation, the decision was made to refuse to accept the withdrawal in the context where its operations were slimming down and its funding was being cut. The Employer stated that in its “strategic planning, it decided to eliminate the Appellant’s position from the organization”.
[30]
The Appellant also explained that he was now only working part time and that he was continuing to struggle with his employment and financial situation.

Submissions

[31]
The Appellant submitted that he had just cause for voluntarily leaving his employment, for the following reasons:

a) He had just cause for leaving on account of health reasons (Request for Reconsideration, GD3-30); b) He found himself in a very stressful situation such that leaving was the only solution (Request for Reconsideration, GD3-30); c) He consulted a doctor regarding leaving his work on August 1, 2013 (Request for Reconsideration, GD3-30); d) With respect to the medical note at GD3-29, the doctor confirmed that his insomnia problem was caused by his workplace stress and these are key elements, which caused him to quit his job. In quitting his job, he put an end to a situation, which caused him a great deal of stress (Request for Reconsideration, GD3-30); e) He strongly believes that he should be paid employment insurance benefits because he paid employment insurance premiums for 14 years (GD2)

[32]
The Respondent submitted as follows:

a) Subsection 30(2) of the Act provides for an indefinite disqualification when the claimant voluntarily leaves his or her employment without just cause. The test to be applied, having regard to all of the circumstances, is whether the claimant had a reasonable alternative to leaving her employment when s/he did. The Appellant had the burden to prove that there was no reasonable alternative (White 2011 FCA 190) (GD4-3 to 4-4); b) The Appellant provided several medical certificates, which confirmed that he was suffering from depression and other related troubles, such as stress, insomnia, adjustment disorder and anxious mood. None of the medical certificates, indicate, however, that he had to quit his job permanently (GD4-3); and, c) The Appellant did not have just cause for leaving his employment on August 9, 2013, because he failed to exhaust all reasonable alternatives prior to leaving. A reasonable alternative to leaving would have been to ask for sick leave, a leave of absence, or vacation, and to have discussed the situation with the Employer prior to having resigned (GD4-4).

Analysis

[33]
It has long been held that the rationale for the general rule, that employees who voluntarily terminate the employment relationship are not entitled to employment insurance benefits, (save and except for in the exceptional circumstances enumerated in the legislation), is that the Act is in essence “insurance” against involuntary unemployment and that an essential rule of insurance is that an “assured shall not deliberately create or increase the risk” (Crewe (1982) 2 All E.R. 745 per Lords Donaldson and Denning) (Tanguay A-1458-84).
[34]
This rationale is also reflected in the dicta of Pratte J. in Tanguay, wherein he stated that to prove “just cause” the claimant must satisfy the Tribunal that s/he had no reasonable alternative than to place “himself on the roles of the unemployed for insurance purposes” (Tanguay A-1458-84, Pratte J.). Proving that there was no reasonable alternative at the time of the departure, is a requirement of the test for just cause and is written into subsection 29 (c) of the Act.
[35]
The law is clear that the employment insurance scheme is not intended to be used to subsidize employees who depart voluntarily for personal reasons and create risks for reasons which do not amount to just cause (Lanteigne 2009 FCA 195).

Did the Appellant depart voluntarily?

[36]
The Tribunal finds as a fact that the Commission has not proven on a balance of probabilities that the Appellant left his employment voluntarily.
[37]
The Tribunal is aware that there is a line of jurisprudence, which holds that a claimant is considered to have voluntarily terminated the employment relationship when a notice of resignation is tendered and an employer has accepted it. This has been held to be the case even where the claimant later recants, retracts, or regrets the decision (CUB 25359A Reader, (1992); (CUB 22680); (1994); (CUB 26595 (1994); (CUB 27424 (1995); (CUB 29407)(1995); (CUB 39743 (1997); and, CUB 50894 Fraser (1999).
[38]
While CUB decisions can be influential, the Tribunal is not bound by them. For the most part, the Tribunal does not disagree with the line of jurisprudence. The Tribunal actually finds that when the mechanics of the operation of the burden of proof are examined more closely, what is really happening is that a presumption is arising, which is allowing the fact finder to presume that the resignation was tendered voluntarily. While there are many reasons why the employer, Commission and Tribunal should be able to presume that ordinarily, a person who is tendering a resignation, is doing so voluntarily, the presumption of voluntariness should also be capable of being rebutted in exceptional circumstances, such as the Appellant’s.
[39]
In this regard, the foregoing line of jurisprudence could be distinguished from the Appellant’s case because the degree of voluntariness inherent in the claimants’ resignations was not called into question in the jurisprudence and because the Tribunal finds that the Appellant has rebutted any presumption of voluntariness sufficiently.
[40]
In the Appellant’s case, the Appellant’s own evidence (his submissions, his letter of withdrawal and his testimony) (Brisebois A-510-96, CUB 72676 (2009), CUB 65097, (2005), CUB 59867, (2004), CUB 47822, (1999), CUB 44203, (1999)) and the medical evidence, show that when the Appellant tendered his resignation, he was reacting involuntarily to his own trauma, which was caused or exacerbated by the stress at his workplace. That the workplace was stressful at the time of the Appellant’s resignation, was also confirmed by the Employer in the Employer’s testimony. That the Appellant was enduring his own trauma was corroborated by the Appellant’s medical evidence. The Tribunal finds that all of this evidence, casts tremendous doubt on the Appellant’s ability to control or understand the nature or effect of his own actions when he submitted the letter of resignation.
[41]
That the Appellant was not operating with a sufficiently clear or sound mind when he tendered his resignation, was obvious from his own statements (GD2, GD2-12, and GD3) and from the medical notes and records, which confirm that he was suffering from an adjustment disorder, insomnia, and anxiety and work place stress when he tendered his resignation letter. That this was entirely out of character and not at all intended is also obvious from his attempt to withdraw his resignation on August 5, 2013 and from his long employment history of 14 years with the same Employer (GD2-12).
[42]
The Tribunal finds that while someone without anxiety, insomnia, depression, an adjustment disorder and mood disorder can be made to face the consequences of their decisions made in haste or recklessness, because they could be seen to have caused the risk of the unemployment situation to have occurred, the same could not be said for the Appellant, only because the Appellant was not acting recklessly or voluntarily. The Appellant’s ability to think rationally and conduct himself in his best interests or as a reasonable person would have done in the circumstances, was impaired clearly by his mental illness.
[43]
The Tribunal also notes that unlike the line of jurisprudence, which the Tribunal reviewed, the Employer did not testify that it refused to accept the withdrawal because it was offended by the Appellant’s resignation or because it found that the Appellant was acting passive aggressively or tendering his resignation as a form of an ultimatum or as part of a negotiation strategy. The Employer did not even mention that it found that the employment relationship was beyond repair following the Appellant’s resignation. What the Employer did state, was that it refused to accept the withdrawal because it made a decision, (presumably after the resignation was tendered) to eliminate the Appellant’s position for financial and administrative reasons.
[44]
In this regard, the Appellant cannot be said to have committed misconduct when he tendered his resignation (Easson A-1598-92; Brisette A-1342-92, Lemire 2010 FCA 314) nor could mala fides or bad faith be imputed to him. As stated above, the Appellant’s conduct was also not sufficiently voluntary or deliberate or conscious or reckless to amount to misconduct in any event (Easson A-1598-92; Brisette A-1342-92; Lemire 2010 FCA 314).
[45]
The Tribunal finds that the Appellant only realized that he did not intend to resign or that he did not resign voluntarily after he sought medical attention. The Tribunal finds that the Appellant should not be castigated for not having sought medical attention earlier because, but for the Appellant’s resignation, he may not have become aware of the extent or the effect of his depression and mood disorder. What is important, is that the Appellant attempted to rectify the situation as soon as he became cognizant of the nature and effect of his actions (Brisebois A-510- 96, CUB 72676 (2009)).
[46]
As such, while the Tribunal has no doubt that the Appellant’s conduct created the risk or caused the risk to occur, it cannot find that the Appellant did so voluntarily or that he conducted himself voluntarily in the circumstances such that the Appellant himself caused the risk to occur. Put another way, although the Appellant provoked the Employer into making a quick decision, the provocation was not sufficiently voluntary. The Appellant’s conduct was not even a factor, which the Employer considered, when it decided not to accept the Appellant’s withdrawal. This is a unique and exceptional situation. In the circumstances, the Commission has proven that the Appellant departed his employment. It has not, however, proven that the Appellant did so voluntarily on a balance of probabilities.
[47]
As the burden of proving that the Appellant left his job voluntarily has not been satisfied, the Tribunal is not required to consider whether the Appellant had just cause for “voluntarily leaving” his employment (Patel 2010 FCA 95; Bell A-450-95; Landry A-1210-92).
[48]
The Tribunal notes parenthetically, that in the hypothetical, had it found that the Appellant did depart voluntarily, it would have been prepared to have found that the Appellant did so with just cause pursuant to paragraph 29(c)(iv) of the Act (White 2011 FCA 190). This is the case even though it appears, at first blush, that the Appellant had not exhausted his reasonable alternatives. The Tribunal would have also of had been prepared to have found that there were no other reasonable alternatives given the Appellant’s inability to cope with his situation or to communicate effectively with his Employer (Brisebois A-510-96, CUB 72676 (2009). To the Appellant, the situation was “manifestly intolerable” (CUB 20926 (1992), CUB 16437, CUB 17738).
